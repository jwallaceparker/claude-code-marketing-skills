# Breakthrough Advertising — Principles for Execution

This is not a summary. These are execution-level principles extracted for skills in copywriting, positioning, and briefs.

---

## 1. Levels of Awareness

### What it is
Your prospect exists at one of five awareness levels. This determines your opening, your assumptions, and how much work your copy must do.

| Level | What they know | Your job |
|-------|----------------|----------|
| **Most Aware** | Know you, trust you, ready to buy | Make the offer. Don't oversell. |
| **Product-Aware** | Know your product, not convinced | Prove you're the best choice. Differentiate. |
| **Solution-Aware** | Know solutions exist, don't know you | Connect your product to the solution they're already seeking. |
| **Problem-Aware** | Feel the pain, don't know solutions exist | Crystallize the problem, then reveal the solution. |
| **Unaware** | Don't know they have a problem | Start with their world, not yours. Enter through identity or story. |

### When it matters
- Writing headlines and opening lines
- Deciding how much education vs. selling
- Structuring the brief's Problem and Differentiation sections
- Choosing which objections to address

### What Claude gets wrong by default
- **Writes to Solution-Aware when audience is Problem-Aware.** Jumps to features before establishing the pain.
- **Over-explains to Most Aware audiences.** Wastes their time with education they don't need.
- **Uses product language with Unaware audiences.** They don't care about your solution yet because they don't feel the problem.
- **Assumes everyone is at the same level.** Different segments need different entry points.

### What good execution looks like
- **Most Aware:** "Scouter Pro is 30% off through Friday. Use code LAUNCH."
- **Product-Aware:** "Unlike enterprise tools, Scouter is built for operators — not brand campaigns."
- **Solution-Aware:** "You need a creator database. Here's why Scouter finds who others miss."
- **Problem-Aware:** "10 hours a week scrolling YouTube. There's a faster way."
- **Unaware:** "Growth operators have a secret weapon for finding creator partners."

## 2. Market Sophistication

### What it is
How many times your prospect has heard similar claims. Sophistication increases as a market matures. What worked in Stage 1 sounds like noise in Stage 5.

| Stage | Market state | What works |
|-------|--------------|------------|
| **1** | You're first | Make the direct claim. "Lose weight." |
| **2** | Competitors appeared | Enlarge the claim. "Lose 20 pounds in 30 days." |
| **3** | Claims feel stale | Introduce mechanism. "The ketosis method that burns fat while you sleep." |
| **4** | Mechanisms copied | Enlarge/differentiate mechanism. "The cold-activated ketosis protocol." |
| **5** | Nothing believed | Sell identity/experience. "Join 50,000 people who've transformed their relationship with food." |

### When it matters
- Writing headlines in crowded markets
- Deciding whether to lead with claim, mechanism, or proof
- Positioning against competitors
- Understanding why "good" copy isn't converting (wrong stage)

### What Claude gets wrong by default
- **Uses Stage 1 tactics in Stage 4 markets.** Direct claims that feel generic because everyone makes them.
- **Adds mechanism when market is Stage 5.** They don't believe mechanisms anymore — they need proof and identity.
- **Doesn't research what claims have already been made.** Writes copy that sounds exactly like competitors.
- **Treats sophistication as fixed.** Different segments within a market may be at different stages.

### What good execution looks like

Stage 1 (new category): "Find creators who sell courses, coaching, and services."

Stage 3 (mechanism needed): "The unified profile system that shows YouTube + Instagram + TikTok in one view."

Stage 5 (proof/identity): "Join 500+ growth operators who stopped scrolling and started closing."

## 3. Mass Desire

### What it is
You do not create desire. Desire already exists in your market — you channel it. Find the force of want that already lives in your prospect's mind, then attach your product to it.

The three dimensions of desire:
1. **Urgency** — How badly they want it now
2. **Intensity** — How much they're willing to give up for it
3. **Degree of repetition** — How often they feel this want

### When it matters
- Identifying what to put in the brief's Problem section
- Understanding why some pain points resonate more than others
- Choosing which desire to channel (time, money, status, freedom)
- Writing headlines that tap existing tension

### What Claude gets wrong by default
- **Tries to create new desire.** Explains why they *should* want something instead of tapping what they already want.
- **Picks weak desires.** Chooses "nice to have" benefits over burning wants.
- **Describes desire abstractly.** "Save time" instead of "close your laptop at 5pm."
- **Misses the underlying desire.** Writes about features when the desire is status, or freedom, or revenge.

### What good execution looks like

**Weak (creating desire):** "You should want to systematize your creator discovery process."

**Strong (channeling desire):** "You already know you're wasting hours on research. Here's how to get them back."

**Abstract:** "Save time on prospecting."

**Concrete:** "Find 100 creators in 10 minutes instead of 10 hours. That's 9 hours back every week."

## 4. Intensification

### What it is
Once you've identified and channeled desire, you intensify it. Make the want more vivid, urgent, and personal. Schwartz's techniques:

1. **Enlarge the claim** — Make the outcome bigger
2. **Prove the claim** — Make it believable with specifics
3. **Extend the claim** — Show secondary benefits
4. **Personalize the claim** — Make it about them specifically
5. **Add urgency** — Give reasons to act now

### When it matters
- Writing body copy after the headline
- Structuring proof sections
- Building emotional weight in the Problem section
- Creating CTAs that compel action

### What Claude gets wrong by default
- **Stops at the claim.** States the benefit but doesn't intensify it.
- **Intensifies with hype instead of proof.** Adds adjectives instead of specifics.
- **Forgets personalization.** Writes to "people" instead of "you."
- **Adds fake urgency.** Countdown timers with no real scarcity.

### What good execution looks like

**Claim only:** "Find creators faster."

**Intensified:**
- **Enlarged:** "Find 100 qualified creators in 10 minutes."
- **Proved:** "Growth operators at 50+ agencies use Scouter to cut prospecting time by 90%."
- **Extended:** "That's 9 hours back every week. Hours you could spend closing deals."
- **Personalized:** "You already know the right partner is out there. Stop missing them."
- **Urgent:** "Founder pricing ends when we hit 1,000 users. We're at 847."

## 5. The Copywriter's Role

### What it is
You are not inventing. You are channeling. Schwartz: "Copy is not written. Copy is assembled."

Your job:
1. Find the mass desire that already exists
2. Identify the prospect's awareness level
3. Assess market sophistication
4. Channel desire through your mechanism
5. Intensify until action is inevitable

### When it matters
- Every piece of copy
- Every brief section
- Every headline decision

### What Claude gets wrong by default
- **Creates instead of channels.** Invents benefits instead of discovering what the market already wants.
- **Writes from product-out instead of desire-in.** Starts with features, works toward benefits — should be reversed.
- **Assumes the copy will convince.** The desire must already exist; copy just directs it.

### What good execution looks like

**Product-out (wrong):** "Scouter has unified profiles, niche browsing, and a Kanban pipeline. These features help you work more efficiently."

**Desire-in (right):** "You're tired of wasting 10 hours a week on research. Here's how to find 100 creators in 10 minutes — and track every conversation in one place."

---

## Quick Reference: The Schwartz Questions

When writing or reviewing copy, ask:

1. **Awareness:** Where is my prospect? What do they already know?
2. **Sophistication:** What have they already heard? What's stale?
3. **Desire:** What do they already want? How intensely?
4. **Channel:** How does my product connect to that desire?
5. **Intensify:** How do I make the want more vivid and urgent?

If the copy isn't working, one of these is wrong.
