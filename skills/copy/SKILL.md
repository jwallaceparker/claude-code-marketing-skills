# Copy

This skill handles the micro-level building blocks of persuasive copy—headlines, hooks, bullets, curiosity gaps, fascinations, body copy. Individual lines and short passages, not full deliverables.

## What This Is (And Isn't)

**This skill is for:** Headlines. Hooks. Subject lines. Bullets. Fascinations. Body copy passages. CTAs. The atoms of persuasion.

**This skill is NOT for:** Full sales pages (use sales-pages), complete email sequences (use email-flows), slide decks (use decks), UI microcopy (use microcopy), strategy, or positioning.

**Relationship to other skills:** This skill reads BRIEF.md for audience/positioning context and VOICE.md for tone. Full-deliverable skills like sales-pages and email-flows implicitly apply these principles—but when you need to zoom in on individual lines, this is the tool.

**Core philosophy:** Good copy sounds like the reader's internal monologue, not like marketing. It enters a conversation already happening in their head, opens loops they need to close, and uses specificity to create believability. Every word earns its place.

Words are subvocalized — read silently but "heard" internally through the brain's auditory processing system (Levitin). Word texture is not decoration; it's a separate channel of emotional information. "Cut through the noise" and "navigate the landscape" deliver similar semantic meaning but radically different timbral experiences. The first is staccato — hard consonants, short vowels, percussive. The second is legato — open vowels, soft consonants, flowing. Choose word texture to match the emotional outcome: urgency wants percussion, reflection wants sustained tones, confidence wants clean staccato. The texture of the words communicates before the meaning arrives.

## What Claude Gets Wrong by Default

These are the failure modes to watch for and correct:

### 1. Generic Headlines
Headlines that could be anyone's. No specificity, no selection of audience, nothing that makes the reader say "that's me."

**Default Claude:** "Transform Your Business with Our Solution"
**After correction:** "You're posting 5 reels a week. Getting zero DMs."

**Leading with desire instead of belief (Albuquerque).** "Want to lose weight fast?" targets a desire the prospect already has — and so does every competitor. "The reason dieting has never worked for you" targets the belief that blocks the sale. Desire headlines compete with everyone; belief headlines own the conversation because they address something specific the prospect holds as true. If the headline could appear on ten competitor pages unchanged, it's targeting desire. If it could only appear on yours because it names a specific belief your mechanism shifts, it's targeting belief — and belief is where differentiation lives.

### 2. Features Instead of Fascinations
Describing what something IS instead of making readers lean forward wondering how it works.

**Default Claude:** "AI-powered analytics dashboard"
**After correction:** "The 'niche stack' method that finds 100 qualified creators in 10 minutes flat"

### 3. Resolved Instead of Open
Completing thoughts so readers can stop. No forward pull, no incomplete loops.

**Default Claude:** "Our system helps you save time by automating repetitive tasks, giving you more freedom."
**After correction:** "Our system handles the one task that eats 47% of most founders' weeks..."

Expectation gaps drive curiosity (Yorke). Open loops work because they exploit the gap between what the reader expects to learn and what you've revealed so far. The wider the gap, the stronger the pull. The gap is not manipulation — it's the natural structure of how humans process incomplete information. Every tease must deliver partial knowledge that deepens the hunger for the full reveal — fake mystery ("I'm about to reveal the secret..." repeated without revealing) punishes the reader's trust.

### 4. Sounds Like Marketing
Formal, corporate, buzzword-laden. Nobody talks like this. The test is inflection: "Your revenue dropped 23% last quarter" is uninflected fact — it lands because it's just true. "Are you tired of watching your revenue plummet?" is editorialized — the writer is performing concern, and the reader can feel it. Uninflected copy is almost always stronger because it respects the reader enough to let the fact do the work. When you catch yourself adding emotional spin to a claim, strip it back to the bare statement. If the bare statement isn't compelling, the problem is the claim, not the tone.

**Default Claude:** "Are you struggling with plummeting conversion rates that are destroying your business?"
**After correction:** "Your conversion rate dropped from 4.2% to 1.1% in six months."

**Default Claude:** "We are pleased to announce our innovative new solution designed to optimize your workflow efficiency."
**After correction:** "We built something you're going to wish existed three years ago."

**Invisible exposition (Howard & Mabley).** Naked explanation is the copy equivalent of "As you know, Bob." "Our proprietary system was developed over 12 years of research" is exposition dumped on the page — the reader didn't ask and doesn't care. Information should arrive inside story, conflict, or the reader's own experience. "Twelve years ago we couldn't find a tool that did X, so we built one" smuggles the same fact inside a narrative the reader is following. If a sentence exists only to inform — with no tension, no story, no open question pulling it — it's exposition, and exposition is marketing's native language. Disguise it or cut it.

**Truth over technique (Weston).** A slightly rough, genuinely felt sentence outperforms a perfectly polished, emotionally empty one. "We built this because we were tired of tools that promised automation but created more work" beats "Our cutting-edge platform leverages AI to streamline your workflow." The first is true — you can feel someone behind it. The second is technique without truth — it follows copywriting rules but says nothing real. The reader's gut distinguishes between copy that came from somewhere and copy that was assembled from best practices. When in doubt, write what's actually true about the product, the founder, the origin story, the frustration that sparked it. Truth is more persuasive than polish because truth is rare and polish is everywhere. The copy that sounds slightly unmarketed — because someone real wrote it from genuine experience — converts better than the copy that sounds perfectly marketed by no one in particular.

**Commit to the claim or cut it (Lumet).** Qualifiers on bold claims — "might," "could potentially," "in many cases" — signal the copy doesn't believe itself. "This could potentially help you reduce churn" is a claim wearing a helmet. "This cuts churn" is a claim that stands behind itself. The reader registers the hedge before they process the content, and a hedged claim is worse than no claim at all — it tells the reader you weren't confident enough to commit but wanted credit for the assertion anyway. If the claim is true, state it flat. If it's not true enough to state flat, it shouldn't be in the copy. Half-committed copy is worse than straightforward copy for the same reason a half-committed performance is worse than a simple one — the audience can feel the flinch.

### 5. Rounds Numbers
Kills specificity by smoothing out memorable details.

**Default Claude:** "Over 1,000 customers"
**After correction:** "847 agencies switched in the last 90 days"

### 6. No Agitation Before Solution
Jumps straight to benefits without making the problem vivid first.

**Default Claude:** "Get more leads with our automated outreach."
**After correction:** "You've sent 200 cold emails this month. 3 replies. One was an unsubscribe."

Effective agitation makes the reader see pain they're already experiencing but haven't articulated — "You've sent 200 cold emails this month. 3 replies. One was an unsubscribe" illuminates; "you're LOSING THOUSANDS of dollars every month you wait" manufactures. The first creates recognition, the second creates skepticism, and the reader's croc brain distinguishes them instantly (Camp). The reader is a flawed protagonist (Yorke) — not broken, but incomplete. They've been trying to solve the problem with an incomplete understanding. The copy names that gap: the flaw isn't stupidity, it's a blind spot in their approach. The mechanism is what completes them. "It's not that you haven't worked hard enough. It's that you've been working hard on the wrong thing" is the protagonist's flaw named without insulting them. Emotion decides first, then logic constructs the justification (Greene). The copy doesn't need to convince the reader's logic before the emotional hook lands — in fact, logic-first copy fails because it asks for a decision before emotional buy-in. Enter through emotional recognition ("You've sent 200 emails, got 3 replies"), then let the reader justify that emotion with logic ("Here's why that happens"). The reader will construct their own rational narrative affirming the emotion you triggered — which is more persuasive than leading with logic and hoping emotion follows.

**Dramatic tension as agitation (Howard & Mabley).** Agitation isn't just naming the problem — it's widening the gap between the reader's hope and their fear. The reader holds two things simultaneously: "this could finally work" and "nothing has worked before." That gap is the buying tension. Effective agitation names both sides. "You've tried templates, sequences, even hired a VA — and your reply rate is still under 2%. But somewhere an agency your size is booking 15 calls a week from cold email alone." Hope and fear in the same passage. Widen the gap before you resolve it — the wider the gap, the more the resolution (your offer) is worth.

**Frame the reader's problem as a weakness, not a deficiency (Truby).** The reader is stuck because of a flaw — not because they're stupid or lazy. A weakness is something the reader doesn't know they have: 'You keep trying to scale by adding more tactics because you were taught that more effort equals more results.' That's a weakness — a deep pattern that creates the problem. 'You're not generating enough leads' is a symptom. The difference matters: naming the symptom produces nodding. Naming the weakness produces the shock of recognition — 'that's exactly what I've been doing.' Frame the weakness with compassion ('not because you lack effort — because effort without a system is just exhaustion') and the reader feels understood rather than attacked.

**The first number anchors every number that follows** (Ariely). The comparison number — cost of the current approach, competitor price, value of the problem left unsolved — should appear before any offer price, because the first number the reader encounters becomes the involuntary anchor against which every subsequent number is evaluated. "You're spending $14,000/month on an SDR who books 6 meetings" makes a $2,000/month tool feel proportionally small — not because of the math, but because the reader's brain locked $14,000 as the reference point before the offer arrived. Without that anchor, the reader compares $2,000 to zero, and every price compared to zero feels expensive. Set the anchor with the problem's cost, not the solution's value — the number that makes the reader wince is the number that makes the offer feel like relief.

### 7. Enthusiasm Substituting for Authority
Exclamation points and superlatives instead of proof and specificity.

**Default Claude:** "This amazing, powerful tool will completely revolutionize how you work!"
**After correction:** "Three agencies fired their prospecting team after month two."

Superlatives and intensifiers are the written form of neediness — they signal that the copy doesn't trust the claim to stand alone. Uninflected statements of fact — "Three agencies fired their prospecting team after month two" — project the confidence of someone who doesn't need you to be impressed (Camp).

**Stillness is power (Caine).** Intensifiers — "incredibly," "absolutely," "literally," "truly," "really" — are the written equivalent of over-gesturing. An actor who waves their arms on every line communicates nothing with their body. A sentence that amplifies every claim communicates nothing with its emphasis. Cut the intensifiers and let the claim stand still. "This saves you 10 hours a week" is a claim with its feet planted. "This literally saves you an incredible 10 hours every single week" is the same claim flailing. A statement that stands still reads as confident because confidence doesn't need amplification. Audit every intensifier: remove it, reread the sentence. If the sentence is weaker without it, the problem is the claim, not the missing modifier. If it's the same or stronger, the intensifier was noise.

### 8. Over-Projecting for the Medium
Copy that shouts when the reader is close. ALL CAPS in body copy, stacked exclamation points, superlatives piled three deep — this is an actor projecting to the back row of a theater when the camera is two feet away (Caine). The reader is six inches from their screen. They're reading in bed, on a train, between meetings. A voice calibrated for a stadium is painful at that distance. Scale down for the intimacy of the medium. "DON'T MISS THIS INCREDIBLE OPPORTUNITY!!!" reads like a street vendor yelling at someone who already stopped to look. "This closes Friday" says the same thing at reading distance.

**Default Claude:** "This AMAZING, POWERFUL tool will COMPLETELY change how you work!!!"
**After correction:** "This changes how you work. Here's how."

### 9. One Register for All Contexts
Same tone whether it's a headline, email subject, or body copy.

**Default Claude:** Writes a 25-word headline in complete sentences
**After correction:** Headlines: 5-15 words, punchy. Body: conversational, room to breathe.

### 10. Tells Instead of Shows
States claims without concrete evidence or examples.

**Default Claude:** "Our customers love us."
**After correction:** "Last Thursday, a customer called to say we'd paid for ourselves in the first week."

**Show the reaction, not the result (Caine).** In film, the most powerful moment is often the listener's face, not the speaker's line. The same principle applies to proof in copy: showing the reader's reaction to the transformation is more persuasive than stating the transformation itself. "She opened her dashboard and went quiet for ten seconds" lands harder than "she 3x'd her revenue" because the reaction is human and unchoreographed — the reader fills in what caused the silence. "He read the proposal twice, then asked where to sign" is more believable than "he immediately signed a $50K contract" because the double-read is a detail nobody invents. When writing proof, testimonials, or case studies, look for the reaction beat — the pause, the silence, the involuntary response — and lead with that. The result can follow, but the reaction is what the reader trusts, because results can be inflated and reactions can't be faked.

**Concrete details that trigger inference beat emotional labels (Mackendrick).** "She stared at the blinking cursor for forty minutes" does more persuasive work than "she struggled with writer's block" because the reader constructs the emotion themselves. The first gives a visual — specific, filmable, sensory — and the reader's brain fills in the frustration, the self-doubt, the paralysis. The second hands them an abstraction and asks them to feel something about it. Abstractions inform. Concrete details recruit the reader's imagination to do the persuading for you. "He checked his phone three times during the pitch" lands harder than "the prospect wasn't engaged" because the phone-checking is a scene the reader has lived — they supply the sinking feeling from their own memory. Every emotional label in copy is a missed opportunity to show a detail that triggers the same emotion more powerfully. The detail is always more persuasive than the label because the reader trusts their own inference more than your characterization. When editing copy, hunt for emotional labels — "frustrated," "overwhelmed," "excited," "struggling" — and replace each one with the concrete, filmable detail that would make someone feel that emotion without being told to.

**Insinuation beats direct claims (Greene: The Art of Seduction).** "We're the best" triggers skepticism because it's an assertion anyone can make. "Here's what happened when 47 companies switched" triggers belief because the reader draws the conclusion. Plant conclusions through story and juxtaposition rather than stating them directly. Self-generated conclusions are trusted more than stated claims because the reader feels they discovered the truth rather than being told it. A case study showing transformation lets the reader draw the conclusion; a direct claim about being "the industry leader" invites them to argue. "I am an expert" triggers evaluation. Teaching something useful proves expertise without saying it. The gap between implication and conclusion is where trust forms. Replace direct claims with the evidence that would make someone reach that claim on their own.

**Deeds over words — demonstration beats assertion (Greene: The Art of Seduction).** Claims are cheap; demonstrations are costly signals. "We're the best" triggers evaluation. "Here's what happened when 47 companies switched" triggers belief. Every claim in copy should be immediately followed by evidence — the deed that proves it. Assertions invite challenge. Demonstrations create belief. "We save you 10 hours a week" is a claim. "Here's what Sarah's Tuesday looks like now vs. six months ago" is a deed. The reader doesn't evaluate the deed the way they evaluate the claim — they witness it. Audit copy for naked claims: any assertion without immediate evidence should either get evidence or get cut.

### 11. Skips the Internal Dialogue
Doesn't anticipate or address the reader's real-time objections.

**Default Claude:** "Sign up today to get started."
**After correction:** "Yes, there's a free trial. No, you don't need to talk to sales."

### 12. Too Many Words
No compression. Uses 20 words where 7 would hit harder.

**Default Claude:** "We offer a comprehensive solution that has been specifically designed to help you achieve better results in less time."
**After correction:** "Same results. Half the time."

### 13. Dead Metaphors Everywhere
"Unlock," "leverage," "game-changer," "seamless," "cutting-edge"—words that have lost all meaning.

**Default Claude:** "Unlock the power of seamless integration"
**After correction:** "It connects in 4 minutes. We timed it."

### 14. Polite Fear
Names the problem but refuses to go to the consequence. "Your conversion rate dropped 73%" is a fact. "Your conversion rate dropped 73% and your runway calculation changed with it" is fear. Good copy completes the consequence the reader is already suppressing — not by manufacturing dread, but by finishing the sentence the reader started in their own head and stopped before the uncomfortable part. The reader already knows the implication. Polite copy pretends it doesn't exist. Effective copy says it out loud, and the reader's relief at seeing the truth named is what builds trust (Whitman).

### 15. Too Many Desires at Once
Copy that triggers multiple emotional drivers in one passage fragments attention. A paragraph that appeals to safety, status, and comfort simultaneously gives the reader three threads and the bandwidth to follow none of them. Pick one dominant LF8 desire per section. The section about financial security should feel like financial security from first word to last — not financial security that detours into social approval and then pivots to comfort. Scattered desire appeals read as generic motivation. A single sustained desire reads as someone who understands exactly what the reader wants (Whitman).

### 16. Anti-Seducer Patterns That Actively Repel (Greene: The Art of Seduction)
Copy can fail by being forgettable. But copy can also fail by actively pushing readers away. Audit copy for these patterns:

- **Insecurity (hedging):** "We think maybe you might benefit from potentially exploring..." Every hedge signals doubt in your own offering. If the claim isn't confident enough to state flat, don't state it at all.
- **Self-absorption:** "We're excited to announce..." Nobody cares about your excitement. Open with the reader, not yourself. Any sentence that starts with "We're proud/excited/thrilled to..." is self-absorption.
- **Impatience (rushing to CTA):** Hard-selling in the first touchpoint. CTA before the problem is vivid. Ask for the meeting before building any relationship. Impatience signals desperation.
- **Clinginess:** "Did you see my last email?" "Just following up again..." "Don't want you to miss this!" Follow-up desperation communicates that nobody else is responding either.
- **Humorlessness (relentless earnestness):** Copy that takes itself too seriously, with no lightness, no self-awareness, no human texture. Earnestness without relief becomes exhausting.

These patterns don't just fail to persuade — they create active aversion. Before testing button colors or headline variations, check if the copy is repelling through these anti-seducer qualities. One of these patterns can undo an otherwise strong piece of copy.

### 17. Technically Correct but Dead (Alexander)
Copy that hits every best-practice checkpoint and reads like a template. The structure is visible. The framework is audible. The output is competent and lifeless. This is the difference between copy that checks boxes and copy that feels alive — inviting, exact, whole. You can sense a checklist was followed. You can hear the formula in the rhythm. Every element is present and nothing moves.

**Default Claude:** "Are you tired of [pain point]? Our [adjective] [solution] helps [audience] achieve [benefit] in just [timeframe]. Plus, you get [bonus]. Try it risk-free today!"
**After correction:** "You've rewritten that same cold email 40 times. Each version sounds slightly more desperate than the last. Here's the one that actually got a reply."

The diagnostic: read the copy and ask — can I sense the framework behind this? If yes, it's dead. Rewrite from the reader's specific situation, not from the formula. Alive copy sounds like a person who happens to be expert. Dead copy sounds like a system following instructions.

### 17. Descriptive Instructions Instead of Generative Ones (Alexander)
Writing to instructions like "make it compelling" or "use emotional language" produces vague, template-sounding copy. These are descriptive — they tell you what good looks like without telling you how to create it. Generative instructions produce output directly: "open with the gap between what the reader believes and what is actually true" is generative — you can execute it. "The headline should create curiosity" is descriptive — you can only evaluate after the fact.

The test: could Claude produce good output from this instruction alone, with no examples? If yes, the instruction is generative. If Claude needs examples to understand what you mean, the instruction is descriptive and needs to be rewritten as an action.

### 18. Agitation that describes instead of evokes (Heath: Switch)
"Manual processes are inefficient and cost you time" is Analyze → Think. The Rider might agree, but the Elephant doesn't move. "That sinking feeling when you open a spreadsheet with 200 rows and realize half the links are broken" is See → Feel. Same problem, different emotional engagement. Agitation copy should re-create the experience, not just name it.

### 19. Lines that say without doing (McKee: Dialogue)

Every line of copy performs an action on the reader — it creates a gap, breaks a belief, makes clicking feel inevitable. Before writing any headline, CTA, or key sentence, name what this line should *do* to the reader. Then write the words that perform that action. If two consecutive sentences perform the same action, one is redundant. The diagnostic is not "what does this say?" but "what does this *do*?" A sentence that informs without acting is dead weight.

### 20. Exposition without ammunition (McKee: Dialogue)

Every piece of information must be loaded with argumentative purpose. "340 companies" is exposition. "The pattern we've seen across 340 companies is that the thing most teams focus on first is the last thing they should fix" is ammunition — it uses the credential to shift a belief. Information placed on a page without connecting it to the reader's specific concern is decoration. The test: does this fact attack a belief the reader currently holds? If it just explains, it's exposition that breaks momentum.

### 21. Monotonous sentence architecture (McKee: Dialogue)

Most Claude-generated copy uses the same sentence structure throughout — typically cumulative (frontload meaning, stack detail). Vary three architectures deliberately. Suspense sentences (withhold meaning until the end — use for headlines, key moments). Cumulative sentences (frontload meaning, stack detail — use for body copy, explanation). Balanced sentences (parallel structure — use for comparisons and memorable claims). A piece written entirely in one pattern is monotonous regardless of how good the individual sentences are.

### 22. Structural misdiagnosis of flat copy (McKee: Dialogue)

When copy is flat, diagnose the structural cause before polishing words. On-the-nose: claiming rather than demonstrating — needs subtext. Redundant: says what context already shows — needs cutting. Excessive: more words than the action requires — needs compression. Inaudible: reads fine but sounds wrong aloud — needs rhythm repair. Each diagnosis demands a different fix. Polishing words on a structurally broken line is wasted effort.

### 23. Capability claims instead of pattern observations (Baker: The Business of Expertise)

"We help companies grow" is a capability claim — it says what you do. Any competitor could say the same thing, and the reader can't distinguish between you and them. "In 47 companies we've worked with, we've seen the same pattern..." is a pattern observation — it demonstrates expertise through what you've noticed. The reader thinks "they've seen enough to recognize what I'm missing." Audit every proof section: is it dominated by what we do (capability) or what we've seen (pattern)? Pattern language transfers authority. Capability language invites comparison.

### 24. Vendor signals in word choice (Baker: The Business of Expertise)

"We're happy to discuss..." / "Feel free to reach out anytime..." / "We'd love to help..." — these are vendor signals. They communicate that demand does not exceed capacity, that you are one option among many hoping to be selected. Expert copy uses constraint language: "We work with a small number of clients..." / "If this resonates, here's how to continue..." / "We're selective about fit..." Every eager phrase positions the writer below the reader. Every constraint phrase positions the writer as someone worth pursuing. Audit for phrases that sound like asking for permission or approval — these are vendor leaks that undermine the authority the rest of the copy is trying to build.

### 25. Words that don't earn their place (Krug: Don't Make Me Think)

Krug's editing heuristic: cut half the words, then cut half of what's left. Not for brevity's sake — for friction reduction. Every word that doesn't advance the reader toward action is friction slowing them down. "In order to" → "To." "At this point in time" → "Now." "Due to the fact that" → "Because." "It is important to note that" → cut entirely. The first pass writes; the second pass cuts. After cutting, ask: did I lose anything real? If not, the original had bloat. If the cut version loses meaning, restore only what's essential. The goal isn't short copy — it's copy where every remaining word carries weight.

## Core Principles

### 0. Copy Creates Feeling, Not Argument (Aftel)

Copy that argues is copy that loses. Like scent, which bypasses the thalamus and speaks directly to emotion, strong copy creates feeling first and justifies rationally second. The reader's gut makes the decision; their brain constructs the justification afterward. Write to create an emotional state — recognition, desire, relief, urgency — before building any logical argument. The emotional reaction is the persuasion; the argument is just permission to act on it.

**The opening determines whether someone stays (Aftel).** Your first sentence has to work immediately or you don't get a second. Like top notes in perfume that last only minutes but determine if someone stays for the beautiful heart and base, your hook earns the right to reveal the substance. A weak opening wastes everything that follows — the reader never gets there. Design the opening to be immediately compelling: pattern-interrupt, recognition, or tension that demands resolution.

**Dilution calibration: some claims need intensity, others need space (Aftel).** Your core promise, your big claim, your central transformation — these need concentration, no hedging, bold and undiluted. Mechanism explanations, objection handling, proof stacking — these need room to breathe, space for the reader to process. Headlines should be concentrated. Body copy can expand. Know which materials need intensity and which need dilution. A headline that tries to explain too much is diluted; an explanation that tries to be punchy is concentrated past the point of clarity.

### 1. Enter the Conversation Already Happening

Your copy joins a conversation that's already playing in the reader's head. They have thoughts, frustrations, half-formed questions. Meet them there.

This means: Know what they're already thinking before you write. Your first line should feel like a response to something they just said to themselves.

**Why it matters:** Copy that starts a new conversation has to do all the work. Copy that enters an existing one has momentum on its side.

**All copy is a knowledge arc (Yorke).** The reader begins in ignorance — they don't know the mechanism, the proof, or the offer. The copy moves them toward knowledge one controlled revelation at a time. The copywriter's job is to manage the rate of revelation: too much too fast kills curiosity (the reader has no reason to keep reading), too slow kills patience (the reader gives up). Every beat should deliver enough knowledge to satisfy the previous gap while opening a new one.

Before writing any headline, fill in the 16-word framework: "Most people believe [X]. But [mechanism] proves [Y]." The headline should address [X] or introduce [mechanism]. If you can't fill in the framework, you're writing without a strategic foundation — you don't yet know what belief you're shifting or what makes the shift credible (Albuquerque).

The best hooks are small containers with vast implications. A precise detail — a number, a specific time, a verbatim complaint — implies a world larger than the line that contains it. Specificity is persuasive because the right detail opens an imaginative space the reader enters (Bachelard).

**Before:**
> "Have you considered implementing a more systematic approach to your social media conversion strategy?"

**After:**
> "You posted 5 reels this week. Zero DMs."

The "after" works because the reader WAS thinking about their failed posts. They see the line and feel seen.

**Before:**
> "Introducing our new client acquisition system for growing agencies."

**After:**
> "Your pipeline dried up in February. It's June. Still waiting for referrals."

The frustration is already there. Name it.

### 2. Specificity Creates Believability

Vague claims are invisible. Specific claims are memorable—and believable.

Odd numbers beat round numbers. "847" beats "over 800." Concrete details beat abstractions. "Last Thursday at 2pm" beats "recently."

**Why it matters:** The brain treats vague claims as marketing (ignore) and specific claims as facts (process). Specificity implies you measured, which implies it's real.

**Before:**
> "We help businesses grow faster."

**After:**
> "We helped 847 agencies cut prospecting time from 10 hours a week to under an hour."

**Before:**
> "Join thousands of satisfied customers."

**After:**
> "4,847 teams switched from Asana in Q4. Here's why."

**Before:**
> "Save time on your workflow."

**After:**
> "37 minutes saved per report. We tracked it for 90 days."

The number doesn't even have to be impressive—it has to be specific.

### 3. The Slippery Slide

Every line has one job: get them to read the next line.

No exit ramps. No complete thoughts that let them stop. Each sentence creates enough incompleteness that they have to continue.

**The mechanism is the unanswered question (O'Bannon).** The slide works because at any given point, there's a question the reader needs answered. "But personalization isn't why it works..." plants a question — *then what is?* — and the reader slides forward to close it. If you read a passage and can't name the active question pulling the reader through it, the slide has stopped. Every sentence either raises a new question or sustains an existing one. The moment all questions are answered, the reader has permission to leave.

**Planting and payoff (Howard & Mabley).** Introduce a specific detail early — a name, a number, a problem — and pay it off later. "The 3pm problem" planted in a headline becomes the framework explained in body copy. The reader holds the planted element as an open question, and that question pulls them forward across sections, not just sentences. This is the slippery slide operating at body-copy scale instead of line-by-line scale. Plant in the hook, pay off in the body. Plant in a bullet, pay off in the CTA. The longer the gap between plant and payoff, the more forward pull it generates — but the payoff must land, or the reader feels cheated.

**The mechanism is the credible explanation (Albuquerque).** In body copy, the mechanism is the specific explanation for WHY the new belief is true — not just that the product works, but the underlying principle that makes the approach fundamentally different. A named mechanism ("the CYP2E1 enzyme," "the DM conversion framework") beats a generic claim ("a new method," "our unique approach") because specificity implies real understanding. The mechanism answers the question the headline raised: if the old belief is wrong, what makes the new one right? Body copy without a mechanism is a claim hanging in air — the reader's internal dialogue fires "says who?" and the slide stops. Body copy with a named, specific mechanism gives the reader something to hold onto, and that grip is what pulls them through to the CTA.

**The self-revelation is the emotional climax of multi-paragraph copy (Truby).** In copy longer than a few paragraphs, the most powerful moment is when the reader sees their own pattern clearly for the first time. This is not the product reveal — it's the moment the reader's frame shifts. 'You've been optimizing the wrong metric. Every time you improved conversion rate, you were just getting better at convincing the wrong people.' The self-revelation reframes everything before it and makes the solution feel inevitable. It must be earned — placed after sufficient agitation and evidence — or it reads as accusation rather than insight.

**Re-hook distribution in long-form copy** (Cannell & Travis): Micro-hooks — rhetorical questions, bold claims, one-line pattern interrupts — should be distributed evenly across the piece or increase in frequency toward the end, not cluster at the opening. Most copy front-loads all its energy in the first paragraph and then settles into explanation. The reader who made it to paragraph four needs a reason to make it to paragraph five. Treat every scroll-length section as needing its own hook moment. If the bottom half of the page reads like a different, duller writer took over, the distribution is wrong.

Every word you cut from a line doesn't reduce information — it creates space the reader's imagination inhabits. The most powerful lines leave room for the reader to fill (Bachelard).

**Why it matters:** People don't read copy—they start reading and either stop or keep going. A single self-contained sentence can be an exit. An open loop pulls them forward.

**Before:**
> "Our software automates your email outreach. It sends personalized messages to prospects at scale. You can customize templates and track responses."

Each sentence is complete. Reader can leave after any one.

**After:**
> "Our software sends 500 personalized emails while you sleep. But personalization isn't why it works..."

Now they need to know why.

**Before:**
> "We offer three pricing tiers to fit your needs. The Basic plan starts at $49/month."

**After:**
> "Three plans. One of them is wrong for you. Here's how to know which."

Can't stop there—they need to find out.

**Conceptual freshness over language polish (Sullivan).** The freshest copy isn't better writing of the same idea — it's a genuinely different angle. Ten polished variations of a generic concept are still generic. Test every headline against "Could a competitor use this unchanged?" If yes, the concept isn't yours yet. Divergent angles first, language quality second — a rough line with a unique concept beats a perfect line with a borrowed one.

**Headline Metaphors Activate Frames.** Headlines don't just summarize — they activate conceptual systems that structure all downstream thinking. "Take control of your pipeline" (CONTROL IS UP, SALES IS MACHINE) primes the reader for power/mastery language in the body copy. "Grow your pipeline" (SALES IS PLANT) primes patience/cultivation language. The metaphor in the headline determines which arguments feel natural in the rest of the copy and which feel like they're coming from a different piece.

Before writing a headline, identify the frame it activates. "Take control" implies you're currently out of control — the body copy must address chaos, lack of visibility, things slipping through cracks. "Grow your pipeline" implies stagnation — the body copy must address slow movement, patience required, nurturing inputs. If the headline activates one frame and the body follows another, the reader feels cognitive friction without knowing why.

**Frame-setting protocol:**
1. Write the headline
2. Name the metaphor it contains (JOURNEY, MACHINE, BUILDING, CONTAINER, WAR, GARDEN, CONTROL)
3. List what that metaphor entails (JOURNEY entails: path, obstacles, destination, getting lost, having a map)
4. Ensure body copy uses language compatible with those entailments

If the headline says "Navigate the chaos" (JOURNEY metaphor) but the body says "Install the system and dominate your market" (MACHINE + WAR metaphors), the frame collapses. JOURNEY headlines need body copy about paths, maps, guides, destinations. MACHINE headlines need body copy about installation, tuning, fuel, efficiency. The headline sets the logic; the body must follow it.

**Spatial Metaphors in Benefits.** UP IS GOOD is hardwired in human cognition. "Rise above the noise," "elevate your messaging," "top-tier quality" feel aspirational automatically because they activate UP orientation. DOWN IS BAD — even when the literal meaning is positive. "Rock-bottom prices" activates DOWN orientation (negative) despite describing savings (positive). "Deep discounts" uses depth (DOWN) which creates subconscious friction with value perception. "Cutting costs" activates downward motion even though the result is positive.

When writing benefit statements, audit for spatial orientation:
- UP benefits: rise, elevate, climb, reach the top, ascend, scale, lift, peak
- DOWN problems: buried, drowning, sinking, stuck at the bottom, falling behind, digging out

Never use DOWN metaphors for positive outcomes. "Deep savings" fights itself — the word "deep" activates negative associations the word "savings" is trying to overcome. Reframe as "massive savings" or "significant savings" (SIZE metaphors, neutral orientation) or "savings that stack up" (UP metaphor, positive orientation).

Test every benefit line: Does the spatial metaphor align with the value direction? If the benefit is positive but uses DOWN language, rewrite with UP or neutral metaphors.

**Dead Metaphor Revival.** Most headlines use dead metaphors — metaphors so overused they've become invisible. "Unlock potential," "drive growth," "boost engagement," "fuel revenue," "accelerate results." These create zero differentiation because every competitor uses the same dead language. The reader's brain doesn't process them as images anymore — they're just noise.

Dead metaphors fail for two reasons: (1) they don't activate imagery (the reader doesn't picture an actual lock being opened when you say "unlock"), and (2) they signal "generic marketing copy" before the reader processes the actual claim. If the headline could appear on fifty competitor sites unchanged, it's dead metaphor territory.

**Revival technique — extend with specific detail:**
Instead of: "Unlock your pipeline's potential"
Revival: "Your potential isn't locked — it's buried under 47 browser tabs, a Slack that never stops, and a CRM you stopped updating in March"

The revival extends the dead LOCK metaphor into the actual problem — not locked, but buried (different metaphor entirely). The specificity (47 tabs, Slack, CRM, March) revives the abstract into concrete.

**Replacement technique — fresh structural metaphor:**
Instead of: "Drive more qualified leads"
Replacement: "Stop hunting for leads. Start building the magnet."

"Drive" is dead (BUSINESS IS VEHICLE). "Magnet" is fresh (LEAD-GEN IS ATTRACTION) and creates a new conceptual frame — you're not pushing harder, you're changing the approach entirely.

**Dead metaphor audit protocol:**
1. Highlight every metaphor in the headline ("unlock," "drive," "accelerate," "fuel," "boost," "scale," "leverage")
2. Ask: Does this create a mental image, or is it just abstract language?
3. If abstract, either extend it with specific detail or replace it with a fresh metaphor from a different source domain

Common dead metaphors to flag:
- CONTAINER: unlock, open up, uncover, reveal
- VEHICLE: drive, accelerate, fuel, steer, navigate
- MACHINE: optimize, scale, systematize, streamline
- UPWARD MOTION: boost, elevate, lift, raise, level up
- BUILDING: build, grow, scale, lay foundation

None of these are wrong — but they're invisible. Either revive them with specificity or replace them entirely. The headline that avoids dead metaphors stands out before the reader even processes the claim.

### 4. Fascinations Over Features

Features describe the product. Fascinations make readers lean forward.

A fascination is a curiosity-driven statement that implies a benefit without fully revealing the mechanism. It's an open loop attached to something they want.

**The reader wants to work (Mamet).** A bullet that implies a benefit without naming it activates more curiosity than one that explains it. "The 3pm method" makes the reader fill in the gap. "How to use the 3pm method to double your output" does the work for them — and kills the pull. Trust the reader to close the distance between the clue and the payoff. The less you complete, the more they lean forward.

Information that feels enclosed is experienced as more meaningful than information that's broadcast. The blind bullet isn't just a curiosity gap — it's a sealed container. The reader values the contents before they open it because the seal signals value. The container must deliver; an empty sealed container is worse than no container (Bachelard).

**Why it matters:** Features inform. Fascinations sell. The reader's curiosity does the work.

**Before (features):**
- AI-powered lead scoring
- CRM integration
- Automated follow-ups
- Custom reporting

**After (fascinations):**
- The "niche stack" method that finds 100 creators in 10 minutes (even in markets you'd assume are tapped out)
- Why your best leads are hiding in your CRM's "dead" list—and the 3-minute check that surfaces them
- The "7-word follow-up" that gets responses from prospects who ghosted you 6 months ago
- The one metric in your reports that actually predicts churn (it's not the obvious one)

Each fascination:
- Opens a loop ("what's the niche stack method?")
- Attaches to a benefit ("find 100 creators in 10 minutes")
- Implies a secret or mechanism ("3-minute check")

### 5. The Internal Dialogue

While reading, the reader reacts, objects, questions. They're not passive. Your copy should anticipate and address their internal responses in real time.

They think "yeah right" → you show proof
They think "how?" → you explain mechanism
They think "but what about X?" → you address it

**Why it matters:** Unanswered objections fester. Addressed objections build trust.

**Before:**
> "We helped 200 agencies double their pipeline last quarter."
> (Reader thinks: "Prove it." Copy moves on. Trust broken.)

**After:**
> "We helped 200 agencies double their pipeline last quarter. Skeptical? Here's one you can call."
> (Reader's objection gets answered before it fully forms.)

**Before:**
> "Our system works in any industry."
> (Reader thinks: "Not mine. I'm different.")

**After:**
> "Our system works in any industry—yes, including yours. We've done HVAC, luxury skincare, and B2B SaaS. Turns out lead gen math is the same."

**Before:**
> "Sign up for our free trial."
> (Reader thinks: "Then you'll hassle me with sales calls.")

**After:**
> "14-day trial. No card required. No sales calls—unless you ask for one."

### 6. Sound Like a Person, Not Marketing

Read it aloud. Would you say this to a skeptical friend? If not, rewrite it.

Contractions work. Short sentences work. Fragments work. Starting sentences with "and" and "but" works. What doesn't work: formal constructions, passive voice, weasel words, hedging.

**Why it matters:** Marketing language triggers skepticism filters. Human language slips past.

**When briefing tone, frame it as what the copy is DOING, not how it should FEEL (Weston).** "This section interrogates their current approach" is playable — the writer can execute interrogation. "This section should feel provocative" is unplayable — provocation is a result, not an action. Action-based briefs produce sharper work because they give the writer something to DO: challenge, confess, dare, reassure, confront, validate. Feeling-based briefs produce muddled work because the writer performs a mood instead of executing an intention. Before writing any section, name the verb: "This headline challenges." "This bullet teases." "This paragraph reassures." If you can only name an adjective ("punchy," "engaging," "compelling"), the brief isn't actionable yet.

**Separate tone from mood (Lumet).** The mood of a passage is what the reader feels — urgency, discomfort, excitement, relief. The tone is the writer's stance — conspiratorial, clinical, irreverent, matter-of-fact. Most copy conflates the two, which is why it sounds like everything else. The gap between tone and mood is where distinctive copy lives. A clinical tone delivering an urgent mood ("Your conversion rate dropped 73% in six weeks. Here's the mechanism.") reads differently than an urgent tone delivering the same mood ("Your conversions are cratering and you need to act NOW."). The first is distinctive — the calm stance against the alarming fact creates tension the reader can feel. The second is generic — the tone matches the mood, so there's no friction, no voice, nothing to remember. Before writing, name both independently: "The mood is discomfort. The tone is conspiratorial." If tone and mood are the same word, the copy will be competent but forgettable. The best copy creates a controlled gap between the two — and that gap is what the reader experiences as voice.

**Before:**
> "We are pleased to announce our innovative new solution that has been specifically designed to optimize your workflow efficiency and enhance your overall productivity."

**After:**
> "We built something you're going to wish existed three years ago."

**Before:**
> "Our team of experienced professionals is committed to delivering exceptional results that exceed your expectations."

**After:**
> "We're good at this. Annoyingly good. Ask anyone who's hired us."

**Before:**
> "Utilizing our proprietary methodology, we systematically address the challenges inherent in modern customer acquisition."

**After:**
> "We figured out why your ads stopped working. It's fixable."

### 7. Exclusion as Craft

What you cut is a deliberate framing choice. The word you remove from a headline, the benefit you withhold from a bullet, the objection you don't address until three paragraphs later — each omission creates forward momentum. Inclusion is default. Exclusion is craft.

This means: Every time you add a word, a benefit, or a clarification, ask whether its absence would pull harder. Often it does. The gap you leave is the thing that makes them scroll.

**Why it matters:** Most copy fails from addition, not subtraction. Writers stuff bullets with benefits until nothing stands out. They answer objections the moment they arise, closing loops that should stay open. Restraint is what separates copy that pulls from copy that pushes.

**Before:**
> "The proven 3-step system for doubling your pipeline using cold email outreach in 30 days"

Everything is named. Nothing is withheld. No gap to close.

**After:**
> "The 3-step system behind 847 agency pipelines"

The mechanism ("cold email"), the timeline ("30 days"), the result ("doubling") — all cut. Each omission is a reason to keep reading.

**Before:**
> "Sign up now — no credit card required, no sales calls, cancel anytime, 14-day free trial, full access to all features."

Every objection answered at once. Nothing left to wonder about.

**After:**
> "14-day trial. No card. No catch."

Three objections handled. The rest wait — giving the reader a reason to keep going to find them.

### 8. Subtext as Depth

Every passage of copy operates on two levels — what it says and what it communicates beneath. A failure story on the surface carries credibility as subtext: the reader absorbs "this person has been through it" without being told. Specificity on the surface carries expertise as subtext: "we tested 14 subject lines over 6 weeks" teaches on top while positioning depth of practice underneath.

**Why it matters:** Copy that works on both levels is stickier than copy that works on one. The reader gets the surface value AND forms a judgment about the source — and the judgment formed through subtext is more durable than one formed through claims, because the reader arrived at it independently.

**The collapse rule:** The moment you explain the subtext, it collapses. "I'm sharing this failure story to show you I'm trustworthy" kills the trust it was building. "We tested 14 subject lines so you can see how thorough we are" turns proof of expertise into a claim of expertise — and claims are what the reader's skepticism filter exists to catch. Subtext works precisely because it operates below the line of explicit statement. If the reader has to be told what to conclude, the copy is doing exposition, not persuasion.

**Before:**
> "We've spent years perfecting our approach so you can trust that we know what we're doing."

Stated claim of trustworthiness. The reader's filter catches it immediately.

**After:**
> "We ran the test on a Tuesday. Thought we had it. Wednesday's data said otherwise. Ran it again Thursday with one variable changed. That's the version you're looking at now."

The subtext — thoroughness, persistence, rigor — is absorbed without being named. The reader concludes "these people are meticulous" on their own, and that self-generated conclusion is stickier than any claim.

---

### 9. Let the Reader Complete the Thought

The reader's self-generated conclusion is always stickier than yours. Don't solve their puzzle for them.

**Why it matters:** When the reader connects the dots themselves, the insight becomes theirs — they own it, they remember it, they act on it. When you hand them the finished conclusion, it's yours — they consume it and move on (Mackendrick).

The phrases that kill self-generated conclusions: "As you can see, this means..." "The takeaway here is..." "In other words..." "What this tells us is..." Each one steps on the reader's moment of realization. The reader was about to arrive at the conclusion — and then the copy arrived first and made the insight feel like a lecture instead of a discovery.

**Before:**
> "We tested 3 pricing pages. Page C converted at 4x the rate of Page A. As you can see, this clearly demonstrates that simplicity outperforms complexity in pricing presentation."

The data was doing the work. "As you can see, this clearly demonstrates" solves the puzzle the reader was already solving.

**After:**
> "We tested 3 pricing pages. Page A had 4 tiers, a comparison table, and a feature matrix. Page C had one price and a button. Page C converted at 4x."

The reader draws "simplicity wins" on their own. That conclusion, because they generated it, is stickier than any sentence you could write.

The question is the most reliable vehicle for self-generated conclusions — facts juxtaposed to raise a question the reader must answer activate more cognition than either stated claims or explicit questions, because the reader doesn't notice they're answering (Camp).

**The practical test:** Read each passage and ask — is there a sentence that restates what the evidence already proved? If the reader would reach the same conclusion without that sentence, cut it. The evidence is the argument. The explanation is the exit ramp.

---

### 10. Two-Pass Drafting

First pass is discovery — rough, everything out, no editing. Get the ideas, the angles, the raw material on the page without judging it. Second pass is compression — cut 30%, sharpen every line, refine the structure. Single-pass copy is premature-edited copy: the internal editor kills the unexpected angles before they reach the page, and what survives is safe, predictable, and indistinguishable from what a competitor would write (Sullivan).

This isn't about being sloppy — it's about separating generation from evaluation. The writer who polishes as they go produces clean mediocrity. The writer who dumps everything first and sculpts second finds the line they'd never have written if they were editing in real time.

## Copy Type Variations

Different formats have different jobs. Match the approach to the format. Design for the medium's constraints, not the idea in isolation — a headline that works in a blog post dies as a subject line. The medium dictates the word budget before you start writing (Sullivan):

| Medium | Word Budget | Constraint |
|--------|-------------|------------|
| Blog headline | ~15 words | Competes with back button |
| Social hook | ~8 words | Competes with infinite scroll |
| Email subject | ~5 words | Competes with 47 other subjects |
| Billboard/ad | ~7 words | Competes with 65 mph |

| Copy Type | Length | Tone | Primary Job | Watch Out For |
|-----------|--------|------|-------------|---------------|
| **Headline** | 5-15 words | Punchy, specific | Stop scroll, select audience | Clever instead of clear |
| **Hook** | 1-3 sentences | Direct, curiosity-forward | Earn the next 3 seconds | Selling too early |
| **Subhead** | 8-20 words | Supporting, bridging | Connect headline to body | Repeating the headline |
| **Bullet/Fascination** | 10-25 words | Curiosity-driven | Open loops | Feature lists |
| **Body copy** | 2-4 sentences per section | Conversational, sliding | Pull forward | Dense walls of text |
| **CTA** | 5-15 words | Confident, clear | Tell exactly what to do | Vague "Learn more" |

**Endowment effect in CTAs** (Ariely). Free trials, customization steps, and interactive elements create psychological ownership before purchase — the reader who has invested time, made choices, or configured something now owns it emotionally and values it more than someone encountering it fresh. CTAs that reference what the reader has already done convert better than purely forward-looking CTAs because they activate loss aversion on effort already spent. "You've spent 5 minutes here — that's the hard part" reframes the CTA as completing something already in progress rather than starting something new. "You've already seen the framework — now build yours" turns the CTA into a continuation, not an ask. The endowment effect means the reader who has engaged with any part of the experience — a quiz, a calculator, a configurator, even reading this far — values the outcome more than someone who hasn't. Design CTAs that name the investment already made, so clicking feels like protecting that investment rather than making a new one. People overweight immediate pain vs. distant gain (Greene). When the benefit is months away but the cost of action is immediate, the immediate dominates. Strong copy collapses this gap: "Start booking calls this week" makes the benefit feel imminent. "This costs you nothing but 15 minutes of attention" minimizes the immediate cost. Frame inaction in present terms — "Every month you delay costs $X" hits harder than "You'll see ROI within 12 months" because one is about now and the other is about a future they unconsciously discount.
| **Email subject** | 3-10 words | Curiosity + self-interest | Get the open | Pure clickbait that kills trust |
| **Social hook** | Under 20 words | Pattern-interrupt | Earn the "see more" | Sounding like everyone else |

## Implementation Workflow

When sharpening or writing copy, follow this process:

### 1. Identify the Job
What are you writing? Headline, hooks, bullets, body copy, subject line, CTA? The job determines the constraints.

### 2. Read Context
Check for BRIEF.md (audience, positioning, problem, proof) and VOICE.md (tone, vocabulary, anti-voice). If neither exists and the ask is substantial, ask for context or request running the brief/voice skills first.

Before writing: What single feeling should remain after reading? Not what should they know — what should they *feel*? Name the feeling. Design toward it. If you can't name the feeling in one word, you're not ready to write yet — because copy without a target feeling wanders between emotions and lands on none of them (Sullivan).

### 3. Identify Awareness Level
Where is the reader on Schwartz's awareness spectrum?
- **Unaware:** Don't know they have the problem
- **Problem-aware:** Know the problem, not solutions
- **Solution-aware:** Know solutions exist, not yours
- **Product-aware:** Know your product, not convinced
- **Most aware:** Ready to buy, need a reason to act now

Match copy sophistication to awareness. Early awareness = lead with problem. High awareness = lead with offer specifics.

### 3b. Identify the LF8 Desire

Name which of the 8 hardwired desires the copy serves. Run the connection chain: Feature → Functional benefit → Emotional benefit → LF8 Desire. If you can't complete it, the copy will stay functional — it'll describe what the product does without connecting to why the reader cares at a biological level. A headline that stops at functional benefit ("saves 10 hours a week") informs. One that completes the chain to LF8 ("close the laptop at 5pm and be present for dinner") moves. The chain forces you past the surface and into the desire that actually drives action (Whitman).

### 4. Find the Anchor Detail

Before writing, name the one anchor detail — the specific number, scene, or verbatim phrase — that makes the abstract claim concrete. The anchor is the single piece of evidence the reader will remember when they've forgotten everything else. "Our clients save 10 hours a week" is the claim. "Last Tuesday, Marcus finished by 2pm for the first time in six months" is the anchor. The claim tells the reader what to believe. The anchor makes them believe it — because a detail that specific could only come from someone who was there. Every passage of copy should be built around its anchor, not dressed with one after the fact. If you can't name the anchor before writing, you don't know enough about the product or the proof to write yet — go back to the brief or the interview (McPhee).

### 5. Write Variations
For headlines, hooks, or bullets: write 5-10 divergent options. Not synonyms of the same idea—genuinely different angles. Different entry points, different emotions, different mechanisms highlighted.

### 6. Write the Transition Line

The line that takes the reader from the problem to the mechanism is the page's central claim compressed into a transition. This single sentence carries more argumentative weight than any headline because it's the moment the reader decides whether the mechanism is worth hearing. "That's why we built a system that does the prospecting for you" is a transition doing no work — it's a bridge made of air. "The fix isn't more outreach. It's outreach that doesn't need you in it" is the central claim compressed into a hinge the reader crosses. Write that line deliberately. If the problem section and the mechanism section could be swapped with different transitions between them, the transition isn't carrying the argument — it's just connecting paragraphs. The transition should be the one line that, if you pulled it out and put it on a billboard, would make someone stop and think (McPhee).

### 7. Structural-Before-Surface Diagnostic
When a passage feels flat, check the tension state before changing word choices. Read paragraph by paragraph: does the tension shift between them — does a new question open, an objection surface, a stakes raise land? If the tension is static across paragraphs, no amount of punchier words will fix it. Most "tone" problems are structural problems (O'Bannon). Restructure the tension arc first, then polish language.

### 8. Check Against Anti-Patterns
Run each variation through the 13 anti-patterns. Does it sound like marketing? Is it specific enough? Are there open loops? Does it enter the conversation?

### 9. Read Aloud
If it sounds robotic, formal, or like something a committee wrote, rewrite. The ear catches what the eye misses.

## Quality Checklist

Before delivering copy, verify:

1. **Sounds like a person** — Would you say this to a skeptical friend?
2. **Specific, not vague** — Odd numbers, concrete details, no weasel words
3. **Opens loops** — Forward pull, incomplete thoughts that demand continuation
4. **Enters the conversation** — Meets readers where they already are
5. **No dead metaphors** — No "unlock," "leverage," "game-changer," "seamless"
6. **Anticipates objections** — Addresses internal dialogue before it becomes resistance
7. **Features → fascinations** — Curiosity-driven, mechanism-implied
8. **Agitation before solution** — Problem is vivid before solution appears
9. **Right register for format** — Headlines are headlines, body is body
10. **Compressed** — Every word earns its place
11. **No enthusiasm without proof** — Superlatives backed by specifics
12. **Awareness-matched** — Sophistication fits where the reader is
13. **One person focus** — "You," not "customers" or "users"
14. **Clear next step** — Reader knows exactly what to do
15. **Passes "so what?" test** — Every claim answered with benefit
16. **Survives the removal test** — Read the passage without each paragraph individually; if it still flows, that paragraph is weight, not structure (O'Bannon)
17. **Tension shifts across paragraphs** — Each paragraph changes the tension state; if two consecutive paragraphs sit at the same tension level, one is redundant or the structure needs reworking
18. **Concrete over abstract** — Emotional labels ("frustrated," "overwhelmed") replaced with filmable details that trigger the same emotion through inference (Mackendrick)
19. **Subtext intact** — The copy positions without stating; no sentence explains what the reader should conclude about the brand
20. **Reader completes the thought** — No "As you can see" or "This means" sentences that restate what the evidence already proved
21. **Before/After states share one LF8 desire** — Both states connect to the same hardwired desire — before shows it blocked, after shows it fulfilled. "Checking your bank balance with dread" (survival blocked) → "Checking your bank balance and feeling nothing" (survival fulfilled). Same desire in both states makes the gap feel personal, not generic (Whitman)
22. **Metaphor Coherence Editing** — Copy fails when it stacks incompatible metaphors from different domains without noticing. "Unlock growth, drive engagement, build your brand" mixes LOCKS/VEHICLES/CONSTRUCTION in one sentence — the reader feels friction but can't name why. Coherence scan: tag every metaphor in the piece, group by source domain (JOURNEY, MACHINE, BUILDING, CONTAINER, WAR, GARDEN), simplify to 1-2 compatible systems. If the headline uses JOURNEY metaphors, the body should use JOURNEY-compatible language (path, map, destination, obstacles) not WAR language (dominate, crush, win). Mixed metaphor systems create cognitive dissonance — each metaphor individually clear but together creating a "this feels off" sensation the reader experiences but can't articulate
