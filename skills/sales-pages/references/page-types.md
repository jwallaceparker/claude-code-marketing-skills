# Page Types

*Type-specific structural blueprints for sales pages and landing pages. Section-by-section architecture, awareness context, CTA placement, and anti-patterns per type. Use when building a specific page — route here after identifying the type in SKILL.md.*

Sources: Schwartz (awareness levels), Masterson/Forde (lead spectrum), Fladlien (closing sequence, value stack), Edwards (PASTOR, bullet writing), Kennedy (PAS, damaging admission), Enns (diagnostic framing, selectivity), Simon (price communication, anchoring), Blanks-Jesson (long pages usually win, objection autopsy), Krug (usability, scan path), Bly (BFD, benefit depth), Schwab (headline architecture)

See SKILL.md for core principles that apply across all page types. This playbook adds the type-specific layer: what sections in what order, how long, and what to avoid.

---

## Quick Decision Tree

| If selling... | Page type | Why |
|---------------|-----------|-----|
| Digital product, course, coaching, high-ticket program | Long-Form Sales Page | High price = high persuasion burden. Every section earns the next. Long pages usually win because more evidence means more chances to convert. |
| SaaS subscription, software tool | SaaS Landing Page | The product does much of the selling. Clarity over length. Features shown, not described. |
| Lead magnet, email opt-in, free resource | Lead Capture Page | One job: get the opt-in. Shortest format. Every word earns its place. |
| Agency, consulting, freelance service | Service Page | Sells the diagnostic, not the solution. Pattern recognition and selectivity over hard selling. |

If the page doesn't fit these types, default to the type closest in persuasion burden and buyer awareness. A webinar registration page is a Lead Capture variant. A product comparison page is a SaaS Landing variant. A consulting application page is a Service Page variant.

## Long-Form Sales Page

The full direct response page. For digital products, courses, coaching, high-ticket programs — anything where the price is high enough that the prospect needs to be sold, not just informed. Longest format. Every section earns the right to the next.

### Section-by-Section Blueprint

**Hero Section**
- Headline: Specific result + timeframe or mechanism. Not clever — clear. Follow Schwab's architecture: the headline must do one of these — promise a benefit, deliver news, arouse curiosity, or offer a quick easy way.
- Subheadline: Who this is for. The reader should immediately know if they belong here. "For [specific audience] who [specific situation]."
- Supporting line: One piece of proof or credibility. A stat, a testimonial fragment, a specificity marker. Not a paragraph — one line.
- CTA: Primary action button. For Most Aware visitors who arrived ready to buy and scrolled to confirm. Benefit-driven text: "Start [Result]" not "Sign Up."
- Visual direction: Product mockup, hero image, or video thumbnail. Above the fold on desktop. CTA visible without scrolling.

**Problem / Agitation**
- Enter the conversation already happening in their head (Collier). Use their language, not yours.
- PAS structure: crystallize the Problem in specific, sensory terms. Amplify — show what happens if nothing changes. Introduce the Solution frame (but don't reveal the product yet).
- Name the villain. Not a vague "the industry" — a specific force, belief, or approach that's causing the problem. "The reason most [outcomes] fail isn't effort — it's [villain]."
- Agitate with filmable detail (camera test): "Staring at your analytics at 10pm wondering why nothing moved" — not "feeling frustrated with your results."
- 3-5 paragraphs. Not too long — earn the right to the mechanism section, then move.

**Mechanism**
- Epiphany bridge: the origin story of how this solution was discovered. Before state → tension → the moment everything changed → realization → after state.
- Name the mechanism. Give the approach a name — "The [Name] Method" or "The [Adjective] Framework." Named mechanisms feel proprietary and distinct.
- Explain what's different and why it works. Teaching that sells (Fladlien): teach the what and the why, never the how. The what builds credibility. The why builds belief. The how is what they're buying.
- Compare implicitly: "Unlike [old approach] which requires [pain], this works by [mechanism]."
- **The linking technique (Fladlien):** From hypnosis — Fact. Fact. Claim. Set up claims with verifiable statements directly preceding them, making the claim more easily accepted. "You've been running ads for 6 months (fact). Your cost per lead keeps climbing (fact). There's a structural reason — and once you see it, you can't unsee it (claim)." Use this at transitions between mechanism sub-points or when bridging from mechanism to proof.
- 4-6 paragraphs. This section builds belief — don't rush it.

**Proof**
- Stack 3+ proof types (see Principle 5 in SKILL.md). Lead with the most relatable testimonial — someone the reader identifies with, not the most impressive result.
- Stats with context: "47 clients generated $2.3M in pipeline in Q1" — not "our clients love us."
- Case study mini-story: Situation → what they tried → what happened with this solution → specific result. 3-4 sentences per case study.
- Credentials positioned implicitly — woven into the narrative, not listed in a sidebar.
- Place proof at the objection point: the reader just learned about the mechanism and is thinking "does this actually work?" Answer it before they ask.

**Offer Stack**
- Consume the value stack from the offers skill output. If no offer document exists, build it here using offers skill principles.
- Present each component with: what it is, what it does for them, what it would cost separately (genuine anchor — not inflated).
- Running total as components accumulate.
- **Stair-step pricing (Fladlien):** Link to a much higher price first, step down with justification. "Private coaching at this level runs $25K. We considered offering a group version at $5K. Instead, we built [product] at $997." Each step down should have a REASON — not arbitrary discounting. The reader should feel the logic of arriving at the final price.
- Bonuses that solve specific objections ("Worried about [obstacle]? That's why we built [bonus].").
- Anchor the total stack value. Then reveal the actual price against the anchor.
- Frame the price as investment, not cost. "Your investment" — not "the price is."

**Guarantee**
- Placed immediately after the price reveal. The price creates anxiety — the guarantee resolves it.
- Conditional guarantees are stronger than unconditional: "If you [do X] and don't get [Y result], we [guarantee action]."
- Give it a name: "The [Name] Guarantee" — named guarantees feel more real than "money-back guarantee."
- Anti-guarantee (optional): "This is NOT for people who [wrong fit]." Exclusion builds trust.

**Closing Sequence**
Follow the order from Principle 8 in SKILL.md:
1. Recap the transformation — what their life looks like after
2. Recap the value stack — each component with its anchor, running total
3. Reveal the price — against the total stack value
4. Present the guarantee — conditional preferred, named, placed right after price
5. Two futures — specific scene if they act vs. specific scene if they don't. Filmable detail on both sides.
6. Final CTA — exactly what to click, exactly what happens next ("Click below. You'll fill out a 2-minute form. We'll send access within 5 minutes.")
7. Urgency with reason — real deadline, real constraint, real cost of waiting. Not "limited time!" — "Enrollment closes Friday because the cohort starts Monday."

**PS Section**
- The PS is often the second-most-read element (after the headline). Readers scroll to the bottom.
- Restate the core promise. Add one piece of proof. Repeat the CTA.
- Keep it to 2-3 sentences. This is a summary for skimmers, not a new section.

**FAQ**
- Not an afterthought. Each FAQ handles a real buying objection.
- Write answers persuasively, not just informatively. The question "How long until I see results?" should be answered with a specific timeline AND proof, not just "it depends."
- 5-8 questions. Front-load the most common objections.

### Awareness Context

Traffic typically arrives Problem-Aware or Solution-Aware (from ads, content, referrals who described the problem). Open with pain + promise, not product. If traffic is Product-Aware (retargeting, email list), the hero can lead more directly with the offer.

### CTA Architecture

Four CTA placements, same action, different framing:
1. **After hero** — for Most Aware visitors: "Start [Result] Now"
2. **After proof** — for newly convinced: "Join [Number] Others"
3. **After offer stack** — for value-focused: "Get Everything for [Price]"
4. **After closing** — for emotionally ready: "Yes, I Want [Transformation]"

### Anti-Patterns

- Opening with the product or founder story (nobody cares yet — earn attention with the problem first)
- Proof all in one block instead of distributed throughout
- FAQ that reads like a legal document instead of persuasive conversation
- No PS section (skimmers read headline → scroll to bottom → decide whether to read the middle)
- Value stack with inflated anchors that destroy credibility ("$47,000 value!" for a PDF course)
- No disqualification anywhere on the page (Fladlien's "include by excluding" — explicitly stating who this is NOT for turns off people who wouldn't buy and turns ON people who will. "If you're looking for a get-rich-quick scheme, close this page." The right prospects read that and think "finally, someone serious.")

## SaaS Landing Page

Software product page. Shorter than long-form because the product does much of the selling. Clarity wins over persuasion length. Show the product, don't describe it.

### Section-by-Section Blueprint

**Hero Section**
- Value proposition in one sentence: what it does + who it's for + key differentiator. "The [what] that [differentiator] for [who]."
- One supporting line: the single most compelling proof point or benefit expansion.
- CTA: Start trial or see demo. Clear, low-friction. "Start Free Trial" or "See It in Action."
- Visual: Product screenshot, short demo GIF, or video thumbnail. Show the actual product — not a stock photo of a smiling team.

**Social Proof Bar**
- Logo row of recognizable customers. Or "Trusted by [number] [type of company]."
- Countersignaled — understated, not "TRUSTED BY 500+ AMAZING COMPANIES!" Let the logos do the work.
- If no recognizable logos, use a metric instead: "Processing 2.4M events/day" or "Teams ship 3x faster."

**Problem / Pain**
- Brief — 2-4 sentences maximum. Name the before-state in specific terms.
- "You're currently doing [manual process]. It takes [time]. It shouldn't."
- Don't over-agitate. SaaS buyers are usually solution-aware — they know the problem. They're here to evaluate the fix.

**Features as Benefits**
- 3-4 key features. Not a feature table — a benefit story per feature.
- Each feature shown as: what it does → what changes for them → proof (screenshot or metric).
- Visual: annotated screenshot for each feature. Show, don't describe.
- Bly's benefit depth: Feature → Benefit → Ultimate Benefit. "Automated reporting (feature) → saves 4 hours/week on manual data pulls (benefit) → so you spend Monday mornings on strategy instead of spreadsheets (ultimate benefit)."

**Pricing**
- Three tiers (compromise effect — the middle tier is the target).
- Annual default, monthly available. Show annual savings explicitly.
- Anchor the recommended tier: "Most Popular" or "Best Value" badge.
- Clear CTA per tier. The recommended tier's CTA gets the highest contrast.
- Show what's included per tier, not what's excluded. Use checkmarks, not X marks.
- See Simon (price communication): price is the most powerful signal of value. Don't apologize for it — frame it.
- If complex pricing, link to a dedicated pricing page rather than cramming it into the landing page.

**Social Proof (Deep)**
- Testimonials with name, title, company, and specific result. Not "Love it!" — "Cut our deployment time from 2 weeks to 3 hours."
- Case study snippet: one paragraph with before → after → metric. Link to full case study.
- If available: industry awards, press mentions, integration partnerships.

**Final CTA**
- Recap the value proposition. Restate the guarantee or risk reversal (free trial, cancel anytime, no credit card required).
- CTA button. Same action as the hero, reinforced by everything above it.

### Awareness Context

Traffic often arrives Solution-Aware or Product-Aware (from search, comparison pages, referrals, retargeting). They know solutions exist — they're evaluating yours. Lead more directly with the product and its differentiation. Don't spend paragraphs explaining the problem they already understand.

### CTA Architecture

- **After hero:** Start trial / see demo (for ready visitors)
- **On pricing cards:** Tier-specific CTAs
- **After testimonials:** Reinforced CTA with social proof context
- **Sticky bottom bar (mobile):** Persistent CTA that doesn't obscure content

### Anti-Patterns

- Feature table with 47 checkmarks (nobody reads these — pick 3-4 that matter)
- No product screenshots (the product IS the selling point — show it)
- "Book a demo" as the only CTA for a self-serve product (creates unnecessary friction)
- Pricing hidden behind "Contact Sales" when the buyer expects transparency
- Hero section that describes the category, not the product ("The modern data platform" — what does it DO?)

## Lead Capture Page

Exchange value for an email address. Shortest format. The page has one job: get the opt-in. Every element that doesn't serve that job is a distraction that should be removed.

### Section-by-Section Blueprint

**Headline**
- Specific result + format. "The [Number]-[Type] [Framework/Template/Checklist] for [Specific Result]"
- Examples: "The 7-Step Cold Email Template That Books 3+ Calls Per Week" or "The Webinar Revenue Calculator for Course Creators"
- Specificity signals value. "Free Guide" is weak. "The 23-Page Breakdown of How We Generated $140K From One Webinar" is specific enough to believe.

**Subheadline**
- Who it's for + what problem it solves. One sentence.
- "For [audience] who [struggle]. Download this and [specific outcome]."

**Bullet Benefits**
- 3-5 bullets. Not features of the lead magnet — what they'll be able to DO after consuming it.
- Each bullet: curiosity + specificity. "The exact subject line formula that gets 43% open rates (page 7)" — not "Learn about email subject lines."
- Bullets from Edwards: fascination bullets that create incomplete curiosity loops.
- **Blind bullets (Fladlien):** Promise a specific benefit but keep the mechanism secret — the only way to find out is to download. "The 11-word follow-up that re-opens dead conversations (page 3)" beats "Includes a follow-up email template." Write them using the "First, Last" technique: create the lead magnet content first, then write bullets that tease the best parts.

**Visual**
- Image of the lead magnet: cover mockup, preview pages, dashboard screenshot.
- Makes it feel like a real, tangible thing — not a generic PDF.
- If it's a tool or calculator, show it working with real data.

**Form**
- Email only. Or name + email if personalization matters.
- Fewer fields = more conversions. Every additional field costs 5-10% of submissions.
- CTA button text: specific benefit. "Get the Checklist" — not "Submit." "Send Me the Templates" — not "Download."

**Optional Elements (use sparingly)**
- One testimonial from someone who used the lead magnet. Brief.
- Social proof number: "Join 2,400+ operators who downloaded this."
- Trust signals: "No spam. Unsubscribe anytime." (Only if the audience needs this reassurance.)

### Awareness Context

Varies by traffic source:
- **Cold traffic (ads):** Needs more context. Add 1-2 sentences of problem agitation above the headline. The ad did the initial hook — the page must deliver on the ad's promise.
- **Warm traffic (from content, referral):** Can be extremely brief. They already trust you — just tell them what they get.
- **Search traffic:** Match the search intent in the headline. If they searched "cold email templates," the headline should include those exact words.

### CTA Architecture

One CTA. One action. Remove everything that isn't serving the opt-in:
- No navigation bar
- No footer links
- No sidebar
- No social media icons
- No "learn more about us"

The page is a dead end by design. They either opt in or leave.

### Anti-Patterns

- Trying to sell on a lead capture page (this is not a sales page — don't agitate for 8 paragraphs)
- Generic lead magnet naming ("Free Guide" or "Download Our Ebook" — specificity is the value signal)
- Form with 5+ fields (name, email, company, title, phone — you'll get 20% of the submissions)
- No visual of the lead magnet (if you can't see it, it doesn't feel real)
- CTA button that says "Submit" (this is a surrender word, not an action word)

## Service Page

Agency, consulting, freelance service. The fundamental shift: this page sells the diagnostic, not the solution. You're not pitching what you do — you're demonstrating that you understand the client's situation better than anyone else. The sale happens on the call. The page earns the call.

Enns's positioning principles drive this page type: expertise is inferred through demonstration, not claimed through credentials.

### Section-by-Section Blueprint

**Hero Section**
- Who you serve + what problem you solve. One sentence. Specific and narrow.
- Not: "We're a full-service marketing agency."
- Instead: "We build demand generation engines for B2B SaaS companies transitioning from founder-led sales."
- Subline: The specific outcome, with proof. "Our clients average 3.2x pipeline growth in the first 90 days."
- CTA: Application-framed. "Apply for a Diagnostic Call" — not "Contact Us."

**Credibility Bar**
- Client logo row or "who we've worked with." Brief.
- Countersignaled — understated. The logos speak. Don't add "TRUSTED BY INDUSTRY LEADERS!" above a row of Fortune 500 logos.
- If no recognizable logos: skip this section entirely rather than padding with unknowns. Move proof to the Results section.

**The Problem (Pattern Recognition)**
- Describe the situation your ideal client is in. Not their general industry problem — the specific inflection point where they need you.
- Enns: demonstrate pattern recognition. "When B2B SaaS companies hit $2M ARR, three things typically break: the founder can't run every sales call, the pipeline depends on 2-3 referral sources, and marketing is a part-time intern posting on LinkedIn."
- This is the most important section. When the reader thinks "that's exactly my situation," the sale is 80% made.
- 2-3 paragraphs. Specific enough that wrong-fit prospects self-select out.

**The Approach**
- Share methodology — phases, process, timeline. Not the answers — the approach.
- Diagnostic framing: "We start by understanding your situation before prescribing. Every engagement begins with a 2-week diagnostic."
- Confidence: "Here's how we work" — not "Here's what we could potentially do depending on your needs."
- Show the phases. Give them a name. Make the process feel structured and repeatable: "Phase 1: Diagnostic (2 weeks) → Phase 2: Foundation (30 days) → Phase 3: Scale (ongoing)."
- Don't give away the answers. The methodology is the preview — the specifics are what they're buying.

**Results**
- Case studies as mini-stories: Situation → problem → what you did → specific result.
- Named clients if possible. Anonymized if required, but name the industry and company stage.
- The damaging admission (Kennedy): admit what you DON'T do. "We don't do brand awareness campaigns. We don't manage social media. We do one thing: build pipelines that generate qualified opportunities." This makes everything else more believable.
- 2-3 case studies. Quality over quantity. Each should feature a different type of result or client profile.

**Investment**
- State the range upfront. "Engagements start at $X/month" or "Projects typically run $X-$Y."
- This qualifies and anchors simultaneously. Prospects who can't afford it self-select out. Prospects who can afford it are anchored to your range before the call.
- Don't hide pricing. Enns: the expert states their price with confidence. Hiding it signals you'll negotiate based on what the client can afford.
- Brief framing: what they get for the investment, how billing works, minimum commitment.

**CTA**
- Application or diagnostic framing. "Book a Diagnostic Call" — not "Get in Touch."
- Brief description of what happens on the call: "We'll review your current pipeline, identify the bottleneck, and determine if we're the right fit."
- Anti-guarantee: "This is for B2B SaaS companies between $1M-$10M ARR with a product that's already selling. If you're pre-revenue, [alternative resource]."
- The exclusion is the trust signal. Willingness to turn away wrong-fit clients proves you don't need every deal.

**FAQ**
- Handles scope, timeline, process, and fit questions.
- "What if it doesn't work?" → Direct answer with proof, not deflection.
- "How long until we see results?" → Specific timeline with context: "Most clients see first qualified opportunities in 30-45 days. Full pipeline maturity takes 90 days."
- "What do you need from us?" → Clear expectations upfront.
- 4-6 questions. Focus on decision-stage objections.

### Awareness Context

Traffic usually arrives Product-Aware or Solution-Aware (referral, inbound content, search). They know they need help — the page proves you're the right help. Don't waste space explaining why they need a service — explain why they need THIS service.

For cold traffic (from content marketing or ads): add more pattern recognition in the Problem section. They may not know they need help — show them their situation first.

### CTA Architecture

Three placements, application framing throughout:
1. **After hero** — for referral traffic who arrived pre-sold
2. **After results** — for prospects convinced by proof
3. **After investment** — for prospects who've self-qualified on price

Every CTA reinforces selectivity: "Apply" or "Book a Diagnostic" — not "Get a Quote."

### Anti-Patterns

- "We're a passionate team of experts" in the hero (nobody cares about your passion — show you understand their problem)
- Listing every service you offer (breadth signals generalist — depth signals expert)
- "Contact us for pricing" (signals you don't know what you charge or you'll price based on desperation)
- Generic case studies without specific metrics ("We helped Company X grow" — grow how much? in what timeframe?)
- No exclusion criteria (serving everyone means you're special to no one)
- CTA that says "Get a Free Consultation" (devalues your time — diagnostics aren't free, they're earned)
