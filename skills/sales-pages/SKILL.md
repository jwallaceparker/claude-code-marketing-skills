# Sales Pages

You write complete sales pages. Not copy advice. Not "tips for better landing pages." A finished page: hero section, problem agitation, mechanism, proof, offer stack, guarantee, CTA — structured by direct-response principles and awareness-level matching. The page copy is ready to hand to a designer or drop into a builder.

This skill consumes the offers skill output. The offers skill builds the offer (value stack, guarantee, positioning, pricing). This skill turns that offer into a converting page. If no offer document exists, you'll build the offer structure inline — but `/offers` first produces better results.

## What Claude Gets Wrong with Sales Pages

**49a. Confusion as charisma killer (Cabane: The Charisma Myth).** Every point of confusion — vague value prop, unclear next step, surprise pricing, unexplained jargon — is a discomfort point that kills the page's magnetism. The reader should never wonder "what is this?" or "what do I do next?" Cognitive discomfort from confusion destroys the charismatic effect built by every other element. A sales page can have compelling copy, strong proof, and a solid offer, but if the reader hits a moment of "wait, what?" the spell breaks. The page should feel effortless: the reader knows what this is, who it's for, what it does, why it matters, and what happens when they click. Any moment where comprehension stalls — a headline that requires two reads, a feature that needs decoding, a CTA whose destination is ambiguous — creates friction that undoes charisma. Run a confusion audit before shipping: Can a distracted reader understand the offer in 30 seconds of scanning? Is the CTA destination obvious? Does any claim require specialized knowledge to parse? Is the price reveal clear or does it require calculation? Confusion points: vague headlines that could describe any product in the category, features described in internal jargon, CTAs that don't state what happens next, pricing that requires adding up multiple line items to understand the total. Every confusion point is a place the reader's brain works harder than it should, and that effort creates discomfort that registers as "something's off" even when the reader can't name the source.

Without guidance, sales page writing fails in predictable ways:

**Feature narration instead of transformation.** Lists what the product does instead of what changes in the buyer's life. "Our platform provides real-time analytics, automated reporting, and team dashboards" is a feature list. "Stop guessing which campaigns are working. Know by 9am Monday — and tell your CEO with one click" is transformation. **Dunford's three-layer filter sharpens this further:** For every feature mentioned, verify it passes through attribute → value → differentiated value. The attribute is what you have ("unified cross-platform analytics"). The value is what customers get ("see all campaign performance in one view"). The differentiated value is what customers get from you that they cannot get from any alternative ("the only tool where you can cross-reference TikTok, Instagram, and YouTube performance in real time without switching dashboards"). If the value can be delivered by any competitor, it's table stakes, not differentiation — and pages that lead with table stakes compete on price, not value. The filter: for each feature on the page, ask "can the buyer get this value from any alternative?" If yes, either remove it or reframe it to show the aspect only you deliver.

**Same opening for every audience.** Ignores awareness levels — pitches the product to problem-aware prospects who haven't been sold on the category yet. A prospect from a cold ad needs problem agitation. A prospect from your email list needs the offer. Same page, different openings.

**No psychological sequence.** Follows AIDA or a logical outline (About → Features → Pricing → CTA) instead of an emotional journey. Sales pages work because each section earns the right to the next. Problem earns mechanism. Mechanism earns proof. Proof earns the offer. Skip a step and the reader drops.

**Sequencing is conversion architecture (Greene: The Art of Seduction).** Each section must complete its job before the next section works. Recognition → problem → mechanism → proof → offer is a sequence that can't be reordered. Moving CTA above proof is a sequencing error — the reader hasn't earned the right to believe yet. Placing mechanism before problem is a sequencing error — the reader doesn't know why they need a solution. The proof section placed before the mechanism explains nothing because the reader doesn't know what they're seeing proof of. Every section out of sequence breaks the page's ability to convert, no matter how strong each section is in isolation. Diagnose sequencing errors before rewriting copy: is each section doing its job before the next section attempts to do its job?

**Proof as an afterthought.** One testimonial block near the bottom. Instead, proof should be woven throughout — after every claim that triggers "does this really work?" Proof through narrative beats proof through stats.

**Hedging everything.** "This might help you potentially achieve better results in some cases." Direct-response copy makes direct claims backed by proof. "47 clients generated an average of $23K in new pipeline within 60 days" — specific, provable, no hedge words. **Commit fully to the angle (Lumet).** A page that hedges between two audiences or two value propositions serves neither. A page that's half-provocative and half-safe is worse than one that commits fully to either direction — the hedged version signals that the page doesn't believe its own argument. If the positioning is "we're the premium option," don't soften it with discount language. If the angle is contrarian, don't qualify it back to consensus. If the page targets founders, don't widen to "founders and marketers and operators and anyone who..." Accept that some readers won't respond. The readers who do respond will respond harder because the page spoke directly to them instead of vaguely at everyone. A page that commits to an angle and loses 30% of visitors converts the remaining 70% at a higher rate than a page that hedges and keeps 100% of visitors lukewarm. Hedged copy is not timid writing — it is visible need, and the reader's System 1 detects it immediately. Write from a posture that the offer exists for a specific person and the page's job is to help that person recognize themselves, not to convince everyone who lands (Camp).

**No visual hierarchy direction.** Walls of text with no scan path, no section architecture, no layout notes. A designer receiving this copy should know the headline size, the CTA contrast, the spacing rhythm, and where the eye goes first.

**Capability claims instead of pattern-recognition evidence (Baker: The Business of Expertise).** "We help companies grow their pipeline" is a capability claim — it says what you do. "In 47 companies we've worked with, we've seen the same pattern: the outbound sequence breaks in week two because of X, and fixing X unlocks Y" is pattern-recognition evidence — it signals expertise earned through repeated observation. The page should lead with what you've learned from seeing the same problem 40+ times, not with what services you offer. Capability claims invite comparison to anyone who offers the same capability. Pattern claims position you as the expert who has seen enough to recognize what others miss.

**Direct claims trigger skepticism — insinuate instead (Greene: The Art of Seduction).** "We're the industry leader" triggers evaluation. "Here's what happened when 47 companies switched" triggers belief. Plant conclusions through story and juxtaposition rather than stating claims directly. Self-generated conclusions are trusted more than stated claims because the reader feels they discovered the truth rather than being told it. A case study showing transformation lets the reader draw the conclusion; a direct claim about being "the best" invites them to argue. Replace "We save you 10 hours a week" with a day-in-the-life story that makes the reader calculate the savings themselves. The gap between implication and conclusion is where trust forms.

**Vendor signals in page structure (Baker: The Business of Expertise).** "Free consultation" / "Always available" / "Flexible on scope" / "Book a call anytime" / "We can also help with X, Y, Z" — these are vendor signals that communicate demand does not exceed supply. Expertise pages communicate the opposite: limited availability, application processes, capacity constraints, willingness to say no. A page that reads "we're hoping you'll choose us" converts worse than a page that reads "we're being selective about who we work with." Audit every element for eagerness: Does this sound like we need their business? If yes, rewrite or remove.

**Eagerness in CTA language (Baker: The Business of Expertise).** "Schedule your free call today!" has an exclamation point that reads as nervous enthusiasm. "Apply to work with us" communicates selectivity. "We'd love to help" signals availability; "We take on four new clients per quarter" signals scarcity. The CTA section is where expertise positioning often collapses into vendor pleading. Write CTAs from the posture of a business with a waitlist, not a business hoping for the phone to ring.

**Generic CTAs.** "Learn More," "Get Started," "Submit" — zero specificity, zero benefit, zero action. CTAs should state the transformation: "Start Building Your Pipeline" not "Get Started." "Get the Templates" not "Download."

**No exclusion copy (Godin).** The page tries to appeal to everyone and ends up resonating with no one. Include a "this is NOT for you if..." section. Exclusion strengthens belonging for those who remain. Universal appeal weakens identity. Saying "this isn't for people who want quick fixes" makes the page feel more real to people who want the deep work. The people who leave weren't going to buy anyway. Covetousness is not countdown timers — it's the feeling of exclusion from something valuable (Greene). Qualification copy ("This only works if you're already doing X") and disqualification sections ("This is not for you if...") are scarcity architecture. They make the reader work for belonging instead of feeling automatically included. Paradoxically, making the offer feel less available to everyone makes it feel more valuable to the right person. The "this is not for you if" section is not filtering copy but a veto-granting move — explicitly granting the reader permission to leave makes those who stay feel genuinely chosen rather than captured, and that agency is what makes the subsequent offer feel like a free choice rather than a funnel (Camp).

**Supply-side openings (Moesta: Demand-Side Sales 101).** "Our AI-powered platform provides real-time analytics and automated reporting" is supply-side — it starts with the product and pushes outward. "When your research takes longer than your outreach, you know something's broken" is demand-side — it starts in the customer's world and pulls toward the solution. The first line of every section should start in the customer's world. If it starts with "we" or the product name, rewrite it. Supply-side copy sounds like a brochure. Demand-side copy sounds like someone describing a problem they actually have. Read the copy aloud: if it sounds like a product description, it's supply-side. If it sounds like a conversation about a real struggle, it's demand-side.

**Resolving tension immediately (Klaff: Pitch Anything).** Opening a loop and closing it in the same paragraph kills forward momentum. "What's the secret? It's our three-step process." gives the reader no reason to keep scrolling — the question was answered before curiosity could build. "There's a reason 90% of outbound fails in the first week — and it has nothing to do with the script." creates a concrete question the reader must resolve by continuing. The page should feel like a story unfolding — each section raising a question that the next section answers while opening a new question. When all questions are resolved simultaneously, narrative drive dies and the reader's attention releases.

**No remarkable test before writing (Godin).** Starting the page without naming the one remarkable thing in a single sentence. If you can't name it, the page has a product problem, not a copy problem. Copy amplifies remarkability; it cannot create it. Before writing the hero section, answer: "What is the remarkable thing here?" If the answer is vague superlatives ("high-quality," "innovative," "best-in-class"), stop. Find the specific, weird, polarizing thing that makes this worth talking about.

**"Very good" language (Godin).** Comprehensive, robust, seamless, cutting-edge, best-in-class, world-class, end-to-end. These are brown-cow words that describe adequacy, not remarkability. They are the uniform of invisibility. If a sentence could appear on any competitor's page, delete it — it's not remarkable, it's filler. Audit the page for these words and replace them with something specific and unexpected.

**Closing with logic instead of emotion.** Ends on a features table or pricing grid. The close should paint two futures and make inaction costly (Duarte) — not a feature recap but a fork: "In 90 days you're either ___ or still ___. Same talent. Different systems. Choose." The final screen should make sitting still feel uncomfortable. Specific scene if they act, specific scene if they don't — then a direct CTA at the emotional peak.

**Premature tension resolution (Godin).** Describing the problem and the solution in the same paragraph. "You're spending 10 hours a week on reporting — but with our tool, that drops to 12 minutes." The reader never felt the gap. They skimmed past the pain because the bridge appeared before they needed it. Let the reader sit with the tension. Dedicate the problem section to making the current state unbearable. Let the mechanism arrive later, in its own section, as relief. The distance between the wound and the remedy is where desire forms. A page that resolves tension the moment it creates it produces nodding, not buying.

**No villain.** The problem exists in a vacuum. Nobody caused it. Nothing to fight against. Every sales page needs a villain — the industry, conventional wisdom, the old method, the thing that's been keeping them stuck. The villain gives the problem a face and the mechanism a reason to exist. The strongest villains come with explicit insider/outsider labels — "operators vs. guessers," "builders vs. browsers" — that make the reader self-select into the in-group by identity, not just by interest.

**The villain should be a belief, not a competitor (Godin).** "Other agencies" is not a villain. "The assumption that more content equals more leads" is. Heretical positioning challenges what the mainstream accepts without questioning. When the villain is a belief the reader already suspects is wrong, they feel like insiders who've discovered something others haven't. That shared opposition creates belonging faster than any value proposition.

**Positioning against competitors instead of alternatives (Dunford).** Most pages compare features to direct competitors — "we're faster than Tool X, cheaper than Tool Y." But Dunford's research shows most B2B deals are lost to "no decision," and the real competition is what buyers would do if your product didn't exist: manual processes, spreadsheets, hiring someone, doing nothing, adjacent category tools. The problem/alternatives section should walk through what prospects currently do (status quo) and why those approaches fail, before introducing your product as the solution. Naming competitors the buyer hasn't heard of introduces new options and delays decisions. Positioning against the status quo makes your solution feel like the natural next step instead of one option among many. The test: before mentioning any feature, establish what the buyer is doing today and why it's not working. This is setup-phase positioning that makes differentiation land harder than product-to-product comparison ever could.

**Opening with credentials or product.** "Founded in 2019, we're a team of passionate experts..." Nobody cares about your founding year until they care about their problem. Earn attention with the problem. Build credibility through proof. Save the origin story for the mechanism section.

**Product-first openings trigger sales resistance (Greene: The Art of Seduction).** The best hero sections don't start with the product — they start with the visitor's situation and reframe the problem before introducing the solution. "Our AI-powered platform automates your workflow" triggers defenses. "You spent 3 hours yesterday doing what should take 10 minutes" enters their world. Direct product-first openings only work on prospects who already want the product and are price-comparing. For everyone else — the majority of traffic — the indirect approach earns the right to introduce the solution by first proving you understand the situation. The reframe comes before the offer. The visitor must feel seen before they'll consider being sold.

**Cargo cult page structure.** A page can have every section — hero, problem, proof, mechanism, offer, CTA — and still convert at zero because the sections have form without substance. The mechanism section follows the template but doesn't actually explain why this works. The proof section includes testimonials but none that address the specific claim above them. The psychological sequence hits every beat but earns nothing because the writer followed the outline instead of understanding what each section must do for THIS audience. If you can't explain the mechanism to a non-expert without jargon, you're building a cargo cult mechanism section.

**Blocking objections instead of accepting and advancing (Johnstone: Impro).** "Is this hard to learn?" / "No, it's incredibly easy!" blocks the prospect's concern and damages trust. "Is this hard to learn?" / "The core setup takes 10 minutes. Most users say the learning curve is about two days before it feels natural." accepts and advances. FAQ sections that start with "No" or "Actually" are blocking. Rewrite to accept first, then build.

**Scale misdiagnosis (Alexander).** When a page isn't converting, the instinct is to rewrite headlines and CTAs — the smallest-scale patterns. But if the offer is wrong, no copy optimization fixes it. If the positioning is wrong, no page structure saves it. Before optimizing copy, check the scales above it: is the offer compelling? Is the positioning clear? Is the audience right? A beautiful page with a weak offer is a beautiful window in a dead building. Before touching copy, verify the offer's unit economics pass the 30-day payback rule (Hormozi): does the offer generate at least 2x the customer acquisition cost within 30 days? If not, it's an offer math problem, not a copy problem — send the user to `/offers` before rewriting headlines.

**Kitchen-sink persuasion (Ortiz: too many methods, Aftel: restraint creates impact).** Stacks every persuasion technique — scarcity, urgency, social proof, authority, guarantee, discount, bonus, countdown timer — hoping volume compensates for depth. A signature perfume has 8-12 elements working in harmony, not 40 fighting for attention — a sales page needs 2-3 dominant persuasion drivers executed deeply, not 7 executed shallowly. Each additional tactic adds noise and increases the chance the reader sees the method. Identify the two or three most relevant persuasion drivers for this audience and offer, and execute them deeply. A page with world-class social proof and a clear guarantee outperforms a page that touches every persuasion principle superficially.

**The page is a temporal composition (Aftel).** The hero section is your top notes — immediate, must be compelling enough to earn the scroll, but volatile if not followed by substance. The middle 60% (problem, mechanism, proof) is where character and substance live — this is the heart of the page. The offer section and close are the base — what lingers in memory after they leave, what they remember when deciding to return or buy later. Don't frontload everything in the hero — let the value deepen through the scroll. The hero earns the right to reveal the mechanism; the mechanism earns the right to make the offer land.

**Every section serves the page's unified intention (Aftel).** When proof competes with story, when testimonials compete with mechanism, when multiple sections fight for attention, the page becomes noise rather than composition. Each section supports the page's single argument — the spine (Tharp). If a section is individually impressive but doesn't advance the unified intention, it breaks composition harmony. The diagnostic: does this section support the page's argument, or does it demand attention for itself?

**Too-perfect proof stack (Ortiz).** Every testimonial is five stars, every result is extraordinary, every claim is superlative. The reader's suspicion activates — they suspect selection bias because they're right to. Include a three-star result: "Great product, onboarding was confusing but support helped me through it." That imperfection makes every five-star review more believable. A page where everything is flawless reads as curated, not credible.

**Product-as-hero copy (Sierra: Badass).** "Our platform enables..." / "Powerful analytics" / "Seamless integrations" are product descriptors. The user doesn't care what the product does — they care what they become. Every sentence that starts with "we" or the product name is suspect. Rewrite every product capability as a user capability: "You will spot the problem before your boss does" beats "Powerful analytics." The test: remove the product from the sentence and see if the benefit still makes sense. "Real-time dashboards" means nothing. "Know which campaign is failing before your morning coffee gets cold" means everything.

**Perceptual exposure as proof (Sierra: Badass).** Show what "great" looks like before explaining how to get there. Case studies that show the actual before/after — the real emails, the real dashboard, the real results — build pattern recognition faster than claims about results. Templates and examples are skill-building tools, not convenience features. A gallery of "what our best users created in their first week" does more for conversion than a testimonial carousel.

**Visible selling mechanics (Ortiz: eliminating the method).** The reader can see the sales framework: problem → agitate → solve → close. Urgency feels manufactured. Social proof feels planted. The transition from "content" to "pitch" has a visible seam. When the structure becomes the content — when the reader can predict the next section — you're performing a template, not communicating. The offer should emerge as the logical conclusion of the argument, not as a separate act the reader was waiting for.

**Wholeness through subtraction (Alexander).** A page with 12 proof points feels like a page trying too hard. A page with 3 proof points perfectly relevant to THIS audience feels whole. Every section must serve both itself and the page's single argument. If a section is well-written but doesn't advance the argument, remove it even if it's good in isolation. Every omission is a structural argument — what you cut shapes what remains. Each cut should be a decision about what the page argues, not just about length. Removing the third case study isn't trimming — it's choosing to let the first two carry more weight, which changes the page's persuasive structure. A page shortened for brevity and a page shortened for argument feel completely different to the reader, even at the same word count (McPhee).

**CTA discoverability failure (Norman).** The buy button is below multiple scrolls, blends into the background, or competes with other elements. Norman's discoverability test: Can users determine what action to take? If they have to hunt for the CTA, the page fails.

**Every exit leaks prospects (Greene: The Art of Seduction).** Remove all exits — no navigation bar, no footer links to blog, no competing CTAs, no "learn more about the company" links. The landing page is an isolation chamber for one decision. Every link that isn't the primary CTA is a leak back to the prospect's default world. The visitor who clicks to read your About page rarely returns to buy. A page with a nav bar isn't a sales page — it's a website page that happens to have a CTA. Conversion-focused pages have one way out: through the offer.

**Placing the highest-effort ask where motivation is lowest (Fogg: Tiny Habits).** The hero section catches visitors at peak motivation. By mid-page, motivation has dropped. By the CTA section at the bottom, it has dropped further. A "Book a 30-minute demo" CTA at the page bottom asks for the scaled behavior at the lowest-motivation point. Either move high-effort asks to emotional peaks (after the set piece, after the strongest proof), or shrink the bottom CTA to a Starter Step: "See it in action — 60 seconds" requires almost no motivation. "Schedule a demo" requires commitment, calendar coordination, and social effort. Frame every CTA as the smallest possible version of the desired action — what Fogg calls "so easy you can't say no."

**Visceral-behavioral conflict (Norman).** The page looks beautiful (visceral) but is confusing to navigate (behavioral). Or the page is highly usable (behavioral) but aesthetically off for the audience (visceral). Both levels must work; one cannot compensate for the other.

**Wide evaluation gulf on the offer (Norman).** The prospect scrolls through the whole page and still isn't sure what they're getting. "What exactly am I buying?" remains unanswered. The offer section must close this gulf completely — if interpretation requires guesswork, the page has failed.

**Problem sections that describe instead of evoke (Heath: Switch).** "Manual processes are inefficient" is Analyze → Think. "That sinking feeling when you open a spreadsheet with 200 rows and realize half the links are broken" is See → Feel → Change. The Elephant doesn't respond to descriptions of problems — it responds to re-experiencing them. Problem copy should make the reader feel the pain again, not just understand that it exists.

## When You Run This Skill

### Step 1: Read the Brief

Read `.claude/BRIEF.md`. This is required. If it doesn't exist, tell the user: "I need a BRIEF.md to write a sales page that matches your product, audience, and positioning. Run `/brief` first, or point me to your brief."

Pull from the brief:
- **Audience/ICP:** Who lands on this page, what they care about, what language they use
- **Problem:** The struggling moment — this becomes the problem/agitation section
- **Objections:** These determine where proof gets placed and what the FAQ must address
- **Proof:** Testimonials, metrics, results — fuel for proof stacking throughout the page
- **Differentiation:** What makes this different — this becomes the mechanism section
- **Voice:** Tone, style rules — unless overridden by VOICE.md

### Step 2: Check for Voice

Check if `.claude/VOICE.md` exists.

**If VOICE.md exists:**
- Read it. This governs voice, tone, and style for the page copy.
- Check if BRIEF.md also has a Voice section.
- If both exist, scan for conflicts: tone direction, style rules, personality descriptors, word avoidance lists.
- **If conflict detected:** Stop. Tell the user which items conflict. Ask them to reconcile before proceeding.
- **If no conflict:** VOICE.md governs voice. BRIEF.md governs everything else.

**If no VOICE.md:** Use the Voice section from BRIEF.md.

**Tone vs mood as voice calibration (Lumet).** Before writing, decide the page's mood AND tone separately — they are different instruments. Mood is what the reader feels in each section: urgency in the problem section, curiosity in the mechanism, confidence in the proof, excitement in the offer. Mood varies across the page and should vary — a page at one emotional register is monotone. Tone is your stance toward the material: conspiratorial ("let me show you what the industry doesn't want you to know"), authoritative ("here's exactly how this works"), empathetic ("you've been working too hard for too little"). Tone holds across the entire page — it's the consistent voice the reader trusts. The same urgency mood reads completely differently with a conspiratorial tone vs. a clinical tone vs. an empathetic tone. A page that confuses tone with mood either flattens the emotional arc (same mood everywhere because the writer is "holding tone") or sounds inconsistent (tone shifting because the writer is chasing mood). Decide the tonal stance once. Let the mood move freely within it.

### Step 3: Check for Offer Document

Check if the user has an existing offer document — output from `/offers` or a similar structured offer brief.

**If offer document exists:** Consume it. Pull the value stack, guarantee, pricing, positioning, and objection map. Don't rebuild what the offers skill already built — reference it.

**If no offer document:** Build the offer structure inline using offers skill principles (value equation, value stack, guarantee). But note to the user: "Running `/offers` first would give you a stronger offer foundation. Want me to build the offer inline or should you run `/offers` first?"

**Offer-first test (Fladlien):** Whether from a document or built inline, state the offer in 2 sentences. If the reaction is "wait, really? How?" — the offer is strong enough to carry the page. If not, the offer needs work before you write copy around it. No amount of copy optimization saves a weak offer.

**Grand Slam test (Hormozi).** Before writing a single section, state the offer in two sentences and ask: "would a reasonable person feel stupid saying no?" If not, the page can't fix the offer — send the user to `/offers` first. A beautifully written sales page around a mediocre offer is a polished container with nothing inside. The page should be the last thing built, not the first.

**Attraction offer structure adjustment (Hormozi).** When the offer document specifies an attraction offer type, adjust page structure to lead with the attraction mechanic. Win Your Money Back offers lead with the guarantee as the hero — the guarantee IS the headline, not a section near the bottom. Pay Less Now offers lead with split-pay framing — monthly cost in the hero, total price revealed later. Giveaway offers lead with the free value and gate the upsell. Each attraction type has a different structural center of gravity; a page that buries the attraction mechanic in the offer stack section wastes the offer's primary conversion lever.

**Tier presentation as complete products (Ramanujam-Tacke).** When presenting tiers on a sales page, frame each tier as a complete product for its segment, not a feature ladder. Name tiers for the user type they serve ("Explorer," "Operator," "Scale") not for size ("Starter," "Pro," "Enterprise"). Each tier's copy should lead with what it delivers, not what it withholds. If the primary message of a tier is what's missing compared to the next tier up, the packaging is broken — send the user to `/pricing` before writing the page.

**Value ladder rung awareness (Brunson).** Before writing, identify which rung this page sits on. A bait-rung page (lead magnet) needs different copy than a frontend page ($47-$297) or a backend page ($10K+). Each rung has a different job: bait pages sell the click, frontend pages sell the transformation, backend pages sell the relationship and access. The copy, proof density, and emotional register all shift with the rung. More importantly, each page should create natural desire for the next rung — the thank-you page after opt-in is the highest-converting upsell real estate because the visitor just committed and their buying momentum is at its peak. A page that converts but creates no pull toward the next rung is a dead end in the funnel, not a step on the ladder.

### Step 4: Identify Page Type and Route

Identify the page type from the user's request or ask:

| "SaaS landing," "product page," "app landing page" | SaaS Landing Page in `page-types.md` |
| "opt-in page," "lead capture," "lead magnet page," "squeeze page" | Lead Capture Page in `page-types.md` |
| "service page," "agency page," "consulting page," "freelance page" | Service Page in `page-types.md` |

If unclear, ask: "What type of page — are you selling a product/program (sales page), software (SaaS landing), collecting emails (lead capture), or pitching a service (service page)?"

**Funnel page type discipline (Brunson).** A squeeze page and a sales page need completely different copy. Squeeze pages have one job: collect the email. No nav, no links, one button, one promise — curiosity or a specific deliverable, nothing else. Sales pages persuade and convert through the full psychological sequence. OTO (one-time offer) pages capture maximum value while buying intent is at its peak — they lead with the yes the visitor just said and extend it, not restart the selling process. Thank-you pages are selling opportunities, not dead ends — the visitor just acted, which means they're more likely to act again in the next 60 seconds than at any other point in the relationship. Every page type has one CTA and one job. A squeeze page with two buttons is a squeeze page that loses half its conversions. A sales page with nav links is a sales page with exit ramps. Name the page type, then enforce its constraints — mixing page types produces pages that do two things poorly instead of one thing well.

**Audience contract awareness (Truby — Anatomy of Genres).** Each page type makes an implicit promise to the reader — violating this contract reads as incompetence even when the copy is technically correct. Long-form sales pages promise transformation through a complete arc (problem → mechanism → proof → offer). SaaS landing pages promise fit assessment and clarity on how the tool works. Lead capture pages promise quick value without commitment. Service pages promise capability demonstration and relationship preview. Before writing, name what the reader expects from this page genre and verify your outline delivers it — breaking the contract creates reader distrust independent of copy quality.

### Step 5: Assess Awareness Level

Determine the primary traffic source — this dictates the opening:

- **Ads (cold):** Probably Problem-Aware or Unaware → agitate, then solve
- **Retargeting ads:** Product-Aware → lead with offer or promise
- **Email list / newsletter:** Solution-Aware or Product-Aware → lead with mechanism or offer
- **Organic search:** Solution-Aware → match search intent in the headline
- **Referral:** Most Aware or Product-Aware → lead with offer or social proof
- **Content (blog, social):** Problem-Aware → acknowledge the problem they just read about

If the page serves multiple traffic sources, optimize for the primary one and use CTA placement to catch the others.

**Traffic-temperature diagnostic for campaign pages (Walker).** Before building the page, ask: is this page receiving cold traffic (ads, organic, strangers) or warm-campaign traffic (preceded by pre-launch emails that already shifted beliefs)? If warm-campaign — the reader has been through a Sideways Sales Letter sequence that taught the opportunity, shifted beliefs, and built desire over multiple touchpoints before they ever hit this page — significantly reduce the teaching and education sections and lean into offer presentation. The pre-launch content already did the convincing; the page's job is to present the offer cleanly and close. A warm-campaign page that re-teaches everything the emails already taught feels redundant and breaks momentum. Cold-traffic pages need the full belief-shifting arc on the page itself because no prior content did that work. The diagnosis changes page architecture: cold pages are long-form persuasion engines, warm-campaign pages are offer-presentation surfaces. Get the temperature wrong and you either bore warm prospects with lessons they've already learned or rush cold prospects past beliefs they haven't formed yet.

### Step 6: Run Intake

Gather what the brief and offer document don't cover. Use `AskUserQuestion` or conversational follow-up.

**Required:**
- What type of page? (if not already clear)
- What's the offer? (from `/offers` output, or describe: product/service, price, guarantee)
- Where is traffic coming from? (determines awareness level and opening)
- What's the one action you want them to take? (buy, book a call, opt in, start trial)

**If available:**
- Existing proof — testimonials, case studies, metrics, credentials
- Existing page to improve (URL or copy) vs. building from scratch
- Design constraints — existing brand, template, builder limitations
- Competitive context — what alternatives the prospect is comparing against

### Step 7: Prepare Raw Material

Before writing, actively hunt for material beyond the brief — customer reviews, competitor landing pages, Reddit threads, support tickets, forum complaints. Capture the specific language and vivid details that make proof and problem sections visceral instead of abstract. The page written from a full reservoir of raw material sounds like it was written by someone who knows the reader's world; the page written from the brief alone sounds like marketing.

Also name the dominant sales page structure for this product category. Deconstruct 3-5 top-performing pages in the category before writing — not a casual scan, a structural teardown. For each page: what is the hero treatment (headline type, visual, subheadline pattern)? What is the section sequence? Where does proof appear and in what form? What is the CTA architecture (how many, where, what language)? What emotional register does each section operate at? Map the structural patterns that repeat across all of them — these are the category conventions the reader expects. Then map the gaps: what do none of them do? What objection do they all ignore? What emotional note is missing from every page in the category? The patterns tell you what the reader considers normal. The gaps tell you where the opportunity lives. Design at least one section that deliberately breaks from the dominant pattern — the section that feels fresh because it doesn't follow the template everyone else followed.

**Competitive landscape check (Hunter).** Beyond structural patterns, map the top 3-5 competitors' actual claims. What promises are they making? What language are they using? What claims are so saturated they've become wallpaper — "save 10 hours a week," "all-in-one platform," "built for teams like yours"? If three competitors already say it, saying it a fourth time doesn't differentiate — it confirms the category but adds nothing. The differentiation opportunity lives in the emotionally true angle nobody else owns: the specific frustration they're all dancing around, the honest admission none of them will make, the sharp position that requires giving something up. Find what's unsaid in the competitive landscape and say it.

**Attention-competition lens (Kane).** The competitive landscape is not just other products — it's everything competing for the prospect's attention in the moment they land. The prospect has 20 tabs open, a phone buzzing, and a meeting in 12 minutes. Your hero is not competing against the three competitor pages you analyzed — it's competing against Gmail, Slack, Twitter, and the back button, simultaneously, for the next 3 seconds. Frame the hero accordingly: the headline must win against everything on the screen, not just everything in the category. A headline that beats every competitor but loses to the subject line in the next tab over is a headline that loses. When reviewing the competitive landscape, add this layer: what is the prospect's attention environment when they land? Cold ad traffic arrives mid-scroll with zero commitment. Organic search traffic arrives mid-research with moderate intent. Email traffic arrives mid-inbox-scan with split focus. The hero must beat the attention environment, not just the category.

**The Red Bus trap (Sutherland).** "Faster/cheaper/better version of X" splits existing demand — it does not create new demand. If the positioning boils down to "we're like the market leader, but slightly improved on one axis," you are a blue bus competing with the red bus for the same riders while the car keeps its full share. Differentiate on a different dimension entirely. "We're like Slack, but faster" is a blue bus. "We're async-first — no real-time chat, no interruptions" is a different vehicle. When reviewing the competitive landscape, test every differentiator: does this give a prospect using the market leader a reason to switch that is NOT "more of the same, slightly better"? If not, the positioning needs a different dimension, not a better adjective.

**Section reuse across campaigns (Frost).** When the project has existing landing pages, audit the section patterns before building new ones — what hero treatments, social proof layouts, CTA blocks, and FAQ styles already exist? New campaigns should be assembly from proven sections, not net-new design. A library of 8-10 battle-tested section organisms — hero, problem, mechanism, proof strip, testimonial block, feature grid, FAQ accordion, CTA banner — that compose into any landing page is more valuable than any single page. The first landing page in a project creates the section library. Every subsequent page should compose from it, extending only when genuinely new patterns are needed.

### Step 8: Concept Test (Pre-Draft Gate)

*Source: Hunter (Screenwriting 434 — concept test)*

Before writing a single section, state the page's core promise in one sentence — the sentence that would make a prospect lean forward and say "tell me more." If it needs a paragraph to explain why it's interesting, the concept is too weak to carry a full page. This is not the page spine (that's structural) — this is the concept test: does the core promise itself create pull? A page with flawless structure and a weak concept converts nobody. If the concept doesn't pass, go back to the offer or the brief before drafting.

**Retelling test (Godin).** That same one-sentence promise must be something a happy customer can repeat to a friend. If the sentence requires context, qualifiers, or a second sentence to land, the page hasn't done its job — because the page's reach extends beyond the page itself. Every customer who buys becomes a potential storyteller, and they will retell the promise in one sentence or not at all. "It shows you exactly which leads are ready to buy this week" gets retold. "It's a comprehensive AI-powered pipeline intelligence platform with predictive analytics" does not. Write the retelling sentence first, then build the page to deliver it. If the page works but the retelling sentence doesn't exist, the page converts visitors but creates no word-of-mouth — and a page without retelling is a page with a ceiling.

**Share trigger diagnostic (Kane).** "Forwardable" is not specific enough. Name which sharing motivation the retelling sentence serves: Identity signaling ("Look how smart I am for finding this")? Practical value ("You need to see this — it solves the exact problem you told me about")? Social currency ("I found this before everyone else")? Emotional resonance ("This perfectly describes what we've been going through")? Each motivation produces a different kind of sharing behavior and reaches a different audience. A page optimized for practical-value sharing spreads through direct messages to specific people. A page optimized for identity signaling spreads through public posts. If you can't name the sharing motivation, the retelling sentence might be memorable but not forwardable — the reader remembers it but has no reason to pass it on.

**Start with the feeling, not the argument (Sullivan).** Before premise testing, answer two questions: "After reading this page, the reader should FEEL [specific emotion] and BELIEVE [specific belief]." Name both explicitly. Then verify every section builds that feeling — not just argues toward the belief. A page can be logically airtight and emotionally inert. The feeling is the design target; the argument is the scaffolding. If the concept test sentence creates pull but you can't name the feeling it should leave behind, the page will inform without moving.

### Step 9: Premise Test (Pre-Draft Gate)

*Source: Egri (The Art of Dramatic Writing — premise test)*

After the concept test passes, state the page's premise as a cause-and-effect sentence with force, conflict, and resolution. "Manual outreach leads to wasted years." "Invisible effort leads to quitting before the breakthrough." "Guessing at what works leads to spending more to earn less." The premise is not the concept — the concept tests whether the promise creates pull; the premise tests whether the page has a single controlling argument. Every section on the page must advance this premise. If a section doesn't, cut it — no matter how well-written it is. A page can pass the concept test (interesting promise) and still fail because it argues six things instead of one. The premise is the through-line that turns a collection of good sections into a single coherent argument. The page's premise is one sentence — not five benefits, ONE. If the page argues pricing AND ease AND results AND status, it's five pages masquerading as one. A page that tries to win on multiple fronts wins on none because each argument dilutes the others. Narrow until the premise is a single blade, not a Swiss Army knife (Sullivan).

**Belief audit gate (Albuquerque).** Before writing any section, state the false belief in Albuquerque's 16-word structure: "Most people believe [X]. But [mechanism] proves [Y]." If you can't fill it in, you don't understand the sale well enough to write the page. This is not the premise test (which checks that the page has a single controlling argument) or the concept test (which checks that the promise creates pull) — this is a belief test: can you name the specific thing the prospect holds as TRUE that makes your offer sound implausible? The premise might be sharp and the concept might pull, but if the blocking belief is unnamed, the page will argue past the real obstacle. Fill in the 16-word structure before drafting. If [X] feels vague — "most people believe it's hard" — you haven't found the belief yet. The belief is specific: "most people believe you need a sales team to fill pipeline." The mechanism is the reason that belief is wrong. The new truth is what replaces it. All three must be concrete enough to build a page around.

### Step 9a: Name the Set Piece (Pre-Draft Gate)

Name the set piece — the single moment on this page that the entire structure builds toward. This is the page's architectural center: the proof story that makes the mechanism undeniable, the before/after that collapses every objection, the number that stops the scroll. Write that moment before writing the hero, because the page's shape is determined by where the peak must land. A page built forward from the hero discovers its climax too late and buries it. A page built backward from the set piece knows exactly what every preceding section must earn (McPhee).

### Step 9a.5: Sketch the Emotional Beat Board (Pre-Draft Gate)

Before writing, sketch the page's emotional arc as a visual beat board — peaks and valleys mapped to scroll sections (Glebas: beat board). Peaks are the high-tension moments: problem agitation, the set piece, urgency/scarcity. Valleys are the relief moments: mechanism explanation, social proof that resolves tension, the guarantee. The shape should oscillate — two adjacent peaks with no valley between will exhaust the reader. A flat middle that neither peaks nor valleys loses them. This catches structural problems invisible in a linear outline: you can see at a glance whether the page has a single climax or two competing ones, whether the middle sags, whether urgency arrives too early. The sparkline (Duarte) and plot shape (Booker) are textual tools — the beat board is the visual complement that lets you see the page's emotional contour as a shape before writing a single section.

### Step 9b: Identify the Big Domino (Pre-Draft Gate)

*Source: Brunson (Expert Secrets — Big Domino)*

Before writing, identify the one belief that, if the reader accepts it, makes all other objections fall. This is the Big Domino. Focus 80% of the page's proof on this single belief. Secondary objections — price, timing, effort — inherit credibility from the Big Domino. If the reader believes "this approach structurally produces pipeline without cold outreach," then "will it work for my industry?" and "is it worth the price?" become smaller questions that resolve themselves. A page that spreads proof evenly across ten objections convinces nobody fully. A page that topples the Big Domino convinces them on the one thing that matters, and the rest tips over. The dominant belief isn't the biggest objection — it's what the prospect holds as TRUE about the category that makes your offer sound implausible (Albuquerque). Objections live on the surface; the blocking belief lives in their model of reality. "Will this work for my industry?" is an objection. "Outbound doesn't work anymore" is a belief. The objection can be answered with a case study; the belief requires dismantling the reader's entire frame before the case study even registers. If you're answering objections but the page still doesn't convert, you haven't found the belief — you've been swatting branches while the root holds.

### Step 9c: Promise Audit (Post-Draft Gate)

*Source: Mackendrick (On Film-Making — obligatory scenes)*

After the draft is complete, audit the page for undelivered promises. Every setup creates a debt: headline claims, teased case study results, "here's what you'll discover" hooks, opened loops, numbers dropped without context, mechanism names introduced without explanation. Track every promise the page makes — explicitly or implicitly — and verify each one is paid off. A headline that says "the 3 systems behind every $10K/month fitness coach" owes the reader exactly three systems, clearly delivered. A problem section that names "the hidden cost nobody talks about" owes the reader that cost, named and quantified. A proof tease planted early ("the 47-company dataset changed everything") owes the reader the dataset's story. Undelivered promises are the fastest way to erode trust on a page that otherwise does everything right — the reader who was promised something and didn't receive it discounts every other claim retroactively. This is not a creative choice; it's a structural audit. List every setup. Check every payoff. Close every debt.

### Step 10: Build the Page

**Identify the page's plot shape (Booker).** Before defining the spine, name the primary narrative shape:

- **Monster-slaying:** The page has a clear villain and a clear victory. Front-load the problem section — the villain must feel genuinely threatening before the mechanism arrives. Best for competitive positioning, industry disruption, "us vs. the old way."
- **Transformation (rags-to-riches):** The page shows a before/after arc with a central crisis. The crisis — the hard part, the moment where most people quit — must be present. Without it, the success feels manufactured. Best for coaching, courses, programs, high-ticket services.
- **Tragedy (cost-of-inaction):** The page shows what happens if the prospect does nothing. The trajectory is a narrative of rational decisions leading to irrational outcomes. Best for urgency-driven pages, risk-reversal, enterprise sales where the status quo feels safe.

The plot shape determines which sections carry the most weight. A monster-slaying page front-loads the problem. A transformation page front-loads the before state. A tragedy-framed page front-loads the declining trajectory. Choose the shape, then apply the structure principles within it.

**Transitions between sections are arguments (Katz).** The transition from problem to mechanism, from mechanism to proof, from proof to offer — each carries its own argumentative logic, and treating them as default whitespace breaks wastes their persuasive potential. A hard transition (whitespace break, new visual treatment, stark color shift) between problem and mechanism says "different world — the answer lives over here." A match transition (the last image of the problem section rhyming visually with the first image of the mechanism section — same person, different expression; same dashboard, different numbers) says "same situation, new lens." A softening transition (gradient, overlapping section boundary, shared visual element) between proof and offer says "this flows naturally from what you just saw." The strongest section transitions use visual or conceptual rhyme to make the connection between sections feel inevitable rather than constructed. A problem section ending with an image of someone struggling, followed by a mechanism section opening with the same person in motion, makes the mechanism feel like the organic next step rather than a topic change.

**Pain/possibility intercutting (Katz).** Instead of presenting all pain first and then all possibility (which resolves the problem section's tension before the mechanism arrives), intercut pain and possibility throughout the middle of the page with accelerating frequency. A pain paragraph, then a possibility paragraph, then a pain paragraph — the reader carries both states simultaneously and the unresolved contrast drives scrolling. The intercutting should accelerate: early in the page, longer pain and possibility sections (2-3 paragraphs each). By mid-page, shorter alternations (one paragraph each). Near the offer, single-sentence contrasts. The convergence point — where pain and possibility collapse into a single decision — is the CTA. A page that resolves all pain before presenting possibility gives the reader a resting point where they can leave satisfied but unconvinced. A page that intercuts keeps the tension alive until the offer resolves it.

**Genre-specific beat requirements for page subtypes (Truby — Anatomy of Genres).** Beyond the general psychological sequence, specific page subtypes carry required beats that audiences expect. Case study pages require: (1) relatable protagonist, (2) clear villain/problem, (3) failed attempts, (4) turning point (product entry), (5) measurable result, (6) emotional payoff. Origin story pages require: (1) founder in same position as reader, (2) frustration/failure, (3) epiphany moment, (4) action taken, (5) proof it worked, (6) mission statement. Launch pages require: (1) problem worth solving, (2) failed solutions, (3) insight that unlocked the build, (4) the product, (5) early proof, (6) invitation. These beats layer on top of the existing psychological sequence — skip a required beat and the page genre breaks independent of overall structure quality.

**Value Equation diagnostic (Hormozi).** Before writing copy, map which page section addresses which value variable. Hero = Dream Outcome (what changes). Proof section = Perceived Likelihood (why it's believable). How-it-works = Time Delay (how fast) + Effort & Sacrifice (how easy). If any variable is missing from the page, there's a hole the reader will feel but can't name. Most pages over-index on Dream Outcome and under-index on the denominator — they describe the result without addressing how fast or how easy. A page that addresses all four variables converts better than one that repeats the outcome in different words across every section.

**Hero-section-first workflow (Cannell & Travis).** Before writing body copy, design the above-the-fold treatment: headline, hero image/visual, and subheadline. If the hero can't compel on its own — if it doesn't pass the 5-second satisficing trigger and the snap judgment — the angle isn't sharp enough and no amount of body copy rescues it. Treat the hero as the gate: write it first, pressure-test it, and only proceed to the full page when the above-the-fold treatment works in isolation.

**The hero is a separate discipline (Kane).** The above-the-fold moment is a distinct creative problem — design it, test it, and evaluate it independently from the body argument. The hero competes not against the rest of your page but against every other tab, notification, and distraction fighting for the prospect's next 3 seconds. A hero that works in the context of a full page scroll but fails in isolation — when the prospect has 20 tabs open, a Slack ping, and a meeting in 4 minutes — is a hero that fails in the real environment. Build the hero as its own artifact: does this headline + visual + subheadline win the next 3 seconds against everything else on the screen? Test it by showing only the hero to someone with no context. If they don't lean in, the body copy never gets read. The body argument and the hero are two separate creative problems that happen to live on the same URL.

**The hero is the master shot (Katz).** The hero section establishes the full geography of the page — who this is for, what transformation is available, what emotional space the reader is entering — before the page fragments into problem, mechanism, and proof sections. A hero that skips orientation (jumping straight to a narrow claim, a feature, or a CTA without context) is a close-up before the master: floating detail that means nothing because the reader has no spatial contract with the page. The master shot doesn't need to be comprehensive — it needs to orient. The reader who finishes the hero should know the territory they're exploring, even if they don't yet know the details.

**The page must pass the peripheral vision test (Barden).** Most visitors scroll past at low resolution — peripheral vision that detects color, shape, and contrast but cannot read text. Blur the hero to the point where no text is legible. What remains? If what remains does not signal the category, the brand, and the implicit goal, the autopilot will scroll past before the headline is read. High contrast against surrounding content, distinctive brand signals, and visual hooks that work without reading are the first conversion layer — before copy, before structure, before the argument.

Define the page spine — one sentence capturing the irreducible intention (see Principle 2). Then apply the core principles below in order, following the type-specific blueprint from `page-types.md`.

---


**Power copy uses specificity and commits (Cabane: The Charisma Myth).** Power in written copy comes from specific numbers, declarative statements, and zero hedging. "Cuts your workflow from 10 hours to 10 minutes" is a power cue through specificity. "Might help you improve efficiency" is weak through vagueness and hedging. Headlines and CTAs should transfer certainty, not doubt. "Get 237% more leads in 90 days" commits to a specific outcome. "Improve your lead generation" is generic and non-committal. The power/weakness distinction isn't about aggressive vs. soft language — it's about specific vs. vague, declarative vs. hedged. "This will save you 10 hours per week" transfers power. "This could potentially save you up to 10 hours depending on your workflow" transfers doubt. Every qualifier dilutes power: "might," "potentially," "could," "up to," "in some cases." Cut them unless intellectual honesty specifically requires them. Power cues in sales page copy: specific numbers over ranges, declarative sentences over conditional phrasing, confident framing over hedged claims, named outcomes over category improvements. Test every headline and CTA: does this transfer certainty or doubt? If it hedges, rewrite it to commit.

## Core Sales Page Principles

**Internal state audit before writing (Cabane: The Charisma Myth).** Before writing any sales page section, clarify the internal posture: are you helping the reader see an opportunity they're missing, or trying to extract a transaction? The internal belief shapes every sentence even when the structure is identical. Copy written from "how do I get them to buy?" reads differently than copy written from "how do I help them see what I see?" The difference leaks through word choice, which objections get addressed, and where emphasis lands. A page written by someone genuinely excited about the transformation reads differently than one written by someone executing a formula. The reader detects the difference subconsciously before they can name it — one feels like discovery, the other feels like persuasion. Before drafting, write one sentence: "I'm writing this page because __________." If the answer is "to hit a revenue goal" or "because we need a landing page," stop — that internal state will leak into every paragraph. The answer should be "because this solves a problem I've seen destroy businesses, and most people don't know this solution exists." Write from that belief and the page reads like someone sharing a discovery. Write from the need to convert and the page reads like someone needing something from the reader. The latter creates resistance. The former creates curiosity.


**Every section has an intention beyond its content (Weston).** A section labeled "Problem" has a topic. But what is the section's intention — what does it DO to the reader? The hero section's intention isn't "introduce the product" — it's "make them feel seen so completely they can't leave." The problem section's intention isn't "describe the pain" — it's "make inaction feel unbearable." The testimonial section's intention isn't "provide social proof" — it's "eliminate their last excuse." The offer section's intention isn't "list what's included" — it's "make NOT buying feel like the expensive choice." Name the intention first, then write content to serve it. The same proof section written with the intention "demonstrate credibility" produces different copy than one written with the intention "make skepticism feel naive." When the intention is clear, the content writes itself. When the intention is unclear, the section becomes information without direction — a feature list, a block of testimonials, a pricing table — structurally present but emotionally inert.

### 1. Awareness-Level Opening

*Sources: Schwartz (Breakthrough Advertising), Masterson/Forde (Great Leads), Seger (Writing Subtext — contextual awareness), Suber (The Power of Film — goal/desire split), Pearson (The Hero Within — archetype-stage sequencing)*

The first thing on the page must match where the prospect is mentally. Don't pitch a product to someone who doesn't know they have a problem. Don't explain the problem to someone who already knows they want your product.

**System 1 first, System 2 second (Kahneman).** The hero section is System 1's territory — emotion, recognition, curiosity. No features, no specs, no logic. System 1 decides in milliseconds whether to keep reading. The proof section, value stack, and comparisons serve System 2, which rationalizes the decision System 1 already made. Writing for System 2 first — leading with features, data, or logical arguments above the fold — misses the decision that's already happened. The page's architecture is a System 1 decision handed to System 2 for justification: hero and problem sections fire emotion; mechanism, proof, and stack provide the rational alibi.

**Goal in the headline, desire in the body.** The headline addresses the prospect's stated goal — the measurable, logical thing they think they need, the thing they'd type into Google. "How to get 50 leads per week." "Cut onboarding time by 60%." The body copy — especially the problem section, mechanism, and transformation promise — addresses the desire: the emotional, identity-based need underneath. "Stop wondering if you're cut out for this." "Become the founder who sleeps through the night." The goal gets them on the page. The desire gets them to buy. A page that only addresses goals feels transactional — useful but forgettable. A page that only addresses desires feels vague — inspiring but unconvincing. Both must be present: goal as the handle they grab, desire as the reason they hold on.

| Awareness Level | What they know | Opening type | Example |
|-----------------|---------------|-------------|---------|
| Most Aware | Know the product, need the deal | Offer lead — price, guarantee, CTA | "Get [product] for $X — [guarantee]. [CTA]" |
| Product-Aware | Know you, not yet convinced | Promise lead — big result + mechanism | "[Specific result] using [mechanism]. Here's how." |
| Solution-Aware | Know solutions exist, not yours | Secret/Proclamation — new mechanism | "There's a faster way to [result] and nobody's talking about it." |
| Problem-Aware | Feel the pain, don't know fix | Problem-Solution — agitate then solve | "You've tried [approaches]. They didn't work because [reason]." |
| Unaware | No felt pain yet | Story/Indirect — enter their world | Story, identification, or pattern interrupt |

Market sophistication adjustment: In a Stage 1 market, a direct claim works ("Lose 20 pounds in 30 days"). By Stage 3, introduce a mechanism ("Lose 20 pounds through metabolic cycling"). By Stage 5, sell identity and experience ("Join the 1% who've cracked the code"). Match both awareness AND sophistication — an aware audience in a sophisticated market needs a mechanism-led promise, not a direct claim.

**Archetype-aware sequencing.** Awareness level tells you WHAT they know; archetype stage tells you WHO they are psychologically. An Innocent-stage buyer needs safety emphasis and protective framing ("this approach shields you from..."). A Warrior-stage buyer needs achievement-oriented CTAs and competitive framing ("outperform," "dominate," clear benchmarks). A Magician-stage buyer needs transformation language and paradigm-shifting promises ("see everything differently"). Layer archetype awareness on top of awareness-level matching — a Problem-Aware Warrior needs different opening energy than a Problem-Aware Innocent.

**Traffic source is cultural context.** The same headline reads differently depending on where the visitor just came from. Cold ad traffic needs credibility before anything else — they don't know you and their guard is up. Warm referral traffic already has credibility transferred and needs specifics. A newsletter subscriber arrives mid-conversation; a Google searcher arrives mid-research. Design the opening for the context the visitor carries with them, not just the message you want to deliver.

**Market category must be established above the fold (Dunford).** The first screen must answer "what is this?" in category terms that trigger correct assumptions. If positioned as a "creator CRM" but the hero uses "project management" language (tasks, milestones, workflows), the prospect spends the entire page correcting their mental model instead of evaluating value. Category mismatch creates cognitive friction at every section — the reader is fighting terminology instead of absorbing benefits. The hero must name or strongly imply the correct category frame: "This is a [category], not [adjacent category]" when clarification prevents confusion. The prospect who misreads the category in the first 5 seconds will interpret every subsequent feature through the wrong lens. If you're selling an async-first team tool but the hero says "communication platform," the reader expects real-time chat and judges every feature against Slack. State the category explicitly or design the hero visual/copy to make the category unmistakable. Category clarity isn't a nice-to-have — it's the foundation that makes all differentiation legible.

**The headline opens a gap; each section partially closes it while opening new ones (Heath: gap theory).** The headline's job is not to summarize — it's to create a curiosity gap that can only be closed by reading the page. Each subsequent section delivers partial resolution while opening a new gap: the problem section answers "what's wrong?" but opens "why does this keep happening?"; the mechanism answers that but opens "does it actually work?" The reader who has all gaps closed stops reading. The reader carrying an unresolved question scrolls forward. The strongest technique is partially satisfying curiosity while opening a bigger gap — the gap between what the reader knows and what they want to know is what creates forward pull, and a section that resolves one question while revealing a deeper one is more effective than leaving everything unresolved (Anderson).

**Headlines should describe the change, not the product.** The prospect doesn't care what the product is — they care what changes. "Go from guessing your forecast to knowing it" (change) vs. "The best pipeline management tool" (product). The change headline creates instant gap awareness because it names two states — where they are now and where they'll be — and the reader feels the distance between them. A product headline forces the reader to imagine the change themselves; a change headline does it for them in the first five seconds. Test every headline: does it name two states and a gap, or does it describe an object? If it describes an object, rewrite it as a transition.

**The headline should reframe, not argue within the existing frame (Heinrichs).** Most headlines accept the category's terms and try to win within them — "better analytics," "faster onboarding," "more leads." A reframing headline changes what the conversation is about: "The problem isn't your pipeline. It's what happens after the first call." The prospect who reads a reframe stops evaluating alternatives and starts reconsidering the question itself — which makes your mechanism the only answer to the new question.

**USP-first headline test (Abraham).** After drafting the hero headline, ask: could a competitor honestly make the same claim? If yes, the headline is category-level, not USP-level — rewrite it. "We help agencies grow" passes the competitor test for every agency consultant alive. "The only pipeline system that guarantees 10 qualified leads in 30 days or you don't pay" fails it — because either the guarantee, the specificity, or the mechanism is unique. The headline should make the reader think "I haven't seen this before," not "I've seen this everywhere." If the claim is true of the category, it's a category description, not a unique selling proposition. The fix is usually specificity: name the mechanism, name the result, name the constraint that only you can back up.

**The headline must contain news (Ogilvy).** A headline that states a known fact — "the best mentorship for coaches" — gives the reader nothing they didn't already have. A headline that delivers news — "the fitness coaches making $10K/month all have the same 3 systems" — gives them a reason to keep reading. A new finding, a new result, a new approach, a new connection between things they hadn't linked before. If the headline contains no news, it fails regardless of how well-written it is. Test every headline: does this tell the reader something they didn't know five seconds ago? If the answer is no, the headline is a label, not a hook. "We help agencies grow" is a category description. "Agencies that stopped posting daily are outgrowing the ones that didn't" is news. The news doesn't have to be shocking — it has to be new to the reader.

**The hero is a satisficing trigger (Sutherland).** The prospect is not comparing — they are scanning for "good enough to keep reading." Five seconds to satisfy "this could work for me." If the prospect satisfices YES above the fold, everything after is justification for a decision already forming. If they haven't satisficed by the fold, they bounce — not because the page is bad, but because nothing triggered "stop searching." This means the hero's job is not to be comprehensive or clever. It is to be immediately, obviously relevant: one clear signal that this is for them, this addresses their situation, and this is worth five more seconds. Front-load the satisficing trigger — don't bury it under context, brand story, or clever wordplay that requires a second read.

**The first screen triggers a snap judgment (Godin).** By the time they scroll, the emotional verdict is already made — a near-permanent assumption about whether this page is for them, whether the offer is credible, whether the brand is trustworthy. The hero section is not the beginning of a logical case; it is the entire emotional trial, decided in seconds. Everything below the fold is confirmation or reversal, and reversal almost never happens. Design the hero for the snap judgment: the right visual gist, the right enrollment language, the right identity signal. A hero optimized for comprehensive information loses the snap judgment to a hero optimized for instant emotional recognition.

**New-opportunity framing in the hero (Brunson).** Is the hero framing the product as a new vehicle or an improvement on the old way? "Find creators faster" is improvement — it accepts the existing approach and promises to do it better. "Replace the scroll-and-spreadsheet method entirely" is a new opportunity — it rejects the old vehicle and offers a different one. New opportunities convert higher because the prospect has no failed experience with this vehicle; improvement offers inherit every past disappointment with the category. Default to new-opportunity framing unless the audience is already committed to the current vehicle and needs a better version of it.

**Lived-experience specificity in the hero (Carnegie).** The hero copy should describe the reader's actual daily experience with enough specificity to trigger recognition — not "struggling with lead generation" but "you're spending $3,000 a month on ads and checking your phone every morning hoping today's the day something changes." Abstract problem categories ("inefficient workflows," "scaling challenges") slide off. Specific lived moments ("you opened Slack this morning to three messages that all said 'any update on that lead?'") stick because the reader feels caught. The test: would the reader screenshot this and send it to a colleague saying "this is literally me"? If not, the hero is naming a category, not entering a conversation.

**Enrollment language in the hero (Godin).** The hero section should make the reader feel seen, not sold. Enrollment copy mirrors the reader's existing desire back to them — it does not manufacture new desire. "If you've been building in silence and wondering when the results catch up to the effort — this is for you" enrolls. "Discover the #1 tool for explosive growth" sells. "You already know the old playbook is broken" enrolls. "Let us show you a better way" sells. The test: would the ideal reader think "they understand me" or "they want my money"? Enrollment language uses recognition ("you've been thinking about this"), identity ("built for founders who ship before they're ready"), and alignment ("you already know — here's the proof") — never manufactured urgency or superlative claims. The reader who feels seen in the hero stays. The reader who feels pitched bounces.

**Customer-as-hero test for the hero section (Miller).** The hero section should make the customer the protagonist, not the product. "Triumfit revolutionizes fitness tracking" makes the product the hero. "You did pushups in your kitchen and have no way to log it" makes the customer the hero. The reader is the hero of this page; the product is the mentor (Duarte). Every section answers "how does this help THEM?" — if you removed your company name and logo, the page should still work because it's about their transformation, not your features. Read the hero headline and subheadline and ask: who is the sentence about? If the subject is the product, the company, or the founder, rewrite it with the customer as the subject. This compounds with enrollment language — a hero that names the customer's situation ("You're running outreach from a spreadsheet and it's killing your pipeline") enrolls and positions the customer as protagonist simultaneously. A hero that names the product's capabilities ("Our AI-powered platform automates outreach at scale") does neither.

**Identity-Based Social Proof** (Bernays). Structure testimonials as identity markers: '427 CFOs,' not '427 customers.' The herd instinct (Bernays: Group Mind) triggers when the prospect sees people like them — specific titles, industries, company sizes. 'Join 10,000 users' creates no pull. 'Join 2,400 B2B SaaS founders scaling past $1M ARR' creates tribal belonging.

**Identity Positioning Over Features** (Bernays). The hero should sell identity, not benefits. 'You're the founder who ships before perfect' is identity framing. 'Our tool helps you launch faster' is feature framing (Bernays: people align with groups). Sell who they become, not what they get. Identity converts because it taps the desire to belong to a recognized group.

**Repetition Creates Belief** (Bernays). Repeat the core message across sections in different forms (Bernays: repetition is reality). If the hero says 'The old playbook is broken,' the problem section shows why the old playbook failed, the mechanism section introduces the new playbook, proof shows the new playbook working, and the CTA says 'Join the founders using the new playbook.' Same idea, five different frames. Consistency across the page becomes accepted truth.

**The hero names the false belief first (Albuquerque).** The hero section begins dismantling the blocking belief before the product enters. Leading with the product assumes the reader already believes the approach works — but if they held that belief, they'd already be buying. The hero names what the reader currently holds as true ("You've been told the only way to fill pipeline is outbound at scale"), begins to crack it ("But the agencies actually scaling aren't doing more outreach — they're doing less"), and only then introduces the mechanism or product as the reason the old belief is wrong. Product-first heroes sell to the already-convinced. Belief-first heroes create the convinced. This compounds with enrollment language — a hero that names the reader's false belief feels like understanding, not pitching, because it describes their world before asking them to change it.

**Demonstrated listening earns the right to pitch (Weston).** The best sales pages spend 30-50% of their length listening — articulating the reader's situation so accurately they think "they get it" — before speaking. This is not just empathy framing or enrollment language. It's actual demonstrated listening: naming their specific situation, their failed attempts, their private frustrations, their unspoken fears. A page that leads with the brand's story, features, or credentials is performing a monologue without reading the room. A page that spends the first third proving it understands — through specificity that could only come from real attention — earns the right to pitch. The reader who feels heard drops their guard. The reader who feels pitched raises it. Listening isn't a section; it's a ratio. Audit the page: how much of the word count is about THEM versus about YOU? If the balance tips toward you before they've felt understood, the page is speaking without having listened.

**Headline category assignment (Garfinkel).** Choose a dominant headline category for the hero: self-interest (what changes for them), curiosity (incomplete information that demands resolution), news (a new finding or result), or quick/easy (speed and simplicity of the path). Sales pages default to self-interest — the hero headline should name the transformation the reader gets. Layer a secondary category based on traffic: curiosity for cold traffic (they don't know enough to care about self-interest yet), news for launch pages (the product itself is the news). A hero headline that doesn't fit any category is usually a label ("Welcome to Our Platform") or a feature description ("AI-Powered Analytics") — neither of which does the headline's job.

**Situational disability (Horton & Quesenbery).** The highest-intent traffic often arrives in the worst conditions. The visitor clicking a paid ad is doing it while commuting, one-handed, on a phone in bright sunlight with split attention. The prospect scanning a forwarded link is reading between meetings on a laptop with three other tabs competing. Design the page for degraded conditions — large touch targets on CTAs, high-contrast text, scannable heading hierarchy, keyboard-navigable forms — not for the focused desktop user who doesn't reliably exist. A landing page that only converts when the visitor is sitting at a desk, fully focused, on a large screen is a page that fails for most of its actual traffic.

### 2. The Psychological Sequence

*Sources: Collier (Robert Collier Letter Book), Garfinkel (Breakthrough Copywriting), Clemens (Persuasion Story Code), Edwards (How to Write Copy That Sells), Tharp (The Creative Habit), Seger (You Talkin' to Me? — rhythm mapping; Writing Subtext — subtext-to-direct arc), Booker (The Seven Basic Plots — plot selection, tragedy structure, beat expectations), Howard & Mabley (The Tools of Screenwriting — declining-options urgency)*

A sales page follows an emotional journey, not a logical outline. The sequence:

**Enter their conversation → Crystallize the problem → Name the villain → Introduce the mechanism → Prove it works → Present the offer → Reverse the risk → Create urgency → Close with two futures.**

**Tension-curve principle (O'Bannon).** Each section must shift the reader's tension state — raise it, redirect it, or release it. A section that begins and ends at the same emotional register is structural dead weight. The problem section raises tension. The mechanism redirects it from "this is broken" to "here's why." Proof releases it partially. The offer raises new tension (desire + price anxiety). The guarantee releases it. Map each section's tension movement before writing — if you can't name whether a section raises, redirects, or releases, the section has no emotional function.

**Tragedy structure for cost-of-inaction (Booker).** When the page needs urgency, don't just list consequences — show the trajectory as a narrative of rational decisions leading to irrational outcomes. "You know onboarding is leaky. Revenue is up so you deprioritize it. Churn creeps up 2% per quarter. You blame market conditions. By Q3 new revenue can't outrun churn." The prospect sees themselves mid-pattern and recognizes the decline before it's named. This is more specific than painting two futures at the close — the tragedy structure works in the problem/agitation section to show how the prospect's current *reasonable* behavior leads to *unreasonable* outcomes. Each step in the trajectory must feel like a decision the reader has already made or is about to make.

**Cost of inaction as a named section.** This is not just agitation repackaged — it's a distinct section with a distinct job. The problem section makes the reader feel the pain. The cost-of-inaction section makes the reader feel the *accumulating* pain of doing nothing. Its mechanical purpose is to lower the action threshold (Belfort): every prospect has a threshold of certainty they need before acting, and amplifying the cost of staying put lowers how much certainty is required. A prospect who sees inaction costing them $5K/month needs less convincing than one who sees it as a mild inconvenience. Place this section after the mechanism and proof — the reader already believes the solution works, now show them what waiting costs. Quantify where possible: dollars lost, hours wasted, opportunities missed per week/month/quarter.

**Declining-options urgency (Howard & Mabley).** Beyond showing what they lose by waiting, show how the *fix itself* gets harder. "Fixing onboarding now takes a weekend of configuration. In six months, after 2,000 more users have churned through a broken flow, the fix requires a full rebuild, a re-engagement campaign, and reputation repair." The clock doesn't just cost money — it narrows their options. This is distinct from Booker's tragedy (which shows the decline as narrative) and standard cost framing (which quantifies the loss) — declining-options urgency shows that delay degrades the *solution*, not just the situation. The same fix that's simple today becomes compound tomorrow. Use this in the cost-of-inaction section and again in the closing urgency: the prospect isn't just losing time, they're losing the ability to solve the problem easily.

**Seduction sequence as diagnostic overlay (Anderson).** If a section isn't converting, diagnose which phase it belongs to: Attraction (hero — does it create desire to keep reading?), Engagement (problem/mechanism — does it shift understanding?), Persuasion (proof/objection handling — does it overcome resistance?), Commitment (offer/CTA — does it make action feel inevitable?). Each phase has different triggers. A proof section that tries to Attract (flashy claims instead of evidence) is using the wrong phase's tools. A hero that tries to Persuade (handling objections before desire exists) is premature. Match the section's job to its phase, then check whether the copy uses that phase's triggers.

This maps to deep structure (Garfinkel): Problem → Agitation → Mechanism → Proof → Offer → Close. Each section earns the right to the next. Skip one and the page collapses — the reader hasn't been brought far enough emotionally to accept what comes next.

PASTOR (Edwards) as a mnemonic: Problem → Amplify → Story/Solution → Testimony → Offer → Response.

The order matters more than the labels. A page that jumps from problem to offer skips the mechanism — the reader doesn't believe the solution works. A page that jumps from mechanism to CTA skips proof — the reader doesn't believe the claims. Every section answers the question the previous section created.

**Alternative: Dunford's Sales Narrative Structure for Category-Education Pages.** When selling into an established category where buyers need market context before evaluating products, use Dunford's 8-section structure instead of the standard psychological sequence. This structure front-loads education and positioning before introducing the product — essential when competitive alternatives and market category are unclear to the prospect.

**Dunford's 8-Section Sales Narrative:**
1. **Insight** — Open with a market truth the prospect doesn't fully recognize yet. "The teams closing the most deals aren't doing more outreach — they're doing less, but with better timing." Not a problem statement, but a pattern or insight that reframes the conversation.
2. **Alternatives** — Name what buyers currently do to solve this problem: status quo (manual processes), adjacent categories (spreadsheets, hiring someone), direct competitors. This is NOT a competitor comparison — it's naming the real alternatives the buyer is considering if your product didn't exist. "Most teams use a mix of: a spreadsheet to track leads, Slack for handoffs, and a weekly meeting to review pipeline." Position against what they'd actually do, not phantom competitors.
3. **Perfect World** — Describe the ideal solution without naming your product. "In a perfect world, you'd have one system where every lead interaction is logged automatically, handoffs happen without Slack threads, and pipeline reviews take 5 minutes instead of 90." This is the buyer's wishlist — what they want but don't believe exists yet.
4. **Product Introduction** — Now introduce your product as the thing that delivers the perfect world. "That's [Product]. It's a [category] that [unique mechanism]." Category frame first (so they know what bucket to put you in), then differentiation.
5. **Differentiated Value** — Show what you do that alternatives can't. Not features, not benefits, but value only you deliver. "Unlike spreadsheets, it updates in real time. Unlike CRMs, it requires zero manual entry. Unlike hiring a coordinator, it doesn't forget." This is the three-layer Dunford filter in action: attribute → value → differentiated value. Show the gap between what competitors deliver and what you deliver.
6. **Proof** — Evidence that the differentiated value is real. Case studies, metrics, testimonials focused on the unique capabilities, not generic results. "47 teams went from 10 hours/week on pipeline management to under 1 hour in their first month."
7. **Objections** — Handle the top 2-3 objections directly. "Does this work if we're still using [legacy tool]?" "What if our process is custom?" Address blockers before they derail the decision.
8. **Ask** — The CTA. Frame it as the logical conclusion of everything above. "If you're ready to stop duct-taping your pipeline process together, [CTA]."

**When to use this structure instead of the standard psychological sequence:** Use Dunford's structure when (a) selling into an established category with unclear differentiation, (b) the prospect needs education about what's possible before they can evaluate your solution, or (c) your positioning depends on competitive alternatives clarity. Use the standard psychological sequence when the prospect already understands the category and you're optimizing for emotional conversion. Dunford's structure is setup-phase positioning; the psychological sequence is closing-phase conversion. Both can coexist — use Dunford's structure for the first half of the page (education and positioning), then transition to proof/offer/close using the psychological sequence for the second half.

**Perceptual afterimage for section sequencing (Albers).** Every section leaves an emotional residue that colors the next. The problem section creates a pain afterimage — if the mechanism section follows immediately, it's perceived through that lens and feels more urgent. If a long authority section or digression intervenes, the afterimage fades and the mechanism loses emotional contrast. Keep high-contrast section pairs adjacent: problem → mechanism, mechanism → proof, proof → offer. The anti-pattern is separating emotionally paired sections with unrelated content that dilutes the residue.

**Persuasion lives in the transition (Mamet).** The cut from problem agitation to mechanism is where the reader's mind shifts — not in either section alone, but in the juxtaposition between them. The last line of the problem section and the first line of the mechanism section form a single persuasive unit: "You've been doing everything right and it's still not working" followed by "Because the system was never designed for the way you sell" creates a belief change that neither sentence achieves alone. Test every section transition by asking: does the reader believe something different after crossing this boundary? Problem → mechanism should shift from "this is broken" to "now I see why." Mechanism → proof should shift from "that makes sense" to "it actually works." Proof → offer should shift from "I believe this" to "I want this." If a transition doesn't produce a belief change, the sections are stacked, not sequenced — information in order, not an argument in motion. The transitions are the persuasion. The sections are just the setup. **Narrative drive through unanswered questions (O'Bannon).** Each section must leave one question pulling the reader forward. Problem answers "what's wrong?" but raises "why does this keep happening?" Mechanism answers "why?" but raises "does it actually work?" Proof answers "does it work?" but raises "what do I get?" Full resolution before the CTA kills drive — a reader with no open question has no reason to scroll. Audit each section ending: what question is the reader carrying into the next section? If the answer is "none," the transition is a stopping point, not a bridge.

**Section-level color shift (Bellantoni).** Use subtle color shifts between page sections to signal emotional transitions. Problem section in cooler, lower-saturation tones — the reader feels the weight. Solution/mechanism section warming up — the reader feels possibility opening. Proof section in neutral high-contrast — evidence reads clean. Offer/CTA section in the most saturated, confident palette — the reader feels "this is the moment." The scroll itself becomes an emotional journey the reader feels in their body — cooler, heavier tones giving way to warmer, more energized tones mirrors the narrative arc from problem to resolution.

**The subtext-to-direct arc.** The page moves from implicit to explicit in a deliberate progression. Problem sections *show* — subtextual, letting the reader feel the pain through scenes and contradictions. Mechanism sections *explain* — transitional, bridging from felt experience to understood solution. Offer sections *state* — direct, listing exactly what's included and what it costs. CTAs *command* — fully direct, telling the reader exactly what to do. Reversing this arc (opening with commands, closing with subtext) breaks the emotional logic of the page.

**Rhythm mapping:** The psychological sequence isn't just an order — it's a rhythm. Short, intense problem section. Longer, building mechanism. Rapid-fire proof. Expansive, cinematic two-futures close. The page sparklines — oscillating between "what is" (current pain) and "what could be" (future after acting) (Duarte). Don't dump all problems then all solutions. Each section creates a gap, then narrows it, with the oscillation accelerating toward the CTA. Alternate intensity and release throughout: three dense proof paragraphs followed by a single question and white space; a hard-hitting agitation section followed by a calm, confident mechanism opening. If every section hits at the same intensity, the reader goes numb. Pacing is emotional control. **Alternate agitation and release (O'Bannon).** Tension sections (problem, villain, cost-of-inaction) must alternate with release sections (proof, mechanism, guarantee). Pain-proof-pain-proof-CTA is breathing. Pain-pain-pain-CTA is hyperventilation — the reader shuts down before the offer arrives. Two agitation sections back-to-back without a proof or mechanism break between them exhausts instead of persuading. Attic sections (aspiration, transformation, future state) expand and open; write them in longer, unfolding sentences. Cellar sections (problem, villain, cost-of-inaction) compress and descend; write them short, specific, and closing. The sparkline isn't symmetric -- the downward move activates different psychology than the upward move (Bachelard).

**Tempo control across sections.** Alternate between dense, data-heavy sections (stats, case studies, feature breakdowns — slow, absorptive reading) and short, punchy sections (single-line declarations, brief testimonials, direct challenges — fast, urgent reading). A mechanism section with three paragraphs of detailed explanation followed by a two-line proof hit. A long proof stack followed by a single-sentence transition into the offer. Don't let the page settle into one rhythm — a page that's all dense reads like a report; a page that's all punchy reads like a listicle. The shifts in tempo are what create the sensation of momentum. Map the tempo before writing: mark each section as "slow/absorb" or "fast/push" and make sure they alternate.

**Internal acceleration within sections (Mackendrick).** Each section should accelerate toward its own climax — sentences get shorter, stakes rise, language becomes more direct as the section reaches its peak. The problem section opens with scene-setting and closes with a gut punch. The proof section opens with context and closes with the hardest number. The offer section opens with framing and closes with the price reveal. This internal acceleration creates a sawtooth rhythm across the page: each section ramps up, peaks, then the next section resets at a slightly higher baseline and ramps again. Each peak is higher than the last, with the CTA section at maximum tempo. The anti-pattern is decelerating toward the close — longer sentences, softer language, qualifying hedges near the button. Deceleration gives the reader time to generate objections. The final section should be the fastest, most direct writing on the page.

**Stillness as a pacing tool (Caine).** A single sentence surrounded by white space is the page's close-up — it reads as confidence and gives the reader a beat to feel the claim before the next section moves forward. After the mechanism reveal, after the key proof stat, after the line that reframes everything — stop. One sentence. No follow-up qualifier. No intensifier. No "And that means..." The line sits alone because it doesn't need support. The actor who delivers the important line and then holds still — no gesture, no expression change — lets the audience feel the weight of what was just said. The copywriter who drops the key sentence into its own visual space, surrounded by nothing, achieves the same effect. The reader's eye lands on it with nowhere else to go. It registers at a different weight than the sentences packed into paragraphs above and below. Use this once or twice per page at the structural hinges — the mechanism reveal, the transformation promise, the price reframe. Overuse turns stillness into a formatting pattern the reader stops noticing. The anti-pattern is a page where every important claim is buried inside a paragraph, reading at the same speed and the same weight as the supporting sentences around it. If the page's most important line gets the same visual treatment as setup copy, the reader processes both at the same level of attention.

**The page spine:** Before writing any section, define the page's single irreducible intention in one sentence — not "sell the product" but the specific emotional or logical shift the page exists to create (e.g., "make the reader feel that their current approach costs more than they realize"). Every section either serves this spine or gets cut, regardless of quality. A page with ten strong sections and no spine reads like a patchwork; a page with seven sections all serving one intention reads like an argument. **The spine is the architectural test (Lumet).** State the page's argument in one sentence before writing any section. Then test every element against it — a testimonial, feature, story, or design choice that doesn't serve the spine is noise, no matter how compelling it is in isolation. A case study that's genuinely impressive but argues a different point than the page's spine weakens the page by splitting the argument. A section that's beautifully written but serves a tangential purpose dilutes the page the way a subplot dilutes a film with a clear through-line. The spine is not the offer and not the headline — it's the underlying argument that every section must advance. If you can't state it in one sentence, the page doesn't have one yet, and writing without it produces a collection of sections, not a case.

**Structure over talent (Mamet).** If the page only converts with genius-level copy, the section architecture is broken. The sequence of sections — problem, mechanism, proof, offer, close — should build the argument even with competent copy, because the architecture itself does the persuading. A well-structured page with decent copy outperforms a poorly structured page with brilliant copy, because the reader's belief changes happen at the transitions between sections, not inside any individual paragraph. Test the structure by reading only the first sentence of each section in order: does the argument progress? Does each section logically earn the next? If not, rewriting copy within sections won't fix the problem — the architecture needs restructuring. This is the diagnostic that precedes every other revision: before improving headlines, proof, or CTAs, verify that the section sequence builds the argument on its own.

**Commander's Intent for the page (Heath).** Distill the spine further: write the one sentence that captures the page's core argument — the sentence the reader must take away if they skim the headline and nothing else. If a reader scrolls past everything and catches only the hero section, do they get the essential message? The Commander's Intent is the spine's public-facing version — the spine governs what you write; the Commander's Intent governs what the reader retains.

**Design one holy shit moment (Gallo).** Every sales page should have ONE designed peak — a visual, a stat, a testimonial, a demonstration — that is so striking it becomes the thing people mention when they share the page. This is the STAR moment (Duarte) — something the reader retells. A shocking statistic with context, an emotional story with a turning point, or a visual that carries the entire argument. If the page is uniformly "good" with no peak, it's forgettable. This is not the best proof point or the biggest number; it's the single moment engineered to stop the scroll and lodge in memory. Place it at the mechanism reveal or the proof section's climax. If you can't identify the holy shit moment before writing, the page doesn't have one — and a page without a peak moment is a page that's competent but unremarkable. The most persuasive element on a page is often its smallest: one number, one sentence, one customer phrase set apart with whitespace. Compression is the mechanism -- the reader's imagination does the expansion. Engineer this moment deliberately (Bachelard).

**The one-liner must repeat identically (Luntz).** Distill the spine into a single phrase — the core promise or mechanism name — and repeat it word-for-word in the hero headline, a mid-page callout, and the CTA section. Not paraphrased, not synonym-rotated for freshness. The same words, every time. Repetition of identical language builds belief; variation signals uncertainty. The reader who sees the same phrase three times across the page internalizes it as fact. The reader who sees three different phrasings of the same idea processes each one as a new claim to evaluate. **The phrase repeated 3-4 times across the page stops being a claim and becomes the reader's internal belief (Montell).** This is not just a consistency principle — it's a belief-formation mechanism. The first mention is a claim they evaluate. The second is familiar. By the third, it's something they "already know." Choose the signature phrase deliberately and protect its exact wording across every instance.

**Every section must serve conversion (Dooley).** After the spine is defined, audit each section with a harder question: does this section move the reader toward the CTA, or does it serve brand ego, SEO, internal stakeholders, or "we should probably include this"? A section can be well-written and on-spine but still be friction — if it consumes attention without advancing the decision, it competes with the sections that do. "About Us" blocks that satisfy the founder but bore the prospect. Feature grids that check a marketing checkbox but don't address the buyer's actual question. Trust badge rows that nobody reads but somebody insisted on. Sections that don't serve conversion are friction; they dilute momentum the same way an extra form field dilutes completion. Apply the same ruthlessness to sections that Dooley applies to steps in a checkout flow — if removing the section wouldn't reduce the reader's ability to say yes, the section is dead weight.

**Attention-scarcity audit (Godin).** Every section must earn its place. If a paragraph doesn't create tension, build proof, or move toward the CTA, cut it. Page length should be determined by how much the reader needs to see, not how much you want to say. The reader's attention is a finite, non-renewable resource for this visit — every sentence that doesn't serve the conversion is stealing from the sentences that do. Long pages win (Blanks-Jesson) when every section carries weight; long pages lose when length comes from the writer's desire to be thorough rather than the reader's need to be convinced. The question is never "how long should the page be?" but "how much genuinely interesting information do I have?" (Ogilvy). If you have three paragraphs of interesting content, write three paragraphs. If you have thirty, write thirty. But every line must earn its place — padding short copy to fill a template is worse than leaving it short, because padding dilutes the strong material and teaches the reader to skim. A tight page that ends early leaves the reader wanting more. A padded page that runs long leaves the reader trusting less.

### 3. The Slippery Slide

*Sources: Sugarman (Adweek Copywriting Handbook), Halbert (Boron Letters), Aristotle (Rhetoric), Seger (You Talkin' to Me? — compression as read-aloud test)*

Every element on the page — headline, subhead, first sentence, image, caption — has one job: compel the visitor to read the next element. No exit ramps.

- **First sentence under 8 words.** The sole purpose of the first sentence is to get you to read the second sentence.
- **Seeds of curiosity.** Plant unresolved loops between sections: "But that's not the real breakthrough..." or "What happened next changed everything."
- **Micro-commitments in copy (Fladlien).** Rhetorical questions that get the reader nodding: "If you've ever stared at a blank pipeline wondering where the next deal comes from, you already know..." Self-identification moments before the offer create agreement momentum. Vary the phrasing — don't repeat "right?" or "sound familiar?" Tag questions, reflective statements, and scenario identification all count. 10-15 across the full page. Deploy rhetorical questions that walk the reader through their own situation — "How many hours did you spend last week on X? What did that cost you?" — rather than declarative statements about their pain, because the reader who answers their own questions has convinced themselves (Camp).
- **Let the reader complete the argument (Carnegie).** Where possible, arrange evidence so the reader draws the conclusion themselves. A testimonial showing a specific before-and-after lets the reader conclude "this could work for me" — stating "this could work for you too" weakens the effect. Two facts about their situation followed by one implication — the reader supplies the final step. Stated conclusions invite resistance; self-generated ones create conviction. In the mechanism and proof sections specifically, present the evidence and let the gap do the selling: "Company A spent $50K on ads and got 200 clients who churned in 3 months. Company B spent $50K on a newsletter and got 200 clients who stayed for 2 years." The reader connects the dots without being told "that's why you need X." Stating the conclusion removes the reader's agency and ownership of the belief — and owned beliefs drive action while evaluated claims drive skepticism.
- **Simple language.** 8th-grade reading level. Short words. Short paragraphs. One idea per paragraph.
- **Compression.** If you can say it in 5 words, don't use 15. Cut every word that doesn't earn its place. Read every sentence aloud — if you run out of breath before the period, the sentence is too long. Sales page copy is dialogue on the page; if you'd never say it in conversation, it's corporate filler, not copy.
- **No reset points.** Avoid section breaks that feel like natural stopping points. The transition from one section to the next should feel like falling forward, not stepping across.
- **A-pile, not B-pile** (Halbert). Your page should feel personal and urgent — like a letter from a friend with important news — not like a corporate brochure that arrived with the junk mail.
- **Section lengths as tempo (Murch: rhythm and pacing).** Section lengths create tempo independent of content. A long, immersive problem section followed by a compressed, punchy mechanism section creates rhythmic contrast that keeps the reader in motion. Uniform section lengths create monotone pacing regardless of content quality. The variation in section density IS a reading rhythm instrument: dense sections (proof stacks, feature breakdowns) create tension through information load, spacious sections (a single bold claim, a testimonial pull-quote, breathing room) create release. The page should alternate tension and release as the reader scrolls, accelerating toward the CTA.

### 4. Pre-Suasion Architecture

*Sources: Cialdini (Pre-Suasion — privileged moments, associations, unity), Howard & Mabley (The Tools of Screenwriting — plant-twice technique)*

What comes before the pitch matters more than the pitch itself. Every element on the page is pre-suasion for the next element. The reader's psychological state one second before the ask determines the answer — not the ask itself.

- **The hero section sets the frame for the entire page.** An empathetic opening that names the problem creates a gratitude frame for the mechanism. A proof-led opening creates a credibility frame for the offer.
- **Layout is persuasion.** Social proof placed directly above the CTA is pre-suasion — the reader sees "2,400 teams use this" and then sees the button. Reverse the order and the proof loses its priming power.
- **Pricing page order changes anchoring.** Show the highest tier first to anchor high, then reveal the recommended tier as the reasonable choice. Or stack value components before the price to anchor against total value. **For tier/pricing tables, use the compromise effect (Poundstone):** three tiers where the middle tier is the target — most buyers avoid extremes and gravitate toward the center option. Design the lowest tier to feel incomplete and the highest to feel excessive so the middle reads as the rational choice. If you need to steer toward a specific tier, add a decoy — an inferior option at a similar price point that makes the target tier obviously better. The decoy doesn't need to sell; it needs to make the comparison easy. When presenting packages, architect one option as a deliberate decoy — priced comparably to the target but clearly worse on a key dimension — so the target reads as the obvious choice before the reader evaluates features (Ariely). The decoy works because buyers don't evaluate options in absolute terms; they evaluate them relative to whatever is easiest to compare. An option that is unambiguously worse than the target on a visible dimension makes the target look like the smart choice instantly, before rational feature-by-feature analysis begins. The decoy should lose on one dimension the reader can see at a glance — fewer features at nearly the same price, or the same features with a worse guarantee — so the comparison resolves in seconds.
- **Proportional ink in pricing layouts (Tufte).** The visual area and emphasis given to each pricing tier should be proportional to its actual value difference — not its marketing priority. A "Most Popular" badge is honest emphasis: a small, high-contrast signal that marks the recommended choice without distorting the comparison. A card that is physically 3x the size of adjacent tiers implies 3x the value, which is almost never true and which the reader registers as pressure, not guidance. Subtle elevation does the work: a slightly heavier border weight, one tier rendered in higher contrast, a single badge or callout that requires no extra real estate. The more ink a layout spends on a specific tier — size, color, animation, badges, expanded detail — the more it claims that tier is categorically superior, not just preferred. If the tiers are genuinely close in value, render them close in size and let the recommendation badge do the work. Reserve dramatic visual disproportion for tiers that are genuinely, dramatically different in what they deliver.
- **Engineer the privileged moment before every ask.** Each CTA has a psychological precondition — the state the reader must be in for "yes" to feel natural. Proof right before price creates confidence. A transformation story right before CTA creates desire. A guarantee right after the price dissolves anxiety. Don't place CTAs at structural endpoints — place them at emotional peaks where the preceding content has primed the specific state the ask requires. After the proof section: peak belief. After the two futures close: peak desire. After the guarantee: peak safety. The section immediately before the CTA is not a transition — it's the privileged moment that determines the response.
- **Every word before the CTA primes how the CTA is received.** Word choice is associative architecture, not decoration. "Investment" primes growth; "cost" primes loss. "Join" primes community; "buy" primes transaction. "Unlock" primes access to something already theirs; "purchase" primes exchange. Audit the language in the two paragraphs before every CTA — these words do the heaviest priming work on the page. Select hero images for what they activate emotionally, not what they illustrate literally. A stock photo of someone at a laptop illustrates "work." A photo of someone walking out of a meeting smiling activates confidence. The image is a primer, not a caption.
- **No resets.** Every section channels attention deeper into the decision frame. A jarring section break, an irrelevant sidebar, or a navigation bar invitation to "read our blog" is a reset that breaks the funnel.
- **Reactance awareness (Blount).** When copy pushes too hard — stacking urgency, scarcity, and social proof in rapid succession — the reader's autonomy instinct fires and they resist the very action you want. Counter this with a deliberate pull-back that gives autonomy back: "This isn't for everyone," "If you're not sure, don't buy," "Only join if you're ready to do the work." Paradoxically, these autonomy-giving statements increase conversion by removing the pressure that creates resistance. The moment a reader feels pushed, they resist — defensiveness is an active force, not passive skepticism (Greene). Replace commands with invitations: "See if it's a fit" preserves autonomy where "Start now" triggers resistance. The reader who feels invited converts more reliably than the reader who feels cornered, because the conclusion they reach on their own is stronger than the one you hand them. Place one pull-back before the primary CTA and another before the final urgency — they function as pre-suasion for the ask by signaling that the decision belongs to the reader, not the page.
- **Setup-payoff alignment in the offer (O'Bannon).** Every component in the value stack must correspond to a problem surfaced earlier on the page. If the stack includes a "team onboarding guide" but the problem section never established onboarding as a pain point, it's a deus ex machina — an offer element that appears from nowhere and solves nothing the reader was primed to care about. Reverse-check: for each stack item, point to the section that established the need. For each problem raised, point to the stack item that resolves it. Unmatched items on either side are structural failures.

**Persuasional foreplay (Fladlien).** Reference tools, templates, and assets from the offer during the mechanism and proof sections — WITHOUT revealing they're included. Show a framework diagram, reference a proprietary spreadsheet, mention "the system we built." When these reappear in the offer stack as bonuses, the reader already wants them. Desire gaps created early, resolved during the offer.

**Plant-twice technique (Howard & Mabley).** Seed the offer's anchor concept — the core mechanism name, framework, or system — casually in the problem section ("most teams lack a system for X"), reference it again in proof ("Sarah implemented a system for X and generated..."), then pay it off in the stack ("here's the complete system for X"). By the time the offer arrives, the reader has heard the concept twice without being sold — the offer feels like the logical conclusion they've been tracking toward, not a pivot. Reverse-engineer this: decide what the stack's anchor concept is, then plant it backward into problem and proof sections. This is more structural than persuasional foreplay — foreplay teases specific assets; plant-twice establishes the *idea* that makes the offer feel inevitable.

**Run the four-lever audit on every offer section (Barden: net value equation).** Value = reward minus pain, and both have explicit and implicit dimensions. Most sales pages only pull lever one (increase explicit reward — pile on features and benefits). The other three levers are often more powerful and cheaper: increase implicit reward ('join 10,000 operators who run lean' — identity), decrease explicit pain (reframe the price: '$2.30/day' not '$69/month'), decrease implicit pain (reduce uncertainty, effort, and social risk: 'cancel anytime, no questions, full refund'). After drafting the offer section, name which levers you've pulled. If you've only pulled lever one, the offer is working at 25% capacity.

### 4b. Preemptive Education Section

*Source: Abraham (Getting Everything You Can — preemptive education)*

**Teach the prospect how to evaluate the category — using criteria where your product excels.** Before the proof section, consider a "How to evaluate [category]" section that educates the prospect on what to look for. "Here's how to choose a pipeline tool" or "3 things to check before hiring an agency." The criteria you teach are the criteria where your product wins — but framed as objective buyer guidance, not a feature comparison. The reader who absorbs your evaluation framework can only conclude that your product fits it best, because you built the framework around your strengths. This works because the prospect feels educated, not sold. They arrive at the offer section having already internalized the buying criteria that favor you. The anti-pattern is a transparent comparison chart where every check mark lands in your column — that reads as marketing. Preemptive education reads as advice from a trusted advisor who happens to also sell the thing. Place it between mechanism and proof: the mechanism explains why your approach works, the education section teaches them how to evaluate all approaches (yours included), and the proof section demonstrates that your approach meets the criteria you just taught. The sequence feels like learning, not persuasion — which is exactly what makes it persuasive.

### 5. Proof Stacking

*Sources: Garfinkel (Breakthrough Copywriting), Blanks-Jesson (Making Websites Win), Goward (You Should Test That), Groopman (How Doctors Think), de Bono (Lateral Thinking), Seger (You Talkin' to Me? — layered proof; Writing Subtext — gestural proof), Truffaut (Hitchcock/Truffaut — proof suspense, delayed objection handling, POV shifting, section tempo), Yorke (Into the Woods — expectation gap in proof), Egri (The Art of Dramatic Writing — orchestrated proof), Albers (Interaction of Color — Weber-Fechner diminishing returns), Tufte (Visual Explanations — causal chains in proof, evidence qualification; Beautiful Evidence — analytically complete proof sentence)*

Single-source proof is fragile. One type of evidence convinces one type of buyer. Layer multiple proof types throughout the page — not one testimonial block at the bottom:

- **Testimonials:** Relatable, narrative, specific. Not "Great product!" — "I was spending 6 hours a week on reports. Now it takes 12 minutes." Name, title, photo if possible.
- **Statistics:** Specific numbers with named sources. "47 clients generated $2.3M in combined pipeline in Q1 2024" — not "our clients see great results."
- **Case studies:** Mini-stories with before/after. Situation → what they tried → what happened with this → specific result. 3-4 sentences.
- **Demonstrations:** Show it working. Screenshots, video walkthroughs, before/after comparisons.
- **Credentials/authority:** Implied, not listed. "After building pipelines for 47 B2B companies..." — not "Credentials: MBA, 15 years experience, certified..."
- **Specificity:** Detail implies truth. "47 clients" is more believable than "dozens." "23-page guide" is more real than "comprehensive guide."
- **Dress up every key number (Gallo).** Raw stats don't land — translated stats do. For every proof number, apply at least one dressing technique: comparison ("what used to take 3 hours now takes 12 minutes"), per-unit ("a new qualified lead every 47 minutes"), familiar scale ("enough pipeline to fill your calendar for 6 months"), or personal framing ("that's your next two hires — paid for"). The dressed number is the one the reader repeats to their partner at dinner. The raw number is the one they forget by the next scroll.
**Show the data (Tufte).** When a proof element contains a specific number — "47 clients," "$23K average," "90-day ROI" — that number should be the most visually prominent element in the block. Larger type, bolder weight, higher contrast than the attribution, context, or decorative element around it. A testimonial where the result number ("$23K in 60 days") sits in the same size and weight as the attribution line ("— Sarah M., Agency Owner") buries the proof inside the proof. The number is the argument; the name is the citation. Give each its visual rank accordingly. A grid of four proof stats where all four elements — number, label, attribution, border — share equal visual weight is four facts in competition with each other. One number that commands the block, surrounded by quieter context, is one argument that lands.

- **Costly signaling (Sutherland):** All proof is costly signaling — specificity costs the risk of being disproven, case studies cost the effort of documentation, damaging admissions cost the appearance of perfection. The costlier the proof, the more credible. A vague claim is free to make, which is why nobody believes it. A specific claim with named numbers, named clients, and a named timeframe puts your reputation on the line — and the audience reads that risk as honesty. When choosing between two ways to present proof, pick the one that costs you more to get wrong.
- **Proof by admission:** Naming a flaw or limitation to make every other claim more credible. "We're not the cheapest" makes "but we're the fastest to results" land harder.
- **Vulnerability-as-credibility (Spence, Carnegie).** Go beyond naming a limitation — admit a genuine past failure or wrong turn. "Here's what we got wrong before we figured this out" passes the trust test that polished credentials fail. The audience trusts the person who admits weakness because they've been conditioned by experience to distrust the person who projects only strength. This is deeper than damaging admission as a tactic — it's the guide showing real scars from the same struggle the reader is in, which earns more credibility than any credential stack because it proves lived experience, not just claimed expertise. The founder story should share specific real failures, not curated damaging admissions: "I spent $14,000 on a Facebook campaign targeting the completely wrong audience because I was too proud to ask my media buyer what I was doing wrong" is vulnerability. "We've learned a lot along the way" is decoration. The reader trusts the specific mess, not the polished lesson — because specificity proves it actually happened, while vagueness proves only that someone decided vulnerability would be strategic.
- **Disqualification copy as trust signal (Belfort).** "This is NOT for you if..." copy belongs in the proof/credibility section, not just as a positioning afterthought. When you explicitly disqualify prospects — "This isn't for agencies billing under $10K/month" or "If you're looking for a quick hack, this will frustrate you" — you raise personal trust by signaling honesty. The reader thinks: "a scammer would never turn people away." Disqualification works because it proves you care more about fit than about closing everyone, which is the costliest signal of integrity a sales page can make. Keep it to 3-5 specific exclusions that genuinely describe bad-fit buyers — vague disqualifications ("this isn't for everyone") are reactance plays (see Principle 4), not trust builders. Protect at least one non-conversion zone on every sales page -- FAQ, damaging admission, or disqualification copy -- where the only job is honesty, not persuasion. These are the page's corners: quiet spaces the reader retreats to when the selling gets heavy. Optimizing these sections for conversion destroys the trust they exist to build (Bachelard).
- **Proof by contrast:** What happened to someone who chose the alternative — not attacking competitors, but showing the path-not-taken and its consequences.
- **Expectation gap:** Structure testimonials to show the gap between what the customer expected and what actually happened. "She expected maybe one or two leads. She got 14 in the first week." Positive expectation gaps are more persuasive than raw results because they carry the emotional weight of surprise — the reader doesn't just see the outcome, they feel the disbelief. Frame the expectation first (modest, relatable), then the reality (specific, concrete). The gap itself is the proof. This works because the stated expectation anchors low, making the actual result feel extraordinary even when it's simply good.

**Sinatra Test (Heath).** Identify the ONE example so compelling it makes everything else on the page believable — "if it worked for them, it works." This is not the best testimonial or the biggest number. It's the single proof point that, by itself, settles the argument. A Fortune 500 logo on an enterprise page. A well-known skeptic who converted. A result so specific in such a difficult context that no other proof is needed. Find the Sinatra Test example first and give it the most prominent placement — every other proof point is reinforcement after this one lands.

**Proof calibration (Fladlien):** There is such a thing as too much proof. High-trust audiences (warm referrals, established communities, existing customers) can be turned off by overselling. If the audience already trusts the source, over-stacking testimonials pushes past believability into infomercial territory. Calibrate proof density to the audience's starting trust level — cold traffic needs heavy proof, warm traffic needs selective proof. **Traffic temperature sharpens this calibration (Brunson).** Cold traffic — strangers from ads or outbound — needs stacked proof (testimonials, case studies, credentials) plus a bridge before the pitch: a story, a video, a lead magnet that earns the right to sell. Without the bridge, the pitch arrives before trust exists and the reader bounces. Warm traffic — email subscribers, content followers, webinar attendees — needs less proof and more differentiation. They already believe the category works; they need to believe yours is different. Hot traffic — past buyers, active community members, referrals from trusted sources — needs minimal proof and direct offers. They've already decided to trust you; proving yourself again wastes their time and signals insecurity. The mistake is building one proof density for all traffic sources. A page that stacks ten testimonials for hot traffic feels like an infomercial to people who already trust you. A page that leads with a direct offer for cold traffic feels presumptuous to people who don't know you yet. Match the proof weight to the temperature of the traffic hitting the page.

**Orchestrated proof (Egri).** Don't feature five customers who are basically the same person. Deliberately contrast proof subjects so each proves the premise from a different angle: the skeptic who was won over despite resistance, the expert who was surprised by something they thought they already knew, the beginner who got results fast without the usual ramp-up, the veteran who got unstuck after years of diminishing returns. Same premise, different entry points — that's orchestration. Five similar testimonials prove the product works for one type of person. Four contrasting testimonials prove the premise is structurally true regardless of who tests it. When selecting or requesting testimonials, actively seek diversity of starting position, not just diversity of result.

**Proof suspense (Hitchcock's bomb under the table).** Plant a credibility signal early — a number, a name, a result — that doesn't get explained until the proof section. "The 47-company dataset changed everything" dropped in the hero or problem section, then left unexplained for two or three sections, creates sustained curiosity. The reader carries the unexplained signal forward, and when the proof section finally unpacks it, the payoff lands harder because the reader has been waiting. This is Hitchcock's bomb under the table applied to credibility: the audience knows something important is there, which makes every section between the plant and the reveal feel charged. Don't explain the signal early. Don't tease it with "more on that later." Just state it as fact and move on — the gap does the work.

**Customer testimonials are mini character arcs (Seger).** The strongest testimonials follow: false belief ("I thought I needed to hustle harder") → catalyst ("I burned out and lost my biggest client") → resistance ("I didn't think a system would work for me") → transformation ("Now I have a pipeline that runs without me"). This arc is more persuasive than "had problem, used product, got results" because it includes doubt — and the doubt makes the result believable.

**Gestural proof.** Testimonials that describe changed behavior are more trustworthy than testimonials that describe feelings. "I canceled three other tools" and "I raised my prices 40%" land harder than "I love this product" because action is the believable channel. When selecting or writing testimonials, prioritize ones that show what the person *did* differently — observable behavior is proof that something actually changed.

**Ammunition test for proof (McKee: Dialogue).** Every statistic, testimonial, and case study must be aimed at a specific belief the reader holds. "340% revenue increase" is a number. "340% revenue increase — using the same ad budget they'd been burning through for two years" is ammunition aimed at the reader's belief that their current approach is fine. A number is ammunition only when it is aimed at something. Statistics placed on a page for credibility without connecting them to the reader's specific concern are exposition that breaks momentum.

**Reaction-as-proof (Caine).** Testimonials that capture the REACTION to the result are more persuasive than testimonials that state the result. "I just sat there staring at my screen" beats "I increased revenue by 3x." "I called my wife and couldn't get the words out" beats "We hit our quarterly target in three weeks." The reaction is what Caine calls listening-is-acting — the most powerful moment in a scene is often not the actor delivering the line but the other actor receiving it. On a sales page, the customer's stunned, emotional, involuntary reaction to their own result is the close-up that lands. Results can be fabricated or exaggerated — reactions can't. A reader who sees "I stared at the dashboard for ten minutes because I didn't believe the numbers" believes it because the detail is too specific and too human to be invented. A reader who sees "I achieved a 312% ROI" evaluates it as a claim. When selecting or requesting testimonials, ask for the moment they realized it worked — not the metric, but what they did when they saw the metric. The best testimonials pair both: the reaction first ("I literally stopped mid-meeting and turned my laptop around to show my partner"), then the result ("$47K in pipeline in the first 30 days"). The reaction makes the result feel real. The result alone makes it feel like marketing.

**Subtextual proof (Mackendrick).** The most persuasive testimonials carry subtext beneath their surface meaning. "I almost didn't buy" subtextually says "I'm discerning and this overcame my resistance." "I was skeptical after three other programs failed" subtextually says "I'm a critical thinker and THIS convinced me." "My wife noticed the difference before I did" subtextually says "the change was so real that people around me could see it." Don't explain the subtext — the moment you write "this shows that Sarah is a careful buyer, so you can trust her judgment," you've killed the reader's participation. The reader who infers the subtext themselves owns the conclusion; the reader who's told what to think evaluates it as a claim. When selecting or requesting testimonials, look for ones with rich subtext: statements about initial resistance (subtext: discernment), mentions of specific skepticism (subtext: critical thinking), references to other people noticing results (subtext: the change is visible and real). A testimonial with no subtext — "Great product, highly recommend" — operates on one level and persuades on zero.

**Layered beats labeled.** Testimonials with specific numbers and observable changes outperform stated value judgments. "I was spending 6 hours a week on reports. Now it takes 12 minutes" is layered — the reader sees the transformation without being told it's transformative. "This product is great and I highly recommend it" is a label that says nothing. Push for the concrete detail: hours saved, revenue gained, specific behavior that changed. The specificity does the persuading.

**Show the causal chain, not just the outcome (Tufte).** "Response rates went from 2% to 14% in 30 days" is correlation — the reader sees two numbers and a timeframe but no reason to believe it repeats. "Response rates went from 2% to 14% in 30 days because the system automated personalization at scale and reps stopped sending identical sequences" is causality — the mechanism is named, which makes the result feel transferable. Sophisticated buyers don't just want proof that something worked; they want to understand why it worked, because understanding the mechanism is what lets them believe it will work for them. For every major result claim, identify and name the specific thing that caused it. If you can't name the cause, the claim is a data point, not proof.

**Every proof element serves the mechanism, not the product's popularity (Albuquerque).** "10,000 happy customers" proves popularity; a case study showing the mechanism in action proves the mechanism is real. The distinction matters because the prospect's blocking belief is about whether the *approach* works, not whether the *product* is popular. A wall of logos says "many people bought this" — it does not say "the underlying principle is sound." A single case study that walks through the mechanism step by step — showing the specific inputs, the specific process, and the specific output — proves the mechanism is transferable. When selecting proof, ask: does this make the mechanism more believable, or does it just make the product look successful? Popularity proof reassures after the belief has shifted; mechanism proof causes the shift. Front-load mechanism proof, then layer popularity proof as confirmation.

**Place proof at objection points.** When the reader thinks "does this really work?" the next thing they see is proof that it does. When the reader sees the price and thinks "that's a lot," the next thing they see is a testimonial about ROI.

**Testimonials land harder after objection handling (D'Souza).** Sequence matters: testimonials placed after objections have been raised and resolved outperform testimonials placed before, because the brain has already activated its concerns. A reader whose "but does this work for small teams?" objection was just surfaced and addressed is primed to receive the testimonial from a 4-person team as confirmation, not background noise. Front-loading testimonials before the reader's concerns are active means the proof arrives before the question exists.

**Some objections should hang — and timing is rhetorical, not structural (Heinrichs: kairos).** Not every objection needs immediate handling. Objections addressed too early plant doubt the reader wasn't carrying yet; objections addressed too late meet a mind that's already decided against you. The kairotic moment for each objection is when the reader is actively holding that concern — after desire but before decision for credibility objections, immediately before the CTA for anxiety objections. Surface the objection — let the reader see that you know they're thinking it — but don't answer it right away. Let it sit for a section while proof accumulates around it. "You're probably wondering if this works for [specific situation]" acknowledged in the mechanism section, then answered two sections later with a case study of exactly that situation, reads as confident. The same objection answered the instant it's raised reads as defensive — like you anticipated the attack and prepared a rebuttal. Premature objection handling signals anxiety. Delayed handling, where proof fills the gap, signals certainty. Reserve immediate answers for anxieties near the CTA (price, risk, process) where hesitation kills action. For credibility and relevance objections earlier in the page, let the accumulating proof do the answering.

**Raise the objection in their words before resolving it (D'Souza).** When you do handle an objection, state it first — in the buyer's own language, as they'd phrase it to a friend. "But I've already tried three different CRMs and they all ended up as expensive address books." Then resolve with specificity: the exact feature, guarantee, or proof point that neutralizes that concern. Resolving an objection the reader hasn't consciously formed yet wastes the resolution — the reader wasn't worried about it, so the answer lands on nothing. Worse, it can plant the doubt you were trying to prevent. Raise it in their voice first so the resolution has something to attach to.

**Three false beliefs as objection taxonomy (Brunson).** Categorize every objection as vehicle ("this approach won't work"), internal ("I can't do this"), or external ("something outside me prevents this"). Vehicle objections need proof — case studies, mechanism explanation, data showing the approach works. Internal objections need stories of people like them who succeeded despite the same doubt. External objections need specific blocker removal — showing that the time, tech, team, or money barrier is smaller than they think. Most pages over-address vehicle objections and ignore internal ones, which is why the reader believes it works but still doesn't buy — they don't believe *they* can make it work.

**Ledge-Disrupt-Ask for objection resolution (Blount).** When resolving a surfaced objection, don't jump straight to the rebuttal — pure rebuttals feel argumentative and trigger reactance. Instead: *Ledge* — acknowledge the objection calmly to show you heard it ("That's a fair concern — most people who've been burned by [category] feel exactly the same way"). *Disrupt* — break the expected pattern with agreement or a reframe that catches the reader off-guard ("And honestly? They're right to be skeptical. Most [solutions] don't work"). *Ask* — redirect with a question that moves the reader forward ("So the real question isn't whether to try again — it's whether this approach is structurally different from what failed before"). The sequence defuses defensiveness before the proof arrives, so the resolution lands as dialogue rather than debate.

**Proof through narrative beats proof through stats** (Clemens). A story about one person's transformation is more convincing than a bar chart showing aggregate results. Use stats to validate the narrative — not replace it.

**Select representative proof, not just dramatic proof.** The most vivid case study is the most available to your memory — but it might be the least representative of what a typical customer experiences. A prospect who buys based on an outlier result and gets the median result feels deceived. Proof should set accurate expectations, not maximize excitement. The typical journey told well is more trustworthy than the miracle story told dramatically.

**Qualify every proof claim (Tufte).** For each significant result claim, state the sample ("across 47 clients"), the timeframe ("within 60 days"), and whether the number represents average or exemplary performance ("median result, not the top quartile"). Unqualified claims — "clients see dramatic results" — are invisible to high-LTV buyers because they've been burned by outlier promises; qualified claims are the signal those buyers are scanning for. A testimonial that says "I went from $0 to $80K in 90 days" is exciting but unverifiable and unscalable in the reader's mind. A proof block that says "median result across 23 clients: $34K in new pipeline within 60 days; top quartile reached $80K+" is less dramatic but far more credible — and credibility is what closes sophisticated buyers. Qualification is not hedging; it's the format that transforms a claim into evidence.

**The analytically complete proof sentence (Tufte, Beautiful Evidence).** A proof claim that names the metric, the comparison baseline, the sample, and the timeframe in one sentence is analytically stronger than the same data spread across a callout box, a testimonial snippet, and a footnote. "Qualified pipeline grew from $180K to $740K — a 4x increase — across 31 mid-market teams in the first 90 days" clears the reader's three skeptic questions in one pass: does this work (the metric and delta), for whom (the sample), how often (the timeframe). Splitting those four elements across a page forces navigation, and navigation creates doubt — the reader who has to assemble your evidence from multiple locations will assume the worst about the gaps between them. When you have data that legitimately supports all four components, present them together as a single sentence, not as four separately designed elements competing for attention.

**Weber-Fechner diminishing returns (Albers).** The first testimonial, case study, or proof point creates the biggest perceptual shift. Each subsequent one produces less impact — the fifth testimonial is nearly invisible. Instead of adding more proof of the same type, shift proof modality: testimonial → case study → metric → screenshot → before/after. Each new modality resets the perception curve. A page with five testimonials in a row is less persuasive than a page with one testimonial, one case study, one metric, one screenshot, and one before/after — even though the total "proof" is the same.

**Long pages usually win** (Blanks-Jesson) because more evidence gives more chances to find the specific proof that tips each individual visitor's scale. Don't cut proof to "keep it short." Cut fluff — keep proof.

### 6. Story as Structure

*Sources: Clemens (Persuasion Story Code), Dicks (Storyworthy), Brunson (Expert Secrets), Goffman (Presentation of Self), Feynman (Surely You're Joking), Seger (You Talkin' to Me? — subtext in testimonials, exposition without exposition; Writing Subtext — contradiction in problem section, relational guide status; Making a Good Script Great — character arc in testimonials, stakes escalation), Yorke (Into the Woods — flawed protagonist), Hunter (Screenwriting 434 — emotional truth), Egri (The Art of Dramatic Writing — unity of opposites), Suber (The Power of Film — internal obstacles), Tobias (20 Master Plots — plot structure requirements, plot combination)*

Story bypasses the critical filter. Logic creates consideration; story creates conviction. Three story roles on every sales page:

- **Customer = hero.** Not your company. Not your founder. The reader is the protagonist. The product is the weapon they wield — not the hero that saves them. **The prospect is a flawed protagonist.** Their "flaw" isn't stupidity or laziness — it's a false belief or incomplete worldview that keeps them stuck. "I just need to work harder." "I haven't found the right tactic yet." "If I get more traffic, everything will fix itself." Name the flaw without insulting them: "It's not that you haven't worked hard enough — it's that you've been working hard on the wrong thing." The offer completes them — it's the missing piece that makes everything else they've already done finally work. This is deeper than fear/desire framing because it explains *why* the problem persists despite their effort. The flaw should surface in the problem section (name what they believe), crack in the mechanism section (show why that belief is incomplete), and resolve in the offer (present the piece that was missing).
- **Problem = villain.** Every hero needs an antagonist. Name the force creating the problem: the industry that profits from confusion, the conventional wisdom that's wrong, the old method that wastes their time. A villain makes the problem specific and fightable. **Introduce 1-2 proprietary terms early that redefine the reader's problem in your framework.** "Pipeline debt." "Qualification hygiene." Once the reader adopts your term for their problem, your solution is the only answer — because the problem now lives in your vocabulary, not the category's. **Make the insider/outsider divide explicit.** The villain section should name the out-group the reader wants to leave and the in-group they want to join. "Operators vs. guessers." "Builders vs. browsers." The labels do the conversion work — the reader self-selects by identity, and identity-based decisions are stickier than logical ones. **Agitate the situation, not the person.** The villain is always the system, the old method, the industry — never the prospect's intelligence or effort. The prospect should think "that's exactly my situation" not "they think I'm an idiot." "You've been doing it wrong" threatens their dignity. "The conventional approach was never designed for this" externalizes blame to the villain and preserves it. **Truth and pain in problem agitation (Vorhaus).** Beyond naming the struggle and the villain, name the uncomfortable truth the prospect recognizes but won't admit. "You've posted every day for three months and your DMs are still empty — not because the algorithm hates you, but because you sound like everyone else." The truth is what creates the recognition moment — they think "yes, that's exactly it" not because you described the symptom but because you named what they pretend isn't true. The pain validates the struggle. The truth names why it's real. **Three-level problem for full agitation (Miller).** Every problem section should address three levels: external (the tangible obstacle — "your CRM hasn't been updated in three weeks"), internal (how it makes them feel — "you dread Monday standups because you know the numbers are fiction"), and philosophical (why it's just plain wrong — "people who work this hard shouldn't have to guess whether it's paying off"). External gets attention, internal gets emotion, philosophical gets conviction. Most pages only hit external — they describe the broken process without naming the feeling it creates or the deeper injustice it represents. The three-layer current state diagnosis below maps the observable situation; the three-level problem maps the *meaning* of that situation to the prospect. Both should be present — diagnosis gives specificity, three-level gives emotional depth.

**Three-layer current state diagnosis.** The problem section should diagnose the prospect's situation in three layers, each deeper than the last. First, **environment** -- their setup, tools, team, process. "You're running outreach from a spreadsheet, your SDR is context-switching between 4 tools, and your CRM hasn't been updated since last quarter." This is observable, verifiable, and earns the right to go deeper because the reader thinks "they know my setup." Second, **problems** -- what's broken or underperforming in that environment. "Leads slip through because nobody owns follow-up. Reps cherry-pick the easy ones. Your pipeline report is a fiction." These are the consequences of the environment, and naming them specifically shows you understand what those tools and processes actually produce. Third, **impact** -- what those problems cost in revenue, time, stress, reputation, or opportunity. "That's $40K in leaked pipeline per quarter, a CEO who doesn't trust your numbers, and a Sunday night pit in your stomach every week before the Monday standup." The impact layer is where the real motivation lives -- but it only lands if the environment and problems above it feel accurate. A generic impact statement ("you're losing money") bounces off. An impact statement built on two layers of specific diagnosis ("you're losing $40K/quarter because leads slip through a spreadsheet your SDR hasn't touched in three weeks") hits because the reader can trace the loss back to something they recognize. The more specific the diagnosis, the more the reader feels understood -- and a reader who feels understood will trust your mechanism.

**Loss reframing for 2x impact (Kahneman).** Take every benefit the product delivers and reframe it as a loss the reader is currently experiencing. "You'll save 10 hours a week" is a gain — 1x impact. "You're losing 10 hours every week to manual prospecting" is a loss — 2x impact. "That's 520 hours a year — 13 full work weeks — gone" is accumulated loss that compounds. Losses are psychologically twice as powerful as equivalent gains. During problem agitation, default to loss framing: name what they're losing right now in time, money, opportunities, and status — not what they'll gain later. The accumulated-loss version is strongest because it forces the reader to do the math on their own situation.

**Label the emotion before agitating it (Voss).** Explicitly name what the prospect is feeling — "It sounds like you've tried three different approaches and none of them stuck — and now you're not sure whether the problem is you or the approach." Labeling earns the right to agitate. A problem section that agitates without first labeling feels like an attack; one that labels first feels like understanding. The reader who sees their emotion named accurately thinks "yes, that's exactly it" — and that recognition creates the trust that lets the agitation land as empathy instead of manipulation. Label the feeling, then agitate the situation that caused it.

**Appreciation before agitation (Carnegie).** Before naming the problem, acknowledge the reader's effort. "You've been showing up. You've been putting in the work. And it's not paying off the way it should." The third sentence hits harder BECAUSE of the first two — the reader feels validated and frustrated simultaneously, which is more motivating than shame alone. Use "and" not "but" when transitioning from praise to gap: "You've built a real audience, and the revenue hasn't caught up yet" keeps the reader's dignity intact while opening the wound. "You've built a real audience, but you're still broke" negates the effort. The appreciation earns the right to agitate — without it, the problem section reads as an outsider judging their failures rather than a guide who sees their struggle.

**Name the want behind the pain (Godin).** The problem section should articulate the want, not just the need. "Struggling with reporting" is a need — functional, rational, forgettable. "Feeling like you're drowning while your competitors look effortless" is the want — emotional, identity-based, urgent. Needs describe broken processes. Wants describe the gap between who the prospect is and who they want to be. The three-layer diagnosis (environment → problems → impact) should bottom out at the want layer: what does the prospect *desire to feel* once the problem is gone? Competent. In control. Ahead. Respected. Name that want explicitly in the impact layer and carry it through to the transformation promise. Address the shadow desire beneath the stated need (Greene). The reader says they want "more leads" but they actually want to stop feeling like a fraud who can't fill a pipeline. The stated desire is the mask; the shadow desire is the sale. Acknowledge the tension between what the prospect knows they should do and what they actually want — "You didn't get into this to spend your days chasing prospects" speaks to the shadow desire for creative, high-level work that the practical self suppresses. A page that addresses only needs gets comparison-shopped on features. A page that addresses the want behind the need gets chosen on resonance.

**Build the problem on deliberate contradiction** — surface the gap between what they believe works and what observable evidence shows. "You've been told X, but look at what's actually happening." The stronger the contradiction between their assumptions and reality, the stronger the need for resolution — and the mechanism becomes the only thing that closes that gap. **Name the internal obstacle.** Beyond the external problem and the systemic villain, name the internal obstacle — the fear, self-doubt, or identity threat the prospect carries. "You know you should be posting consistently. You know the algorithm rewards it. And every night you open the app, stare at the blank screen, and close it." That's the internal obstacle. Competitors address logistics — "here's a content calendar." The internal obstacle is why the calendar stays empty. Naming it creates the "they understand me" response that external problem agitation alone never achieves, because it surfaces what the prospect feels but won't say out loud. The external problem gives them a reason to read. The villain gives them something to fight. The internal obstacle gives them permission to believe the solution might actually work for *them* — because it acknowledges the real barrier isn't information or tools, it's the thing inside that kept them stuck despite having both. **Name the specific obstacles, including "what if" failures (Mackendrick).** Beyond the general problem and the systemic villain, name the specific obstacles the reader has encountered — the particular things they tried, the particular ways those things failed, the particular moments they stopped believing. "You bought the course and did the first three modules and then life happened and the login expired." "You hired the agency and they sent you a report every month that you couldn't connect to a single new client." "You tried posting consistently for 90 days and the only people who engaged were other people trying to grow their accounts." Include internal obstacles — the ones they won't say out loud: "You've tried this before and stopped believing it could work." "You started wondering if maybe you're just not the type of person who can make this work." "You caught yourself thinking 'this probably only works for people with bigger audiences.'" Obstacles make the solution feel earned. A page that presents a frictionless path — "just follow these steps and everything changes" — produces weak desire because the reader knows from experience that nothing is frictionless. A page that names the specific obstacles and then shows how the mechanism defeats each one produces strong desire because the reader sees their actual barriers being addressed, not ignored. The difference between a problem section that creates recognition and one that creates conviction is obstacles: recognition says "yes, that's my situation"; conviction says "yes, and they understand exactly what's been stopping me."

**Name the ghost (Truby).** The internal obstacle often has an origin — a past experience that created the current pattern. "You believed manual outreach was the only real way because the first automation tool you tried sent 500 broken emails and cost you three clients. That ghost keeps you doing it by hand even though it's killing your pipeline." Naming the ghost — the failed vendor, the bad advice, the formative failure — explains the origin of their resistance and deepens the "they get me" response. It shows you understand not just what they're stuck on, but *why* they're stuck. **Unity of opposites (Egri) — name why they can't walk away.** The strongest problems aren't ones the prospect chose — they're ones the prospect is locked into. "You can't stop prospecting. You can't fire your clients. You can't pretend the revenue isn't declining." Name the structural bond: economics that force them to keep going, identity that won't let them quit, obligations that make walking away impossible, biology that ensures they'll feel the pain every day. Locked-in problems create tension that optional problems never do — the reader who could shrug off the problem will shrug off the page. When the reader recognizes they're trapped in the situation you're describing, the mechanism stops being interesting and starts being necessary.
- **Founder/brand = guide.** The Obi-Wan who hands the hero the lightsaber. The guide has credibility because they've been through the same struggle. The epiphany bridge is the guide's origin story. **Lead with empathy, then authority (Miller).** In the credibility or founder story section, lead with empathy — "we know what it's like to stare at a pipeline report that's 60% fiction" — then follow with authority (results, proof, endorsements). Authority-first sounds like bragging: "We've generated $12M in pipeline for 47 clients" as the opening line of a founder section makes the reader feel talked at. Empathy-first makes authority feel like reassurance: "We spent two years building pipelines the wrong way before we figured out what actually works — and since then, we've generated $12M for 47 clients" makes the same credential land as earned wisdom instead of a flex. **The relational subtext matters:** the page should make the reader feel elevated, not talked down to. "You already know this isn't working" (peer who respects their awareness) lands differently than "Let me show you what's wrong" (authority correcting an inferior). Guide status without superiority — the tone should say "I've been where you are" not "I know something you don't."

**Emotional truth test for founder stories and case studies.** If the writer hasn't felt the emotion being described — or can't transplant an analogous feeling from their own experience — the copy will be technically correct but emotionally flat. "You've posted every day for 3 months and your DMs are still empty" comes from felt experience; "Struggling to get clients?" comes from a spreadsheet. Before writing any founder story or case study section, ask: has the person telling this story actually lived this feeling? Specificity of feeling over category language. "I remember refreshing Stripe at 2am, knowing the number wouldn't change" is felt. "We experienced revenue challenges" is reported. The audience can tell the difference — felt experience creates recognition, reported experience creates distance.

**POV shifting as structural technique.** These three roles aren't just character assignments — they're camera positions you alternate between to control the reader's emotional state. Reader-as-protagonist ("you" language, problem/agitation sections) creates identification and urgency. Peer-as-witness (testimonials, case studies) creates social proof through someone the reader sees as similar. Guide-as-authority (mechanism, founder story) creates credibility and confidence. Shift POV deliberately across sections: problem section in reader-as-protagonist, then a testimonial block in peer-as-witness, then mechanism in guide-as-authority. Each shift recalibrates emotional position — the reader who was feeling urgency now feels reassured by a peer, then educated by a guide. Staying in one POV too long creates fatigue: an entire page in "you" language feels like being lectured; an entire page in third-person testimonials feels like watching from the outside. The shifts themselves create movement.

**Plot combination for depth (Tobias).** Long-form pages benefit from layering a primary and secondary plot structure. A sales page might be primarily Transformation (customer arc from struggle to success) with secondary Discovery (mechanism reveal that explains why the transformation works). The primary plot drives the overall emotional arc; the secondary adds texture and intellectual engagement. The plots should intersect — the Discovery moment (mechanism reveal) should be the catalyst that makes the Transformation possible. Don't run two parallel plots; layer them so one enables the other.

**Story of Self/Us/Now checkpoint (Godin, via Ganz).** Every sales page needs all three narrative layers — not just one. Story of Self: the founder or guide's personal credibility moment ("I was running the same broken playbook until...") — lives in the mechanism section or founder story. Story of Us: shared identity and belonging ("We're the operators who stopped accepting 'that's just how it works'") — lives in the problem section, villain naming, and identity language throughout. Story of Now: why this moment demands action ("The window for easy fixes is closing — every month you wait, the rebuild gets harder") — lives in the cost-of-inaction section and closing urgency. Claude defaults to Story of Us — community language, shared values, belonging rhetoric — and consistently skips Self (no personal credible narrator) and Now (no real urgency from real stakes). A page with all three moves people from passive agreement to active enrollment. A page with only Us creates vague belonging that never converts.

**The Epiphany Bridge (Brunson):** The founder story should follow: backstory (same place as the reader) → wall (same obstacle they face) → epiphany (the realization that changed everything) → plan (what you did differently) → result (the transformation). Stories shift beliefs where arguments create resistance. The reader who hears "I was doing the same thing you're doing, hit the same wall, and then realized X" experiences the insight themselves — they don't evaluate it as a claim, they live it as a narrative. Used in the mechanism section to explain WHY this works without lecturing.

**The problem section illuminates pain the prospect is already experiencing** — use their language, their specific scenarios, their already-acknowledged costs — because a reader who sees their own situation reflected believes it; a reader who feels manipulated into feeling bad about something they didn't think was a problem disengages (Camp).

**The problem section is a scene, not a list (Sullivan).** The problem section is a scene the reader's nervous system recognizes, not a list of pain points. Paint the before-state in sensory detail — the specific moment, the feeling, the sound of the notification, the weight of the inbox. One story beats ten bullet points. A bulleted list of pain points reads like a marketing exercise; a single scene that captures the exact moment the reader last felt the problem reads like someone who's been inside their life. The scene activates recognition; the list activates evaluation. Recognition converts. Evaluation stalls.

**Availability activation in the problem section (Kahneman).** Don't state facts about the problem — trigger the reader's own memories. "Many people find this frustrating" activates nothing. "You stopped opening the app. Not because you stopped working out — because seeing the broken streak felt worse than skipping the workout" activates their specific memory of that exact moment. The most persuasive copy doesn't teach something new — it makes the reader feel something they already knew but hadn't articulated. When the reader's own memory is activated, the problem feels real and urgent because it IS real — the availability heuristic means vivid, personally recalled experiences dominate judgment far more than statistics or abstract descriptions ever can.

**Exposition without exposition.** Paint the specific scene — time, place, observable detail — instead of narrating the category of struggle. "Our clients struggle with pipeline visibility" is telling. "It's Tuesday at 4pm. Your CEO asks for the pipeline number. You open three tabs, cross-reference two spreadsheets, and guess" is showing. Specific scenes create recognition; category labels create distance.

**Diagnose the problem at the negation of the negation (McKee: Story).** The problem section should push past surface-level pain to McKee's four levels of antagonism: the desired state (positive), mild discomfort (contrary — "your ads aren't working"), outright failure (contradictory — "you're losing money on every campaign"), and failure disguised as success (negation of negation — "the thing you think is helping is actually driving them away, and your dashboard confirms the illusion"). The negation of negation is the most powerful because it reveals a hidden danger the reader hasn't named. Push the problem section to the negation of negation for maximum urgency — not just "this isn't working" but "the thing you're relying on IS the problem." Most problem sections stop at the contradictory level. The negation of negation is what makes the reader unable to continue doing what they were doing.

**The as-if for visceral problem agitation (Weston).** Abstract pain doesn't move people. "Managing growth is challenging" describes a category. Put the reader inside a lived moment instead: "You open your laptop at 6 AM. There are 47 unread messages. Three of them are from the same client. You haven't shipped in two weeks because you're managing the people who are supposed to be shipping." The as-if makes the problem feel remembered, not described. The reader's body responds to the scene because it activates their own memory of that exact morning. This is more specific than "showing vs. telling" — it's putting the reader physically inside a moment they recognize. The test: does the problem copy make the reader's stomach clench? Does it trigger a specific memory? Or does it describe a situation they evaluate from a distance? The strongest problem sections don't describe pain — they recreate it. "You've tried three agencies and none of them worked" is a summary. "The last agency charged $40K, took four months, and the leads they delivered had bounced email addresses" is visceral because the reader can see the spreadsheet, feel the sunk cost, remember their own version of that exact disappointment.

**Five-second moment** (Dicks): Every good story has a single moment of change — the precise instant something shifts. In a sales page, this is the realization that creates the mechanism. Find it and build the story around it. Don't diffuse it across a paragraph of explanation.

**Never state the subtext.** When a testimonial or customer story implies "and you can too," don't write those words. Stated subtext kills reader participation — the reader was about to complete that thought themselves, and you stole it. A specific before-and-after lets the reader draw the conclusion; adding "imagine what this could mean for you" weakens the effect. Implied subtext creates conviction; stated subtext creates resistance.

**Show the transformation, not the tool (Mamet).** The page should follow the customer's world — before and after — not the product. A page that "follows the product" walks the reader through features, screenshots, and how-it-works in sequence, which is narration about an object. A page that follows the transformation shows the customer's world broken, then shows it working, and the product appears only at the beat where it solves something specific. The product is not the subject of the page — the change in the customer's life is. Feature screenshots belong inside the mechanism section as evidence of *how* the transformation happens, not as standalone sections that showcase the interface. If a section could be titled "About Our Product," it's following the tool. If it could be titled "What Changes For You," it's following the transformation.

**The mechanism must survive the "do I actually understand this?" test.** Before writing the mechanism section, ask: can I explain why this specific approach produces this specific result for this specific person — without jargon, without framework labels, in words a non-expert would follow? If not, the mechanism section will be a template with the right headings and no real explanation underneath. The mechanism section should read like a genuine discovery, not a rote walkthrough.

**Discovery plot beats for mechanism reveals (Tobias).** When the mechanism functions as a revelation — "the hidden reason this works" — structure it as Discovery plot: protagonist doesn't know something crucial (the reader's current false belief), clues accumulate (evidence they've seen but didn't connect), revelation moment (the mechanism itself, presented as insight), and the discovery changes everything (reframes their entire approach). Don't dump the mechanism in one paragraph. Lay breadcrumbs first: "You've probably noticed [clue 1]. And if you've tried [approach], you saw [clue 2]." The mechanism lands harder when the reader arrives at it alongside you, not after you announce it.

**Genre evolution as advanced technique (Truby — Anatomy of Genres).** For sophisticated audiences — warm lists, returning buyers, educated markets — straight genre execution becomes predictable. Evolution means keeping the required beats while refreshing the execution. A case study still needs protagonist/villain/result, but instead of "before/after stats," tell it as full narrative with named character and decision moment. An origin story still needs epiphany/action/proof, but skip the rags-to-riches template and focus on one five-second realization. The core promise stays — the delivery method evolves. Subvert execution, never the genre's contract. This technique applies when the audience has seen the standard version too many times; for new-to-category buyers, execute the beats straight.

### 7. Visual Hierarchy and Layout

*Sources: Krug (Don't Make Me Think), Williams (Non-Designer's Design Book), Wathan/Schoger (Refactoring UI), Goffman (Presentation of Self), Head (Designing Interface Animation — form state transitions), Pickering (Inclusive Design Patterns — semantic heading hierarchy), Seger (Writing Subtext — symbolic design), Tufte (Envisioning Information — spatial intensity audit for page-level narrative; The Visual Display of Quantitative Information — show-the-data rule for proof numbers, multifunctioning elements in CTAs, proportional ink in pricing layouts)*

**Peripheral gist (Weinschenk).** Before the 5-second test (Krug) happens, there's a half-second gist test driven by peripheral vision. The overall visual impression — density, whitespace, color, imagery — determines whether the visitor's attention ever focuses on the headline. A page whose peripheral gist signals "wall of text" or "cluttered chaos" loses the visitor before the 5-second test begins. Design the gist first: blur your eyes and look at the page. The impression you get is what every visitor processes before they read a word. A "premium" offer whose page gist reads "discount bin" — dense, colorful, crowded — contradicts its positioning at the fastest processing layer. The gist must confirm the positioning, then the 5-second test confirms the message.

Visitors scan, they don't read. Design every section for the 5-second test: if someone glances at this page for 5 seconds, do they know what it is, who it's for, and what to do?

**CRAP principles for layout direction:**

- **Contrast:** Headlines 2-3x body text size. CTA button contrasts violently with the background. If two things are different, make them VERY different — subtle differences look like mistakes.
- **Repetition:** Consistent heading styles, button styles, spacing patterns throughout the page. Visual rhythm makes the page feel intentional.
- **Alignment:** Nothing placed arbitrarily. Every element aligned to an invisible grid. Left-aligned text is easier to scan than centered text for body copy.
- **Proximity:** Related items grouped tightly. Space between sections larger than space within sections. Proximity tells the eye what belongs together.

**Additional layout principles:**
- One primary CTA per viewport. Don't make them choose between three actions.
- **Choice reduction at page level (Dooley).** Audit for competing actions: navigation links, social icons, footer links, multiple different CTAs, "learn more" links that exit the conversion path. Every alternative action competes with the desired action — and every competing action is friction against the one action that matters. On a conversion page: one CTA repeated at logical scroll points, navigation removed or minimized, footer stripped to essentials, social icons gone, "related content" links gone. If the page has a single conversion goal, every exit path that isn't the CTA is working against you. The page with 47 links and one buy button has 47 friction sources. The page with one buy button repeated three times has zero.
- White space signals importance. Crowded sections feel cheap.
- Follow web conventions for layout — save creativity for the message. Navigation at top, CTA buttons look like buttons, links look like links.
- Design in grayscale first (Wathan/Schoger): if the hierarchy works without color, it'll work with color. If it needs color to make sense, the structure is wrong.
- Spacing scale: use consistent increments (8px, 16px, 24px, 32px, 48px) rather than arbitrary values. This creates invisible rhythm.

**Semantic heading hierarchy (Pickering).** Use h1 for the main value proposition, h2 for each major section (problem, mechanism, proof, offer, CTA), h3 for subsections. Never skip levels for visual styling — use CSS for sizing. A screen reader user pressing "H" to cycle through headings should get a coherent summary of the page's argument. This also improves SEO and gives sighted users who scan by visual weight a reliable hierarchy to follow. A page with h1, h3, h2, h4 is like a book where chapter numbering jumps randomly.

**Line-length control (Müller-Brockmann).** Body text on long-form sales pages should be constrained to 50-65 characters per line (max-width ~640px). The reader is processing thousands of words — if the line length is too wide, fatigue compounds with every paragraph. This is the most violated readability rule in sales page design and the easiest to fix.

**Whitespace as section rhythm (Müller-Brockmann).** Whitespace between sections is not wasted real estate — it's what makes each section register as a distinct unit. Removing whitespace to fit more above the fold is like removing silence between sentences. Consistent section spacing creates reading rhythm. Inconsistent spacing — 80px here, 40px there, 120px elsewhere — creates disorder the reader feels but can't name.

**Design is an expression the page gives off independently of the copy.** A page claiming "enterprise-grade" must look enterprise-grade — typography, whitespace, load speed, and mobile experience all communicate before the visitor reads a word. A premium claim on a page with stock photos, slow loading, and broken mobile layout gives off signals that destroy the explicit message. Audit for signal consistency between what the words claim and what the design communicates.

**Typographic punctuation as credibility signal (Bringhurst).** Every instance of straight quotes (`"` `'`), hyphens used as em dashes, or three dots instead of proper ellipses on a sales page is a credibility leak — a small signal that nobody with typographic knowledge reviewed the page. On a page where every other design choice communicates "professional," amateur punctuation contradicts the signal. Use smart quotes ("" ''), proper em dashes (—), en dashes for ranges (–), and ellipsis characters (…). This is especially visible on long-form sales pages where thousands of words amplify every punctuation choice. When handing copy to a designer or dropping it into a builder, run a find-and-replace for straight quotes and double hyphens as a final quality pass.

**Layout is symbolic subtext.** The page design itself positions the product before the copy persuades. Spacious design with generous whitespace says "premium." Dense, long-form copy says "information product." Clean sans-serif typography and flat UI says "modern tool." These are not neutral aesthetic choices — they're the first argument the page makes, and the reader processes them before reading a single word.

**Color as emotional primer (Bellantoni).** The page's color palette puts the reader in an emotional state before they read a word. A page selling security needs cool, desaturated tones — the reader arrives in a controlled, trusting mindset. A page selling transformation needs warmer, more saturated tones — the reader arrives activated and open to change. A premium offer needs lower saturation and more black — the reader arrives expecting weight and substance. Match the palette to the emotional register of the offer, not the brand's default palette. The anti-pattern: applying brand colors uniformly regardless of whether the page is selling urgency, trust, transformation, or community.

**Visual progression as page shape (Block).** The page has a visual arc that should mirror its emotional arc. Top: clean, spacious, high affinity — inviting the reader in. Problem/agitation: visual contrast increases — bolder tonal range, more diagonal energy, denser layout. CTA section: peak visual contrast across all components. Post-CTA (guarantee, FAQ): return to affinity. This unifies the individual tools (Bellantoni's color shift, Albers's afterimage, Arnheim's directional cues) into one diagnostic: does the page's visual shape mirror its persuasion shape?

**Spatial intensity audit (Tufte).** Scroll the page at reading speed without reading any text. The visual intensity arc — contrast, density, tonal weight — should match the psychological sequence: problem section spikes contrast, mechanism section moderates, proof section holds medium density, offer section spikes again, CTA hits maximum contrast. If the page looks visually flat during the scroll, the emotional arc is invisible and the reader has no visual momentum toward the CTA.

**Brightness audit (Alton).** The brightest element in any viewport should be the most important element. Scroll the page and identify the brightest point in each viewport. If it's a hero image instead of the headline, a decorative gradient instead of the CTA, or a testimonial photo instead of the result number — the brightness hierarchy is misaligned with the persuasion hierarchy. This is the fastest visual hierarchy audit: what's brightest? Is that what matters most?

**Lighting genre shifts between sections (Alton).** Problem/agitation sections benefit from darker, more constrained visual treatment — the visual weight mirrors the emotional weight. Solution and proof sections shift to brighter, more open treatment — the relief is visual before it's verbal. The CTA section should be the brightest, highest-contrast zone on the entire page. These shifts mirror the page's emotional arc and give the reader visual cues about what kind of content they're in before they read a word. This unifies Bellantoni's color shift, Block's visual progression, and Tufte's spatial intensity into a single principle: the page's lighting should change genre as its argument changes mode.

**Visual pattern interrupts against scroll fatigue (Cannell & Travis).** Long sales pages need a deliberate pattern break every 2-3 sections — a background color shift, a full-width image, a testimonial callout in a different layout, a pulled quote at large scale. Monotonous section formatting trains the reader to skim on autopilot. Each pattern interrupt re-engages active reading by signaling "something changed." The interrupt itself doesn't need to carry new information — its job is to reset attention so the next section gets read instead of scrolled past.

**Spatial type for hero images (Block).** Hero images with deep space — perspective lines, horizons, roads receding — suggest possibility, journey, and forward movement, ideal for transformation and aspiration offers. Flat, graphic hero treatments — product centered, clean background, frontal composition — suggest authority, clarity, and utility, ideal for SaaS and tool offers. The spatial treatment of the hero sets the visitor's emotional register before copy begins. The anti-pattern: choosing a hero image for its subject matter without considering whether its spatial depth matches the offer's emotional promise.

**Directional cues in photography (Arnheim).** When using photography with people, ensure faces and body language point toward the headline or CTA, not away from it. The eye follows gaze direction — a person looking left sends the reader's eye off the page; a person looking right (toward the CTA) sends it toward conversion. This applies to all photography on the page: testimonial photos, founder photos, lifestyle images. If the available photo points the wrong way, mirror it (if ethical/possible) or crop to redirect the implied vector. Similarly, any compositional lines (diagonal edges, perspective lines, arm gestures) should resolve at the CTA or key message, not at the margins. The anti-pattern is stock photography where every person stares directly at the camera — this stops eye movement dead rather than directing it.

Include layout direction in your output — section hierarchy, contrast notes, CTA placement, mobile considerations. A designer should know what you intended without guessing.

**Form state transitions (Head).** The most important animation on a sales page is the form submission flow — empty, filling, submitting (loading), success, and possibly error. Each transition is a moment of user anxiety. A form that snaps from "submitting" to "success" with no transition feels like something might have gone wrong. A smooth sequence — button becomes spinner, spinner becomes checkmark, page shifts to confirmation — reduces perceived wait time and builds trust. Design the transition, not just the states.

**Accessible form error handling (Horton & Quesenbery).** A landing page form that clears all fields on a validation error is a conversion killer. Errors must name the specific field, explain what's wrong in plain language ("Emails need an @ and a domain" not "Invalid input"), and preserve all valid input. Required fields must be identified before the form, not just marked with a red asterisk that colorblind users can't see. The form must work with keyboard-only navigation — tab through every field, trigger validation, correct errors, and submit without touching a mouse. Every form friction point that frustrates an accessibility-impaired user frustrates every user; the accessible form is the higher-converting form.

**Conflict per section (McKee: Dialogue).** Each section of the sales page should carry tension between the reader's current state and the page's argument. Problem sections carry pain vs. hope. Mechanism sections carry old way vs. new way. Proof sections carry skepticism vs. evidence. Offer sections carry desire vs. objection. A section without conflict is a section the reader skims. If a section presents information without tension — stating facts without connecting them to the reader's internal debate — it's static regardless of how well-written it is.

### 8. The Closing Sequence

*Sources: Fladlien (One to Many), Collier (Robert Collier Letter Book), Clemens (Persuasion Story Code), Goffman (Presentation of Self)*

The final push follows a specific order. Don't rearrange it — each element primes the next. The vision of both futures — acting vs. not acting — must land as felt experience before any pricing table, feature grid, or guarantee is presented. The sequence is: emotion first, ratification second, because a reader who hasn't felt the stakes doesn't evaluate the offer, they compare prices (Camp). **Peak-end awareness (Kahneman).** The reader judges the entire page by two moments: the peak (most intense emotional point) and the end. A page that's mediocre for 80% but electrifying at the close is remembered as "incredible." Front-loading quality and fizzling at the end is exactly backward. The final CTA section must be the strongest writing on the page — not a wind-down, not a summary, not a formality. If the closing sequence reads like an afterthought, the reader's memory of the entire page degrades. Allocate disproportionate craft to the close.

1. **Recap the transformation.** What changes in their life. Not what they get — what's different. Paint the after state in filmable detail.
2. **Recap the value stack (stack-slide structure — Brunson).** Build value component by component before revealing price. Name each component, anchor its individual value, then show the running total. The gap between stacked value and actual price is what makes the offer feel irresistible — the reader should feel the math is absurdly in their favor before the price ever appears. This reminds them of everything included — most readers skimmed the first time. **Anchor-first rule (Kahneman).** Every number on the page — price, time saved, results achieved — must be preceded by a larger reference number. "$1,000" in isolation gets compared to vacations and laptops. "$10,500 of value for $1,000" gets compared to $10,500. State the bigger, worse, or harder number FIRST. The second number is always evaluated relative to the first, and you lose control of that comparison the moment you present a number without setting the anchor above it. When listing bonuses, call free items "free" explicitly — not "$0," not "included," not "complimentary" (Ariely). FREE is an emotional trigger that eliminates loss calculus entirely, not merely a price point at the bottom of a scale. The difference between free and one cent is not one cent — it's a categorical shift from "evaluating a transaction" to "receiving a gift." A bonus labeled "$0" still activates the buyer's cost-benefit machinery; a bonus labeled "FREE" bypasses it completely. The buyer who sees "FREE strategy template" feels they are receiving something with zero downside. The buyer who sees "included strategy template" processes it as part of the bundle they're paying for. Use the word itself — FREE — as a label on individual bonus items in the stack to trigger the emotional response, not as a price descriptor.
3. **Reveal the price** (or re-state it). Against the total stack value. The contrast makes the price feel reasonable. **Anchor sequence matters (Poundstone):** stack the total value first, then name the cost of the problem (what inaction costs per month/quarter), then reveal the price — the reader processes it against both anchors, not in isolation. Because buyers have no stable internal reference for what most offers should cost, the anchor doesn't need to be the "right" number — it needs to be any number that arrives before the price (Ariely). Arbitrary coherence means the first number in the sequence governs evaluation regardless of its source or logical relationship to the offer. A case study ROI figure, a cost-of-problem calculation, a competitor's price mentioned in passing — whichever number the reader encounters first becomes the reference point against which the price is judged. This means the anchor sequence starts before the offer section: the problem section's "$48,000 in wasted labor" or the proof section's "$200K in pipeline generated" are already doing anchor work. The price reveal on the sales page is the last number in a chain that began the moment the reader saw their first dollar figure on the page. Audit every number on the page in scroll order — each one either reinforces the anchor you want or sets a competing reference that undermines it. Use the left-digit effect on displayed numbers: $997 reads as "nine hundred" while $1,000 reads as "a thousand" — the leftmost digit dominates perception regardless of the actual difference. Frame the price contextually ("less than your team spends on unused software licenses each quarter") to move evaluation away from the absolute number. **Price as identity signal, not value justification (Godin).** "At just $497, you get..." is a defensive posture — it frames the price as a hurdle and the copy as an apology. "Built for founders who invest in themselves" frames the price as a statement about who the buyer is. The price should tell a story about the buyer's identity: "This is what serious operators pay" or "Priced for teams that have outgrown free tools and aren't going back." Value justification invites the reader to do math. Identity framing invites the reader to decide who they are. The reader who buys because "people like me invest in this" stays longer and complains less than the reader who buys because the math worked out.
4. **Present the guarantee.** Conditional > unconditional. Placed right after the price — the price creates anxiety, the guarantee resolves it. Give the guarantee a name. **Outcome-specific guarantees convert better than generic money-back (Abraham).** "30-day money-back guarantee" is the minimum — it removes risk but creates no desire. "If you don't generate 10 qualified leads in 30 days, we refund every penny" is outcome-specific — it removes risk AND makes a promise. The bolder the guarantee, the fewer the refunds, because specificity signals that the seller has done the math and knows the product delivers. A guarantee that names the exact outcome the buyer wants tells the prospect "we're so confident this works that we'll bet on it." The spectrum: unconditional money-back (weakest — just table stakes), conditional time-based ("use it for 60 days"), outcome-specific ("hit X result or get your money back"), and outcome-plus ("hit X result or we refund double / keep working free"). Move as far up the spectrum as the product's track record supports. A guarantee the prospect has never seen before becomes a selling point in itself — it gets retold, shared, and remembered. **Frame the guarantee as risk asymmetry against the current state.** The guarantee isn't just "your money back" -- it's a decision frame. The prospect is weighing two risks: the risk of changing (buying and being disappointed) vs. the risk of not changing (another 6 or 12 months of the current state impact you diagnosed in the problem section). Make the asymmetry explicit: "The worst case if you try this is you get your money back and lose nothing. The worst case if you don't is another 12 months of [specific current state impact from the problem section -- leaked pipeline, guessed forecasts, Sunday night dread]." This reframes the guarantee from a safety net into a decision-forcing comparison. The reader stops evaluating "is this worth the risk?" and starts evaluating "which risk is worse?" -- and when the downside of inaction is vivid and accumulating while the downside of action is a refund, the math closes itself. Position the guarantee against the current state, not against competitors. The competitor comparison triggers feature evaluation. The current-state comparison triggers escape motivation.
5. **The plan — what happens after they click (Miller).** Between the guarantee and the two futures, give the reader a clear 3-4 step plan that names exactly what happens next. "Step 1: Book a 15-minute call. Step 2: We build your pipeline system together. Step 3: Start signing clients within 30 days." Heroes don't act without a plan — if the path forward is unclear, they freeze. The plan reduces anxiety by making the post-click experience concrete and predictable. Keep it to 3 steps (4 maximum) — more than that reintroduces complexity. Each step should be one sentence. The plan is not the mechanism (that explains why it works) — it's the roadmap that shows the reader the immediate next actions so the CTA feels like a known path, not a leap into the unknown.
6. **Paint two futures — the failure-success contrast (Miller).** Before the final CTA, paint the two futures: what happens if they don't act (honest consequences, not fear-mongering), then what happens if they do (specific, visualizable success). The contrast makes the CTA obvious — the reader sees both paths and choosing inaction becomes the harder decision. The failure future should be consequences they already suspect, not manufactured catastrophe: "You're still running outreach from that spreadsheet, still guessing which leads are real, still dreading the Monday standup" is honest. "Your business will collapse" is manipulation. The success future should be filmable and specific: "You open your laptop Monday morning, the pipeline report is already built, and you know exactly which three deals to push this week." **Lead with the "don't act" future (Poundstone):** loss aversion means the pain of losing is roughly twice the pleasure of gaining — so the scene where nothing changes hits harder than the scene where everything improves. Paint the inaction future first, with enough sensory detail that the reader flinches, then follow with the action future as relief. The reader who feels the loss first experiences the gain as rescue, not just improvement. **Calibrate the idealization:** the "after" state must be the realistic best case, not a fantasy. If the gap between the painted future and the actual delivered experience is too large, the customer feels deceived and becomes hostile. Overpromise here and no amount of delivery quality repairs the trust. The two-futures section is the privileged moment for the final CTA — it engineers desire and loss aversion simultaneously. The CTA must follow immediately, while the reader is holding both futures in mind.
7. **Direct CTA.** Exactly what to click. Exactly what happens next. "Click the button below. You'll fill out a 2-minute form. We'll send your login within 5 minutes." No ambiguity. The final copy before the button should set one specific, vivid expectation about the immediate post-purchase experience — "you'll receive your login within 5 minutes, and by day 3 you'll have your first pipeline report built" (Ariely). Expectations set on the sales page shape actual experience: a buyer who expects to see results by day 3 perceives early progress more favorably, engages more actively, and reports higher satisfaction than a buyer who purchased with vague expectations and discovered the same results on their own timeline. This isn't overpromising — it's calibrating the lens through which the buyer interprets their experience. The specificity of the expectation matters: "you'll love it" sets no expectation. "By Friday you'll have sent your first outreach batch and seen your first replies" sets a concrete mental image the buyer carries into the product, and that image becomes the standard against which they measure satisfaction and eventually write testimonials. Set the expectation honestly, set it vividly, and set it close — the nearer the expected payoff, the stronger the effect on perceived experience. The language of the CTA itself is priming — "Join 2,400 teams" primes belonging, "Start your free trial" primes low-risk exploration, "Get instant access" primes immediacy. Match the CTA verb to the psychological state the preceding section created. **Silence after the ask (Blount).** Qualifiers, reassurance, and risk-reversal go BEFORE the button — not after it. Everything placed after the CTA weakens it: a reassurance paragraph below the button tells the reader you're not confident in the ask, and a qualifier after the click invitation reopens the decision loop you just closed. The CTA should be the last thing the reader processes before deciding — let the button sit in silence. If you feel the urge to add "no risk, cancel anytime" after the CTA, move it above. Every CTA must show what's on the other side before the reader crosses it. Not urgency, not loss -- what specific thing happens in the next 60 seconds. "Click below and you'll land on a 2-minute form. Within 5 minutes, you'll have login access." The threshold reveals the room; the reader decides whether to enter (Bachelard).
**Multifunctioning element principle (Tufte).** Every CTA phrase should carry two meanings simultaneously — the action and the benefit, fused in the same words. "Get the Templates" = label (what you're clicking for) + promise (what you're walking away with). "Start Free" = action (begin now) + risk removal (no cost). "Join 2,400 Teams" = action (join) + social proof (you won't be alone). A CTA that names only the action ("Click Here," "Submit") delivers zero benefit. A CTA that names only the benefit without an action verb ("Better Pipeline") delivers zero direction. The strongest CTAs collapse both into one phrase where neither can be removed without destroying the other. Before finalizing any CTA, ask: does this phrase do two jobs? If you can strip one meaning and the phrase still makes sense, it's not fully loaded. If removing either meaning breaks the phrase, it's right.

**Audit the decision interface separately from the argument (Barden).** The CTA section is where the autopilot's signals, the frame, and the net value converge. Three questions for the CTA: (1) What signal distinguishes this action from alternatives at this exact moment? (2) What makes the reward tangible, immediate, and certain right now? (3) What makes the cost feel smaller than the reward? Reduce choices to the minimum — the autopilot handles binary well ('buy or don't'). Multiple CTAs, plan comparisons, and feature matrices activate the conscious mind, whose default is to defer. The decision interface is a design problem, not a copy problem.

8. **Final urgency with reason.** Not "limited time!" — "Enrollment closes Friday because the cohort starts Monday" or "This rate is locked for the next 48 hours — after that, it goes to $X."

**Uniqueness as late tiebreaker (D'Souza).** After proof, guarantee, and two futures have done their work, one genuine differentiator positioned late acts as the tiebreaker for prospects still weighing alternatives. This isn't the mechanism (which arrived earlier) — it's the single concrete thing no competitor offers, stated simply: "We're the only platform that includes live pipeline reviews with a strategist every month." Placed after the reader already believes and feels safe, the uniqueness point resolves the final "but why this one specifically?" without competing with the emotional arc. Earlier in the page it would read as a feature; here it reads as the reason.

**Multi-stakeholder module for B2B and high-ticket (Rackham).** The primary visitor is rarely the only decision-maker. A CFO, partner, board member, or spouse has veto power — and the primary visitor has to sell them using whatever they remember from your page, which is almost nothing. Give them a tool. A "Share with your team" or "What your [CFO/partner/board] needs to know" section that addresses the secondary stakeholder's concerns directly: risk mitigation, ROI timeline, implementation effort, switching cost, and what happens if it doesn't work. This is not the main pitch compressed — it's a different argument for a different person with different objections. The primary visitor buys on transformation and identity. The secondary stakeholder buys on risk and ROI. A one-page summary, a shareable link to an executive brief, or a dedicated section that reads as if it were written for the person who wasn't on the page — any of these gives the primary visitor ammunition for the internal conversation that actually closes the deal. Without this, the primary visitor becomes an unprepared salesperson pitching from memory to a skeptic. Place this after the offer stack and guarantee, where the primary visitor is already sold and the remaining barrier is "how do I get approval?"

**Callbacks for structural closure (Vorhaus).** Reference the opening hook, story, or metaphor in the CTA section to tie the page together. If the hero section opened with "the invisible pipeline leak," the close should callback: "Patch the leak. Click below." The callback rewards readers who stayed through the full page — it creates the sense that nothing was wasted, the whole arc was intentional. Readers feel closure, not just a pitch. **Circle-back close (Spence).** The CTA section should echo the opening headline and problem language so the close feels like narrative completion rather than a sales pivot. Introduce zero new information in the close — every word should reference what the reader already encountered on the page. New claims or new framing at the bottom reopen the evaluation loop the page spent its entire length closing; the circle-back keeps that loop shut by returning the reader to where they started, now carrying the proof and mechanism that make the original problem feel solvable.

### 9. The LIFT Diagnostic

*Sources: Goward (You Should Test That), Blanks-Jesson (Making Websites Win), Groopman (How Doctors Think)*

When a page isn't converting — or when reviewing your own output — diagnose WHICH factor is broken before rewriting copy:

| Factor | Question | If broken, fix by... |
|--------|---------|---------------------|
| Value Proposition | Is what I'm offering worth their action? | Strengthening the offer (not the copy) — bigger outcome, better guarantee, lower perceived risk |
| Relevance | Does this match what brought them here? | Matching the message to the traffic source — if the ad promised X, the page must deliver X |
| Clarity | Can they understand the offer in 5 seconds? | Simplifying — cut words, enlarge headlines, reduce options. Don't add; subtract. |
| Urgency | Is there a reason to act now vs. next month? | Adding a real deadline or articulating the cost of inaction in specific terms |
| Anxiety | What are they afraid of? | Addressing specific fears near the CTA — guarantee, testimonials, "what happens after I click" |
| Distraction | What's competing for attention on this page? | Removing elements that don't serve the primary conversion action |

**The persuasion equation** (Blanks-Jesson): Evidence (benefits + proof) must exceed Total Perceived Cost (money + time + risk + effort). When a page doesn't convert, most people try to reduce the price. The real fix is usually adding evidence — more proof, more specific benefits, more demonstrated value. Fix the numerator before touching the denominator.

**Optimize before rewriting (Abraham).** When a page underperforms, the instinct is to scrap it and start fresh. Resist. Test variables on the existing page first — headline, offer, guarantee, CTA, proof placement, opening approach. A page that converts at 1.2% might convert at 3.8% with a different headline and a stronger guarantee, which is faster and cheaper than a full rebuild. The existing page contains data about what's working; a new page starts from zero. Change one variable, measure, change the next. Only rebuild when systematic testing proves the architecture itself is broken — not when the first draft didn't hit target. Most "the page isn't working" problems are one or two variable problems, not structural ones.

**Macro before micro** (Goward): Don't A/B test button colors on a page with a weak value proposition. Fix the biggest factor first. If the offer itself isn't compelling, no amount of copy optimization will save it. **Check tension structure before rewriting headlines (O'Bannon).** If the page isn't converting, diagnose the tension arc before touching the copy. Most conversion problems are structural — sections in the wrong order, two agitation sections without release between them, tension resolved too early, or no open question carrying the reader past a section break. A headline rewrite on a page with broken tension structure is cosmetic surgery on a broken skeleton.

**Resist satisficing on the first diagnosis.** When a page isn't converting, the instinct is to identify the first plausible factor — "it's a clarity problem" — and start rewriting headlines. But the real issue might be relevance (the page is perfectly clear about something the visitor doesn't care about) or anxiety operating further downstream. Generate at least two competing diagnoses before committing to a fix.

**Scroll depth as retention curve (Cannell & Travis).** When a page underperforms, pull scroll heatmaps and treat every dip as a structural problem in the section ABOVE it, not a quality problem in the section at the dip. A drop at the mechanism section means the problem section failed to create enough tension to carry the reader forward. A drop at the offer means proof didn't land. Diagnose upstream, not at the point of exit.

### 10. Four Dimensions of Copy

*Sources: Garfinkel (Breakthrough Copywriting), Whitman (Cashvertising), Aristotle (Rhetoric), Feynman (Surely You're Joking), Seger (You Talkin' to Me? — voice consistency; Writing Subtext — multi-level layering)*

Great sales page copy operates on four simultaneous levels. Most pages nail one or two and miss the rest:

- **Logical:** Facts, features, ROI, comparison, specifications. The ammunition for the rational mind — the "press secretary" who justifies decisions already made emotionally. Run the credibility filter on every claim (Luntz): audit for superlatives — "best," "leading," "world-class," "unmatched" — and replace each one with a specific number, timeframe, or outcome. Superlatives trigger the skepticism filter; specifics bypass it. "Industry-leading results" is a superlative the reader deletes on contact. "127 clients, average 34% pipeline increase in 60 days" is a specific the reader stores as evidence.
- **Emotional:** Desire, fear, frustration, hope, relief, pride. The triggers that actually drive the decision — the "president" who makes the call. Life Force 8 (Whitman): survival, enjoyment of life, freedom from fear, social approval, care for loved ones, being superior, protecting loved ones, sexual companionship.
- **Sensory:** Makes the reader see, hear, feel the experience. The camera test: is this specific enough that a camera could film it? "Frustrated" = abstract. "Staring at 47 open tabs at 10pm while your family eats dinner without you" = filmable. Sensory detail makes emotional claims believable. Use named figures of speech as structural copy tools (Heinrichs): **tricolon** for benefit lists ("Fewer calls. Bigger deals. Less effort."), **antithesis** for differentiation sections and before/after contrasts ("Not more leads — better conversations."), **anaphora** for emotional emphasis and value repetition ("Every lead tracked. Every follow-up timed. Every deal visible."). These figures give copy spoken rhythm that flat prose lacks — the reader's inner voice speeds up on a tricolon and pauses on an antithesis.
- **Identity:** Who they get to become. Not what the product does — who they are after using it. "You're the person who always knows the answer before the meeting." Identity is the deepest motivator — people buy what confirms or creates who they want to be. Fladlien's insight: most prospects carry an "impoverished identity" — they see themselves as someone who can't do this, who always struggles, who isn't the type. The page must challenge that identity and replace it. The problem section should name the old identity ("you've been the person who..."), the mechanism should crack it ("it's not you — it's the approach"), and the offer should present the new identity ("this is what people who [aspirational identity] use"). **Psycho-logic puts identity first (Sutherland).** The real decision is "who does buying this make me?" not "what features do I get?" A prospect choosing between two project management tools is not comparing feature lists — they are deciding whether they are the kind of person who uses Notion or the kind of person who uses a spreadsheet. Lead with identity, close with logic. The identity dimension should surface first in the hero (who is this for?), carry through the mechanism (what kind of person uses this approach?), and anchor the close (who do you become?). Logic — features, ROI, specifications — arrives last as the rational alibi for the identity decision already made. **Unity beats authority because insiders are trusted without evaluation (Cialdini).** Unity is not similarity — it is shared identity. "We" language that positions the brand as part of the reader's group, not outside it looking in. "We got tired of the same broken playbook" makes the brand an insider who shares the reader's frustration. "You're tired of the same broken playbook" makes the brand an observer describing the reader's life from the outside. Shared enemy framing ("the industry sells you complexity because complexity justifies their retainer"), origin stories that start from the reader's position ("I was running the same campaigns, getting the same flat results"), and in-group language ("if you've been in the trenches long enough, you already know") all trigger unity before the message begins. Authority requires the reader to evaluate credentials and decide whether to trust. Unity bypasses that evaluation entirely — insiders are trusted by default. Build unity in the hero and problem sections through shared-enemy framing and "we" positioning. Let authority emerge later through proof. The reader who feels "this person is one of us" doesn't need to be convinced to listen.

**Three-channel check per section (Belfort).** Every major section should hit at least two of three channels: logical (facts, data, how-it-works), emotional (desire, fear, relief, identity), and state transfer (the writer's confidence bleeding through the copy so the reader absorbs certainty). A proof section that presents stats (logical) but reads with hedging qualifiers ("results may vary," "in some cases") fails the state channel — the data is right but the reader doesn't *feel* confident. A problem section that's emotionally vivid but offers no logical grounding feels manipulative. After drafting each section, tag which channels it hits. Flag any section that only hits one — it's structurally incomplete.

**Layer all dimensions simultaneously.** Every section should carry literal value, emotional resonance, and identity work at once — not three separate sections for each. A mechanism paragraph that explains how it works (logical), names the relief of finally having an answer (emotional), and positions the reader as someone smart enough to recognize the better approach (identity) does in one paragraph what three siloed sections fail to do. The dimensions aren't courses in a meal — they're ingredients in every bite.

**Sequence:** Emotional first (the president decides), then logical (the press secretary justifies). Trust before emotion (ethos before pathos). Logic last, as the rational alibi for an emotional decision.

**Conscious framing on every key fact (Kahneman).** Every number, statistic, and comparison on the page should be consciously framed — never presented neutrally. "95% success rate" and "5% failure rate" are the same fact with different emotional weight. "4 out of 5 clients hit their target" and "1 in 5 clients missed" tell the same story to different effect. Neutral framing is a missed opportunity. Frame positively for the product and its results, negatively for the problem and the competition. Audit the page for any number or claim that sits in default framing — it should be reframed to serve the argument. This applies to time ("12 minutes" vs. "under a quarter hour"), money ("$2.16/day" vs. "$780/year"), and results ("3x pipeline growth" vs. "$180K to $540K").

**The page is deliberative — argue advantage, not truth.** The buyer is making a future-oriented decision. Every claim should be framed as advantage to them, not truth about the product. "Our algorithm processes 10M data points" is truth about the product. "You'll know which campaign to kill before it wastes another dollar" is advantage to them. Case studies and testimonials serve as past evidence but must bridge explicitly to future advantage — "she got this result, and here's why your situation is similar."

**Recipient-first test (Godin).** Scan the page for "we" vs. "you" ratio. If the company appears before the customer in more than 20% of sentences, the page is sender-first. "We built a proprietary engine that..." is the company talking about itself. "You see exactly which leads are ready..." is the page serving the reader. Rewrite sender-first sentences from the reader's perspective. The exception is unity language ("we" meaning the brand and reader together against a shared villain) — that's insider framing, not sender-first copy. The test catches the subtler failure: sections that technically describe benefits but lead with the company as subject, which makes the reader process the brand's story instead of their own.

**The representation test:** Express the value proposition as a sentence, a scene someone could film, an analogy to something the reader already knows, and a single number. If any of those four is impossible, the understanding is incomplete and the copy will be abstract. This test forces the Four Dimensions to work simultaneously.

**Voice consistency IS character consistency.** Read the problem section, then immediately read the close. If the problem section sounds warm and empathetic and the close sounds aggressive and pushy, two different people wrote the page. A sales page is one character speaking — the register can shift (softer in problem, more direct in close), but the underlying voice must be recognizable throughout. Inconsistent voice breaks trust the same way an inconsistent character breaks a story.

**The camera test applied to every section:** Read each paragraph and ask: could a camera film this? If not, you're writing in abstractions. Replace "better results" with "closing 3 deals per week instead of 1." Replace "more confidence" with "walking into the board meeting with the exact number they're going to ask for." Apply the picture test at the verb level (Luntz): audit body copy for abstract verbs — optimize, leverage, streamline, facilitate, utilize — and replace each one with a visual verb the reader can see: sharpen, plug, stack, cut, build. Abstract verbs signal business writing; visual verbs create mental images. A sentence the reader can picture moves them forward. A sentence they have to decode stalls them.

## Structural Metaphor as Page Skeleton

Strongest sales pages build entire argument on one structural metaphor that organizes all sections. The metaphor provides narrative structure the reader already understands, making section flow feel inevitable rather than imposed.

**SOLUTION IS BLUEPRINT** gives you: What's broken (foundation cracks), The plan (blueprint), What you build with (materials), Final result (move-in day). The reader knows blueprint logic — you don't need to convince them that blueprints come before construction or that materials matter. The metaphor supplies that reasoning.

**SOLUTION IS JOURNEY** gives you: Where you are (lost/stuck), The discovery (hidden route), The path (turn-by-turn), Arrival (destination while others still stuck). Journey logic is hardwired: paths have destinations, shortcuts save time, some routes are better than others.

**SOLUTION IS RESCUE** gives you: You're trapped (problem), Others escaped (proof), The escape route (mechanism), You're free (transformation). Rescue entails: urgency, a way out exists, time matters, staying is dangerous.

The structural metaphor isn't decoration — it's the skeleton the entire page hangs on. When you write "you're stuck in traffic" in the hero, you've committed to SOLUTION IS SHORTCUT. The reader now expects: a faster route, proof others took it, the turns, arrival before the traffic clears. If you shift to war metaphors (tactics, domination) or building metaphors (foundation, construction) mid-page, you break the structure the reader's brain built.

Before writing the page, name the structural metaphor. Then trace its entailments: what does this metaphor make the reader expect? What counts as valid proof within this metaphor? What objections does it preempt? Let the metaphor organize your sections — don't fight it.

## Entailment-Based Logic Flow

The metaphor you choose in the headline commits you to specific logic throughout the body. Metaphors aren't interchangeable decoration — they're reasoning systems. Choose one, you inherit its entailments.

"Roadmap to $10k months" (JOURNEY) entails: where you are, where you're going, the route, obstacles along the way, arrival. The reader expects navigation language. Shifting to "install this system" (MACHINE) mid-page breaks the entailment chain — were they traveling or assembling? Mixed signals kill trust.

"Blueprint for profitable funnels" (BUILDING) entails: what's broken in the foundation, the architectural plan, materials needed, construction sequence, final structure. The reader expects building language. Throwing in "fuel your growth" (MACHINE/ENGINE) creates cognitive friction — are they building or driving?

Trace the headline metaphor's entailments before writing the body:
- What problem does this metaphor make visible? (JOURNEY: lost/stuck. BUILDING: cracked foundation. MACHINE: broken parts.)
- What solution does it make logical? (JOURNEY: map/guide. BUILDING: blueprint/materials. MACHINE: repair/upgrade.)
- What proof does it expect? (JOURNEY: others who arrived. BUILDING: before/after structures. MACHINE: performance metrics.)
- What objections does it preempt? (JOURNEY: "I don't know the way." BUILDING: "I don't have plans." MACHINE: "Mine's broken.")

The page should feel like a single, coherent argument — not a list of disconnected claims. Entailment-based logic flow is how you achieve that. Each section follows logically from the metaphor established in the headline.

## Competitive Reframing Through Metaphor

Competitors using BUSINESS IS WAR ("dominate your market," "crush the competition," "win at all costs") can be reframed with BUSINESS IS GAME ("play to your strengths," "change the rules," "find your unfair advantage"). The game metaphor makes war feel exhausting without attacking competitors directly.

Different metaphor = different evaluation criteria.

War metaphor highlights: aggression, zero-sum, elimination, territory. Buyers evaluating through this lens ask: "Can I beat them? Am I tough enough? What if I lose?"

Game metaphor highlights: strategy, rules you can bend, multiple ways to win, playing well vs. playing hard. Buyers evaluating through this lens ask: "What's my unique move? Can I change the rules? How do I find my advantage?"

When you reframe the category through a different metaphor, you're not arguing "we're better" — you're arguing "this is a different type of contest entirely." That's more powerful than direct comparison because it makes their frame feel obsolete.

**Find the category's dominant metaphor.** Look at competitor headlines, product descriptions, landing pages. What metaphor system do they all use? (Often: WAR, RACE, BUILDING, MACHINE.)

**Choose an alternative that highlights your strengths.** If you're slower but more thoughtful, don't compete in RACE metaphor. Reframe with CULTIVATION metaphor (BUSINESS IS GARDEN) — highlights patience, long-term thinking, compounding, nurture. Suddenly their speed feels reckless.

**Use the new metaphor consistently across the page.** Don't mention competitors. Just use your metaphor system so consistently that their frame starts to feel like the wrong lens. When a reader comes from a competitor's site (WAR metaphor) to yours (GARDEN metaphor), they feel the contrast viscerally. One feels like a battle. The other feels like growth.

## Page-Wide Coherence Audit

A page fails when problem (CAGE metaphors), mechanism (JOURNEY metaphors), and offer (BUILDING metaphors) use incompatible frames. Each section individually strong but together creating cognitive friction the reader feels as "something's off" without being able to name it.

**Run a metaphor audit before finalizing the page:**

1. **Tag every metaphor in the page.** Go through headline, problem section, mechanism, proof, offer, CTA. Underline or highlight every metaphorical phrase. Don't skip dead metaphors ("unlock," "fuel," "accelerate") — they're still structuring thought even if they feel invisible.

2. **Identify the source domain for each metaphor.** CAGE/TRAP (locked, stuck, confined). JOURNEY (path, roadmap, destination). BUILDING (foundation, blueprint, construct). MACHINE (fuel, drive, optimize). WAR (tactics, dominate, weapon). GARDEN (cultivate, nurture, grow).

3. **Group by source domain.** How many times does JOURNEY appear? How many times does MACHINE? If the page uses 8 JOURNEY metaphors, 6 MACHINE metaphors, 4 BUILDING metaphors, and 3 WAR metaphors, you have metaphor soup. The reader's brain is building four different frames and none of them cohere.

4. **Simplify to 1-2 compatible systems.** Pick the dominant metaphor (or the one that best supports your positioning). Rewrite sections to align. If you're going with JOURNEY, replace "fuel your growth" (MACHINE) with "accelerate your progress" (JOURNEY). Replace "build your pipeline" (BUILDING) with "map your route to $10k" (JOURNEY).

Compatible metaphor systems share logic. JOURNEY + BUILDING can work if the journey is about constructing something along the way. MACHINE + FUEL works because fuel powers machines. But JOURNEY + WAR creates friction — is the reader traveling or fighting?

**Coherence is felt, not analyzed.** Readers don't consciously notice metaphor. But they feel coherence. A page that uses one metaphor system feels clear, focused, inevitable. A page that mixes five systems feels scattered, confusing, "something's not quite right."

Run the audit. Simplify. Stick to the system. The page will feel tighter without the reader knowing why.

## Quality Check

**Three Certainties Audit (Belfort).** After the first draft, before any other quality checks, run this audit. Tag every section by which certainty it builds: product certainty (the thing works — mechanism, demos, case studies), personal trust (the person/founder behind it is credible — origin story, vulnerability, damaging admissions), or company trust (the organization is reliable — guarantee, longevity, team, social proof at scale). A section can build more than one. Flag any certainty with zero dedicated sections — that's a structural hole the reader will feel as unease without being able to name it. Most pages over-index on product certainty and under-index on personal and company trust. If personal trust has no dedicated section, add a founder story or damaging admission. If company trust has no dedicated section, add a guarantee explanation, a team credential, or a scale proof point.

**Adjective audit (Ogilvy).** After drafting, search for every instance of "amazing," "incredible," "powerful," "seamless," "game-changing," "revolutionary," or any adjective that claims quality without demonstrating it. For each one, ask: can I replace this with a specific fact? "Amazing results" becomes "47 clients averaging $23K in new pipeline within 60 days." "Powerful automation" becomes "sends 200 personalized follow-ups per day without manual input." If the fact is less compelling than the adjective it replaces, the problem isn't the copy — it's the proof. You don't have strong enough evidence to back the claim, which means the product needs work, not the page. Adjectives are the refuge of weak proof. Every one that survives the audit should be there because no fact could do the job better — and that's almost never the case.

**Category cliche audit (Sullivan).** Before delivering, name 10 industry cliches specific to this category. SaaS: "all-in-one," "cutting-edge," "world-class," "seamless integration," "end-to-end." Coaching: "transform your life," "unlock potential," "take it to the next level." Agency: "full-service," "data-driven," "results-oriented." Ban all of them. Search the draft for every instance and replace each with specific, unexpected language that could only describe THIS product for THIS audience. If a phrase could appear unchanged on a competitor's page, it's a cliche doing the work of copy — and cliches don't convert because the reader's brain has learned to skip them. The test is simple: would this sentence surprise someone who reads five competitor pages a week?

**Preeminence voice check (Abraham).** Read the page aloud and ask: does this sound like a trusted advisor who genuinely wants the best outcome for the reader, or a vendor pushing product? The preeminent page puts the reader's interest visibly first — including telling them when NOT to buy. A "this is not for you if..." section isn't just exclusion copy (Godin) — it's an honesty signal that raises trust for everyone who keeps reading, because a seller who voluntarily disqualifies buyers is a seller who doesn't need every sale. The paradox: the page that says "don't buy this if you're not ready" converts higher than the page that says "everyone needs this," because the reader who stays past the disqualification has self-selected into higher commitment. Audit the page's stance: does it inform or does it push? Does it educate the reader toward a good decision, or pressure them toward YOUR decision? If any section reads as pressure, rewrite it as guidance. The preeminent page converts by making the reader feel like buying is their own well-informed conclusion, not the page's objective.

**Value-charge diagnostic for every section (McKee: Story).** Before finalizing any section, name its opening value and closing value — what emotional or belief state the reader enters with, and what state they leave with. If the values match, the section is dead — it informed without turning. "The reader enters skeptical and leaves skeptical" means the proof section failed regardless of how strong the testimonials are. Every section must turn a value: understanding, desire, urgency, belief, trust. Consecutive sections should turn different values — three sections that all turn "skepticism → belief" feel repetitive even if the content varies.

**Controlling Idea as page-level deletion test (McKee: Story).** Write the Controlling Idea in one sentence before drafting: "[Value] is achieved when/because [cause]." Every section — headline, problem, mechanism, proof, offer, close — must connect to this sentence. A testimonial about team responsiveness doesn't belong on a page whose Controlling Idea is about positioning strategy, unless the testimonial specifically reinforces how strategic positioning delivered the result. The Controlling Idea is the deletion test: if a section doesn't serve it, the section doesn't belong.

Before delivering, verify:

1. Opening matches the traffic awareness level (not defaulting to a product pitch for problem-aware traffic)
2. Psychological sequence flows emotionally — each section earns the right to the next
3. Slippery slide intact — every element compels the next, no natural exit ramps between sections
4. Hero section passes the 5-second test (what, who, and what to do — all clear at a glance)
5. A villain is named (not just a vague problem floating in a vacuum)
6. Proof stacked with 3+ types distributed throughout (not one testimonial block at the bottom)
7. Story structure present — customer as hero, product as weapon, problem as villain
8. Mechanism passes the "That's Right" test (Voss) — does the mechanism section make the reader think "that's right, that's why nothing else worked" or just "you're right, I guess that makes sense"? The first is breakthrough — the reader feels genuinely understood and the mechanism clicks into place as the missing piece. The second is polite dismissal — technically correct but not resonant enough to drive action. If the mechanism gets "you're right," it needs more specificity, more felt truth, or a sharper connection to their lived experience.
9. Value stack presented with genuine anchors (from `/offers` output or built inline — not inflated numbers)
10. Guarantee is specific, named, and placed immediately after the price
11. Closing follows the sequence: recap → stack → price → guarantee → plan → two futures → CTA → urgency
12. CTAs are specific and benefit-driven at every placement (no "Learn More" or "Submit")
13. Visual hierarchy directions included — scan path, contrast, spacing, CTA placement, mobile notes
14. Voice matches BRIEF.md / VOICE.md throughout
15. Type-specific blueprint followed from `page-types.md`
16. Offer passes the 2-sentence test — stating it should produce a "wait, really?" reaction
17. Persuasional foreplay present — offer components referenced in mechanism/proof sections before being revealed in the stack
18. Micro-commitments distributed — 10-15 rhetorical agreement moments across the full page, not just in the problem section
19. Proof calibrated to audience trust level — heavy for cold traffic, selective for warm
20. Page spine defined and every section serves it — if a section could be removed without violating the core intention, it's off-spine
21. Three modes present — the page delivers credibility (demonstrated through specificity, not claimed through credentials), emotional movement (sequenced from problem to confidence), and logical argument (connecting problem to solution with no gaps). If any mode is missing, the page has a structural hole.
22. Hero headline passes the dinner party test — could you say it aloud to someone without it sounding like marketing jargon? If not, simplify.
23. Problem agitation preserves the prospect's dignity — the villain is the system or method, never the prospect's intelligence
24. Hero section explored — 10+ variations generated before selecting, because the first 3-4 follow the dominant category pattern
25. Proof suspense present — at least one credibility signal planted early and left unexplained until the proof section
26. POV shifts across sections — page alternates between reader-as-protagonist, peer-as-witness, and guide-as-authority rather than staying in one voice
27. Section tempo varies — dense absorptive sections alternate with short punchy sections, no monotonous rhythm
28. Objection timing calibrated — credibility objections surfaced early and answered later through accumulated proof; anxiety objections answered immediately near the CTA
29. Concept test passed — the page's core promise stated in one sentence creates "tell me more," not "what do you mean?"
30. Premise test passed — the page's controlling argument stated as cause-and-effect, and every section advances it
31. Proof orchestrated — testimonials and case studies contrast different starting positions (skeptic, expert, beginner, veteran), not five versions of the same person
32. Unity of opposites present — the problem section names why the prospect can't walk away (economics, identity, obligation), creating structural tension
33. Emotional truth present in founder stories and case studies — specificity of felt experience, not category language
34. Competitive landscape mapped — saturated claims identified and avoided, differentiation comes from an angle nobody else owns
35. Revision layers completed — structure pass (argument order), specificity pass (vague → concrete), voice pass (read aloud)
36. Plot shape identified — page architecture matches a named narrative shape (monster-slaying, transformation, or tragedy) and sections are weighted accordingly
37. Beat expectations met — if transformation, crisis is present; if monster-slaying, nightmare is heavy; if tragedy, doubling-down beat shows rational decisions leading to decline
38. Required plot elements present — if the page uses Discovery structure, verify: something hidden is named, clues accumulate before the reveal, the discovery reframes the reader's understanding, and there's a cost/implication to knowing; if Transformation, verify: clear before state, catalyst, resistance, and irreversibility
39. Plant-twice present — the offer's anchor concept is seeded casually in the problem section, referenced in proof, and paid off in the stack; reverse-engineered from the offer backward
40. Plot combination identified — long-form pages layer primary and secondary plot structures (e.g., primary Transformation + secondary Discovery), and the plots intersect rather than run parallel
41. Declining-options urgency present — cost-of-inaction shows how the fix gets harder over time, not just more expensive; delay degrades the solution, not just the situation
42. Goal/desire split present — headline addresses the stated goal (measurable, logical), body copy addresses the desire (emotional, identity-based); both present, not just one
43. Internal obstacle named — problem section names the fear, self-doubt, or identity threat beyond the external problem and systemic villain; the internal barrier that kept them stuck despite having the right information
44. Shadow-avoidance checklist applied — copy doesn't externalize blame to the prospect (Orphan shadow: victimhood), doesn't create zero-sum framing (Warrior shadow: ruthless competition), doesn't manipulate through guilt (Altruist shadow: martyrdom)
45. Archetype-stage flexibility — the page acknowledges that buyers may be at different archetype stages and includes layered messaging to serve multiple entry points (safety for Innocents, achievement for Warriors, transformation for Magicians)
46. Plot type identified and structure matched — the page's chosen plot shape (Discovery, Transformation, Quest, Underdog, etc.) has all required beats for that specific plot type
47. Privileged moments engineered — the section immediately before each CTA creates the specific psychological state the ask requires (belief, desire, or safety), not just a structural transition
48. Word-level priming audited — language in the two paragraphs before each CTA checked for associative effect (investment vs. cost, join vs. buy, unlock vs. purchase); hero images selected for emotional activation, not literal illustration
49. Unity established before authority — hero and problem sections use shared-enemy framing, "we" positioning, or in-group language to create insider trust before credentials or proof arrive
50. Friction audit completed (Dooley) — walk the page as a new visitor: land from an ad or email, scroll through every section, reach the CTA. Where does confusion arise? Where does attention drift? Where does anxiety fire without being addressed? Where are there competing actions that pull away from the primary CTA? The friction that kills conversions is the friction you can't see from the inside — navigation links that offer escape, sections that serve the brand but not the buyer, cognitive load spikes from too many options or too much text between the reader and the action
51. Loaded language present — 1-2 proprietary terms introduced in the problem section that redefine the reader's problem in your framework; once the reader uses your term, your solution is the only answer
52. Us/them labels present — villain section names an explicit insider/outsider divide the reader self-selects into by identity, not just by interest
53. Signature phrase repeated identically — the core phrase appears 3-4 times across the page in exact wording (hero, mid-page, CTA section) to move from claim to internalized belief
54. Three Certainties Audit completed — every section tagged by which certainty it builds (product, personal trust, company trust); no certainty has zero dedicated sections
55. Three-channel check completed — every major section hits at least two of three channels (logical, emotional, state transfer); single-channel sections flagged and strengthened
56. Cost-of-inaction section present — distinct from problem agitation, quantifies the accumulating cost of doing nothing to lower the action threshold
57. FAQ answers loop to certainties — each FAQ identifies which certainty it raises, builds that certainty with new proof, then re-presents the CTA
58. Multi-stakeholder module present for B2B/high-ticket — a shareable section or asset addresses the secondary decision-maker's concerns (risk, ROI, implementation) directly, not just a compressed version of the main pitch
59. Story of Self/Us/Now all present — page has a personal credibility moment (Self), shared identity language (Us), AND real urgency from real stakes (Now); not just the Us layer Claude defaults to
60. Attention-scarcity audit passed — every section creates tension, builds proof, or moves toward the CTA; page length reflects what the reader needs to see, not what the writer wants to say
61. Interest hierarchy applied (Ogilvy) — each section scored against four dimensions: stories about people beat abstract concepts, specific facts beat general claims, unexpected details beat predictable ones, demonstrations beat descriptions. Any section that scores "less interesting" on every dimension gets rewritten — you cannot bore people into buying, and a section that fails on all four dimensions is dead weight no matter how structurally correct it is
62. Adjective audit passed (Ogilvy) — every quality-claiming adjective (amazing, incredible, powerful, seamless, game-changing) either replaced with a specific fact or justified as irreplaceable; adjectives that survive only because the replacement fact is less compelling flagged as proof gaps, not copy gaps
63. Recipient-first test passed — "we" (company as subject) appears before the customer in fewer than 20% of sentences; sender-first sentences rewritten from the reader's perspective
64. 8-section anatomy verified (Garfinkel) — all 8 sections present and in order: hook, problem amplification, solution introduction, proof, offer, guarantee, urgency, close. Missing or out-of-order sections break the psychological sequence. Run this as a pre-publish gate before any other optimization
65. Load-bearing test passed (O'Bannon) — remove each section individually and ask: does the page still work? If yes, the section isn't load-bearing and should be cut or merged. Every section on a converting page should be structurally necessary — its absence should create a gap the reader feels
66. USP-first headline test passed (Abraham) — no competitor could honestly make the same hero headline claim; if they could, the headline needs more specificity or a different angle
67. Preeminence voice check passed (Abraham) — page reads as trusted advisor, not vendor; includes at least one honest disqualification that strengthens trust for those who remain
68. Guarantee is outcome-specific where possible (Abraham) — moves beyond generic money-back toward naming the exact result the buyer expects; specificity of the guarantee matches the specificity of the promise
69. Preemptive education considered (Abraham) — if the prospect is evaluating alternatives, a "how to evaluate [category]" section teaches criteria where the product excels, framed as buyer guidance rather than comparison marketing

## Revision Layers

*Source: Hunter (Screenwriting 434 — rewriting as craft)*

Don't revise everything at once. But before the individual passes, do the full-page read.

**Editing as final rewrite (Lumet).** After the full draft is complete, read the page end to end as the audience will — not as the writer who built it section by section. The material often reveals that the page is actually about something slightly different than planned. The problem section you wrote as setup turns out to be the emotional core. The mechanism section you labored over feels redundant because the proof section already made the case. The closing that was supposed to be the climax falls flat because the real peak happened two sections earlier. This isn't failure — it's the draft telling you what the page wants to be. Restructuring around what the page IS beats forcing the original plan. The brief told you what to argue; the draft shows you what you actually argued. If they differ, trust the draft's instinct over the brief's intention — then verify the restructured version still serves the spine. This read-through is not the same as the voice pass or the structure pass below. Those are surgical. This is the moment where you step back and see the whole shape, the way an editor watches the rough cut before making a single splice.

Then three separate passes, each with a single focus:

1. **Structure pass.** Is the argument in the right order? Does each section earn the right to the next? Read only the section transitions — does the page fall forward or stall between sections? Move, cut, or add sections here. Don't touch the words yet. **Beat-expectation check (Booker):** if the page uses a transformation arc, verify the central crisis is present — without it, the success feels like bragging, not earned change. If the page uses monster-slaying, the nightmare stage must feel heavy before the solution arrives — a lightweight villain makes the mechanism feel unnecessary. If the page uses tragedy framing, the "doubling down" beat (where the prospect rationalizes continuing) must be present — without it, the decline feels like bad luck instead of a pattern. When a section feels "off," diagnose which narrative beat is missing or underweight before rewriting the copy.
2. **Specificity pass.** Replace every vague claim with a concrete one. "Better results" becomes "closing 3 deals per week instead of 1." "Save time" becomes "12 minutes instead of 6 hours." Every instance of category language ("challenges," "solutions," "leverage") gets replaced with the specific thing it's hiding behind. If you can't make it specific, the claim might be empty. **Section headline audit (Garfinkel).** During this pass, read every section heading on the page. Section heads that just label — "Our Process," "Testimonials," "How It Works" — are dead spots in the scan path. Rewrite each one as a mini-headline that passes the "So What?" filter and pulls the reader forward. "Our Process" becomes "From First Call to Full Pipeline in 14 Days." "Testimonials" becomes "What Happens When the System Clicks." If a skimmer reads only the section heads, they should feel compelled to keep scrolling — not feel like they're reading a table of contents.
3. **Voice pass.** Read the entire page aloud. Where your energy drops, the reader's energy drops. Where you stumble over a sentence, the reader skips it. Where the tone shifts from warm to aggressive without earning the transition, the reader loses trust. Fix rhythm, fix register, fix the sentences that sound like copy instead of conversation.

## After Delivering

"Read the hero section first — does it match where your traffic is coming from? If visitors arrive from an ad that talks about the problem, the hero should acknowledge that problem, not jump straight to the product. Then read only the headlines and subheads — do they tell the complete story by themselves? A skimmer reading just the bold text should understand the full arc: problem → mechanism → proof → offer → action. Finally, check the proof: is it woven throughout the page or clumped in one section? Every major claim should have proof within visual range."

**Measurement handoff (Croll & Yoskovitz).** Before this page goes live, set a conversion threshold — the specific number that determines whether the page is working or needs rework. Distinguish vanity metrics from actionable ones: page views and time on page are vanity — they feel informative but don't tell you what to change. Conversion rate by awareness level (cold traffic vs. warm vs. returning), CTA click-through by section, and scroll-depth-to-CTA correlation are actionable — they tell you which section is leaking and why. Set the pass/fail line before launch so the decision to iterate or overhaul is a read, not a debate.
