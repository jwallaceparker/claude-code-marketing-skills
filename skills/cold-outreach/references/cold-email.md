# Cold Email

*The complete playbook for cold email outreach. Every email, full copy, subject lines, send timing, response handling.*

Sources: Ross/Tyler (Predictable Revenue), Blount (Fanatical Prospecting; Objections — objection types, Ledge-Disrupt-Ask), Hughes (Combo Prospecting), Dixon/Adamson (Challenger Sale), Collier (Robert Collier Letter Book), Schwartz (Breakthrough Advertising), Sugarman (Adweek Copywriting), Klaff (Pitch Anything), Fladlien (One to Many — reframes), Voss (Never Split the Difference — labeling, accusation audit, calibrated questions), Rackham (SPIN Selling — implication questions), Cialdini (Pre-Suasion — privileged moment, priming), Carnegie (How to Win Friends — genuine interest, appreciation-first), Kennedy (Ultimate Sales Letter — internal dialogue, damaging admission), Moesta (Demand-Side Sales 101 — struggling moment, four forces)

See SKILL.md for core outreach principles that apply across all channels. This playbook adds cold email-specific structure, mechanics, and patterns.

## Cold Email Intake

Before writing, gather these details from the user. Use `AskUserQuestion` or conversational follow-up — don't dump all questions at once.

**Required:**
- Who is the ICP? (title, company size, industry, stage)
- What's the offer or ask? (service, product, meeting, intro, demo)
- What proof or results can we reference? (case studies, metrics, named clients)
- Who is the sender? (name, title, company — for the signature and voice)

**If available:**
- What triggers should we target? (funding, hiring, product launch, market shift, competitor move)
- Known objections specific to this audience (check BRIEF.md Objections section)
- Any existing relationship, warm connection, or referral source?
- What CRM/tool? (for merge tag formatting: `{{first_name}}`, `{first_name}`, etc.)
- What's the ultimate conversion goal? (book a call, start a trial, schedule a demo)

If the user doesn't have all of this, draft with placeholders marked `[PLACEHOLDER: ...]` and note what's missing.

## Anatomy of a Cold Email

Five components. Every cold email has exactly these, in this order.

### 1. Subject Line

3-7 words. The subject line's only job is to get the email opened — not to summarize the email, not to pitch, not to be clever. It should feel like it came from a person, not a marketing platform.

**What works:**
- **Curiosity:** "quick question about [their specific thing]"
- **Direct relevance:** "[their company] + [topic]"
- **Referral:** "[mutual connection] suggested I reach out"
- **Pattern interrupt:** "not another cold email about [thing]"
- **Specificity:** "[their company]'s [specific metric or initiative]"

**What doesn't work:**
- "Quick question" (no specificity — everyone uses this)
- "Exciting opportunity for [Company]" (screams marketing)
- "Following up" (there's nothing to follow up on)
- "Touching base" / "Circling back" (no prior relationship exists)
- Title Case On Every Word (feels formal and broadcast)
- ALL CAPS anything

**Formatting:** Lowercase often outperforms Title Case. It feels personal — like a coworker sent it, not a sales team.

### 2. Opener (1-2 sentences)

The opener must pass the croc brain filter (Klaff): Is this relevant? Is this novel? Is this simple? The prospect decides in 3 seconds whether to keep reading. The opener must earn those 3 seconds.

**What works:**
- **Trigger reference:** "Noticed you just took over [role] at [Company]."
- **Specific observation:** "Your recent post about [topic] — the point about [specific detail] stuck with me."
- **Bold reframe:** "Most [role]s in [industry] are still doing [common approach]. It's costing them [specific consequence]."
- **Research-backed hook:** "[Company] is hiring for [role] — that usually means [inference]."

- **Struggling moment** (Moesta): "It's Friday 4pm. Your client asks for this month's results. You realize the data is in three different tools." Paint a scene the prospect has lived. If they think "that's literally my Tuesday," you've earned the next sentence.
- **Appreciation + gap** (Carnegie): "You've scaled to 50 reps without a dedicated enablement team — impressive. That usually means tribal knowledge is carrying the load, and it breaks when you add 10 more."

**What doesn't work:**
- "Hi, I'm [Name] from [Company]. We help [type of company] to [benefit]." — The prospect stopped reading at "we help."
- "I hope this email finds you well." — This is filler. It finds them in their inbox, same as everyone else.
- "I love what [Company] is doing in the [industry] space!" — Vague flattery that applies to anyone. The prospect knows you didn't look at anything.
- "I wanted to reach out because..." — Throat-clearing. Delete and start with the next sentence.

### 3. Body (2-3 sentences)

The value narrative. Lead with an insight or result — not a product description. The Challenger approach (Dixon/Adamson) — confident, teaching-oriented, willing to reframe the prospect's assumptions: teach them something about their own problem. Your product is the vehicle for the insight, not the opening act.

**Structure options:**
- **Insight + proof:** "[Reframe about their problem]. We helped [similar company] [specific result] by [mechanism]."
- **Pattern + contrast:** "The [role]s pulling ahead in [industry] figured out [insight]. The ones falling behind are still [old approach]."
- **Implication chain** (Rackham): Problem → implication → deeper implication. "Your reps are booking meetings that don't close [problem] → your AEs waste 40% of their time on dead pipeline [implication] → your forecast is unreliable and the board questions whether outbound works [deeper implication]." Two to three sentences that transform "that's annoying" into "we need to fix this."
- **Push + pull** (Moesta): Name the problem AND paint the alternative. "Right now, [problem]. Imagine instead [what life looks like after]." Push alone creates anxiety. Pull alone is wishful. Together they create momentum.

The body should never list features. Features are what YOU care about. Outcomes are what THEY care about. "Our platform has AI-powered analytics" is a feature. "47% reduction in manual reporting time" is an outcome.

### 4. CTA (1 sentence)

One ask. Not two. Not three. One clear, low-friction next step.

**Early in sequence (low friction):**
- "Worth a quick conversation?"
- "Does this resonate with what you're seeing?"
- "Mind if I send you the framework?"
- "Open to a 15-minute call this week?"

**Later in sequence (higher friction):**
- "I have Thursday at 2pm or Friday at 10am — either work?"
- "Here's my calendar: [link]"
- "Want me to put together a quick analysis for [Company]?"

**Never:**
- "Book a call or check out our site or reply with your thoughts" — three asks is zero asks
- "Let me know if you have any questions" — passive, gives them nothing to act on
- "Would love to learn more about your needs" — Relationship Builder, not Challenger. You should already know their needs.

### 5. Signature

Name, title, company. That's it.

No 10-line signature blocks. No social media icons. No "Sent from my iPhone." No legal disclaimers. No promotional banners. No inspirational quotes. Every line in the signature that isn't name/title/company makes the email look more like marketing and less like a person.

## First-Touch Patterns by Trigger Type

Not fill-in-the-blank templates — structural patterns showing how the trigger connects to the value narrative. Each pattern follows: trigger hook → value narrative → CTA.

### New Hire / Role Change
"Noticed you just took over [role] at [Company]. The first 90 days in that seat, most [role]s discover [specific problem]. [One sentence insight about the problem — what the smart ones do differently]. Worth a quick conversation about what we're seeing?"

### Funding Round
"Congrats on the [round]. At [stage], the most common [function] mistake is [specific mistake]. [One sentence about what the winners do]. Happy to share what we've seen work — interested?"

### Job Posting
"You're hiring for [role]. That usually means [inference about their situation — scaling, backfilling, building new function]. [Value narrative: what companies in that position typically miss]. [CTA]."

### Competitor Move
"[Competitor] just [specific action]. That changes [specific dynamic] for companies like [theirs]. [Insight about what this means for the prospect]. [CTA]."

### Content / Post Trigger
"Your [post/article] about [topic] — [specific reference that proves you actually read it, not just the title]. [Related insight that extends their thinking or challenges it]. [CTA]."

### No Specific Trigger (Problem-Aware)
"[Bold stat or claim about their problem]. [Reframe: why the common approach to this problem makes it worse]. [One proof point]. [CTA]."

## Sequence Structure

Default 6-touch sequence over 25-28 days. Each message has a distinct job and adds a new angle — not the same pitch rephrased.

| # | Day | Job | Approach | Length |
|---|-----|-----|----------|--------|
| 1 | 1 | Open the door | Trigger + value narrative + soft CTA | 75-125 words |
| 2 | 3-4 | Different angle | New proof point or different reframe | 50-100 words |
| 3 | 7-8 | Social proof | Specific case study or result | 50-75 words |
| 4 | 12-14 | Challenger reframe | Teach — reframe their problem, new insight | 50-100 words |
| 5 | 18-21 | Direct ask | Clear offer, specific CTA with time options | 40-75 words |
| 6 | 25-28 | Breakup | Permission-based close, leave door open | 30-50 words |

**Key principles:**

**Each message is a new angle.** If message 1 leads with a trigger + value narrative, message 2 should come from a completely different direction — a case study, a reframe, a contrarian insight, a relevant piece of content. The prospect who didn't reply to angle A might reply to angle B. Five versions of angle A will not help.

**Length compresses.** The first email is the longest — it has the most work to do (establish relevance, deliver insight, earn the CTA). Each subsequent email gets shorter. By the breakup, it's 2-3 sentences.

**Thread replies, don't start new emails.** Follow-ups should reply to the previous email in the same thread. The thread history provides context without the sender having to recap. And it shows persistence, not spam.

**Timing adapts.** If a trigger is time-sensitive (funding round, new hire), tighten the first 3 touches to days 1, 2, and 5. The window of receptivity around a trigger decays fast (Hughes). If there's no trigger urgency, the default spacing works.

**Each email primes the next** (Cialdini, Pre-Suasion). The subject line creates a privileged moment — "quick question" primes conversational mode, "your pipeline gap" primes problem-awareness mode. Choose the frame you want. Within the sequence, each email should deepen the same thread from a new direction. Don't introduce competing ideas across emails. Email 1 focuses attention on the problem. Email 2 arrives while they're already thinking about it and delivers a different angle. The sequence works because each message builds on the frame the previous one established.

### The Breakup Email

The final email works by triggering loss aversion. Give them permission to say no:

"[First name], I've reached out a few times about [topic] — I'll assume the timing isn't right. If [trigger/problem] becomes a priority, here's where to find me: [link or email]. No hard feelings."

**The "Fully Informed Decision" variant (Fladlien):** An alternative breakup frame: "I'm not asking you to decide yes or no. I'm asking you to make a fully informed decision — and the only way to do that is a 15-minute conversation. If after that it's not a fit, I'll never email you again." This reframes the CTA from "commit" to "gather information," which feels lower-risk.

Breakup emails consistently produce the highest reply rates in a sequence. The prospect realizes the opportunity to engage is about to disappear.

## Follow-Up Principles

Every follow-up must justify its existence with new value. Not new words — new value.

**What counts as new value:**
- A different angle on the same problem
- A case study or proof point not mentioned before
- A relevant piece of content (article, framework, benchmark)
- A new trigger event that's occurred since the last email
- A reframe that makes them see the problem differently
- A direct, time-specific ask they can act on immediately

**What doesn't count:**
- "Just bumping this to the top of your inbox"
- "Did you get a chance to read my last email?"
- "Circling back on this"
- "I know you're busy, but..."
- The same pitch reworded with different adjectives

**Micro-commitments in follow-ups (Fladlien):** Embed one tag question or check-in per follow-up to build compliance momentum. "Makes sense, right?" or "Sound familiar?" These small yeses make the CTA feel like a natural next step rather than a jump. Don't use lazy versions ("Who wants more revenue?") — make them specific to the prospect's situation.

**Compression rule:** Each follow-up should be shorter than the last. If message 1 is 100 words, message 4 should be 60 words. By message 6, you're at 30-40 words. The prospect already has context from the thread — don't rebuild it every time.

## Response Handling

Design the branches before writing the messages. Different replies demand fundamentally different next actions.

### Interested
Advance immediately. Momentum is everything. Propose a specific time — not "when works for you?" (that puts the burden on them and adds friction).

"Great — how's Thursday at 2pm ET? If that doesn't work, here's my calendar: [link]."

Keep it to 2-3 sentences. Don't re-pitch. They already said yes — just make the next step easy.

### Referral
"Thanks for pointing me to [Name] — really appreciate it. Would you be open to making a quick intro? If easier, I can mention you reached out."

Then start a new sequence to the referred contact with the referral as the opener: "[Referrer name] suggested I reach out — [value narrative tailored to the new contact]."

### "Not Now"
Acknowledge it. Don't push. Ask for permission to follow up later.

"Totally get it — timing matters. Mind if I circle back in [specific timeframe, e.g., Q2]? In the meantime, here's [relevant content] in case it's useful."

Add them to a long-term nurture. Set a reminder. Re-approach with a new trigger when the time comes.

### Preemptive: The Accusation Audit
Before objections surface, name the worst things the prospect might be thinking (Voss). "You're probably thinking this is just another cold email, and that whatever I'm selling won't work for your situation." One well-placed accusation audit — usually in follow-up 2 or 3 — neutralizes objections before they form. The prospect can't weaponize a concern you've already acknowledged.

### Objection
Address the specific objection directly. One objection per reply.

**First: label the emotion** (Voss). Before reframing the logic, acknowledge what they're feeling. "It sounds like you've been burned by vendors who over-promised before" or "It seems like the timing feels wrong with everything else on your plate." The label shows you heard them. Only after they feel heard do they process your reframe.

**Then: Ledge-Disrupt-Ask** (Blount). (1) Ledge — buy a micro-moment: "That makes sense" or "That's exactly why I reached out." (2) Disrupt — break their expected pattern with a reframe or unexpected angle. (3) Ask — move forward with a question, not a pitch.

**Know the objection type** (Blount):
- **Reflexive** ("We're good," "Not interested") — Not a real objection. Disrupt with a calibrated question (Voss): "What would need to change for this to be worth a conversation?"
- **Buying** (price, fit, timing) — Real concern, real engagement. Use specific reframes:
  - **Price:** Change the denominator. "Compared to [alternative cost — a hire, an agency, the status quo], this is [fraction] of the investment."
  - **Time:** Show net time. "Implementation takes [X hours]. You'll save [Y hours] in the first month alone."
  - **Fit:** Narrow with specificity. "Fair question — this works best for [specific situation]. Based on [what you know about them], that's exactly where you are."
  - **Trust:** Lead with proof, not reassurance. "[Similar company] had the same concern. Here's what happened: [specific result]."
- **Red herring** ("No budget" masking "I don't see the value") — Surface the real issue with a calibrated question: "Setting budget aside, is this something that would make a difference for your team?"

### No Reply (Full Sequence Complete)
Rest 60-90 days. Then re-approach with a completely different angle:
- New trigger event
- Different value narrative
- Different entry point (e.g., different person at the same company)
- Content-led approach instead of direct outreach

**"New information creates new decisions" (Fladlien).** A new case study, a new feature, a new offer, or a market shift is genuine new information that justifies re-contact. "Last time I reached out, we didn't have [X]. Now we do — and it changes things for teams like yours." The new information is the reason; the email is the vehicle.

Do NOT run the same sequence again with the same information. If six emails from angle A didn't work, angle A is dead for this prospect.

### Unsubscribe / Stop
Respect it immediately. Remove from all outbound sequences. No "Are you sure?" No "One last thing." Done.

## Cold Email Anti-Patterns

**"Hi [Name], I'm [Name] from [Company]. We help [type of company] to [benefit]."** — The prospect stopped reading at "we help." This is a pitch, not a conversation. The opener should be about their world, not yours.

**250+ word cold emails.** This is a newsletter, not outreach. The prospect didn't ask for this. They're scanning, not reading. If it doesn't fit in a phone screen without scrolling, it's too long.

**"I'd love to learn more about your challenges."** — Relationship Builder language. You should already know their challenges from the 3x3 and BRIEF. The Challenger doesn't ask what's wrong — they tell the prospect what they're missing.

**Calendar link in email 1.** Asking a stranger for 30 minutes on their calendar before you've delivered any value. It's like proposing on the first date. Save the calendar link for message 3+ or after they've shown interest.

**Same follow-up reworded 5 times.** If they didn't reply to the value narrative, more value narrative won't help. Change the angle — proof, reframe, content, direct ask.

**Fake personalization.** "I love what [Company] is doing in the [industry] space!" — This is a merge tag wearing a mask. Real personalization references something specific that proves you actually looked.

**HTML-formatted cold emails with images and buttons.** This looks like marketing. Cold emails should look like a person typing an email — plain text, no images, no styled buttons, no header graphics.

**Long signature blocks.** Social media icons, promotional banners, legal disclaimers, three phone numbers, and an inspirational quote. Each line makes the email look less personal. Name, title, company. Done.

**"I was wondering if perhaps you might be open to potentially exploring..."** — Hedge-filled, qualifier-stuffed language. This is fear of rejection masquerading as politeness. Be direct. "Worth a conversation?" beats "I was wondering if you might potentially be interested in perhaps chatting sometime."

## Length Guidelines

| Position in sequence | Target length | Notes |
|---------------------|--------------|-------|
| First touch | 75-125 words | Longest in the sequence. Earns the relationship. |
| Follow-ups 2-3 | 50-100 words | New angle, compressed delivery |
| Follow-ups 4-5 | 40-75 words | More direct, less buildup |
| Breakup | 30-50 words | Permission to say no |
| Response to interest | 20-40 words | Don't re-pitch. Just advance. |
| Objection handling | 40-80 words | One objection, one reframe, one CTA |

These are guidelines — voice and context adjust them. A client whose VOICE.md says "write long, conversational emails" gets 20% more room. A SaaS product with a technical audience might need tighter, more direct language.
