# Ad Hooks

The first 3 seconds of an ad decide everything. Get those wrong and the rest doesn't matter — nobody sees it.

Ads are interruption. The hook's job is not to sell — it's to earn permission for the next step (click, follow, opt-in). A hook that tries to close the sale is skipping the entire relationship ladder (Godin). The hook earns the right to the education section. The education earns the right to the CTA. The CTA earns the right to the landing page. Each step earns the next — skip a rung and the viewer falls off.

This skill writes opening hooks, written ad copy (the text that accompanies a video ad), and video ad scripts using 18 hook architectures that actually convert. Not "Attention [industry]! Are you tired of [pain point]?" — that's the copy equivalent of a sleeping pill.

The hooks are structural templates. The voice comes from the product. Same hook, completely different energy depending on who's talking.

## Step 0: Get Context

Before writing a single word, find the conversation already happening in the reader's mind.

**Search for these files in the project (check `.claude/`, project root, and parent directories):**
- `BRIEF.md`: positioning, audience, messaging, voice
- `VOICE.md`: tone, personality, language patterns

**If you find them:** Read them. You're looking for six things: the product, who it's for, how they talk about their problem (their words, not yours), the voice, the proof points, and the CTA. Confirm with the user and start generating.

**If you DON'T find them:** Interview the user. Before writing, question whether hooks are even the real problem — "I need better ad hooks" sometimes means "my offer isn't compelling" or "my targeting is wrong." If the issue is upstream, name it before generating hooks.

**Remarkability diagnosis (Godin):** Before writing hooks, ask — is this product remarkable enough that people talk about it? If not, hooks will underperform no matter how good the copy. Ads for unremarkable products burn budget. If the product is in the "very good" trap — functional but forgettable — flag it. Better hooks won't fix a product nobody would mention at dinner.

Then interview in batches of 2-3, conversationally:

**Batch 1: The Offer**
- What's the product or offer? (Be specific — not "a course" but "a 6-week mentorship for fitness coaches at $2k")
- What's the ONE result someone gets? (The transformation, not features.)

Before writing any hook, run 3+ open questions to the client that make them articulate the audience's dominant desire in their own words -- "What do your best customers say when they describe the moment they decided to buy?" -- because the client's unstructured answer reveals the conversation the hook must enter (Camp).

**Batch 2: The Audience + Their Conversation**
- Who's this for, specifically? (Not "entrepreneurs" — "online fitness coaches stuck below $5k/month who post content but can't convert")
- What are they already thinking about? What keeps them up at night? (This is the conversation you're entering — the dominant desire, not the polite version.) Dig past the surface desire they'd state in polite company. "I want to grow my coaching business" is surface. "I need to make $3,000 more per month or I'm going back to a gym job" is dominant (Collier). The dominant desire is immediate, urgent, and more primal — it's the one that makes someone act today instead of "someday." Test every hook: does this speak to what keeps them up at night, or the version they'd tell a stranger at a networking event?
- What do they currently believe that's wrong? (This becomes your paradigm shift.)

**Batch 3: Voice + Proof**
- How should this sound? Direct/no-BS, irreverent/chaotic, authoritative/expert, or friendly/warm?
- Got proof? Numbers, results, testimonials, anything concrete. Specific beats impressive — "122 leads at $0.68" beats "amazing results."

**Batch 4: The Ask**
- What's the CTA? ("DM COACH," "book a call at [url]," "click the link," "try it free")

Adapt the interview to what you already know. Don't re-ask what they've already told you.

**Belief diagnostic before architecture selection (Albuquerque).** Before selecting which of the 18 architectures to use, answer: What does the prospect currently believe that would make them scroll past? The architecture choice becomes the vehicle for shifting that belief. If you can't state the blocking belief in one sentence — "My prospect believes [X], and this prevents them from buying because [Y]" — you're choosing architectures by feel instead of by function. A "You Don't Need" hook shifts a belief about requirements. An "Everyone Thinks" hook shifts a belief about consensus. An "I Got This Result" hook shifts a belief about possibility. The belief determines which vehicle carries it most efficiently. Selecting architecture before identifying the belief is like choosing a camera angle before knowing what the shot needs to show.

**Scratching protocol:** Before writing hooks, spend time in the audience's own channels — the subreddit, the Facebook group, the YouTube comment section, Amazon reviews of related products. Capture 5+ phrases they use to describe their problem in their exact words. This raw material produces hooks that sound like the audience's inner monologue, not like a marketer's best guess. At least one hook in every batch should use their exact language.

**Winner deconstruction step (Kane):** Before creating, study. Find 10 top-performing ads in the category — sorted by shares, not impressions. Shares are the truest signal of resonance because they require active effort from the viewer. Analyze what each winner does in the first 3 seconds: what emotion does it trigger? What pattern does it break? What makes someone want to pass it along? Extract the structural moves, not the surface execution. You're not copying — you're identifying what the audience has already voted for with the highest-cost action they can take (sharing). This step comes before architecture selection because the winners reveal which beliefs the audience is ready to have shifted and which emotional triggers are live right now.

## The Foundations

**Warmth before competence in hook openings (Cabane: The Charisma Myth).** Hooks that demonstrate understanding (warmth) before demonstrating capability (competence) outperform hooks that lead with credentials. "You've posted 47 times and got 3 likes — I built something that fixes this" follows the right sequence: warmth first (I see your struggle), competence second (I solved it). "I built a tool that gets you 10x engagement — here's how" leads with competence (I have the solution) before warmth (I understand your problem), and the reader evaluates the claim skeptically before feeling understood. The warmth-first hook stops the scroll because the reader feels seen before being sold. The competence-first hook gets skipped because the reader hasn't been given a reason to care about the solution yet. This applies across POVs: Aspirational hooks can start with the struggle before the result ("I was stuck at $3K/month for two years — then I found the pattern that changed everything"). Agitational hooks start with the mirror before the promise ("Every app you try has 47 features you don't need and the one you do is buried in settings — sound familiar?"). Educational hooks start with the problem before the insight ("Most people optimize for the wrong metric — here's what actually moves the number"). The sequence is warmth → competence, not competence → warmth. Lead with understanding, follow with capability.


**Power cues vary by hook POV (Cabane: The Charisma Myth).** Each hook POV (aspirational, agitational, educational) uses a different blend of power and warmth cues. Aspirational hooks use declarative power: "I went from $0 to $400K in 6 months — here's the system." The power comes from the specific outcome stated with confidence. Agitational hooks use presence power: mirroring the reader's exact frustration with specific detail shows you see their reality, which is a form of power through understanding. "You've posted 47 times this month and got 3 likes total — I know why" projects power through specificity of the mirror. Educational hooks balance power (confident claim or counterintuitive insight) and warmth (positioned as helping, not flexing): "Most creators get this backwards — here's what actually works." The confident reframe is power; the "here's what works" framing is warmth. Aspirational = high power through results, moderate warmth through accessibility. Agitational = high presence power through precise mirroring, high warmth through shared frustration. Educational = balanced power (expertise) and warmth (positioned to help). A hook that mismatches the power-warmth ratio to its POV feels off: an aspirational hook that apologizes for the result ("I got lucky and went from $0 to $400K") undercuts its own power. An educational hook that flexes credentials ("As someone with 10 years of experience...") is power without warmth and reads as arrogant rather than helpful.

Four principles that everything else builds on.

### 1. Enter the Conversation Already Happening

Your ad doesn't START a conversation — it JOINS one. Before your ad appears, the reader is already thinking something. Your hook must articulate what they already feel but haven't put into words. Schwartz calls this channeling mass desire — you don't create wanting, you tap into wanting that already exists.

When someone reads your hook and thinks "that's exactly what I was thinking" — you've entered the conversation.

**The hook is a small door into a vast emotional space** (Bachelard). The best hooks create intimate immensity — a precise, small detail that opens into a feeling much larger than the words contain. The mechanism is the gap between the container (the specific fact) and the implication (the emotional world it activates). If the hook and the feeling it produces are the same size, it's a statement. If the hook is small and the feeling is vast, it's working.

The best hooks function as implied questions that make the reader diagnose their own situation -- "You posted 5 reels. Zero DMs" makes the reader measure themselves rather than asserting their pain from the outside, which they can resist (Camp).

**Conversation test:** Read your hook. Was the reader ALREADY thinking this? "You posted 5 reels this week. Zero DMs." — yes, they were thinking that. "Have you considered a systematic approach to social media conversion?" — no, nobody thinks in that language.

Find their conversation through: customer language in the BRIEF, reviews, DMs, Reddit threads, sales call transcripts. Use THEIR words, not yours. Halbert's rule: research the audience's exact language before writing a single word.

**Trigger their memories, don't deliver new information** (Kahneman): The strongest hooks activate the viewer's own experiences rather than stating facts they haven't lived. "You stopped opening the app — not because you stopped working out, but because the broken streak felt worse than skipping" activates a specific personal memory. "Many people struggle with fitness consistency" activates nothing. When a viewer's own memory fires, the problem feels real and urgent because it IS real — they lived it. Before stating what you are, make what you're NOT available in their memory. The hook that reminds beats the hook that informs.

**Beware attribution error** (Groopman): Writing hooks based on the category of person ("fitness coaches") instead of what this specific audience segment actually thinks. The persona is not the person. "Fitness coaches want more clients" is a category assumption. "Fitness coaches who post 5 reels a week and get zero DMs feel like the algorithm hates them" is the actual conversation. If your hook could apply to every person in the category, you haven't entered anyone's conversation.

**Sympathy before stakes** (Truffaut/Hitchcock): Even in 8 seconds, the first 2 seconds should establish common ground before introducing stakes. Hitchcock's rule: the audience must care about the person before they can feel tension about the situation. A hook that opens with a threat or bold promise before establishing any connection gets attention but not investment — the viewer watches without caring. "You posted 5 reels this week" is common ground (they recognize themselves). "Zero DMs" is the stakes (now they feel it). Reverse the order — "Zero DMs? You're posting too much content" — and it reads as accusation, not empathy. The common ground can be a shared situation, a shared frustration, or a shared identity. It only needs a beat — one clause, one image, one recognition moment — but skipping it makes every hook that follows land on a stranger instead of an ally.

**Emotional truth test for story-based hooks** (Hunter): When a hook claims personal experience — "I went from broke to $50k/month," "Yesterday I closed three deals" — that experience must be genuine. Audiences detect manufactured authenticity faster than any other form of dishonesty. A first-person hook where the speaker hasn't lived the story reads as performance, and performance triggers the same skepticism as a polished ad. If the founder actually went broke, the specificity of the memory carries the hook. If they didn't, no amount of architectural skill saves it. Before approving a story-based hook, ask: did this actually happen? If not, find the story that did — the real one is always more specific, less polished, and more compelling.

### 2. Write to One Person, Not an Audience

A hook is not a broadcast. Picture one specific person — their face, their frustration, their phone in their hand at 11pm. Write to THAT person.

"If you're a business owner..." → addressing a crowd. "You've been posting every day. Five reels this week. Zero DMs." → talking to someone.

Hooks that describe the viewer's actual daily experience create recognition faster than hooks that name problem categories (Carnegie). "You've been posting every day for 6 months and your DMs are still empty" beats "Struggling with engagement?" — the specificity IS the hook. When the viewer's lived reality appears on screen, they feel seen, and feeling seen earns the next 3 seconds. The test: could someone in that situation screenshot the hook and send it to a friend saying "this is literally me"? If not, you're still writing to a category, not a person.

Count your "you" vs "we" words. The ratio should overwhelmingly favor "you." The first 3 seconds are about THEM, never about you or your product. This means the viewer is the protagonist of every hook, not the product (Miller). "Our platform has AI-powered creator discovery" is product-as-hero — the product is the subject of the sentence, and the viewer is a spectator. "You spent 4 hours finding creators yesterday. Here's how to do it in 10 minutes." is customer-as-hero — the viewer is the subject, living inside their own problem, and the product is just the guide that helps them win. Test every hook: is the viewer the subject of the sentence? If the product, brand, or founder is the grammatical subject of the opening line, flip it. The viewer scrolling past your ad is mid-quest in their own story — the hook must cast them as the hero of that story, not ask them to watch yours.

**Narrower audiences produce higher-converting hooks (Godin).** "Fitness coaches posting 5 reels/week with zero DMs" beats "fitness coaches." "Solo founders stuck below $5K MRR who refuse to take VC money" beats "entrepreneurs." The specificity signals "this is for ME" — and that belonging signal converts better than broad reach. Hooks for narrow audiences feel personal. Hooks for everyone feel like ads.

**Design hooks for the edges (Godin).** The obsessives (otaku) and spreaders (sneezers), not the mainstream. The center of the audience does not spread. The edges — the people with obsessive interest in your category — cannot shut up. A hook that resonates with the edges gets shared. A hook that tries to reach everyone reaches no one.

**Identity hooks invoke tribal belonging (Godin).** The strongest "one person" hooks don't just describe a situation — they name who the viewer IS. "People like us do things like this" is the deepest hook because it bypasses rational evaluation entirely. The viewer doesn't weigh the claim; they recognize themselves. Structure: name the tribe + name their behavior + contrast with the out-group. "Bootstrapped founders don't waste money on agencies. Here's what they use instead." "Solo operators who close their own deals don't need a 12-person sales team." The identity claim does three things simultaneously: qualifies the viewer (self-selection), creates belonging (I'm one of these people), and sets up the paradigm shift (what do people like me actually do?). Identity hooks outperform rational hooks because tribal belonging is a deeper motivator than information — the viewer acts to stay consistent with who they just agreed they are.

**POV as a hook variable** (Truffaut/Hitchcock): Whose eyes is the hook told from? This isn't just a stylistic choice — it's a psychological lever. First-person hooks ("I discovered..." / "I went from...") create identification — the viewer steps into the speaker's shoes and projects themselves into the result. Third-person hooks ("She went from..." / "This coach...") create spectation — the viewer watches from outside, which builds proof but less personal urgency. Second-person hooks ("You're making this mistake..." / "You posted 5 reels...") create confrontation — the viewer can't look away because the hook is pointed directly at them. Each POV triggers a different emotional response. When generating a batch, vary POV deliberately. If every hook in the batch is second-person, the confrontation becomes monotonous. Mix identification, spectation, and confrontation for range.

### 3. One Concept Per Ad — The Big Idea

Every ad is ABOUT one thing. Not three benefits. Not five features. One selling concept that makes the whole message click. Sullivan calls this the Big Idea — the single, surprising, emotionally resonant concept that makes someone see the product differently.

"Our mentorship includes content systems, sales frameworks, and fulfillment templates" → features list. No concept.
"The difference between $2k months and $10k months isn't hustle. It's three systems connected instead of running separately." → one concept. Everything else supports it.

The hook should express the selling concept. The body should develop it. The CTA should resolve it. If you can't state your ad's concept in one sentence, you don't have one yet.

**Compression implies, not summarizes** (Bachelard). A hook that compresses the viewer's entire situation into 8 words doesn't reduce — it implies a world. The smaller the container, the more the viewer's imagination expands to fill what's implied. "You posted 5 reels. Zero DMs." works because it's two small, specific facts that open into an immense implication. Never make a hook longer in the name of being complete — completeness kills the expansion.

**Commander's Intent test** (Heath): Before writing a single hook, write one sentence of intent — the ONE thing the viewer must take away if everything else fails. Not the tagline, not the CTA — the irreducible message. "If the viewer remembers only one thing, it should be ___." If two people on the team would fill that blank differently, the ad doesn't have a concept yet. Every element of the ad — hook, education, CTA — either supports the Commander's Intent or gets cut.

**Name the argument before selecting architecture (McPhee).** After the Commander's Intent is set, write one sentence naming the argument this ad makes — not the topic, not the CTA. "The argument is: manual outreach is a tax on founders who should be closing." "The argument is: your content isn't failing — your conversion system is missing." This is distinct from the Commander's Intent (which names the takeaway) and the spine (which tests whether the hook delivers it). The argument names the logical claim the ad advances. Select the architecture that opens that argument most efficiently — a "You Don't Need" hook opens an argument about a false requirement, an "Everyone Thinks" hook opens an argument about a wrong belief. If you can't state the argument in one sentence, the ad isn't ready for architecture selection.

**Every hook is a micro-premise** (Egri). A concept becomes a hook when it's compressed into a cause-and-effect proposition. "Posting without a system → burning out in 6 months." "Manual outreach → pipeline that disappears every quarter." The cause is the problem the viewer recognizes; the effect is the consequence they fear. If the hook doesn't contain an implied cause-and-effect — even compressed into 8 seconds — it's a statement, not a premise. Statements inform. Premises create tension, because the viewer immediately asks "is that true for me?" Test every hook: can you extract a "doing X leads to Y" from it? If not, it's floating — it has no argument driving it forward.

**The spine test** (Lumet): Every hook has a spine — a single argument, not a topic or a product name, that the opening must deliver in the first seconds. The spine is not "fitness coaching" (topic) or "our mentorship" (product) or "lead generation is hard" (pain). The spine is the argument: "you've been lied to about how clients actually find coaches." The Big Idea and Commander's Intent identify what the ad is about. The spine tests whether the hook actually delivers that argument in its opening beat. A hook can have a clear concept and still fail the spine test if the opening 3 seconds circle the argument without stating it. "Tired of struggling with lead gen?" circles the topic. "You're sending 50 DMs a day to people who were never going to buy" states the argument. Every word in the hook either serves the spine or dilutes it — and dilution in 3 seconds is death.

### 4. Be Oppositional, Not Boring

Stanley's meta-principle: the opposite of boring is interesting, and the fastest way to be interesting is to do the opposite of what everyone else does. This isn't contrarianism for its own sake — it's paradigm shifting. The brain doesn't reward surprise alone — it rewards prediction-then-surprise. The dopamine hit comes from the moment expectation is set up and then violated (Levitin). Even in a 3-second hook, there must be a micro-setup: half a second of familiar pattern, then the break. "How I grew to 10K followers..." (setup — the viewer predicts a growth hack) "...by losing my biggest client" (violation — the prediction breaks). Without the setup, the surprise has no expectation to violate, and the neurochemical reward doesn't fire.

The paradigm shift sequence:
1. What's the accepted belief in this industry?
2. How can I challenge it?
3. What belief replacement leads them to buy?
4. How does my product become the obvious answer to the new belief?

When the ad's intent to sell is visible, defensiveness fires and the viewer scrolls (Greene). The strongest hooks don't feel like ads — they feel like useful intel, a surprising fact, or a personal story. Visible intent ("Buy now," "Limited time," "Don't miss") is the fastest defensiveness trigger. Invisible intent ("I tested 47 subject lines — here's what I found") earns attention because it reads as value, not pitch. Test: if you covered the brand name and CTA, would the hook survive as interesting content on its own?

Oppositional hooks should reframe without telling the viewer they're wrong (Carnegie). "Facebook ads used to be the obvious play — here's what changed" respects the viewer's prior decision. "Stop wasting money on Facebook ads" attacks it. The Carnegie bridge — "I used to think the same thing" — turns a correction into a shared journey. Reframes convert because the viewer feels understood. Attacks create defensiveness because the viewer feels judged. Any time a paradigm shift hook implies the audience has been stupid, it's lost them — the shift should make the old belief feel reasonable given what they knew, and the new belief feel inevitable given what's changed.

**New opportunity beats improvement** (Brunson): Hooks that frame a new vehicle outperform hooks that promise to improve the current one. "Stop doing X — there's a new way" beats "Do X faster." New opportunity hooks trigger curiosity because the viewer has no failed history with the vehicle; improvement hooks trigger skepticism because they've already tried and failed at the thing you're promising to fix. "A better way to run Facebook ads" is improvement — they've been burned. "Why I stopped running Facebook ads entirely" is new opportunity — no scar tissue. When generating paradigm shift hooks, check: is this offering a new vehicle, or a better version of the old one? The new vehicle is almost always the stronger hook because it sidesteps every past failure in one move.

**Paradigm shifts are reframes** (Heinrichs): The paradigm shift doesn't argue within the audience's existing frame — it changes what the argument is about. "You don't need more content" doesn't counter the content argument on its merits; it redirects the entire conversation to a different subject (DM conversion). When generating paradigm-shift hooks, test whether you're arguing within the frame or changing the frame. Arguing within: "Your content strategy is wrong." Changing the frame: "Content isn't the game. Conversations are." The reframe is stronger because the audience can't rebut a conversation they didn't expect to be having.

**Answer their internal dialogue immediately** (Kennedy): Every paradigm shift triggers an instant objection in the reader's mind — they're already arguing back before they finish the sentence. The strongest shifts anticipate this and answer it in the next line, creating a one-two punch: shift, then preempt. "You don't need more content. I used to think daily posting was essential too. Here's what changed." The shift lands ("you don't need more content"), the reader pushes back ("easy for you to say"), and the next line disarms the pushback by showing you held the same belief. This is real-time objection handling compressed into copy — don't let the reader sit with their objection long enough to scroll. The internal dialogue is always some version of "but that can't be right because..." — name what comes after "because" and answer it before they finish the thought.

**Finding the right belief to shift** (Albuquerque): The paradigm shift sequence above assumes you know the belief. Most hooks fail because the writer guesses the belief from their desk instead of finding it in the prospect's language. The belief identification process: (1) Map the desire — what do they want? (table stakes, everyone knows this). (2) Map the solutions they've tried — what have they already been told to do? (3) Map the failures — what do they blame? What story do they tell themselves about why nothing has worked? The dominant belief crystallizes here, in the justification for why they stopped trying. (4) State it: "My prospect believes [X], and this prevents them from buying because [Y]."

**Watch for belief anchoring** (Groopman): After identifying the dominant belief, ask: "Is this the belief that most blocks the sale, or is it the belief that was easiest to find?" The first belief discovered is not necessarily the most important. If you mapped the belief from your desk rather than from customer language, it's a hypothesis — test it alongside at least one alternative before building the batch around it.

**The 16-word test** (Albuquerque): Before writing the hook, fill in: "Most people believe [current belief]. But [mechanism] proves [new belief]." If you can't fill it in clearly, you don't understand the sale well enough to write a hook. The mechanism — the specific, named, credible explanation for WHY the new belief is true — is what separates a paradigm shift from a naked claim. "You don't need more content" is a claim. "The DM conversion framework proves that 80% of coaching clients come from conversations, not content" is a mechanism. Name it, make it specific, make it lead to your product.

**Self-revelation in reframe hooks:** The strongest reframes make the viewer recognize their own pattern. Not "here's what you're doing wrong" but "you've been running this exact pattern for six months without seeing it." When the hook creates a moment where the audience sees their flaw clearly for themselves, the reframe lands harder because they drew the conclusion — you just held up the mirror.

Every great hook takes something the audience assumes is true and breaks it. "You don't need more content to get more clients. You need better DMs." — paradigm shift. "Get more clients with our platform" — invisible.

**Surface claim and emotional subtext are two layers** (Seger): The strongest hooks carry a surface claim the viewer processes consciously and an emotional subtext they feel without analyzing. "You posted 5 reels this week. Zero DMs." — surface claim is about DM volume; subtext is "you're failing and you know it." Don't collapse the two layers by stating the subtext. Let the viewer complete it — a conclusion they reach themselves cuts deeper than one you hand them.

**Uninflected hooks hit hardest** (Mamet): Present flat facts without emotional editorializing. "You're posting 5 reels a week. Getting zero DMs." is uninflected — it states what's happening and lets the viewer feel the weight. "Isn't it frustrating when your content falls flat?" is inflected — it tells the viewer what to feel, which lets them resist. The uninflected beat trusts the juxtaposition to carry the emotion. Two facts placed next to each other create meaning the viewer supplies — and self-generated emotion is stronger than directed emotion. The test: strip every emotional adjective and rhetorical question from the hook. If the bare facts still land, the hook is uninflected and strong. If it collapses without the editorial, the facts weren't doing the work — the copywriter was. Default AI copy inflects everything: "Struggling with...?" "Tired of...?" "Isn't it time...?" These are all inflection — they tell the viewer what to feel instead of presenting what's true and letting the feeling arrive on its own.

**Antithesis is the structural engine** (Aristotle): The strongest paradigm shift hooks use antithesis — setting two ideas against each other for contrast and memorability. "Not more content. Better DMs." "Others chase followers. You close deals." When generating hooks, try framing the paradigm shift as an antithesis: the old belief against the new one in two short clauses. Antithesis compresses a paradigm shift into a structure the brain can't ignore. The two-futures pattern (Miller) is antithesis applied to the viewer's trajectory: juxtapose the failure path and the success path in concrete detail, and the contrast itself becomes the hook. "Last year: 200 cold emails, 2 replies. This year: 50 emails, 23 meetings. Same product." The viewer sees both futures — the one they're living and the one they could have — and the gap between them creates instant tension. The specificity matters: vague futures ("struggle vs. success") don't land, but concrete futures with numbers, timelines, and recognizable details make both paths feel real. Any of the 18 architectures can carry a two-futures structure — an "In The Last" hook (#18) can contrast last quarter with this quarter, a "Client B/A" (#12) can contrast the client's before-future with their after-future, an "Everyone Thinks" (#10) can contrast the future most people are headed toward with the future available to those who shift the belief.

**The strongest paradigm shifts are heretical (Godin).** They name what the audience has been told that's wrong — not just a new tip, but a challenge to the status quo. "What your marketing agency won't tell you" is heretical. "5 marketing tips" is not. Heretical hooks challenge what everyone assumes is true. The reader feels like an insider discovering something the mainstream missed. This creates belonging before the ad even sells anything.

**The indirect hook earns the scroll (Greene: The Art of Seduction).** The hook that works hardest doesn't look like an ad. Questions that provoke thought, counterintuitive claims, story fragments that demand completion — these earn attention because they bypass ad-pattern recognition. Direct pitches ("Get more clients with...") only work on people who already want the product and are actively shopping. For everyone else — the majority of any cold audience — the indirect approach earns the right to sell by first earning the right to be heard. Test: would someone share this hook as content even without the brand name attached?

**Contradiction creates cognitive itch (Greene: The Art of Seduction).** Contradictions earn attention because they violate expectations. "The laziest way to build a six-figure business." "Why the best copywriters write less." "How losing my biggest client doubled my revenue." Contradiction forces the brain to resolve the paradox — and resolution requires watching. Pair unexpected elements that shouldn't go together: lazy + success, less + better, loss + gain. The cognitive itch from unresolved contradiction is what earns the click. A hook without tension is a statement; a hook with contradiction is a question the viewer must answer by continuing.

**Insinuation questions prime the conclusion (Greene: The Art of Seduction).** Questions that lead to predetermined conclusions are more powerful than direct claims because the reader draws the conclusion themselves. "How much time did you spend last week on tasks that could be automated?" The reader calculates, feels the pain, and is primed for the solution — before you've offered anything. "You're wasting time on manual tasks" is a claim they can argue with. The question makes them prove the claim to themselves. Structure: ask a question that can only be answered one way, let the reader reach the uncomfortable conclusion, then present the solution. Self-generated conclusions are trusted more than stated claims.

**Tricolon and anaphora as hook mechanics** (Heinrichs): Beyond antithesis, two other figures of speech generate hooks mechanically. Tricolon — three parallel elements with the third delivering the turn — powers stacking hooks: "More posts. More reels. Zero clients." The first two set the pattern; the third breaks it. Anaphora — repeating the same word or phrase at the start of successive clauses — powers repetition-based hooks: "You tried the course. You tried the ads. You tried the agency. None of them fixed the real problem." Anaphora builds frustration through accumulation, making the paradigm shift land as relief. Name these when generating: "this is an antithesis hook," "this is a tricolon hook," "this is an anaphora hook" — naming the figure sharpens the execution.

**Clash of context as reframe generator** (Vorhaus): Put high-stakes problems in low-stakes language or vice versa for surprise and reframing. "Why your SaaS pricing strategy is a bad Tinder profile." "You're running a $500K pipeline like a yard sale." The mismatch between the problem's gravity and the comparison's casualness stops the scroll and makes the viewer reconsider the familiar problem through an unexpected lens.

**Topoi as hook generators** (Aristotle): When stuck for hook angles, run through these classical argument patterns: redefine the terms (the problem isn't X, it's Y), argue from the greater (if Fortune 500 companies need this, so do you), turn the argument (they say you need X — exactly, and here's the real version of X). Each topos that applies to the product becomes a hook angle. "You Don't Need" (#6) is the redefinition topos. "Everyone Thinks" (#10) is turning the argument. Naming them gives you more patterns to generate from.

**Commit fully or don't commit** (Lumet): A hook that partially commits to provocation is worse than one that fully commits to being direct. Hedged hooks get scrolled. "Your ads might not be working as well as they could" is a hook that doesn't believe itself — the qualifiers ("might," "as well as they could") signal uncertainty, and uncertainty in a hook is death. "Your ads are burning money. Here's the proof." commits. The viewer may disagree, but they can't ignore it. This applies across every hook type: a paradigm shift that hedges the shift ("some people find that..."), a pain hook that softens the pain ("you may have noticed..."), a result hook that qualifies the result ("in many cases...") — all of these are half-committed, and half-committed hooks occupy the dead zone between safe and bold where nothing converts. If the angle is provocative, be fully provocative and accept that some viewers will bounce. If the angle is empathetic, be fully empathetic without hedging toward authority. The hook that tries to be two things is zero things. Bold wrong beats timid right — because bold gets watched and can be corrected, while timid gets scrolled and teaches you nothing.

**Ground hooks in physical locations (Dicks: cinema of the mind).** "5 reels, 10K views, zero DMs" puts the viewer in front of their own phone. "I was sitting in a WeWork at 11pm eating cold pizza when I realized my business model was broken" puts them in the scene. Abstract hooks ("Are you struggling with lead gen?") leave the viewer in their feed. Location-grounded hooks transport them into a moment — and a viewer inside a moment is a viewer who's stopped scrolling. This applies most to story-based architectures (#16 Yesterday I, #12 Client B/A, #13 UGC) but any hook benefits from one concrete physical detail that triggers the viewer's mental camera.

**Include by excluding (Fladlien):** The strongest hooks repel the wrong people to attract the right ones. "This isn't for beginners" simultaneously filters out tire-kickers and makes serious buyers lean in. The "If You" hook (#5) does this naturally, but the principle extends to any hook type: adding a qualifier or exclusion sharpens the audience and increases perceived value. "By repelling more people than he attracted, he became the leader of the free world."

**Comic premise as hook angle multiplier** (Vorhaus): Frame hooks around a "what if" exaggeration that treats something absurd as normal. "What if cold outreach actually worked because you stopped sounding like a salesperson?" "What if your landing page converts better by doing less, not more?" Each of the 18 architectures can spawn an exaggerated premise version — the premise gives permission to be bold and sets up escalation the viewer wants to follow.

## Why Default Ad Copy Fails

**Anxiety-based hooks kill brand warmth (Cabane: The Charisma Myth).** Hooks built on shame or anxiety ("Stop leaving money on the table!") create discomfort that destroys charisma. Fear-based urgency works short-term but erodes long-term brand trust because the reader associates the brand with the feeling of inadequacy. "You're doing this wrong!" might stop the scroll, but it makes the reader feel stupid, and people don't follow brands that make them feel stupid. Power-based urgency ("The window is closing — here's why timing matters") preserves charisma by creating urgency through information rather than shame. The difference: anxiety-based hooks say "you're failing," power-based hooks say "here's what you don't know yet." Shame-based language: "Stop making this mistake," "You're leaving money on the table," "Why you're failing at X," "The one thing you're doing wrong." These hooks work on cold traffic because they trigger loss aversion, but they damage brand warmth — the reader clicks out of fear, not interest. Power-based alternatives: "Here's the pattern 90% of people miss," "The thing nobody tells you about X," "Why the next 30 days matter more than the last 6 months." These create urgency through insight rather than inadequacy. The reader clicks out of curiosity, not anxiety. A brand built on anxiety-based hooks attracts people running from pain. A brand built on power-based hooks attracts people running toward opportunity. The first group is reactive and hard to retain. The second is proactive and compounds loyalty.

Without guidance, AI-generated ad copy has six tells:

1. **Doesn't enter the conversation.** Starts its own topic instead of joining what the reader is already thinking. "In today's competitive landscape..." — nobody was thinking about competitive landscapes. Luntz's reception principle makes this precise: the problem is never what you intended the hook to say — it's what the viewer *heard*. Write every hook, then read it as the person scrolling past. What did they hear in that half-second? If the answer is "another ad," the hook failed regardless of what you meant.

2. **"Are you tired of [pain]? What if [solution]?"** The universal AI opener. Generic, predictable, invisible. Uses this template for every product, every audience, every time.

3. **Feature narration.** "Our platform offers cross-platform analytics, a built-in pipeline, and 95+ sub-niches." Nobody cares about features in the first 3 seconds. Features are Section 2 material at best.

4. **No curiosity gap.** Explains everything. Closes every loop. Sugarman's core principle: create a gap between what they know and what they want to know. "I add 100-200 subscribers every day using the list snowball method" — what the hell is the list snowball method? That gap IS the hook. Default copy would explain the method immediately and kill the tension.

5. **Complexity triggers suspicion.** Simpler hooks feel more true (Kahneman). Complex language, jargon, and long sentences trigger System 2 resistance — the viewer's brain flags difficulty as danger. Short sentences, familiar words, and clear rhythm trigger System 1 trust. "How long. How hard. That's it." processes instantly. "Leverage our proprietary effort-weighted analytics" triggers resistance. The fluency test: read the hook aloud — any stumble means rewrite.

6. **Performing need signals low status.** An ad that performs need -- stacked urgency language, fake countdown pressure, "you MUST act now" -- signals low status and low demand, both of which repel the buyer it's trying to close. The hook that projects indifference to whether this specific viewer converts reads as high-value (Camp).

7. **Adjectives instead of facts.** "Amazing results," "powerful platform," "industry-leading solution." Every adjective is a confession that you don't have a specific fact. Run every hook through the adjective audit (Ogilvy): find each adjective, kill it, and replace it with the specific number it was hiding. "Amazing results" → "122 leads at $0.68 each." "Powerful platform" → "20,347 creators across 95 sub-niches." "Rapidly growing community" → "added 847 members last month." The specific number IS the hook. The adjective is the hook's coffin — it buries the concrete detail that would have stopped the scroll and replaces it with a word the viewer's brain has learned to skip. Garfinkel's rule: specificity implies real measurement. Specificity IS credibility. And specific numbers are costly signals (Sutherland) — "122 leads at $0.68" costs the risk of being checked; rounded claims like "hundreds of leads" cost nothing and signal nothing. The precision itself communicates honesty because a liar would keep the numbers vague and unfalsifiable. If you can't find a specific number to replace the adjective, you don't know enough about the product to write the hook yet — go back to the interview.

8. **Hooks that explain instead of evoke (Heath: Switch).** "We help businesses improve their marketing" is Analyze → Think. The viewer's Elephant doesn't engage. "You've sent 200 cold emails this month. 3 replies." is See → Feel. The hook re-creates a familiar pain. Ad hooks have 3 seconds to engage the Elephant — data and explanations talk to the Rider, which processes too slowly for scroll-stopping.

## Why Hooks Work: The Psychology

Before reaching for a template, understand what makes ANY hook land. These mechanisms explain why the 18 architectures work — and let you adapt when a template doesn't fit. Before choosing an architecture, derive the mechanism from first principles: what is this specific prospect thinking right now? What belief, if shifted, would make them stop scrolling? The architecture is a delivery vehicle for that shift — pick the vehicle after you understand the cargo.

**Scroll-stopping is psycho-logical, not rational** (Sutherland). The viewer does not stop scrolling because they rationally assess that the content might be useful. They stop for identity recognition ("that's me") or status threat ("I should know this"). These are psycho-logical triggers — they fire before the conscious mind evaluates the claim. "You posted 5 reels this week. Zero DMs." stops the scroll because the viewer recognizes themselves, not because they've decided the information is valuable. "3 outbound frameworks that convert" stops the scroll because not knowing them feels like a gap in competence. When evaluating hooks, ask which psycho-logical trigger fires — identity recognition or status threat. If neither fires, the hook is appealing to rational interest, which is too slow to stop a thumb.

**Choose hook architecture by emotional response** (Tobias): Different hooks trigger different emotions through different plot micro-structures. Pursuit hooks ("In the last 30 days...") create urgency. Discovery hooks ("Everyone thinks... but...") create revelation. Transformation hooks (client B/A) create recognition. Map your hook selection to the specific emotion you need — not just the category of content.

**The enthymeme — let them finish the argument** (Aristotle): The strongest hooks are enthymemes — arguments where the viewer supplies the conclusion. "I found 200 qualified creators in 10 minutes" implies "you could too" without stating it. The viewer's self-generated conclusion is more persuasive than your stated one because they own it — they built it themselves. If you find yourself adding "and you can too" after a result hook, you're weakening the enthymeme. Every "I Got This Result" hook (#3), every "Show You The Numbers" hook (#17) works because the viewer completes the syllogism. Trust them to do it.

**Hooks are pure System 1** (Kahneman): The viewer's brain decides in 200 milliseconds whether to keep watching. System 1 responds to emotion, pattern recognition, and surprise — it does not respond to logic, features, or analysis. Any hook that requires thinking is a hook that loses. If the viewer has to process the hook, they've already scrolled. Write for the brain that decides before the person knows they've decided.

**The first frame triggers categorical snap-judgment (Levitin).** The viewer's brain categorizes the ad into a discrete bucket within the first frame — "marketing," "content," "friend's post," "worth watching." This snap categorization is nearly irreversible. A first frame that looks like an ad gets filed as "marketing noise" before the hook has a chance to land. Design the first frame to trigger the wrong category on purpose — user-generated texture, screenshot format, raw footage aesthetic — so the ad enters the attention stream before the brain's marketing filter activates. Once categorized as "marketing," no amount of clever copy overcomes the category assignment.

**The croc brain gate** (Klaff): Your hook hits the primitive brain first — the part that filters for danger, novelty, and simplicity. If it's complex, abstract, or threatening, the croc brain kills it before the rational mind ever sees it. Hooks must be: simple enough to process instantly, novel enough to flag as interesting, non-threatening enough to not trigger defense.

**Use the as-if to make abstract hooks visceral (Weston).** "Are you struggling with lead generation?" is abstract — the viewer evaluates it analytically. "You refresh your inbox for the fourth time today. Still nothing." is an as-if — a specific lived moment the viewer steps into before they can evaluate it. The as-if works because it bypasses the analytical filter: the viewer is inside the moment before they realize it's an ad. Any abstract pain hook can be converted to an as-if by asking: what is the viewer physically doing when they feel this pain? Name the time, the screen, the physical sensation. The as-if is the hook-scale version of Dicks' cinema of the mind — but where cinema of the mind grounds stories in locations, the as-if grounds hooks in the viewer's own body and routine.

**Attention sets the frame** (Cialdini): Whatever you draw attention to FIRST becomes the lens for everything after. A hook that focuses on a problem makes the solution feel urgent. A hook that focuses on a result makes the method feel desirable. A hook that focuses on a competitor makes comparison the frame. You're not just grabbing attention — you're choosing what they'll care about for the rest of the ad. The hook doesn't just grab attention — it triggers a snap judgment that frames everything after it (Godin). A premium hook triggers "this is for serious people." A budget hook triggers "this is a deal." A tribal hook triggers "this is for people like me." The snap judgment IS the story the viewer tells themselves, and they tell it before the second sentence loads. Every element that follows is filtered through that initial frame. If the snap judgment is wrong — premium product with a discount-bin hook — no amount of good copy downstream corrects it.

**Every frame costs stolen attention** (Godin): The viewer gave you zero permission to take their time. Every frame, every word costs attention they didn't agree to spend. The hook must pay for that attention immediately — with value, surprise, or recognition. A hook that takes 3 seconds to "set up" before delivering anything has spent the viewer's attention budget without compensating them. The transaction is: you interrupted, now pay up. Value means they learned something useful. Surprise means their prediction broke. Recognition means they saw themselves. If the first beat doesn't deliver at least one of these, the viewer's instinct — "this isn't worth my stolen time" — is correct, and they scroll.

**Unexpected holds attention** (Heath): The brain's prediction machine spikes attention when predictions are wrong. "You don't need more content to get more clients" violates the prediction. That violation creates a gap — they need to know WHY. Surprise opens the door; mystery keeps them in the room. But surprise alone fades — Heath's gap theory of curiosity says you must open a knowledge gap that can only be closed by watching or clicking. Surprise gets attention; the curiosity gap holds it. A hook that surprises without planting an unresolved question loses the viewer the moment the novelty wears off.

**The audience wants to work** (Mamet): A hook that states a fact implying a problem — without naming the problem — activates the viewer's imagination more powerfully than one that names the problem explicitly. "You spent $4,000 on ads last month. You got 3 leads." The viewer does the math. The viewer feels the waste. You never said "that's a terrible return" — they concluded it themselves, and their own conclusion is unassailable. This is why open loops work at a deeper level than mere curiosity: the viewer isn't just wondering what comes next, they're actively constructing meaning from the gap you left. The more work the viewer does, the more invested they become — because now the insight is theirs, not yours. When writing hooks, ask: am I doing the viewer's work for them? If the hook names the emotion, states the conclusion, or explains the implication, you've stolen the viewer's job. Leave the gap. They'll fill it — and they'll be unable to scroll past something they're mid-way through solving.

**First-frame puzzle** (Mackendrick): The opening frame should present something slightly wrong, unexpected, or incomplete — a micro-puzzle the viewer's brain automatically tries to resolve. That resolution attempt holds them for the next three seconds, which is where the hook lives. "A coach with 50,000 followers just cancelled her content calendar" — the viewer's brain fires: why would she do that? The puzzle isn't a mystery box or a clickbait tease — it's a genuine incongruity that the viewer can almost but not quite explain. The resolution attempt is involuntary; the brain cannot encounter something slightly off and not try to fix it. This makes the first-frame puzzle the most reliable scroll-stopper because it doesn't depend on the viewer caring about the topic yet — it exploits a cognitive reflex that fires before interest or relevance are evaluated. For video hooks, the visual itself can carry the puzzle: a founder smiling while deleting their ad account, a dashboard showing zero spend next to record revenue, someone walking away from a laptop mid-meeting. The image is slightly wrong, and the wrongness is what buys you the next three seconds.

**Tension, not just curiosity** (Godin): A curiosity gap makes the viewer want to know. Tension makes them unable to NOT resolve it. The distinction matters: curiosity is optional ("huh, interesting"), tension is compulsory ("I can't leave this unresolved"). The strongest hooks create tension — a felt gap between where the viewer is and where they could be, between what they believe and what might be true, between their identity and their behavior. "Everyone thinks posting more leads to more clients" creates curiosity. "You posted 5 reels this week. Zero DMs. And you're about to post a sixth" creates tension — the gap between effort and result is unresolved and the viewer is living inside it. When evaluating hooks, ask: does this create a gap the viewer MUST resolve, or one they could shrug off? If they can scroll past without discomfort, the hook has curiosity but not tension.

**Question stacks accelerate drive** (O'Bannon): A single curiosity gap plants one question. The strongest hooks plant a question stack — they answer a small question while opening a bigger one, creating forward momentum the viewer can't resist. "I signed 12 clients in 30 days" answers "did it work?" (yes, obviously) while simultaneously opening "how?" and "can I do this too?" The partial answer is the accelerant — the viewer gets just enough to feel progress, which pulls them deeper. Compare: "Want more leads?" plants one question and stops. It creates curiosity but not drive — the viewer can shrug. "I signed 12 clients in 30 days without running a single ad" answers "did it work?" while opening "how is that possible?" — the answered question makes the open question feel answerable, which is what turns curiosity into investment. When evaluating hooks, count the questions. A hook that plants one question and stops creates a gap. A hook that answers one question while opening two creates a slide. The slide is what carries the viewer into the ad body.

**The expectation gap as hook engine** (Yorke): The strongest pattern interrupts exploit the space between what the viewer assumes and what's actually true. "You think X, but actually Y" is the structural formula. Stack expectation gaps to build frustration: "You tried the course — still no clients. You tried the ads — still no clients." Each gap compounds the tension because the viewer's prediction keeps failing. Or use a single sharp gap: "I spent $50,000 on ads before I realized I was making this one mistake." The gap between what was expected and what happened IS the drama that holds attention. This works because expectation gaps are miniature stories — every gap contains an implied before and after, a protagonist who believed something and discovered otherwise. When generating hooks, identify the expectation the audience holds and break it. The break is the hook.

**Every hook compresses a plot into 3-8 seconds** (Booker). Before writing a hook, name the plot shape it runs on — this sharpens the hook the same way naming the architecture does. Monster hooks name a villain: "The algorithm is burying your content." Quest hooks name a journey: "From cold list to closed deal in 14 days." Comedy hooks exploit the gap between appearance and reality: "You look busy. Your pipeline says otherwise." Tragedy hooks show a trajectory — flaw, rationalization, consequence: "You know your outbound is broken. You tell yourself next quarter will be different. It won't." Rebirth hooks show transformation: "I was running the same playbook for two years before I realized it was the playbook that was broken." Not every hook maps to a plot, but the ones that do carry narrative momentum — the viewer is mid-story before they realize they're watching an ad. When generating a batch, check: does this hook have a plot shape, or is it a static claim? Static claims inform. Plot-shaped hooks pull the viewer forward.

**Surprise vs. suspense — two different hook engines** (Truffaut/Hitchcock): Shock hooks are surprise — a pattern interrupt, an unexpected visual, a jarring claim. They stop the scroll but don't sustain watching. Tension hooks are suspense — they plant a known threat and let the viewer carry it. "Your ads are bleeding money and here's the proof" is Hitchcock's bomb under the table compressed to 8 seconds: the viewer knows what's at stake, which makes everything that follows tense. The best hooks combine both: surprise to stop the scroll, suspense to hold attention through the ad. A shock opening that doesn't plant a tension line loses the viewer the moment the surprise fades. A tension hook without an initial jolt never gets seen. When generating hooks, identify which engine each one runs on — and look for ways to layer both.

**Dramatic irony hooks** (Howard & Mabley): The viewer watches a character struggle with something the viewer already knows the answer to — and the gap between the character's ignorance and the viewer's knowledge IS the tension. "She's still manually sending follow-ups to every lead" works because the viewer already suspects automation exists. The character doesn't know what the viewer knows, and that asymmetry creates a compulsion to keep watching — part empathy, part superiority, part "just tell her already." Dramatic irony hooks are a variant of any story-based architecture (#12 Client B/A, #16 Yesterday I, #3 I Got This Result) where the hook deliberately withholds from the character what the viewer already possesses. The viewer leans in not to learn something new, but to watch the resolution of a gap they can already see. Structure: show someone doing the hard way (the viewer recognizes the inefficiency), then reveal the shift. The viewer's foreknowledge does the persuasion — you just held the mirror.

**Outcome-first dramatic irony** (Mackendrick): Open with the outcome before showing the journey. "She signed 12 clients in March. In January, she almost quit." The viewer now watches the struggle with foreknowledge of the ending — and that foreknowledge transforms the struggle from tedium into anticipation. Without the outcome first, a story about someone struggling to build a coaching business is just another sad tale the viewer has no reason to sit through. With the outcome first, every beat of the struggle carries dramatic irony — the viewer knows it works out, so each obstacle becomes "how does she get past THIS one?" instead of "is this going to be depressing?" This is Hitchcock's bomb under the table applied to ad structure: telling the viewer the bomb goes off (the result) before showing the scene (the journey) creates more sustained tension than hiding the outcome and hoping curiosity alone holds attention. The technique works across architectures — an "In The Last" hook (#18) can flash the result then rewind, a "Client B/A" (#12) can show the after before the before, a "Yesterday I" (#16) can open with the win then unpack how it happened. The key constraint: the outcome must be specific and surprising enough that knowing it makes the journey more interesting, not less. "She built a successful business" is too vague to create foreknowledge worth carrying. "She signed 12 clients at $3K each from a list of 200" is specific enough that the viewer holds it as a lens through every beat that follows.

**Concrete sticks, abstract slides** (Heath): "122 leads at $0.68 each" has hooks everywhere — the number, the price, the specificity. "Amazing results from our platform" has nothing to grab. Sensory detail, specific numbers, named methods, particular people — these are what memory latches onto. Heath's Velcro theory explains why: concrete details are tiny hooks that memory latches onto from multiple angles, while abstract language is a smooth surface nothing sticks to. Precise numbers ("122 leads at $0.68") give memory dozens of hooks — the exactness, the contrast, the implied measurement. Rounded numbers ("hundreds of leads") and vague language ("great results") offer no hooks at all. When choosing between a precise detail and a rounded one, precise wins every time — not just for credibility, but for memorability.

**Emotional hooks are legitimate** (Aristotle): Emotions are judgments about the situation, not irrational noise. A hook that makes the viewer feel the cost of inaction is helping them accurately assess consequences — not manipulating them. The fear of wasting another six months is a real judgment about a real cost. Write emotional hooks without the "manipulation" anxiety. The only dishonest emotional hook is one that creates fear about something that isn't actually at stake.

**Belief vs desire in hooks (Albuquerque).** A hook that targets desire ("Want to lose weight?") competes with ten thousand other hooks because every competitor addresses the same desire — the prospect already wants the thing, and naming the want tells them nothing new. A hook that targets the blocking belief ("The reason your diet keeps failing has nothing to do with willpower") owns the conversation because it addresses something specific the prospect holds as true. Desire hooks are interchangeable across competitors. Belief hooks are specific to your mechanism. When evaluating a batch, check: is this hook naming what the prospect wants, or naming what the prospect believes that's preventing them from getting it? If it's desire, every competitor can run the same hook. If it's belief, only you can — because the belief connects to your specific mechanism and paradigm shift.

**One belief blocks the sale** (Albuquerque): Behind every scroll-past is a dominant belief the prospect holds as TRUE about solving their problem. Not a desire (they already have the desire) — a belief about HOW things work that makes your offer irrelevant before they read it. "The only way to get coaching clients is to build a huge following" — if that belief is intact, no hook about DM conversion lands. The strongest hooks don't just grab attention; they target the one belief that, once shifted, makes the sale feel inevitable. Hooks that shift the right belief outperform hooks that merely surprise, because surprise fades but a new mental model persists through the rest of the ad.

## Choose Your Hook: The Lead Spectrum

**Objective-first** (Mamet): Before selecting a hook architecture, write the hook's one-sentence objective — what it must accomplish. Not a vague goal like "get attention" but a specific job: "select business owners who spend on ads," "create a curiosity gap about conversion rates," "qualify coaches stuck below $5K/month." The objective determines the architecture, not the other way around. Reaching for an architecture before defining the objective is like choosing a camera angle before knowing what the shot needs to convey. Write the objective sentence. Then scan the 18 architectures and the awareness spectrum below. The architecture that serves the objective is the one to use — not the one that sounds clever, not the one that worked last time. If two architectures serve the objective equally, pick the one with less competition in the current market.

The 18 hooks below are FORMATS. But which format you reach for depends on how aware your audience is (Schwartz) and how sophisticated the market is. This is the strategic layer.

| Awareness Level | What they know | Best hook types | Why |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Most Aware** | Know you, ready to buy | Outrageous Offer (#4), Show Numbers (#17) | Just make the offer. Proof and deals close. |
| **Product-Aware** | Know your product, not convinced | I Got This Result (#3), Client B/A (#12), UGC (#13) | Prove it works. Results over claims. |
| **Solution-Aware** | Know solutions exist, don't know you | While Everyone Else (#7), Million Ways (#11), You Don't Need (#6) | Differentiate. Why you, not them. |
| **Problem-Aware** | Feel the pain, don't know solutions | If You (#5), Everyone Wants (#9), You Know You Should Be (#15) | Enter their pain. Name what they feel. |
| **Unaware** | Don't know they have a problem | Yesterday I (#16), Request (#1), Everyone Thinks (#10) | Sneak in sideways. Story or paradigm shift. |

**Traffic temperature changes hook register** (Brunson): Cold traffic needs pattern interrupts — something unexpected that stops the scroll because these people have never heard of you. Warm traffic can reference familiarity — "You grabbed the guide, here's what to do with it" — because they've already opted in or engaged. Hot traffic can be direct — "New workshop dropping Friday" — because the relationship already exists. Using a cold hook on hot traffic wastes the trust you've built; it feels like starting over with someone who already knows you. Using a hot hook on cold traffic wastes impressions; directness reads as presumption to a stranger. The awareness spectrum above maps the what — which hook type fits. Traffic temperature maps the how — what register and energy level the hook should carry. A "You Don't Need" hook (#6) aimed at cold traffic needs a pattern interrupt opening; the same architecture aimed at warm traffic can skip the interrupt and reference what they already know.

**Ethos-first for cold and high-ticket audiences** (Heinrichs): When the audience doesn't know you or the price demands trust before emotion, lead with a credibility signal before the emotional hook — a specific credential, a named result, a recognizable client. Heinrichs' sequence is ethos→pathos→logos: establish character first, then deploy emotion, then provide logic. A hook that opens with pathos to a cold audience triggers skepticism because they haven't granted you standing to make emotional claims yet. "I've built 200 funnels for SaaS companies" (ethos) before "and here's the one mistake killing yours" (pathos) earns the right to the emotional punch.

**Market sophistication modifier** (Schwartz): In Stage 1 markets, direct claims work — "Lose 10 pounds in 30 days." By Stage 5 (saturated), the same claim is invisible because everyone's made it. Saturated markets need identity, proof, or mechanism — not bigger claims. Research what claims have already been made before writing hooks.

**Archetype-matching** (Pearson): Different hook types resonate with different archetype stages. Orphan-stage prospects respond to validation and empathy ("You've tried X, Y, Z — none worked"). Wanderer-stage prospects respond to exploration and comparison ("There's a thousand ways to X, but this one..."). Warrior-stage prospects respond to competition and achievement ("While everyone else..."). When selecting hook architecture, match the dominant archetype stage of your target audience.

**Status-role hook selection (Godin):** Before choosing hook architecture, diagnose whether the audience plays a dominion game or an affiliation game. Dominion audiences seek to stand above peers — they respond to superiority hooks: "While Everyone Else" (#7), "You Don't Need" (#6), identity reframes that position them as smarter or more advanced than the market. Affiliation audiences seek to belong — they respond to belonging hooks: "If You" (#5), "Everyone Wants" (#9), identity hooks that name their tribe and invite membership. Using dominion hooks on an affiliation audience sounds arrogant. Using affiliation hooks on a dominion audience sounds soft. The diagnosis: does the buyer want to be seen as ahead of peers (dominion) or connected to peers (affiliation)? Founders scaling past competitors play dominion. Community builders and creative collectives play affiliation. The hook register must match the status game or it repels the audience it's trying to attract.

**High-arousal emotion check** (Berger): When the campaign goal includes sharing — not just clicks or conversions — score each hook's target emotion for arousal level before selecting. Awe, amusement, anger, and anxiety are high-arousal emotions that drive sharing; contentment and sadness are low-arousal and don't, even when they're strongly felt. A hook that makes the viewer feel warm and satisfied may convert on click but die on share. If virality matters, choose architectures that target high-arousal responses — paradigm shifts that trigger awe, clash-of-context hooks that trigger amusement, risk reframes that trigger anxiety.

**The offer-strength modifier:** A genuinely outrageous offer can go direct even to cold audiences. "I'll build your entire sales system or you don't pay" works even if they've never heard of you. The stronger the offer, the more direct you can be. Run Fladlien's offer-first test: state it in 2 sentences. If the reaction is "wait, really?" — the offer itself is the hook. If not, the hook has to do more work.

**The four reframes as hook angles (Fladlien):** Beyond the 18 architectures, these four reframing patterns generate hooks from any offer:
- **Price reframe:** Change the denominator. "$3/day for a system that replaces a $5K/month VA." When a hook mentions price, never state the raw number — frame it contextually: per day, versus the waste it prevents, or against a competitor's cost (Poundstone). Raw numbers trigger isolated evaluation where the viewer compares the price to zero; contextual framing triggers relative evaluation where the viewer compares the price to something worse they're already paying. "$2,000" feels expensive. "$2,000 — less than what you spent on ads that didn't work last month" feels like a correction.
- **Time reframe:** Show net time. "6 hours to set up. Saves 6 hours every week."
- **Identity reframe:** Connect to who they want to become. "This is what $50K/month operators do differently." Fladlien: "Almost all people have an impoverished identity. We go to war against it."
- **Risk reframe:** Make inaction the risk. "The risk isn't trying this. It's doing nothing for another 6 months." The strongest risk reframes compress a tragedy arc (Booker) into the hook — flaw, rationalization, consequence in three beats: "You know your outbound is broken. You tell yourself next quarter will be different. It won't." The prospect recognizes the flaw, cringes at the rationalization they've used themselves, and feels the consequence land. This works because tragedy is the plot of self-deception — the character could change but doesn't. When the prospect sees their own rationalization named, the hook becomes a mirror they can't look away from.

These work as standalone hooks or as the education/body of an ad that opened with one of the 18 architectures.

**When in doubt:** Problem-Solution hooks are the safest middle ground. But "safe" rarely wins. Test bolder choices.

---

**Sentence architecture for hooks (McKee: Dialogue).** Match hook architecture to the hook's job. Suspense architecture ("After testing 47 variations across 6 months — we found the one thing that moved the needle") for mystery/revelation hooks. Cumulative architecture ("Lost the deal. Lost the client. Lost the team. Then found the pattern.") for emotional escalation. Balanced architecture ("Other tools show you what happened. Ours shows you why.") for contrast hooks. The architecture is the skeleton — diagnose whether a hook falls flat because of weak copy or because the sentence structure doesn't match the hook's intended effect.

## The 18 Hook Architectures

Structural templates from Stanley's Unboring Ads. Adapt voice from the product context. The format is the skeleton — the product and voice are the flesh.

**Certainty channel tags** (Belfort): Each architecture opens a primary certainty channel — logical (the viewer's rational case), emotional (the viewer's felt experience), or state transfer (the speaker's confidence bleeds through). Match the channel to what the audience needs most. Cold audiences with high skepticism need logical first. Audiences who already believe but haven't acted need emotional. Audiences who trust the category but not the person need state transfer. The tags below mark each architecture's primary channel — many hooks blend channels, but the primary tells you what's doing the heavy lifting.

| Channel | Architectures |
|---|---|
| **Logical** | #3 I Got This Result, #4 Outrageous Offer, #17 Show Numbers, #18 In The Last |
| **Emotional** | #5 If You, #9 Everyone Wants, #12 Client B/A, #13 UGC, #15 You Know You Should Be, #16 Yesterday I |
| **State transfer** | #1 Request, #2 Do This Thing, #6 You Don't Need, #7 While Everyone Else, #8 You're Gonna Think I'm Lying, #10 Everyone Thinks, #11 Million Ways, #14 Would If |

### 1. The Request
"Give me [time] and I'll [promise]." Micro-commitment + specific promise.
→ "Give me 14 seconds and I'll show you why your DMs aren't converting"

**Audience contract:** This hook promises a revelation or insight in exchange for seconds of attention. The ad body must deliver a genuine insight, not generic advice — breaking the contract reads as clickbait.

### 2. The Do This Thing
Direct command with weird specificity. Authority + curiosity about WHY.
→ "Send this exact DM to the next person who likes your post"

### 3. I Got This Result
"I [result] doing [named method]." Proof + neologism creates intrigue gap.
→ "I found 200 qualified creators in 10 minutes using what I call the niche stack method"
**Key:** Invent a name for the method. Name they don't understand + result they want = can't stop watching.

**Audience contract:** This hook promises a replicable method behind the result. The ad body must reveal the mechanism — withholding it entirely or making it impossibly complex breaks the contract.

### 4. The Outrageous Offer
Lead with the offer if it's genuinely crazy. Risk reversal is the strongest conversion lever.
→ "I'll build your entire sales system or you don't pay"
**Rule:** Better offer = earlier reveal. Weak offer = build up to it.

### 5. The If You…
Qualifying call-out. Self-selection + algorithm targeting.
→ "If you're an online fitness coach posting every day but still can't break $5k/month..."
**Double function:** Qualifies the viewer AND tells the algorithm who to find.

### 6. You Don't Need
Challenge what they think is required. Relief + paradigm shift.
→ "You don't need a huge following to sign $2k coaching clients"

### 7. While Everyone Else
"While everyone else [wrong thing], you can [better thing]." In-group superiority.
→ "While everyone else is spending $500/month on influencer platforms, you can find better creators for free"

**Decoy variant** (Ariely): Present two bad options — expensive or slow — and position the target as the asymmetrically dominant third, so the CTA feels like an obvious escape rather than a pitch. "You can spend $5K/month on an agency. You can grind 4 hours a day doing it yourself. Or you can use the system that does it in 10 minutes." The two bad options aren't strawmen — they're the real alternatives the viewer is already weighing. The third option doesn't just win on one dimension; it wins on every dimension the viewer cares about. The contrast does the selling: the viewer doesn't evaluate the offer in isolation, they evaluate it relative to the two options they already know are painful. Any contrast hook ("While Everyone Else," "You Don't Need," "Everyone Thinks") can carry this three-option structure where two losing alternatives frame the third as the only rational choice.

### 8. You're Gonna Think I'm Lying
Pre-empt skepticism. Acknowledging the objection disarms it.
→ "You're gonna think I'm lying, but my worst-performing ad was the one I spent the most money producing"

### 9. Everyone Wants
"Everyone wants [X], but [obstacle]." Universal desire + validated frustration.
→ "Everyone wants to scale past $10k/month, but every time you try, something breaks"

### 10. Everyone Thinks
"Everyone thinks [false belief]." Then correct it. Paradigm shift.
→ "Everyone thinks you need more content to get more clients. You need better DMs."

**Ghost hooks:** Name the past experience that created the audience's current false belief. "The 2021 advice about posting daily that made everyone believe volume beats strategy." The ghost is the origin wound — reference it and you explain why they believe what they believe, which makes the paradigm shift feel empathetic instead of accusatory.

**Audience contract:** This hook promises a belief shift with evidence. The ad body must support the paradigm shift with proof or mechanism — stating the reframe without backing it violates the contract.

### 11. The Million Ways
"There's a thousand ways to [X], but this one [superlative]." Overwhelm reduction.
→ "There's a thousand tools for finding creators. This is the only one built for people who actually close deals."

### 12. Client Before/After
One person's specific transformation. Not statistics — one human, one story.
→ "She booked her first 3 sales calls the week she joined. She'd been posting for 6 months with zero."

Client proof hooks must establish the character's identity and struggle before the result — "a 55-year-old former teacher who'd tried three other tools and was about to give up" lands harder than "a small business owner" because specificity creates identification before the result creates desire (Clemens). The viewer needs to see themselves in the person before they can want the person's outcome. A result without a character is a claim. A character without a result is a story that goes nowhere. But character first, then result — the identification must precede the desire, because desire without identification produces skepticism ("good for them") while identification followed by desire produces wanting ("I could be them").

### 13. UGC Testimonial Montage
Rapid-fire multiple testimonials. Social proof through volume.
→ 5 people in 30 seconds each sharing one specific win. Best for retargeting.
**First-take energy is the UGC quality benchmark** (Caine): The quality that makes UGC hooks work is spontaneity — the feeling of genuine discovery, not rehearsed delivery. If it feels rehearsed, it's lost the thing that made the format work. Shoot the first take even if the framing is off. The first time a customer tells their story unprompted is almost always the most authentic version — subsequent takes gain polish but lose the unrehearsed truth that the audience reads as real. Over-coaching UGC talent ("bring more energy," "say it again with more excitement") drains the spontaneity that made UGC persuasive in the first place.
Even in rapid-fire montage, each testimonial clip should lead with the struggle before the win (Clemens). "I was a 55-year-old former teacher who'd tried three other tools" followed by "and I booked 4 clients in my first month" hits harder than opening with the result. The struggle creates identification — the viewer thinks "that's me" — and the result that follows rides on that identification rather than standing alone as a claim to evaluate.

### 14. Would [Blank] If [Blank]
"Would you [result] if [action]?" Forces them to imagine the outcome.
→ "Would you sign more coaching clients if every DM conversation followed a proven script?"

### 15. You Know You Should Be
"You know you should be [doing X], but you're not. Why?" Guilt + empathy + excuse removal.
→ "You know you should be testing more ads. But you don't have hooks, you don't have scripts, you're overthinking every word."

### 16. Yesterday I
"Yesterday I [result], here's how." Recency creates urgency.
→ "Yesterday I found 47 creators in a niche I thought had nobody. Here's the search I ran."
**Compressed Epiphany Bridge** (Brunson): Story hooks can compress the full origin-story arc into 3-5 seconds: "I was [backstory], hit [wall], then realized [epiphany]." → "I was spending $5k/month on ads, hit a wall at 30 leads, then realized my DMs were the real pipeline." This is the origin-story hook architecture — backstory creates identification, the wall creates tension, the epiphany creates the curiosity gap. The compression matters: in long-form, the bridge unfolds over minutes; in a hook, it must land in one breath. Cut the backstory to one clause, the wall to one image, the epiphany to one insight.

**Compress the five-second moment into the hook (Dicks).** A story-based hook isn't a story — it's the single moment of change, compressed to its minimum. "I stared at $47 in my Stripe account and realized I'd been building the wrong thing for two years" is a five-second moment in one sentence. It works because it's internal (a realization), specific (the $47, the two years), and grounded in a location (staring at a screen). The hook earns the story. A hook that opens with "Let me tell you about my journey" is a frame for a frame — just show the moment.

### 17. Show You The Numbers
"Let me show you exactly [the numbers]." Transparency + proof.
→ "Let me show you exactly how many coaching clients came from DMs last month"
**Counterintuitive statistics generate the strongest attention spikes** (Gallo). Data that contradicts the audience's assumptions — "The average cold email gets 1.7% reply rate. Our top template gets 11.3%. The difference is not what you think" — outperforms data that confirms expectations. The "that can't be right" reaction is a scroll-stopper because it creates an information gap the viewer needs to resolve. When using this architecture, lead with the stat that violates the assumption, not the one that validates it.

**The first number anchors everything** (Ariely): In any hook that involves price, cost, or quantified effort, the first number the viewer encounters sets the frame for every number that follows. A hook that opens with the expensive status quo — "$5,000/month on agencies" — anchors the viewer so that "$47/month" feels proportionally tiny, even if $47 would have felt expensive in isolation. The anchor doesn't need to be a direct price comparison — any large number contextualizing the problem's cost sets the frame. "200 hours of manual outreach" anchors so that "10 minutes" feels like a miracle. "50 cold emails for 2 replies" anchors so that "5 emails for 23 meetings" feels impossible and therefore fascinating. When writing hooks that will mention a price, cost, or time investment, always lead with the larger number. The viewer's brain doesn't evaluate the second number on its own merits — it evaluates it as a proportion of the first.

### 18. In The Last [Time Period]
"In the last [time], I [result]." Recency + proof.
→ "In the last 30 days, I've booked 12 sales calls without spending a dollar on ads"
**Critical:** Test word order. Lead with the result or the time period — one will outperform.

## After the Hook Lands

The hook stops the scroll. Now you need to keep them. This is where most ads die — the hook works but the next line lets them go.

**Structure the education section as a story arc, not a logical sequence (Clemens).** The body after the hook should follow character → problem → turning point → result, not claim → evidence → claim → evidence. The analytical brain generates objections in response to claims — "really? prove it" — but follows along with narrative because stories engage a different processing mode. A logical sequence invites evaluation at every step; a story arc carries the viewer through to the conclusion before the analytical brain catches up. "Most coaches fail because they don't have a system" is a claim that triggers "well, I have a system." "Sarah was posting 5 reels a day, burning out, getting zero DMs — until she stopped optimizing content and started optimizing conversations" is a narrative the viewer follows without resistance. When the education section reads as a series of claims, every line is a potential exit. When it reads as a story, the viewer needs to see how it ends.

**Hook-to-body payoff discipline** (Howard & Mabley): The hook plants a question or concept that the body MUST pay off. A hook that promises "the weird reason your ads stopped converting" must deliver that reason — not pivot to a testimonial, not swerve into a feature list, not substitute a different insight. The hook and its payoff are a contract: the hook writes the check, the body cashes it. When the body fails to pay off the specific promise the hook made, the viewer feels cheated even if the body content is good — because it's not the content they were promised. Before writing the body, restate what the hook promised. Then verify the body delivers exactly that. If the body would work equally well under a different hook, the payoff isn't specific enough.

**Hook exit timing (Murch: blink as cut).** The hook ends at the viewer's cognitive completion point — the instant curiosity fires. One extra beat after that and the hook deflates because the viewer's attention has already moved to "tell me more" while the ad is still setting up. The hook writes the check (Howard & Mabley above), and the moment that check registers in the viewer's mind is the cut point. Not one beat before (the curiosity hasn't fired yet) and not one beat after (the curiosity has fired and is decaying while you're still hooking). Time the transition from hook to body at the apex of the viewer's "wait, what?" response.

### The Slippery Slide (Sugarman)
Every line exists for one reason: to compel the next line. If the reader can stop after any sentence and feel satisfied, rewrite that sentence.

**First sentence after the hook should be short and incomplete.** "Here's what nobody tells you." "It comes down to one thing." "I was wrong." These force the next line.

**Plant seeds of curiosity** — forward references that create open loops. "There are three systems. Miss one and everything breaks." Now they need to know the three. Don't close this loop for at least 2-3 more lines.

**Two curiosity mechanisms, not one** (Bachelard). Open loops pull through incompleteness — the viewer needs to know what happens next. Sealed containers pull through implied value — the viewer senses something valuable is enclosed and wants to open it. "Here's the mistake I made" is an open loop. "What most coaches get wrong about lead gen" is a sealed container. Use open loops for stories. Use sealed containers for insider knowledge. They feel different to the viewer and serve different hook architectures.

### Paint the Picture
Don't describe the product. Describe their life AFTER using it. In sensory detail. Concrete detail is everything (Heath) — the brain can't ignore specific images.

**The hook grabs with the goal. The body holds with the desire** (Suber). The hook addresses the stated goal — the measurable, logical thing they'd type into Google: "I got 47 leads in 30 days." The body/education section addresses the desire — the identity shift underneath: "I stopped feeling like a fraud." These are almost never the same thing, and the gap between them is where emotional weight lives. If the hook and body both address the goal, the ad feels transactional. If both address the desire, the ad feels vague. Goal opens. Desire holds.

**Product description (won't convert):** "Our mentorship includes content systems, sales frameworks, and fulfillment templates."

**Mental image — goal level (starts the picture):** "Imagine opening your phone tomorrow and seeing 4 DMs from people who want to hire you as their coach."

**Mental image — desire level (completes the picture):** "You haven't chased anyone. They came to you. And for the first time, your business feels like it actually works." The first image addresses the goal (more clients). The second addresses the desire (feeling like a real business owner). Paint the picture at the desire level — that's where the sale happens. The strongest after-state hooks paint the picture in sensory detail — what they see, hear, feel in a specific moment (Collier). Not "get more clients" but the phone buzzing at lunch, the inbox with four names in it, the Monday morning where they don't dread opening Stripe. When the viewer pictures themselves inside that moment, they've psychologically committed before the ad even makes the pitch. The mental image does the selling; the CTA just gives them a door to walk through.

The hook's job is to create a vision the viewer is already emotionally inside before the body copy begins -- once they feel themselves in the after-state, the rational mind's job switches from evaluation to justification, and that switch is when the ad stops being something to resist and becomes something to act on (Camp).

**Identify the set piece beat before writing the script (McPhee).** For video ad scripts, find the set piece — the single most vivid, emotionally loaded moment — at roughly 50-60% of the way through before writing anything else. This is the scene the viewer will remember: the founder deleting their ad account, the dashboard hitting a number, the moment the old way breaks. Write that moment fully first. Then write the hook that earns it and the CTA that follows from it. Most scripts are written linearly — hook first, body second, CTA last — and the emotional peak lands wherever the writer runs out of steam. Writing the set piece first guarantees the script has a destination, and every beat before it builds toward something specific rather than accumulating without direction.

The sale happens when they PICTURE themselves in the after-state. Logic justifies the decision their imagination already made.

**Anchor against the cost of the problem before revealing price** (Poundstone): When the hook transitions toward an offer, the first number the viewer sees becomes the anchor that every subsequent number is judged against. Make that first number the cost of the problem — wasted ad spend, lost revenue, months of stalled growth — not your price. "You're spending $3,000/month on ads that don't convert" anchors at $3,000, so a $497 system feels like a fraction of what they're already losing. If your price appears before the problem cost, the viewer anchors on the price in isolation and judges it against nothing — which always feels expensive.

**Calibrate the idealization** (Goffman): The mental image of the after-state is idealization — the best achievable version of life with the product. If the painted picture is too far from what the product actually delivers, you create an idealization gap that collapses on first contact. The prospect buys the vision, receives the reality, and feels deceived. Paint the realistic best case, not a fantasy. "Imagine opening your phone tomorrow to 4 DMs from people who want to hire you" works if clients actually get DMs. "Imagine waking up to a six-figure pipeline every morning" doesn't if the average result is three leads a week.

### Shareability Check (for Viral Campaigns)
When the goal is shares — not just conversions — check two things before finalizing the ad. First, does sharing this ad make the sharer look good? Berger's Social Currency principle: people share things that make them appear smart, funny, or in-the-know, so the ad needs to give the sharer status, not just the viewer information. Second, is the brand load-bearing in the story? If someone can retell the hook without mentioning the product — "I saw this crazy stat about DMs" — the brand gets shared off. The product must be structurally necessary to the punchline, the proof, or the revelation so that retelling it requires naming it.

**Share trigger evaluation (Kane):** Every hook should pass the identity question: "Would someone share this? And what does sharing it say about them?" People share content that signals who they are — smart, contrarian, ahead of the curve, part of a tribe. A hook that makes the sharer look good gets zero-cost distribution on top of paid reach. Run every hook through this filter: if someone screenshots this and posts it to their story, what identity are they performing? If the answer is nothing — if sharing it doesn't say anything about the sharer — the hook will convert but won't spread. The hooks that spread are the ones where sharing IS the value proposition for the viewer. This isn't just for "viral campaigns" — even conversion-focused hooks benefit from organic sharing because every share is a trusted endorsement the algorithm rewards.

### The Linking Technique (Fladlien)

From hypnosis: **Fact. Fact. Claim.** "You're running ads every day (fact). Your cost per lead keeps climbing (fact). The DM conversion framework fixes that permanently (claim)." By preceding a claim with two verifiable facts, the claim inherits their credibility. Powerful in the education section — set up your key selling point with two statements the reader already agrees with.

**Callbacks in multi-touch sequences** (Vorhaus): When running ad sequences or retargeting campaigns, reference hooks, phrases, or metaphors from earlier ads for recognition and cohesion. "Remember the tax attorney in the tiki bar from last week? That's your customer right now." The callback rewards attention, creates narrative continuity across touchpoints, and makes the prospect feel like they're in an ongoing conversation rather than seeing disconnected ads.

### Micro-Commitments in Longer Copy

For longer-form ads (YouTube, Facebook, email-style), weave tag questions and check-ins (Fladlien): "...right?" "...see what I mean?" "Makes sense, doesn't it?" Each micro-yes makes the CTA easier. Don't use the hackneyed "Who wants to make more money?" Instead: "Wouldn't it be nice if your pipeline filled itself?" Use 1-2 per ad, positioned after a key claim.

**Hard vs. soft commitments (Fladlien):** Hard commitments are tag questions embedded in narration that assume agreement: "makes sense, right?" "you've seen this before, haven't you?" Soft commitments are genuine check-ins that feel conversational: "sound familiar?" "ever notice that?" Hard commitments push; soft commitments invite. Use soft commitments early in the ad when trust is low, hard commitments later after you've delivered value. The lazy version — "who wants more money?" "wouldn't you love financial freedom?" — feels manipulative because it demands agreement on something too obvious to be a real question. Real micro-commitments ask for agreement on a specific insight you just delivered, not a universal desire.

### Problem → Agitate → Solve
If using a Problem-Solution lead, don't rush from problem to solution. AGITATE.

**Problem:** "You're posting content every day but nobody's DMing you about coaching."

**Agitate — external:** "And it's getting worse. Every month, more coaches enter your niche. Some know less than you. But they're signing clients because they have a system for turning views into conversations."

**Agitate — internal** (Suber): "And you're starting to wonder if you're just not cut out for this." The agitation should escalate from external obstacle to internal obstacle. External agitation names the situation getting worse. Internal agitation names what the viewer feels but won't say out loud — the fear, the self-doubt, the identity threat. The internal obstacle is what makes someone stop scrolling, because it names the thing they carry alone. "Your outbound isn't working" is external. "And you're starting to wonder if the problem is you" is internal. Competitors who only agitate externally never create the "they get me" response. Miller takes this further with three problem levels: the external problem (the tangible situation — "I can't get clients"), the internal problem (how it makes them feel — "I feel invisible"), and the philosophical problem (why it's wrong — "Coaches who actually help people deserve a real business"). The strongest hooks often skip the external entirely and target the internal or philosophical level. "That feeling when you post daily and hear nothing" is an internal hook — it names the emotional experience. "People who do great work shouldn't have to beg for attention" is a philosophical hook — it names a moral injustice. Both outperform "Get more clients" because the external problem is the one every competitor is already naming. The internal and philosophical levels are where the viewer feels uniquely understood.

**Solve:** "There are three things connecting their content to clients. You're missing at least one."

The agitation is where the emotional weight lives. Without it, the solution feels optional.

**Name why they can't walk away** (Egri). The strongest pain hooks target problems the viewer is locked into — not problems they could shrug off. "You can't stop prospecting." "You can't fire your clients." "You can't pretend the pipeline isn't shrinking." These hit harder than hooks about optional improvements because the viewer can't dismiss them. "Want more leads?" is an invitation they can decline. "You can't stop prospecting, and every week without a system costs you 10 hours you'll never get back" is a structural constraint they're already living inside. When writing pain hooks, ask: is this problem optional or inescapable? If the viewer could simply decide not to care, the hook has no teeth. The problems locked in by economics, identity, obligation, or competitive pressure — those are the ones that create tension the viewer can't scroll past.

### Compression Test (Seger)
Read every line of the ad aloud. If you'd never say it in conversation, cut it. Every sentence that doesn't advance toward desire or action is an exit ramp — the reader's attention is looking for one. After the hook section is drafted, run the compression pass before anything else. The lines that survive are the ad.

**Sound/texture test (Luntz).** Read every hook aloud and listen to the opening syllable. Plosive consonants — "b," "p," "d," "t," "k," "g" — create a percussive punch that stops the scroll. Sibilant openings — "s," "sh," "soft c" — slide past. "Break your pipeline" hits harder than "Streamline your pipeline" before the brain processes the meaning. When two hooks test equally on concept, the one with the harder opening sound wins.

**Match pacing to plot type even in compressed form** (Tobias): Pursuit hooks accelerate — short sentences getting shorter, momentum building. Discovery hooks build — observations stacking toward the reveal. Even in 8 seconds, the pacing itself carries emotional weight. A Discovery hook that rushes the reveal feels empty; a Pursuit hook that pauses for reflection loses urgency.

**Tempo escalation within short-form ads** (Mackendrick): Even a 15-second ad has three acts, and each should be faster than the last. The first third establishes tempo — sets the rhythm the viewer's nervous system locks onto. The second third accelerates — shorter sentences, quicker cuts, rising stakes. The final third is fastest — compressed, direct, driving into the CTA. This micro-escalation carries the viewer to the action because deceleration at any point gives them a natural exit. "You posted 5 reels this week. Zero DMs." (established tempo) → "Meanwhile, coaches with half your following are closing clients every week." (acceleration — shorter clause, higher stakes) → "Three systems. That's the gap. Link in bio." (fastest — fragments, direct, CTA). The anti-pattern is uniform tempo: every sentence the same length, every beat the same weight. Uniform tempo is a flatline — the viewer's attention has no momentum carrying it forward. Escalation creates a gravity that pulls the viewer through the ad even when they meant to scroll past.

### The Damaging Admission (Cialdini, Kennedy)
Admit one real limitation. Voluntarily. Before they discover it. Pre-suasion principle: willingness to share bad news makes your good news credible.

"This isn't cheap. $2,000. If you want a $47 course, this isn't it."
"We have 20,000 creators. Not 2 million. We index creators who sell things, not everyone on the internet."

If you only say positive things, everything sounds like marketing.

**The admission is a trust accelerator, not a concession** (Kennedy): A damaging admission upfront makes every claim that follows more believable — the reader's internal logic is "if they'd admit THAT, the rest must be true." This works across hook types, not just in the education section. An "I Got This Result" hook that opens with a limitation before the result hits harder: "It took me 6 months of terrible ads before I cracked this — 47 leads last week at $0.82 each." A paradigm shift hook that admits the old way used to work for you too earns the right to say it doesn't anymore. The admission doesn't weaken the ad; it lowers the reader's defenses so everything after it lands without the "yeah, right" filter. The test: if removing the admission makes the ad sound better to YOU but less believable to THEM, keep it.

## Ad Copy Structure

Every ad has two parts: the **creative** (video) and the **copy** (written text). Same video with different copy can perform 10x differently. Stanley proved this: same video, 4 copy versions — winner got 122 leads at $0.68, losers got 0 leads.

### The 3-Section Model

**The complete architecture is Hook/Story/Offer** (Brunson). The hook earns the first 3 seconds. But a hook without story and offer is an attention grab with no payoff. The hook stops the scroll in the first line or frame; the story in the body creates connection, shifts a belief, and builds emotional investment; the offer in the CTA gives them somewhere to go. Weak ads skip the story — they go hook to offer and feel like a cold pitch. The 3-section model below maps directly: Section 1 is the hook, Section 2 is the story, Section 3 is the offer. Each section earns the next. Remove the story and you're asking a stranger to buy. Remove the offer and you entertained someone who never acts.

**Section 1: Hook (90% of your effort)**
Use one of the 18 architectures. This is the opening line of the written copy AND the first 3-5 seconds of the video script.

**Section 2: Education/Value (the Story)**
Teach ONE concept. Make them think "oh, that was actually useful." Follow the slippery slide — every line compels the next. Use the mental image and PAS structure here. This is where the story lives — not "story" as in fiction, but the narrative arc that connects the hook's promise to the CTA's ask. The story creates the belief shift that makes the offer feel inevitable instead of random.
- Stories about ONE person, not statistics
- Keep tight for social ads (2-4 sentences). Longer for YouTube.
- This section develops the selling concept from the hook — it doesn't introduce a new topic.

**Name each section's intention as a verb (Weston).** The hook's intention isn't "introduce the topic" — it's "make them feel caught" or "interrupt their assumption." The story section's intention isn't "provide value" — it's "make them realize their current approach is broken beyond self-repair." The CTA's intention isn't "get them to click" — it's "make staying where they are feel intolerable." When the intention is a verb the copy can play, the section has direction. When the intention is a label ("the education section"), the copy drifts.

- **Rhythm variation** (Seger, Collier): Vary sentence length deliberately — short sentence, longer build, short landing. All-staccato copy fatigues as fast as all-long-form. The rhythm itself carries energy: a short punch after a longer sentence feels like emphasis even before the reader processes the words. Hooks should sound like one person talking to another, not an institution broadcasting. Short. Then longer. Then a fragment. Then a question. Every sentence the same length is monotone even if the words are different — and monotone kills attention before the viewer consciously registers why they scrolled past.
- **Rule of three** (Vorhaus): When listing benefits, features, or examples, use pattern-then-break — two beats establish the pattern, the third delivers the twist or payoff. "Faster setup. Easier onboarding. A support team that actually replies." The first two set expectations; the third lands the hook.

**Tonal shifts within the ad** (Belfort): Each section runs on a different tonal register. The problem section runs on empathy — "I know what this feels like" energy, not authority. The mechanism/education section shifts to authority + reasonable man — "here's how this actually works, and it's simpler than you think." The CTA shifts to declarative certainty — no hedging, no softening, direct conviction. The anti-pattern is monotone copy: same tonal register from hook to CTA. Monotone authority sounds like a lecture. Monotone empathy sounds like commiseration without a path forward. Monotone hype sounds like an infomercial. The shift itself carries persuasive force — empathy earns trust, authority delivers the mechanism, certainty closes the gap. Read the ad aloud and listen for the gear changes. If it sounds the same all the way through, the tone isn't shifting.

**Tone vs mood — two separate dials** (Lumet): The mood is what the viewer feels — discomfort, curiosity, urgency, recognition. The tone is the speaker's stance toward the material — conspiratorial, clinical, empathetic, irreverent. These are independent choices, and collapsing them produces flat hooks. A pain-point hook has a mood of discomfort. But a conspiratorial tone ("here's what nobody's telling you about why your DMs are dead") lands on a different audience than a clinical tone ("the data shows a 3% reply rate on unsegmented outreach") or an empathetic tone ("I know what it's like to post every day and hear nothing back"). Same mood, three completely different hooks that attract three different people. When generating a batch, hold the mood constant and vary the tone across hooks — or hold the tone constant and vary the mood. The combination you choose shapes who responds. Most default copy collapses tone and mood into one register ("urgent and urgent," "empathetic and sad") because it never separates the two dials. The gap between tone and mood is where distinctive hooks live.

**Section 3: CTA (The Snapper)**
Not just "click here." Give a real reason to act NOW.
- Use the CTA from the brief/context
- Real urgency: capacity limits, deadline, cost of waiting. Not fake countdown timers.
- Soft CTAs ("Worth a look?") can outperform hard CTAs ("Book now") — test both
- End with what they'll LOSE by waiting, not just what they'll gain by acting: "Every week without a system, coaches who know less than you are signing clients you could have had."
- **"Free" is not a price — it's an emotional override** (Ariely): When a hook leads to a zero-cost CTA (free audit, free trial, free download), emphasize what they're getting, not what they're saving. "Free" doesn't mean the viewer runs a cost-benefit calculation and finds the cost acceptable — it means the cost-benefit calculation never runs at all. The word "free" removes the entire deliberation step that every other price triggers. This is why hooks leading to free offers should load the value side: "Get the full 47-page playbook — free" outperforms "Save $97 — get it free" because the first version fills the space where deliberation would have been with value imagery, while the second version reintroduces the very cost-benefit frame that "free" was supposed to bypass.
- **The subtext-to-direct transition** (Seger): The problem and education sections work through subtext — showing behavior, painting pictures, letting the viewer draw conclusions. The CTA shifts to direct statement: do this thing now. This transition from indirect to explicit is where the hook converts. If you stay subtextual through the CTA, nobody acts. If you go direct too early, they feel sold to. The shift itself carries force — directness hits harder after restraint.

### CVCS for Long-Form Ad Scripts (60s+)

For longer video ads (60s+ YouTube, UGC scripts, VSL-style), each section of the education/body should move through four layers (Fladlien): **Context** (why this matters now — market shift, emerging problem, timing), **Vision** (what the result looks like — paint the after-state), **Commitment** (implied agreement — a micro-commitment that locks in the vision), **Strategy** (mechanism preview — how it works without giving away the full system). CVCS prevents the common failure of long ads that hook well but lose viewers mid-way. Without it, the body becomes an info-dump or a rambling testimonial. Each CVCS cycle re-earns attention for the next 15-20 seconds.

**Re-hook cadence for video ads longer than 15 seconds** (Cannell & Travis): Attention doesn't sustain — it renews or dies. Every 10-15 seconds needs a visual cut, tonal shift, or new question to prevent mid-roll decay. A CVCS cycle naturally creates re-hook points, but even within a single cycle, the camera angle should change, the speaker's energy should shift, or a new open loop should fire. If you can watch 15 seconds of the script without encountering a fresh reason to keep watching, viewers won't keep watching either.

**Critical strategy** (Stanley): Record all three sections separately for video. Mix and match hooks with education sections without re-recording everything. Test different hooks with the same education. Test different education with the same hook.

## Pattern Interrupts

Hooks grab with words. Pattern interrupts grab with the unexpected. Layer them. The pattern interrupt jars the croc brain (Klaff) out of its default scroll state and forces it to pay attention. But a pattern interrupt without a curiosity gap is a flash that fades (Heath). The interrupt stops the scroll; the gap keeps them watching. A bizarre visual or jarring opening that doesn't plant an unresolved question gets a half-second of attention and nothing more. Pair every pattern interrupt with an open loop — the interrupt is the door, the gap is the reason to walk through it.

**Vibrating boundaries for the 3-second hook** (Albers): When two elements of equal visual weight but maximum difference are placed adjacent — a bold claim against a contrasting background, a before/after split with equal-intensity opposing colors — the boundary vibrates and the eye cannot scroll past (Albers: Interaction of Color). This is the visual equivalent of a pattern interrupt. Use for the first frame of video ads or the hero image of static ads. The constraint: vibrating boundaries are exhausting over sustained exposure — use them for the 3-second hook moment only, then resolve to a stable composition for the body of the ad. An entire ad that vibrates is unreadable. One vibrating moment that arrests scrolling, followed by calm delivery, is the pattern.

**Thumbnail as hook** (Cannell & Travis): For video ads with a preview frame — YouTube pre-roll, autoplay-off placements, embedded video — the still frame shown before play must itself function as a pattern interrupt. If the thumbnail looks like every other ad in the feed, the video never gets played and the hook never fires. The thumbnail is the hook for the hook: high-contrast, curiosity-inducing, readable at thumbnail scale. Text on thumbnails should be 3-5 words maximum. A great script behind a generic thumbnail is a tree falling in an empty forest.

**Figure-ground contrast for feed-stopping** (Arnheim): In a social feed, every post competes for figure status against the feed's ground (Arnheim). Your ad must register as unmistakable figure through extreme contrast, bold shapes, and clear spatial boundaries — not through complexity or detail. A simple bold shape with high contrast against the feed's background reads as figure instantly. A detailed, nuanced composition gets absorbed into the feed's ground and gets scrolled past. Design the first frame of every ad to win the figure-ground competition: bright against dark, large simple shape against small complex surroundings, isolated element against dense feed. The Albers vibrating boundary hooks attention; the figure-ground principle ensures the ad registers as a distinct object worth attending to.

**First-frame color priming (Bellantoni):** The first 0.5 seconds of a video ad is pure color impression — the viewer hasn't processed text, face, or movement yet. If the color palette doesn't match the emotional payload of the hook, alignment is lost before the hook fires. Urgency hooks need warm, saturated environments. Trust/authority hooks need cool, desaturated environments. Transformation hooks need purple or warm-to-cool shifts. Community/belonging hooks need orange-warm tones. For static ads, the same applies: the dominant color is the first argument the ad makes. Counterintuitively, desaturated ad creative can outperform saturated creative in a saturated feed — it breaks the pattern through calm in a noisy environment. The anti-pattern: accepting whatever color palette the AI tool generates without specifying the emotional direction.

**Selective component contrast (Block):** The most effective scroll-stopping visuals use contrast in one or two components against affinity in the rest. A black-and-white image (tonal contrast, color affinity) with a single saturated element (color contrast) works because it's selectively intense. A bright subject on a dark background (tonal contrast) in a calm horizontal composition (spatial affinity) works because the contrast is focused. Everything at maximum — diagonal composition, saturated colors, bold type, high tonal contrast, fast movement — produces visual noise where nothing leads. One component screaming against a calm background creates a focal point. Everything screaming creates a wall. The Albers vibrating boundary and Arnheim figure-ground principles are specific applications of this: they create selective contrast in one dimension while the rest stays controlled.

**Whitespace as positioning signal (Müller-Brockmann).** Whitespace in ad creative communicates price point before the number does. Luxury brands use maximum whitespace. Discount brands fill every pixel. A headline with generous space around it commands more attention in a feed than a headline crammed between images. Even a 1080×1080 ad should have an underlying grid with deliberate empty zones — the emptiness is working. If the offer is high-ticket, the amount of empty space signals premium before the copy does.

**Animation sequencing in video ads (Head).** Multi-element animated ads need choreographed reveal order — attention-grabber first, context second, offer third, CTA last. The CTA should have the final and most distinctive animation (scale, pulse, glow) because it's the transition from watching to acting. All elements animating simultaneously creates visual noise the eye can't parse. The stagger creates a 3-5 second mini-story arc.

**The peripheral vision filter (Barden).** The hook is encountered peripherally — low resolution, like looking through smeared glass. At this resolution, the autopilot detects color, shape, contrast, and rough spatial patterns. It cannot read text. If the peripheral signal does not trigger relevance, the eyes never move to focus. Test all ad creative at squint distance: blur until text is illegible. What remains? If what remains looks like 'an ad,' the autopilot's marketing filter kills it before the hook has a chance. This is upstream of every other visual direction — the Mercado framing, the Brown lighting, the Block contrast all serve nothing if the peripheral filter rejects the ad before focused attention arrives.

**Visual (for video ads):**
- Doing something unrelated while talking (eating, walking, solving a puzzle) — Stanley's "two things at once" strategy creates two layers of interest
- Unusual location (car is inexplicably effective, weird angle, floor)
- Props that make no sense in context — but choose them for the meaning they carry, not just visual chaos (Seger). Props and settings are symbolic: a whiteboard says "teacher," a warehouse says "operator," a messy desk says "in the trenches." The wrong symbol undercuts the hook before the words land.
- Starting mid-action, no intro — **the moment before** (Caine): "I just found out something about..." enters mid-life. "Hey guys, today I want to share..." starts from zero. Give talent a moment before — something that happened just before recording that they carry into the take. The viewer won't know the backstory, but they'll feel the energy. The moment before creates the illusion the viewer is interrupting a real life, not watching a performance that began when the camera turned on.
- Low production signals "person talking to me" — high production signals "ad" and triggers the filter
- **Scale it down for video** (Caine): Video hooks shot at presentation scale feel like ads. Hooks shot at conversational scale feel like a person talking to you. Direct talent to talk to one person, not an audience — whatever energy they think is right, cut it in half. The camera is a microscope; it magnifies every gesture and vocal push. A slight pause replaces a shout. Conversational intimacy is the register that reads as authentic on screen.
- **Direct talent with playable actions, not emotional results (Weston).** "Be excited about the product" produces performed excitement the camera detects instantly. "Show them the thing you couldn't believe when you first found it" produces genuine energy because the talent has something to DO. "Be relatable" is unplayable — "admit the thing you're embarrassed about" is playable. Every on-camera direction should be a verb the talent can execute: dare, confess, challenge, reveal, interrupt. The emotion arrives as a byproduct of the action. When writing video ad scripts, include a one-line direction per section in the format "PLAY: [verb + object]" — e.g., "PLAY: catch them doing the thing they know isn't working." This gives talent (or AI video direction) an intention that produces authentic energy instead of manufactured enthusiasm.
- **Lens choice is emotional distance** (Brown): A wide lens (24mm) puts the viewer IN the scene — energetic, immersive, slightly distorted, perfect for UGC-style hooks where proximity = authenticity. A 50mm approximates human vision — neutral, trustworthy, good for talking-head authority hooks. An 85mm compresses the background and isolates the speaker — intimate, flattering, cinematic, ideal for testimonial and emotional hooks. When scaling a winning hook to higher production, specifying the lens is the single highest-leverage visual direction you can give a videographer.
- **Shot size is emotional distance, independent of lens** (Mercado): ECU (extreme close-up) for raw intensity and critical detail — the Stripe notification, the tear, the finger hitting "publish." CU (close-up) for empathy and connection — the default talking-head hook frame. MCU (medium close-up) for conversational intimacy — head and shoulders, the natural UGC frame. MS (medium shot) for casual authority — founder at a whiteboard, gestures visible. WS (wide shot) for context and scale — the warehouse, the office, the environment that tells the story. Shot size and lens choice are independent variables: a CU on a 50mm reads neutral and intimate; a CU on a 24mm reads immersive and slightly distorted. Specify both when directing video.
- **Framing architecture** (Mercado): Where the subject sits in the frame carries meaning before a word is spoken. *Headroom:* tight headroom creates pressure and urgency; generous headroom signals calm authority. *Lead room:* subject looking toward empty space reads as forward momentum and possibility; subject looking toward the frame edge reads as trapped or cornered. *Looking room:* the subject's gaze direction creates implied space the viewer fills with expectation — a founder looking screen-left toward a dashboard implies the results are coming. The webcam-on-laptop default often wastes headroom and ignores lead room entirely — both are free compositional tools.
- **Short-side framing for tension hooks** (Mercado): Place the subject on the side of the frame they're NOT looking toward. This breaks the looking-room convention and creates compositional unease — the viewer feels something is wrong before they can articulate why. Use for disruption hooks, confrontation hooks, or any hook where tension is the opening emotion. The composition itself becomes a pattern interrupt. Resolve to conventional framing when the hook transitions to the body or CTA — sustained short-side framing exhausts the viewer the same way sustained vibrating boundaries do (Albers).
- **Camera angle is power geometry** (Mercado): Low angle (camera below eye level) = dominant, expert, authoritative — use for result hooks, authority hooks, and any frame where the speaker should project power. Eye level = peer, relatable, conversational — the default for story hooks and UGC. High angle (camera above eye level) = vulnerable, diminished, seeking help — use for problem-state hooks and before-state footage. Dutch angle (tilted horizon) = instability and disruption — use sparingly, it reads as gimmicky fast. The webcam-on-laptop default is a slight high angle, which subtly diminishes the speaker — propping the laptop up to eye level is the simplest production upgrade for talking-head hooks.
- What you DO on camera is gestural subtext (Seger) — scrolling a dashboard, packing orders, marking up a whiteboard all communicate expertise through behavior, not credentials. The viewer reads the action and draws their own conclusion about competence. Action is the trustworthy channel because it's harder to fake than words.
- **Open on the decisive moment, not the setup** (Glebas): Frame 1 should capture the peak of the hook's story beat. Don't open with "Let me tell you what happened" — open with the dashboard showing 47 leads. The decisive moment is the image that needs no caption. If the viewer needs to hear the voiceover to understand the hook, the visual isn't doing its job. This is the video equivalent of starting mid-action: skip the windup, show the payoff, then explain.
- **Visual narrative — one detail triggers the inference** (Mackendrick): Select the single visual detail that makes the viewer construct the meaning you want. A close-up of someone deleting an app and smiling says "freedom" without narration. A founder packing boxes in a garage while a Stripe notification pings says "real growth, no pretense." The specific image is the prompt; the abstract meaning is the viewer's job. This is visual narrative compressed to ad scale — you don't narrate the conclusion, you show the one concrete detail that makes the conclusion inevitable. The test: mute the video. Does the first visual alone tell a story? If the image requires voiceover to carry meaning, it's illustration, not narrative. Illustration supports what's being said. Visual narrative says something the words don't — and the two channels working independently deliver more than either could alone.
- **Visual proof hierarchy** (Glebas): Replace narrated claims with visual evidence. "I grew 40%" becomes a screen recording scrolling through results. "Clients love us" becomes a screenshot of an actual client message. Hierarchy of proof in video: real screen recording > real photo > custom graphic > text on screen. Move up this hierarchy at every opportunity. A claim the viewer can SEE on screen beats a claim they only hear — because seeing is their own verification, not your assertion.
- **Lighting setup matches the emotional beat** (Brown): Testimonial segments get soft, high-key lighting (trustworthy, open). Problem-state hooks get low-key, high-contrast lighting (tension, unease). Product reveals get clean, motivated lighting from a practical source (the laptop glow, the window). Naming the setup — Rembrandt for one-light drama, butterfly for beauty, broad for friendly — gives production teams or AI film tools specific direction instead of "good lighting." The key-to-fill ratio is the single most important lighting decision: 2:1 is soft and friendly, 8:1 is dramatic and tense.
- **Camera movement is grammar** (Brown): A slow dolly push toward the speaker during a reveal creates intimacy and escalating importance. A tracking shot alongside the speaker walking creates journey and companionship. Handheld creates documentary immediacy and energy. Static creates authority and confidence. Match the movement to the hook type — result hooks benefit from static authority, story hooks from tracking movement, curiosity hooks from a slow push that mirrors the viewer leaning in. "Camera moves forward" is not a direction — dolly, zoom, and Steadicam advance all produce different emotional effects.
- **Reaction-based hooks — show the reaction, not just the result** (Caine): A face registering genuine surprise at a dashboard is more compelling than a screenshot of the dashboard. The audience watches how results land on a person, not just the results themselves. In film, the reaction shot is the most powerful shot because it shows the EFFECT of the story — and effect is what the viewer is tracking. Structure reaction hooks by showing a real person seeing their numbers, opening an inbox, reading a testimonial for the first time. The unscripted pause, the widening eyes, the involuntary smile — these are proof the viewer trusts because they can't be faked at scale. Any result hook (#3, #12, #17, #18) can be elevated by showing the human reaction to the result instead of just the result itself.
- **POV three-beat sequence** (Katz): The full identification architecture for video hooks — three shots that force the viewer to experience the discovery rather than observe it. Shot 1: the prospect in their situation (looking at their dashboard, checking their inbox, staring at their pipeline — the "looking" shot). Shot 2: what they see, from their approximate position (the empty inbox, the flat graph, the product result — the "see" shot). Shot 3: their reaction (the exhale, the grin, the "wait, what?" — the "react" shot). Caine's reaction hooks cover the reaction beat; Katz's POV sequence adds the full three-beat architecture that creates identification BEFORE the reaction. The viewer doesn't just see the reaction — they experience what caused it. Any result hook, discovery hook, or transformation hook gains power from the POV structure because the audience processes the discovery in sequence rather than being shown the conclusion.
- **Parallel action as intercut before/after** (Katz): Instead of showing all "before" and then all "after" in a video ad, intercut between the two worlds with accelerating frequency — 3 seconds of struggle, 3 seconds of success, 2 seconds of struggle, 2 seconds of success, 1 second each until the contrast becomes undeniable. The intercutting builds tension through comparison; the acceleration creates urgency. The convergence point — where the two worlds collide — is the product reveal or CTA. This is more effective than sequential before/after because the viewer carries both states simultaneously and feels the gap viscerally. The accelerating frequency is the cinematic technique that makes the audience feel "I can't stay in the before-world any longer."
- **Master-shot-first sequencing** (Katz): When a video hook introduces a new scenario or location, a brief establishing wide shot (even 1-2 seconds) orients the viewer in the space before the tight hook detail creates curiosity. A CU of a face reacting means nothing if the viewer doesn't know whose face or where they are. This is distinct from shot size (Mercado — which covers what each size does emotionally) — it's about the sequence: establish geography, then fragment into detail. Skip the master and every subsequent close-up floats in undefined space. For hooks under 5 seconds, the master can be as brief as a half-second wide flash — just enough to anchor the geography before the tight detail lands.

**Audio (for video ads):**
- **The voice dominates everything** (Chion — vococentrism): In any video ad with voiceover, the presenter's voice must sit above the music bed (3-6 dB). Music ducks under speech automatically. If there's a tension between voice clarity and music impact, voice wins every time — an inaudible voiceover with great music is a failed ad. The viewer's brain locks onto the human voice above all other audio; when the voice is unclear, the brain strains to decode speech and stops processing visuals, music, and everything else. When writing hook scripts, plan the audio hierarchy: which words land over silence (maximum impact), which land over music (emotional support), which land over SFX (punctuation).
- **Sound-visual weld points** (Chion — synchresis): Plan 2-3 sync points per video hook where a sound event lands on the exact frame of a visual event. A bass hit on the product reveal. A swoosh on a text animation. A beat of silence syncing with the moment before the CTA. The brain irresistibly welds any sound to any visual event when the timing is precise — the pairing doesn't need to be "logical," it needs to be frame-accurate. These weld moments create attention spikes the viewer can't ignore. Not every cut needs a sync point — overuse flattens the hierarchy and becomes cartoonish. Reserve them for the hook's structural peaks: the pattern interrupt, the reveal, the CTA.
- **Emotional accuracy over literal accuracy** (Chion — rendering): Direct video ad sound effects for the feeling they should produce, not what the object actually sounds like. A real mouse click is inaudible; a rendered click — crisp, with a subtle bass undertone — makes the product feel precise and premium. A real notification sound is generic; a rendered notification sound — bright, satisfying, slightly musical — makes the result feel earned. Real punches are quiet; movie punches make you flinch. The stylized, designed sound is emotionally truer than the real one. When directing SFX for video ads, specify the emotion: "a notification sound that feels like winning" not "a notification sound."
- **Voiceover vs. talking-head is an audio architecture decision** (Chion — acousmatic): Voiceover-over-b-roll commands more authority than talking-head delivery because the unseen voice activates the listener's imagination and projects a larger-than-visible presence — Chion calls this the acousmatic effect. The moment you show the speaker, the authority shifts from projected to embodied. Use VO format for authority hooks, result hooks, and mechanism hooks where the visual is product footage or screen recordings. Use talking-head for story hooks, vulnerability hooks, and community hooks where face-to-face connection matters more than authority. Many premium ads open with acousmatic VO on abstract visuals to build authority, then reveal the speaker to pay off the curiosity — the de-acousmatization IS the reveal moment.
- **Silence as temporal tool** (Chion — temporal effects): A 1-2 second pause before a key line stretches perceived time and forces the viewer to lean in — the silence makes them replay the preceding line in their minds. Dense, layered audio (voice + music + SFX simultaneously) compresses perceived duration and creates urgency. Alternate between the two: dense audio through the setup, silence before the payoff. In a 30-second hook, one well-placed silence is worth more than continuous audio because it breaks the temporal pattern the viewer has habituated to. The silence is the audio equivalent of the whitespace that makes a headline command attention (Müller-Brockmann).

**Written (for ad copy):**
- Open with a single-person story: "John didn't know he was going to die that night" > "Thousands struggle with..."
- Challenge: "You probably won't read this."
- Paradoxical question: "Could the secret to signing more clients be talking to fewer people?"
- Don't ruin the ending at the beginning. Tension is everything.

**Ad copy is scanned in F-pattern, not read sequentially (Weinschenk).** Users scan the first 3-5 words of each line, then skip to the next line. Only 20-28% of text is actually read. This means: (a) every line of ad copy must front-load its meaning — the verb and the payoff come first, not the setup, (b) if the first five words of any line are filler ('What I want to tell you is...'), that line is invisible, (c) bold the single phrase that carries the argument — a scanner who reads only bolded text should still get the hook. Written ad copy that reads like prose (setup → build → payoff across sentences) fails at scan resolution. Written ad copy that reads like a stack of front-loaded punches succeeds.

**Cross-domain structure imports** (de Bono): Before writing, pick one domain outside advertising and import its opening structure as a hook template. A trial lawyer's opening statement. A movie trailer's first beat. A standup comedian's setup. A doctor's diagnostic question. These feel different from every other ad because they borrow structure from outside the ad domain — the viewer's pattern-matching filter doesn't flag them as "ad." A hook structured like a journalist's lead ("Last Tuesday, a fitness coach in Denver did something her competitors thought was stupid") bypasses the filter entirely.

**Signals arrive before messages (Barden).** The autopilot reads the visual signal — color, layout, texture, spatial pattern — before it processes any text. A signal that says 'this is not for me' or 'this is an ad' kills the hook before the first word is read. Design the signal layer first: what does this ad communicate in the first 0.2 seconds, before anyone reads anything? Then write the copy to land in the context that signal created. A perfect hook placed inside a signal that reads as 'generic marketing content' is a perfect hook nobody sees.

**The peripheral gist categorizes the ad before text is processed (Weinschenk).** The viewer's peripheral vision determines the category — 'ad,' 'content,' 'message from a friend,' 'news' — within a fraction of a second. This categorization happens through layout, color, density, and spatial pattern, not through text. Weinschenk's research quantifies what Barden's peripheral filter describes: the gist is the first gate, and it operates on visual structure alone. A video hook where the first frame's gist reads 'produced ad content' triggers the skip reflex before the hook line lands. A first frame whose gist reads 'someone talking to me' or 'something unexpected' passes the gate.

## Testing & Variation

Whoever tests the most ads wins. This is not motivational — it's math (Stanley).

**Feed-level competition framing (Kane):** Your hook isn't competing against other ads — it's competing against everything in the feed. The friend's vacation photo. The meme. The news article. The ex's story. Every piece of content in the scroll is your competition, and most of it is personally relevant to the viewer in a way your ad never will be. This reframes what "good enough" means: a hook that beats other ads in your category but loses to a friend's engagement announcement still gets scrolled. When evaluating hooks, don't ask "is this better than competing ads?" Ask "would this stop someone mid-scroll past their friend's baby photos?" That's the real bar. The feed doesn't sort by category — it sorts by interestingness, and your hook is in a cage match with everything.

**Testing hierarchy** (Hormozi): When hooks aren't performing, follow this order — test hooks first (the hook determines whether anyone watches — if nobody gets past 3 seconds, nothing else matters), then test audiences (the right hook to the wrong people still fails), then test offers (the offer determines whether anyone buys), then test creative elements last (colors, fonts, thumbnails are marginal gains). Most users optimize creative elements when the hook is the real bottleneck. When generating hook variations, frame them explicitly as A/B test candidates — "test these three hooks against each other before changing anything else."

**$10-20 rapid testing protocol (Kane):** Produce 5 hook variations per concept. Run each with $10-20 spend. The only metric that matters at this stage is 3-second retention — did people stop scrolling? Not clicks, not conversions, not CPM. Three-second retention tells you whether the hook works as a hook. Kill the bottom 3 performers. Take the top 2 and iterate — new variations of what's already proven. This is cheaper and faster than trying to think your way to the perfect hook. The math: $50-100 buys you definitive data on 5 hooks. That same budget spent on one "perfect" ad teaches you nothing about what actually resonates. Volume of tested hooks beats quality of untested hooks every time.

**Variable isolation discipline (Kane):** Test one variable per batch. Hooks independently from body copy. Visuals independently from words. Architecture independently from topic. When you change the hook AND the body AND the thumbnail simultaneously, a win teaches you nothing — you don't know which change drove the result. A loss teaches you even less. The discipline: pick the variable, hold everything else constant, run the test, read the data. This feels slower than changing everything at once, but it produces compounding knowledge — after 10 isolated tests you know exactly which levers move this audience, and every future hook benefits from that intelligence. Changing everything at once produces 10 data points that each mean nothing.

**The production paradox:** iPhone ads almost always beat high-end production. People want a real human talking to them like a real human, not polished corporate feel. High production signals "ad." Low production signals "person talking to me." This is role distance in action (Goffman) — the creator is so competent in their domain that they don't need production to carry the message. The casualness of the format signals mastery of the substance. But this only works when the content demonstrates real expertise. An amateur with nothing to say in an iPhone video looks amateur, not authentic. Role distance requires competence first.

**Motivated light for scaled production** (Brown): When scaling a winning iPhone hook to higher production, the risk is that better lighting makes it feel like "an ad." The fix: motivated light — every light source should appear to come from something plausible (a window, a lamp, a screen glow). A key light that has no apparent source reads as "set" even if the viewer can't articulate why. Place a practical light in frame (a lamp, a ring light visible in the background) and let the key appear to come from that direction. This is the difference between "upgraded production that still feels real" and "now it looks like a commercial."

**Testing progression:**
1. **Selfie/simple phone** — always start here. Test the IDEA before anything else.
2. **Record in vehicle** — car setting is inexplicably effective and consistently outperforms.
3. **Weird places** — winning concept → record everywhere. Every new location = 3-4 new ads.
4. **Higher production** — rarely worth it. Ad fatigue means a produced ad might only run 2 months. In that time you could have tested 50 iPhone ads.

**The 9 variation axes** — once you have a winning hook, multiply it:
1. **Outfit** — same script, different clothes (38% lift from a branded shirt alone)
2. **Background** — kitchen, gym, outside, car
3. **Body position** — sitting, standing, walking, lying down
4. **Props** — anything that creates visual interest or chaos
5. **Volume** — whispering vs normal vs loud
6. **Camera angle** — selfie vs tripod vs someone else filming
7. **Music** — with/without, different genres
8. **Energy** — record when energetic vs relaxed; your state affects performance
9. **Wild card** — be nuts. 1 in 5 might be remarkable.

**Don't anchor on last week's winner** (Groopman): Track which hook architectures win for this specific audience, but don't let the most recent winner dictate the next batch. Availability bias says "the If You hook worked, use it again." The data might say a different architecture resonates now that the audience has seen twenty If You hooks from competitors. Rotate architectures deliberately, even when one is hot.

**Mine the accidents** (Tharp): When a hook performs unexpectedly — high engagement on the wrong section, surprising demographic response, comments focusing on something you didn't emphasize — that's an accident. Don't dismiss it. Analyze what the audience told you. Build the next batch from what they revealed, not what you assumed. The best hook pivots come from results you didn't plan for.

**Structural diagnosis before surface fixes** (O'Bannon): When a hook underperforms, distinguish structure from surface before rewriting anything. Structure problems: wrong hook architecture for this audience, wrong tension mechanism, wrong plot shape — the hook's skeleton doesn't fit the sale. Surface problems: weak copy, wrong thumbnail, poor visual execution — the skeleton is right but the flesh is wrong. Fix structure first. Polishing copy on a structurally broken hook produces better-written hooks that still fail. Diagnosis: if the hook gets views but no clicks, the structure may be sound but the payoff isn't landing (surface). If the hook gets skipped entirely, the architecture may not match the audience's awareness level or the tension mechanism may be wrong (structure). If the hook gets clicks but no conversions downstream, the structure worked but the ad body broke the promise (a different problem entirely). When a user reports underperformance, ask what's failing before generating replacements — the fix for a structural problem is a different architecture, not a better version of the same one.

**The multiple representations test** (Feynman): If you can't express the selling concept through at least 3 different hook architectures, you may not understand the concept deeply enough. Each architecture is a different representation of the same idea. If the concept only works as an "If You" hook but collapses as a "You Don't Need" or "Everyone Thinks," the concept might be thin — or your understanding of it is.

**Platform conventions are cultural subtext** (Seger): The same hook reads differently on TikTok vs. LinkedIn because each platform has its own unspoken codes. A casual selfie hook signals authenticity on TikTok but signals low-effort on LinkedIn. A polished carousel signals credibility on LinkedIn but signals "ad" on TikTok. The platform's conventions create a subtext layer independently of your words — account for it when adapting hooks across platforms.

**Default output should include:** When generating hooks, suggest 2-3 variation ideas for the top hooks (different visual approaches, word order tests, platform-specific adjustments).

## Compliance Guardrails

These prevent ads from getting rejected on paid platforms (Stanley):

**Cannot do:**
- Time-based income claims: "Make $10k in the next 90 days" → REJECTED
- Guaranteed outcomes for others: "I guarantee you'll make more money" → REJECTED
- Claims with time + money: "How to make $10k/month in 90 days" → REJECTED

**Can do:**
- Promise YOUR action: "I'll improve your funnel or you won't pay" → OK
- Statements without time guarantees: "How to make $10k a month" → OK
- Possibility statements: "You could make more money, but nothing is guaranteed" → OK

If you need crazy claims, you probably don't have a good enough product or hook. Flag compliance risks when generating hooks for paid platforms.

## What to Produce

When the user asks for ad hooks, deliver a **batch**. Volume beats perfection — but volume with variety, not volume of the same angle.

**Lateral generation before selection** (de Bono): Before writing the batch, name the dominant-pattern hook — the first, most obvious hook for this product and audience. That's the hook every competitor is already running. Then run one reversal (flip the claim, the audience, the emotion) and one cross-domain import (borrow a structure from outside advertising) to generate at least 3 non-obvious angles. Generate 12-15 hooks without evaluating any — the best hooks live at positions 8-12, after the obvious options are exhausted. Only after generating all of them, select the 5-8 strongest. The suspended judgment phase is critical: evaluating too early kills the non-obvious hooks before they fully form.

**Big Idea test for campaign hooks** (Ogilvy): When generating hooks for a campaign — not a single ad — test whether the winning concept is a Big Idea or a gimmick. Ogilvy's test: Did it make me gasp when I first saw it? Is it unique? Could it generate 50 variations? A hook that only works once is a gimmick — it gets one ad, then you're back to the blank page. A hook concept that spawns unlimited angles is a Big Idea. "The fitness coaches making $10K/month all have the same 3 systems" is a gimmick — it works once. "The system your clients use matters more than the effort you put in" is a Big Idea — it generates hooks about systems vs. hustle, about hidden infrastructure, about what the top 1% do differently, about why hard work alone fails. Before committing a campaign to a hook direction, ask: can this concept generate 50 variations without repeating itself? If not, keep looking for the Big Idea underneath the gimmick.

**Market saturation check** (Hunter): Before generating the batch, scan what hooks competitors are currently running. If "I went from X to Y" hooks are everywhere in the space, that architecture is saturated — the audience has pattern-matched it as noise regardless of how good your version is. If every competitor leads with transformation stories, a mechanism hook or a paradigm shift might be the fresher play. The goal isn't to avoid proven architectures — it's to avoid the specific executions the audience has already seen twenty times this week. The freshest angle is often the one nobody else is running, not the one that performed best in a vacuum.

**Default output: 5-8 hooks using different architectures, plus 2-3 complete ad copy blocks.**

For each hook, include:
- The hook line itself
- Which architecture it uses (so they can request more of that type)
- A brief note on what makes it work (which psychological lever it pulls)

For complete ad copy blocks, include:
- Written ad copy (the text for the ad)
- Optional: video script notes (what to say/do on camera)
- Visual/pattern interrupt suggestion
- 1-2 variation ideas for testing

**Always offer to generate more.** The user should never run out of hooks.

## Hook Metaphors Set Thinking Frame

Hooks don't just grab attention — they activate conceptual systems that determine what the viewer thinks next. The metaphor in the first 3 seconds sets the frame through which everything that follows gets processed.

"You're leaving money on the table" (OPPORTUNITY IS PHYSICAL OBJECT) activates thinking about carelessness, loss, something tangible that should have been picked up. The viewer's next thought is "what did I miss?" The frame: I was negligent.

"You're building on a broken foundation" (BUSINESS IS STRUCTURE) activates thinking about risk, collapse, structural integrity. The viewer's next thought is "what's cracked?" The frame: I'm in danger.

Same business problem. Different metaphor. Different emotional response. Different action threshold.

**Frame-selection protocol for hook design:**

1. **Identify the emotional state you want to create.** Fear? Urgency? Curiosity? Exclusivity? The metaphor determines which emotion activates naturally.

2. **Map problem to metaphor that activates that emotion.**
   - LOST (journey) → confusion, need for direction
   - TRAPPED (cage) → suffocation, need for escape
   - BLEEDING (container leak) → urgency, need to stop loss
   - BROKEN (machine) → frustration, need for repair
   - BLIND (vision) → vulnerability, need to see

3. **Write the hook using that metaphor's language.** Don't mix systems. If you open with JOURNEY metaphor ("stuck on the treadmill"), continue with journey language ("here's the shortcut") not building language ("here's the blueprint") or war language ("here's your weapon").

4. **Test the frame.** Show the hook to someone unfamiliar with the product and ask: "What's the problem this is addressing?" Their answer reveals what frame you activated. If they say "they don't know what to do next" (LOST frame) but you meant to activate urgency (BLEEDING frame), rewrite.

The metaphor isn't decoration. It's the cognitive scaffold the viewer builds in the first 3 seconds. Everything after that either reinforces the scaffold or collapses it.

## Highlight What They Ignore

Effective hooks use metaphors that shift what the audience notices. If everyone in the category obsesses over one metaphor system, your hook should use a different one — it highlights what competitors hide.

**Find the category's dominant metaphor.** Scroll through competitor ads. What metaphor system appears in 80% of hooks?

Marketing agency space: WAR metaphors everywhere. "Dominate your market." "Crush the competition." "10x your revenue." "Scale fast." War highlights: aggression, speed, domination, winning.

**Identify what that metaphor hides.** War metaphor makes the audience focus on speed and aggression. What does it make them ignore? Direction. Sustainability. Whether they're fighting the right battle.

**Hook with a metaphor that highlights what's hidden.** "You're running fast in the wrong direction" (JOURNEY + DIRECTION). Suddenly the viewer who's been focused on speed starts thinking about aim. Pattern interrupt.

**Examples by category:**

- **SaaS productivity tools:** Category uses SPEED metaphors ("faster," "accelerate," "instant"). Hook with DIRECTION metaphor: "You're optimizing the wrong workflows."

- **Coaching/courses:** Category uses TRANSFORMATION metaphors ("become," "unlock," "level up"). Hook with FOUNDATION metaphor: "Your marketing skills won't matter if your offer is broken."

- **Fitness:** Category uses BATTLE metaphors ("destroy fat," "crush your goals," "beast mode"). Hook with CULTIVATION metaphor: "Your body isn't the enemy — treating it like one is why you're stuck."

The technique: name the dominant metaphor, identify what it ignores, hook with the opposite frame. The viewer's brain recognizes the pattern break immediately.

## Ontological Metaphors Make Abstract Concrete

Abstract problems scroll past. Concrete ones stop thumbs.

"Inefficient processes" is abstract. The viewer's brain doesn't build a picture. No visual. No urgency.

"Your pipeline is leaking 40% of qualified leads" (PIPELINE IS CONTAINER WITH HOLES, LEADS ARE LIQUID) creates instant visual. The viewer sees the leak. Urgency activates automatically because liquids leaking = loss in progress.

**Ontological metaphors turn abstract concepts into concrete objects, substances, or containers.** Once the concept has physical form, it can be acted upon: you can fix leaks, fill containers, grab objects, open doors.

**Technique: Identify the abstract pain point, map to ontological metaphor.**

Abstract: "Your business has inefficiencies."
Concrete: "Your business bleeds $8K monthly from three holes you can't see." (BUSINESS IS CONTAINER, MONEY IS LIQUID, PROBLEMS ARE HOLES)

Abstract: "Content strategy could be better."
Concrete: "Your content calendar is a junk drawer — 40 drafts, zero system." (CALENDAR IS PHYSICAL CONTAINER, CONTENT IS PHYSICAL OBJECTS)

Abstract: "Sales process needs improvement."
Concrete: "Your sales process is a maze — prospects enter, get lost, never buy." (PROCESS IS PHYSICAL STRUCTURE WITH PATHS)

**Common ontological metaphors for hooks:**

- **Container + substance:** "pipeline leaking," "funnel clogged," "vault empty"
- **Object:** "leaving money on the table," "tool sitting unused," "keys you haven't found"
- **Physical barrier:** "wall blocking growth," "ceiling you keep hitting," "door you can't open"
- **Machine:** "engine stalled," "gears grinding," "system broken"
- **Path/structure:** "stuck in maze," "building on quicksand," "bridge to nowhere"

The abstract version makes the viewer think. The concrete version makes them see. Hooks that make viewers see stop scrolls. Hooks that make them think get skipped.

## Anti-Patterns (Kill These on Sight)

| Pattern | Why It's Death | Instead |
|---------|---------------|---------|
| "Are you tired of [pain point]?" | Every AI writes this. Invisible. | Any of the 18 architectures. |
| "What if I told you..." | Condescending. Reads like a pitch. | Direct claim or curiosity gap. |
| "Attention [industry]!" | 1990s infomercial energy. | "If You" hook, naturally. |
| "In today's fast-paced world..." | Means nothing. Nobody thinks this. | Cut. Start with the hook. |
| Adjectives without facts | "Amazing," "powerful," "revolutionary" = empty. | Replace every adjective with a specific fact. |
| Listing features first | Nobody cares until they care about you. | Result, proof, or paradigm shift. |
| Perfect grammar, perfect flow | Signals "written by a machine." | Punchy. Incomplete. Rough-edged. |
| Explaining everything | Closes every loop. No curiosity. | Open loops. Name a method they don't know. |
| "We" or company name in hook | Nobody cares about your company in the first 3 seconds. | Talk about THEM or a result. |
| Hedging ("might," "could potentially") | Weak. Why would they trust uncertain copy? | Direct claims. Commit. |
| Describing feelings | "You might feel frustrated" — labeling. | Paint the picture: "5 reels. 10k views. Zero DMs." |
| No admission of limitation | Everything positive = everything unbelievable. | One honest limitation builds trust for all claims. |
| One precious hook | Treating it like fine art. | 5-8 minimum. Volume wins. |
| Cargo cult hooks | Perfect structure (architecture, curiosity gap, specificity) but generic insight. A straw airplane. | Structure is the runway, not the plane. The insight must be genuine. |
| Category-level hooks | "Fitness coaches want more clients." That's a category, not a conversation. | Enter ONE person's specific situation, not the demographic's. |
| Repeating last week's winner | Availability bias — the architecture that just worked becomes the default. | Rotate architectures deliberately. The audience gets fatigued faster than you think. |
| Fantasy after-states | "Wake up to a six-figure pipeline every morning." If the product doesn't deliver that, the ad is a lie. | Paint the realistic best case. Idealization gap kills trust on contact. |
| Shadow-trigger hooks | Hooks that accidentally activate shadow archetypes — Orphan victimhood ("the world is rigged against you"), Warrior ruthlessness ("crush your competition into dust"), Altruist guilt ("after everything you've sacrificed"). | Match archetype energy without triggering its dark side. Orphans need empathy + path forward. Warriors need challenge + meaning. Altruists need impact + boundaries. |
| Closing in the hook | Trying to sell in the first 3 seconds. "Buy our $2k mentorship" in the hook skips the relationship ladder. | The hook earns the click. The page earns the sale. Each step earns the next. |
| Recognizable persuasion format | The hook uses a structure the viewer has seen hundreds of times and they categorize it as an ad before the value lands (Ortiz: eliminating the method). If the viewer can name the tactic ("that's a curiosity gap hook"), the method is showing. | Same persuasion principle, different wrapping. The best hooks feel like a person saying something genuinely interesting, not a marketer executing hook architecture #7. |
| Hook disconnected from funnel (Alexander) | A hook optimized for clicks that attracts the wrong audience for the landing page. The hook pulls attention but the viewer doesn't convert because the page doesn't match what the hook implied. | Before writing, ask: what page does this lead to? What offer? What awareness level? A hook is embedded in a system. |
| Template formula sensing (Alexander) | If you can sense the hook formula — "Stop scrolling if...", "Wait, did you know..." — the hook is dead. The viewer categorizes it as "ad" before the value lands. | Adapt the pattern to THIS audience, THIS platform, THIS awareness level. Alive hooks feel like a person; dead hooks feel like a template. |
| Multiple hooks in one ad | Tries to land three compelling ideas in a single ad, hoping one sticks. Each additional hook dilutes the others (Ortiz: too many methods / moment of magic). | One hook, one payoff, one moment the viewer remembers. If you have three strong hooks, make three ads. |
| Front-loaded energy, sagging middle | All the intensity lives in the first 3 seconds — then the education section coasts on explanation without re-earning attention. The hold must escalate, not plateau (Cannell & Travis). | Every 10-15 seconds needs a re-hook: a new question, a tonal shift, a visual cut. The middle should be the hardest-working section, not the safest. |
| Naked exposition hooks | "Did you know 73% of coaches fail in their first year?" — statistics and explanations delivered raw are invisible exposition (Howard & Mabley). The information arrives as a lecture, not an experience. The viewer's brain files it as "educational content" and scrolls. | Deliver the information inside a scene or a character's experience. "She posted every day for a year. Made $400 total." carries the same data — failure rate, time invested, poor return — but the viewer LIVES it instead of being told it. Facts wrapped in experience stick. Facts delivered as facts slide. |
| Undiscoverable value proposition (Norman) | The hook is buried, the benefit is unclear, the CTA is hidden. Norman: "It is possible to determine what actions are possible." In an ad, the user must determine what this is and what to do in under 3 seconds. If they can't, discoverability has failed. | Make the value proposition immediately perceivable. The hook names the benefit; the CTA names the action. No interpretation required. |
| Visceral disconnect (Norman) | The ad looks off, cheap, or misaligned with the brand — before anyone processes the message. Norman's visceral level: "First impressions... Appearance. How something looks and feels before any interaction." Negative visceral response kills engagement before the hook lands. | Visual quality, brand alignment, and aesthetic polish must be addressed before optimizing copy. The eye judges before the brain reads. |
| Vague CTAs with unclear ability costs (Fogg: Tiny Habits) | "Learn more" asks the viewer to wonder what they're committing to. How long? What happens next? The ability cost is unknowable, so the viewer defaults to "probably too much effort" and scrolls. | "Watch the 60-second demo" defines the exact cost. "See your results in 30 seconds" makes ability calculable. The scroll-stop is the Prompt, the hook is Motivation — but the CTA must have maximal Ability (clear, low-effort, defined cost). A strong hook that ends in a vague CTA burns the motivation it built. |
| Category-based hooks instead of struggling-moment hooks (Moesta: Demand-Side Sales 101) | "Busy professionals who need better tools..." describes a demographic. "It's Tuesday morning. You open Slack to 14 messages. Three are urgent. You switch to your project tool — it says everything is on track. You know it's not." describes a scene. Categories are invisible because everyone skips past self-descriptions. Struggling moments are vivid because the reader sees themselves in a specific moment with time, place, and emotion. | Enter through the struggling moment, not the category. The hook should name a scene the viewer has experienced — not a demographic the viewer belongs to. If the hook could apply to an entire industry, it's too abstract. |
| Censor-filtered hooks (Johnstone: Impro) | The first draft says "This app is for people who exercise in their underwear at 6am and feel guilty about it." The censor rewrites it to "This app is for fitness enthusiasts looking for a flexible tracking solution." The first is specific, human, memorable. The second is corporate wallpaper. The hook that makes you uncomfortable is usually the hook that works. | Keep the first draft. The censor wants safety; the audience wants specificity. |
| Product-benefit hooks instead of capability hooks (Sierra: Badass) | "This tool saves you 10 hours a week" describes what the product does for you. The viewer evaluates the claim and decides if they believe it. Product-benefit framing invites skepticism because it's the product talking about itself. | "What would you do with your Fridays back?" describes the viewer's upgraded life. The scroll-stopping hook enters the viewer's desired context — the bigger skill or life they're trying to build — not the product's feature set. The viewer imagines themselves in the after-state instead of evaluating a claim. |
| Capability claims instead of pattern observations (Baker: The Business of Expertise) | "We help businesses grow their revenue" says what you do — a claim any competitor could make. The viewer categorizes it as "just another service" and scrolls. | "In 47 companies we've analyzed, we've seen the same pattern: the bottleneck is never where founders think it is." Pattern observations demonstrate expertise through what you've seen. The viewer thinks "they understand something I don't." Pattern evidence establishes authority; capability claims invite comparison. |

## Quality Checklist

Before delivering, run every hook and copy block through this:

- [ ] **Conversation test:** Was the reader already thinking this? Or am I starting a topic they didn't ask for?
- [ ] **One-person test:** Am I writing to a crowd or to one specific human?
- [ ] **3-second test:** Would this stop MY scroll? Be ruthlessly honest.
- [ ] **Four-second credibility test (Belfort):** Does the hook establish all three in the first line? Sharp (specific, not vague — a precise number, a named method, a concrete detail). Enthusiastic (convicted, not hyped — the speaker believes this, not "OMG amazing"). Expert (credentialed, not claiming — demonstrated knowledge, not "as an expert"). A hook that's specific but flat lacks enthusiasm. A hook that's passionate but vague lacks sharpness. A hook that claims authority without demonstrating it lacks expertise. All three must land in the opening line or the viewer's snap judgment is "not worth my time."
- [ ] **Architecture used:** Maps to one of the 18 (or a deliberate hybrid).
- [ ] **Awareness match:** Hook type fits the audience's awareness level and market sophistication.
- [ ] **Curiosity gap:** Something unresolved pulls them to the next line.
- [ ] **News test (Ogilvy):** Does this hook contain information the viewer didn't have? "Great coaching program" is a claim — it tells them nothing new. "The fitness coaches making $10K/month all have the same 3 systems" is news — it contains a fact they didn't know and need to. Test every hook: strip the adjectives, strip the promises. Is there NEWS left? If not, the hook is asserting, not informing, and assertions don't stop thumbs.
- [ ] **Tension, not just curiosity:** Does the hook create a gap the viewer MUST resolve, or one they could shrug off? Curiosity is optional; tension is compulsory.
- [ ] **Status-role match:** Is the hook using dominion register (superiority, exclusivity) or affiliation register (belonging, tribe)? Does it match what the audience is actually playing for?
- [ ] **Specificity (Velcro test):** Exact numbers, names, details — not vague claims or adjectives. Precise details are memory hooks; rounded numbers and abstract language are smooth surfaces.
- [ ] **Concrete and sensory:** Can they SEE, HEAR, or FEEL something? Abstract = invisible.
- [ ] **Voice match:** Sounds like the brand/person, not like AI marketing copy. Read it aloud.
- [ ] **One concept (Commander's Intent):** The ad is ABOUT one thing. Can you state the one thing the viewer must take away? If not, the ad has no concept yet.
- [ ] **Oppositional:** Challenges a held belief, not just agrees with common wisdom.
- [ ] **Belief identified:** Can you state the dominant belief being shifted in one sentence? If not, you're guessing.
- [ ] **Mechanism present:** The belief shift has a specific, named explanation — not a naked claim.
- [ ] **Slippery slide:** Can they stop reading after any sentence? If yes, rewrite it.
- [ ] **Mental image:** Can the reader SEE themselves in the after-state?
- [ ] **Show don't tell:** Invoking feelings, not describing them.
- [ ] **No feature leading:** First sentence is a result, challenge, story, or provocation.
- [ ] **Compliance safe:** No time-based income claims or guaranteed outcomes for paid platforms.
- [ ] **Reframe angle considered:** Could a price, time, identity, or risk reframe produce a stronger hook?
- [ ] **Cargo cult test:** Does this hook have the form of a good ad (architecture, curiosity gap, specificity) AND the substance (a genuine insight about the prospect's situation)? A perfectly structured hook with a generic insight is a straw airplane.
- [ ] **Enthymeme intact:** Does the hook let the viewer draw the conclusion, or does it state the conclusion for them? If it ends with "and you can too," cut it.
- [ ] **Idealization calibrated:** Is the after-state painted in the ad achievable with the product? If the best case study doesn't match the mental image, dial it back.
- [ ] **Dominant pattern dodged:** Is this the first, most obvious hook for this product? If so, every competitor is already running it. Generate a less obvious angle.
- [ ] **Lateral variety:** Did the batch come from at least 3 different angles, or are all 5-8 hooks variations of the same insight?
- [ ] **Question stack:** Does the hook answer a small question while opening a bigger one? A hook that plants one question and stops creates curiosity. A hook that answers one while opening two creates drive into the ad body.
- [ ] **Surprise + suspense:** Does the hook only shock (surprise) or does it also plant a tension line that sustains watching (suspense)? Best hooks layer both.
- [ ] **Plot shape named:** For hooks with narrative momentum, can you name the plot shape (monster, quest, comedy, tragedy, rebirth)? Naming the shape before writing sharpens the hook.
- [ ] **POV varied:** Does the batch mix first-person (identification), third-person (spectation), and second-person (confrontation)? All one POV = monotone.
- [ ] **Brand fingerprint (McKee: Dialogue):** Could a competitor run this hook unchanged? If yes, the hook is generic — it doesn't carry the brand's voice. Distinctive hooks are proof of identity. The hook should be specific enough that only this brand could say it.
- [ ] **Sympathy-first:** Do the first 2 seconds establish common ground before introducing stakes? Stakes without sympathy land on a stranger.
- [ ] **Spine delivered:** Does the hook state the argument in the first seconds — not circle the topic, not name the product, not describe the pain, but deliver the actual argument? If the spine can't be extracted from the opening beat, the hook is circling.
- [ ] **Tone vs mood separated:** Can you name the mood (what the viewer feels) AND the tone (the speaker's stance) independently? If they collapse into the same word, the hook is flat. Vary one or both across the batch.
- [ ] **Fully committed:** Does the hook commit completely to its angle, or does it hedge with qualifiers ("might," "could," "in many cases")? A half-committed hook occupies the dead zone between safe and bold. Cut the qualifiers or cut the hook.
- [ ] **Micro-premise intact:** Can you extract a cause-and-effect ("doing X leads to Y") from each hook? If the hook is a statement without an implied argument, it floats.
- [ ] **Locked-in problem:** Do pain hooks target problems the viewer can't walk away from? If they could shrug it off, the hook has no teeth.
- [ ] **Emotional truth:** Do story-based hooks claim experiences that actually happened? Manufactured personal stories collapse on contact with the audience.
- [ ] **Market saturation checked:** Have you scanned competitor hooks before generating? If an architecture is everywhere in the space, the audience has already tuned it out.
- [ ] **Structure vs. surface diagnosed:** When revising underperforming hooks, have you identified whether the problem is structural (wrong architecture, wrong tension mechanism) or surface (weak copy, wrong visual)? Don't polish a broken skeleton.
- [ ] **Decisive moment (video):** Does the first frame show the payoff, not the setup? If the viewer needs voiceover to understand what they're seeing, restage.
- [ ] **Visual proof (video):** Are claims shown on screen (screen recordings, screenshots, real photos) rather than just narrated? Move up the proof hierarchy: recording > photo > graphic > text.
- [ ] **Permission-earning:** Does the hook earn the right to the next step (click, follow, opt-in) — or does it try to close the sale? The hook's job is to earn permission, not to convert.
- [ ] **Hook-to-body payoff:** Does the body deliver exactly what the hook promised? If the body would work equally well under a different hook, the payoff isn't specific enough.
- [ ] **Attention paid for:** Does the first beat deliver value, surprise, or recognition? The viewer didn't agree to give you their time — if the hook takes before it gives, they're right to scroll.
- [ ] **Share trigger (Kane):** Would someone share this ad? What does sharing it say about them? If sharing it doesn't signal identity (smart, contrarian, in-the-know), the hook converts but won't spread.
- [ ] **Retelling test:** Would someone repeat this hook to a friend? If the hook is only compelling in ad format, it's not retellable — and hooks that don't get retold don't spread. The best hooks compress into a sentence someone would say at dinner: "Did you know that..." or "I saw this thing that said..." If the hook requires the ad's visual, the platform's context, or the full copy to land, it fails the retelling test. Retellable hooks carry the brand with them because the product is structurally necessary to the story.
- [ ] **Volume:** Batch of 5-8 hooks minimum with variation ideas.
