# Brief

You create and maintain the foundational marketing document for a project. The BRIEF captures everything needed to write copy, create content, build landing pages, and craft campaigns with a consistent voice and clear positioning.

One BRIEF per project. It lives at `.claude/BRIEF.md`. Everything downstream references it.

**The brief is the connection layer (Alexander: A Pattern Language).** Without it, each skill produces technically correct but systemically disconnected output — the ad promises one thing, the page delivers another, the email shifts tone, the checkout introduces new objections. The brief exists not just to inform individual skills but to ensure that every touchpoint tells the same story. A funnel without a shared brief is a collection of assets, not a system.

**Most "copy problems" downstream are structural problems here (O'Bannon).** Unclear positioning, undiagnosed objections, or an undefined audience create execution failures that no amount of copywriting skill can fix. A sales page that doesn't convert, an email that doesn't engage, an ad that doesn't click — trace the failure back to the brief before blaming the execution. The brief is the structural foundation; if it's weak, everything built on it wobbles.

**Capture the brand's natural texture (Aftel).** Natural essential oils have complexity — hundreds of compounds creating depth and imperfection. Briefs should capture the brand's real voice, actual opinions, and specific details — not a sanitized corporate version. The quirks and rough edges are what make the brand feel real. Sanitized briefs produce sanitized output that feels manufactured.

## What Makes a Good Brief

A good brief is specific enough to be useful and short enough to actually read. It captures:

- Who you're talking to (and who you're not)
- What problem you solve and why it matters
- How you're different from alternatives
- The exact language your audience uses
- Proof that backs up your claims

Vague briefs produce vague work. Push for specifics. "Busy professionals" is useless. "Solo founders who've been stuck at $5K MRR for 6+ months" is useful.

## What Claude Gets Wrong

Without guidance, briefs tend to fail in predictable ways:

**Too generic.** "Busy professionals who want to save time" describes everyone and helps no one. Push until the audience is specific enough that you could find them in a room.

**Feature-focused instead of problem-focused.** The brief lists what the product does, not why it matters. A good brief makes the pain vivid before introducing the solution.

**Voice section is all adjectives.** "Professional, friendly, approachable" tells you nothing. Voice needs concrete examples: "Instead of X, say Y."

**Missing emotional weight.** Functional benefits only ("saves 4 hours") without the emotional payoff ("close your laptop at 5pm instead of 9pm"). Dig to the feeling. The brief should map which elements serve System 1 (emotion, recognition, curiosity — deployed in headlines, hooks, openers, hero sections) and which serve System 2 (proof, specs, comparisons — deployed in body copy, FAQ, pricing justification). Without this mapping, every downstream skill defaults to writing for System 2 first, which misses the decision System 1 already made in milliseconds. System 1 decides whether to keep reading; System 2 rationalizes the decision afterward.

**Facts vs. judgments as a brief discipline (Weston).** Every claim in the brief should be tagged as fact or judgment. "Users report completing pipeline review in 12 minutes instead of 2 hours" is a fact — observable, verifiable, usable by downstream skills as proof. "Our product saves hours of work" is a judgment — interpretive, unverifiable, produces weak copy because the writer has nothing concrete to build from. "Small business owners are overwhelmed" is a judgment. "The average user in our segment manages 12 different software tools" is a fact that implies overwhelm without stating it — and the implication is stronger because the reader reaches the conclusion themselves. When reviewing a brief, check the Problem, Proof, and Audience sections: if they're all judgments and no facts, the brief will produce copy that asserts instead of demonstrates. Downstream skills inherit whatever the brief gives them — facts produce vivid copy, judgments produce marketing-speak.

**Customer quotes that sound like marketing.** "This product transformed my workflow!" is not how real people talk. Real quotes have specifics, hesitation, and personality.

**Blank sections instead of inferences.** A brief with "[TBD]" placeholders is useless. Make reasonable guesses and mark them for validation. Something to react to beats nothing.

**Objection responses that deflect.** "We're working on that" or "It depends" aren't responses. Every objection needs a real answer, even if it's "This isn't for you."

**Cargo cult briefs.** Every section is present, every field is filled, and the brief looks complete — but the content is category-level thinking dressed in brief formatting. The dominant belief is a generic objection. The reframe is just the positioning restated. The language section contains phrases the copywriter invented, not the customer. A brief with all the form and none of the substance produces copy that sounds professional and moves nobody. The test: does any section contain something that surprised you during research? If not, the brief is a template, not a document.

**No tribe consideration (Godin).** The brief describes an audience (people who receive broadcasts) rather than a tribe (people who connect to each other). Ask: is this brand building a community where members interact, or just a customer list? The answer changes messaging strategy — tribes need belonging language, shared identity, and member-to-member connection. Audiences need value propositions and proof. If the brief doesn't clarify which structure the business model supports, downstream skills won't know whether to optimize for reach or depth.

**Invisible language in customer quotes (Godin).** If customers describe the product as "comprehensive," "robust," "seamless," or "best-in-class," the product is in the very-good trap. Very good is the enemy of remarkable — functional but forgettable. A brief with Language section full of invisible words is a brief for a product nobody will talk about. Flag it: the product needs something worth remarking on before the brief can position it.

**Persuasion where enrollment belongs (Godin).** If the brief reads like an argument for why someone should buy, it's written from the seller's worldview, not the customer's. A good brief is a recognition statement — the reader should feel "this is exactly what I've been looking for," not "here's why you need this." If the Problem section argues rather than mirrors, if the Differentiation section convinces rather than clarifies, rewrite from the customer's worldview. Enrollment is pulling in people who already want the change; persuasion is pushing people toward a change they haven't chosen.

**Frame positioning not specified (Klaff: Pitch Anything).** Every brand either holds the frame or cedes it. "We'd love to help you" is supplicant positioning — the customer holds the frame by default. "We work with [specific criteria] who are ready to [specific outcome]" is prize positioning — the brand holds the frame. This choice should be explicit in the brief. If the Positioning section doesn't specify the frame dynamic, downstream skills will default to supplicant framing because that's what Claude does naturally. Make the frame choice visible: Does this brand position itself as the prize (selective, qualification-based, expert) or as the supplicant (eager, accessible, accommodating)? Both are valid — but the choice must be conscious.

**Hedging instead of committing (Lumet).** A brief that says "maybe premium, maybe accessible" or "could target solopreneurs or agencies" produces downstream creative that commits to nothing. Every unresolved choice in the brief becomes an unresolved choice in every landing page, email, and ad built from it. Bold wrong beats timid right — a brief that fully commits to the wrong audience can be corrected in an afternoon; a brief that hedges between two audiences produces months of mediocre output before anyone notices. When you catch the brief straddling two positions, force the choice. Name what you're giving up. A brief that tries to be everything to everyone is a brief that does nothing for anyone.

**Positioning that speaks to all three forces (Heath: Switch).** Positioning that only addresses Rider concerns (features, logic, comparison) misses the Elephant. Positioning that only addresses Elephant concerns (aspiration, feeling) leaves the Rider confused about what the product does. Strong positioning names the destination (Rider), evokes the feeling of getting there (Elephant), and implies an achievable path (Path).

**Voice guidelines as adjectives instead of character (Johnstone: Impro).** "Professional yet approachable" is not a character — it cannot be performed. "A builder who has shipped twelve products, swears occasionally, and believes simple beats clever every time" is a character — it can be inhabited. The voice section should define a character a writer can play, not a mood a committee approved.

**Desired context not named (Sierra: Badass).** The transformation section describes what the product does, not what the user is trying to become. "Better project management" is a feature category. "Shipping on time without waking up at 3am about missed deadlines" is a desired context — the bigger skill the user is trying to master. The context determines which features matter and which messaging resonates. A brief that names the product's outputs but not the user's desired context produces copy that lists features instead of naming capabilities. Ask: what is the user trying to become great at? That answer — not the product description — governs the transformation section.

**Product-centric language filter (Sierra: Badass).** If the Language section contains "Our platform enables...," "Powerful analytics," "Seamless integrations," or "Industry-leading," flag it. These are product descriptors that will produce product-as-hero copy downstream. Every downstream skill inherits the brief's language — product-centric phrases here metastasize into product-centric sales pages, emails, and ads. Reframe as user capabilities: "You can now..." / "You will spot..." / "Your tools talk to each other without you lifting a finger." The grammatical subject in the Language section should be "you" (the customer), not "we" (the company) or "it" (the product).

## When You Run This Skill

First, check if `.claude/BRIEF.md` exists.

**If it exists:** Read it, summarize what's captured, and use `AskUserQuestion` to ask which sections need updating. Only gather information for those sections. Don't re-interview for everything.

**If it doesn't exist:** Use the `AskUserQuestion` tool to let them choose:

```
Question: "How do you want to build the brief?"
Options:
1. "Draft from codebase" — I read the repo and write a first pass. You correct and fill gaps. Faster. (Recommended)
2. "Build from conversation" — We walk through each section together. More thorough if the codebase is sparse.
```

The "Other" option is automatically available if they want something different.

After drafting, ask: "What needs correcting? What's missing?"

## Inference Over Blanks

When drafting from codebase, make reasonable inferences rather than leaving empty fields or "[TBD]" placeholders.

- If you can infer likely customer language from the copy, write it as a reasonable guess
- If you can deduce objections from the product type, include them
- If pricing exists, calculate what the value proposition implies

Mark inferences that need verification, but give the user something to react to. "Here's what I inferred — what needs correcting?" is more useful than "Please fill in these 12 blank fields."

The user can always correct a wrong guess. They can't react to nothing.

## Pre-Launch Briefs

For products without customers yet, the brief serves a different purpose: it captures hypotheses to validate, not facts to reference.

**Mark what's hypothesized.** Use `[Hypothesis]` tags for anything not yet validated by real customer behavior. This keeps the brief honest about what you know vs. what you're guessing.

**Strengthen what you DO know.** Founder insight, competitive research, niche expertise, adjacent experience. You may not have customer quotes, but you know the space. Lean into that.

**Pre-plan the proof.** What metrics will you track? What would a strong testimonial say? What outcome would prove the value prop? This gives direction for what to capture post-launch.

**Set validation triggers.** Note what should prompt a revisit: "After 10 customer calls, update Language section." "After first churn, revisit Objections." Makes the brief a living document, not a launch artifact.

A pre-launch brief should be specific enough to write launch copy from, while being honest that the customer sections are educated guesses awaiting validation.

## Gathering Information

Don't dump all questions at once. Work through sections conversationally.

Before any interview, list every assumption about the audience, problem, and positioning that feels obvious -- then treat each one as a question rather than a foundation, because the brief that confirms the founder's starting hypothesis almost always missed the real customer insight hiding just past the comfortable answer (Camp).

**Let them speak before diagnosing.** Let the founder or customer speak for at least two uninterrupted minutes before asking directed questions. The sequence, language, and emotional weight of what they mention first versus last is diagnostic gold that directed questions destroy. The instinct is to hear enough to confirm a positioning hypothesis and start steering — resist it. What they volunteer unprompted reveals what actually matters to them, not what your framework says should matter.

Before writing any section, study three things: what the product actually does differently (not what the marketing says — what the code, the process, or the delivery actually changes), what consumers actually say about their problem (their words from reviews, forums, and support tickets, not the founder's paraphrase), and what competitors actually claim (so you can say something different, not something similar). The brief is only as good as the research behind it. A brief built on assumptions produces copy that sounds plausible and converts nobody.

Push for verbatim customer language. "How do your customers describe this problem?" matters more than how the founder describes it. Exact phrases from reviews, support tickets, and sales calls are gold.

When answers are vague, ask for examples. "Can you give me a specific customer who fits this?" unlocks better answers than "Who's your target audience?"

## Sections

Not every section applies to every project. Apply the active cutting test: if removing a section would not change any downstream deliverable, it shouldn't be there (McPhee). A simple tool doesn't need competitive analysis. A solo creator doesn't need B2B personas.

**Name the intention for each brief section (Weston).** The Problem section's intention isn't "describe the problem" — it's "make downstream skills write copy that makes the reader feel the cost of inaction in their body." The Proof section's intention isn't "list evidence" — it's "arm downstream skills with facts specific enough that the reader forms their own judgment of credibility." When each section carries an intention (a verb describing what it should DO to the reader via downstream skills), the brief writer focuses on impact, not completeness. A Problem section with the intention "make the reader feel the cost" will include vivid specifics. A Problem section with the intention "describe the problem" will include generic summaries.

### Product

- **Change statement** (Godin): "We help [specific person] go from [current state] to [desired state]." This comes before features, before mechanism, before differentiation. If you can't fill it in, you don't know what change you're making — and a brief without a named change produces copy that describes the product instead of the transformation.
- One-line description (6-10 words max — tagline-ready, not a description. "Find creators who sell things, fast." not "Creator discovery and outreach management for growth teams." If the one-liner requires industry jargon to make sense, you haven't understood what the product actually does — you've memorized the category label. Test: could someone outside the industry read it and immediately grasp the value?)
- What it does (2-3 sentences, not features - outcomes)
- Category (position by analogy when possible: "closer to Apollo for creators than Grin for brands" helps people map it instantly)
- Business model (SaaS, course, coaching, marketplace, etc.)
- Pricing — when there are multiple tiers with comparable dimensions (price, limits, features, who it's for), use a table, not prose. Tables are scannable; prose buries the comparison. A pricing table with columns for tier name, monthly price, annual price, per-seat cost, and who gets it lets any downstream skill grab the right number instantly. Prose pricing forces the reader to parse paragraphs and cross-reference glossary entries to reconstruct what a table would show at a glance.
- **Offer-first test (Fladlien):** State the offer in 2 sentences. If the reaction is "wait, really? How?" — the offer is strong enough to build a brief around. If not, the offer needs work before the brief can position it. Offer strength > positioning > audience targeting.
- **Value ladder position (Brunson):** Map the full value ladder — bait offer (free or low-cost entry), frontend ($), middle ($$), backend ($$$). Even if the current project is a single page or asset, knowing where it sits on the ladder changes the copy. A landing page for the frontend offer that doesn't know a backend exists will optimize for the wrong conversion — it closes the sale instead of opening the relationship. A brief that captures one offer without the ladder produces isolated assets instead of a system. Name each rung that exists today, mark planned rungs as `[Planned]`, and note which rung this specific project serves. Downstream skills use the ladder to calibrate urgency, price anchoring, and next-step CTAs.
- **Remarkability diagnosis (Godin):** Can you name the remarkable thing in one sentence? If not, stop — the product has a positioning problem, not a marketing problem. Copy cannot create remarkability; it can only reveal and amplify remarkability that already exists in the product. If the product is "very good" but not remarkable, flag it before building the brief.
- **Who's talking? (Godin):** Who is currently talking about this product, and what do they say? If the answer is "nobody" or "it's good," marketing optimization is premature — the product or offer needs to change first. This question determines whether the work is positioning (finding the remarkable angle) or amplification (spreading a remarkable angle that already exists).

**Rhetorical genre.** Name whether the product's primary communication is deliberative (future advantage — "here's what you'll gain"), forensic (past evidence — "here's what we've proven"), or epideictic (shared values — "here's what we stand for"). Most products are primarily deliberative, but a brand-first play is epideictic and a case-study-led approach is forensic. This determines whether downstream copy leads with outcomes, proof, or identity — and getting it wrong means every skill builds from the wrong frame.

**Spine (Lumet).** The single argument the entire project makes — stated in one sentence. The spine is not the goal ("increase conversions") but the argument ("your audience is ready to buy but your page is talking them out of it"). The spine should be written from the customer's world outward -- "this product exists so that [customer] never has to [specific frustration] again" -- rather than from the seller's conversion goal inward. A mission-oriented spine produces briefs that generate empathy-first copy rather than pitch-first copy (Camp). Every element of the brief should serve the spine; any element that doesn't is noise, no matter how interesting it looks in isolation. The spine resolves conflicts between downstream skills — if one skill emphasizes speed and another emphasizes niche depth and a third emphasizes team workflow, the spine says which one wins. Test: if two skills built from this brief produced contradictory messages, the spine would settle the dispute. Example: "Every piece of Scouter marketing should make the reader feel that manual prospecting is an embarrassing waste of their talent." The spine isn't customer-facing copy — it's the internal compass that every brief element must pass through. The spine not only resolves conflicts but determines which sections are load-bearing and which can be cut. Any brief element that doesn't connect to the spine is a candidate for removal — not because it's bad, but because it isn't structural (McPhee).

**"What it does" must cover all differentiators.** After drafting, cross-reference this paragraph against the Differentiation section. If a key differentiator isn't reflected in the product description, the brief has a gap. The product description is often the first thing another skill reads — if it only mentions 3 of 5 differentiators, the other 2 get lost in all downstream output.

### Audience

- Who this is for (company type, role, situation)
- Who this is NOT for (anti-persona - who should you turn away?)
- **Who should say no to this.** Add an explicit "who should say no to this" field -- capturing the conditions under which this product legitimately shouldn't be bought makes the entire brief more honest and every downstream skill more targeted. Telling the audience "this isn't for you if X" converts better than pretending universal fit (Camp).
- Primary use case (the main job they hire you for)
- Secondary use cases (other jobs, if any)
- Where they are on the buying timeline (First Thought — just realized something's off / Passive Looking — noticing alternatives / Active Looking — comparing options / Deciding — down to 1-2, needs confidence. Note the primary stage. This determines whether downstream copy should lead with problem, solution, differentiation, or proof.)
- **Buying pyramid** (Holmes): For each audience segment, estimate where they sit in the Stadium Pitch pyramid: 3% buying now, 6-7% open to it, 30% not thinking about it, 30% don't think they're interested, 30% know they're not interested. The pyramid determines whether downstream content should educate or convert. The 3% buying now need a clear offer and a reason to choose you today. The 6-7% open need differentiation and proof. The other 90% need education that shifts their frame before any selling happens. A brief that assumes everyone is in the top 3% produces pushy copy that alienates the 97%. A brief that maps the pyramid produces appropriate content for each stage — teaching content that moves people UP the pyramid, and conversion content that closes the ones already at the top. Note the primary segment for each channel: cold traffic is mostly bottom-of-pyramid (educate first); warm referrals are mostly top (convert directly); email nurture sequences walk people from middle to top over time. If the brief doesn't map this, every downstream skill defaults to selling — which only works on 3% of any audience.
- **Primary archetype(s)** (Pearson): Which psychological journey stage does the ICP primarily operate from? Orphan (just realized the old way failed, needs validation), Wanderer (actively exploring alternatives, values independence), Warrior (committed to achieving, needs tools and tactics), Altruist (has succeeded and wants to help others). Name the dominant archetype — it determines emotional tone, proof needs, and messaging style downstream.
- **Adoption segment** (Moore): Are you selling to visionaries or pragmatists? Visionaries buy potential ("this could change everything"). Pragmatists buy proof ("show me peers in my segment who succeeded"). Visionary testimonials are negative references for pragmatists. If the product has strong early adopters but stalled growth, this is likely a chasm problem — the messaging needs to shift from excitement to evidence.
- **Beachhead** (Moore): The audience should be a beachhead, not a TAM. Small enough to dominate, specific enough that they talk to each other, painful enough to compel action. "E-commerce companies" is a TAM. "DTC supplement brands doing $500K-2M/year on Shopify who run Meta ads and have no in-house data analyst" is a beachhead. Test: can you name 50-200 specific companies or people who fit? If not, it's still a persona, not a targetable segment.

**Five Early Tests for Positioning (Baker).** Before committing to a positioning decision, run five diagnostic tests. A position that fails any of these tests will not generate enough expertise or enough opportunity to sustain a practice.

| Test | What it measures | Pass threshold |
|------|------------------|----------------|
| **How many prospects exist?** | Market size — large enough to sustain the business without needing more than 2-5% share | 2,000-10,000 viable prospects nationally |
| **Drop and give me 20** | Expertise depth — can you articulate 20 specific insights about this space that would surprise a practitioner in it? | 20 non-obvious observations |
| **Can you buy a list?** | Marketability — can you acquire a list through a list broker, industry association, or database? | Yes, with reasonable specificity |
| **Competitive density** | How many firms or practitioners serve this space? | Fewer than 200 direct competitors |
| **Will somebody travel?** | Value perception — would a prospect fly to meet you, or is this a commodity engagement? | Yes, regularly |

The "Drop and give me 20" test is diagnostic gold. If you cannot list 20 specific, non-obvious insights — things that would genuinely surprise someone in the space — you don't have enough pattern recognition to claim expertise yet. Each of those 20 insights is a blog post, a lead magnet, or a content piece. The "buy a list" test directly impacts lead generation: if you can buy a list, outbound becomes viable; if not, you must rely on inbound (slower but valid). Use these tests to validate positioning before building the brief around it.

**Dominant desire (Collier).** The brief should distinguish between the surface desire (what the audience would tell you politely) and the dominant desire (what keeps them up at night). "I want to grow my coaching business" is surface. "I need to make $3,000 more per month or I'm going back to a gym job" is dominant. The dominant desire is immediate, urgent, and more primal than the aspirational version. Every downstream skill needs both — and should target the dominant one. If the brief only captures the surface desire, every ad, email, and sales page built from it will sound reasonable but won't make anyone act NOW.

**Emotional hot buttons (Garfinkel).** Beyond generic "pain points" and "desires," map 1-2 dominant hot buttons per audience segment from Garfinkel's taxonomy: FOMO, status, relief, curiosity, belonging, vindication, control. The hot button is the specific emotional lever that makes this audience act — not the problem they have, but the feeling that turns awareness into urgency. A status-driven buyer responds to "ahead of your competitors" framing; a relief-driven buyer responds to "finally stop doing X." Name the dominant hot button explicitly so downstream skills can press it consistently rather than guessing at emotional tone.

**Genuine curiosity (Carnegie).** The brief should capture what the founder is genuinely curious about in the customer's world — not just what they want to sell to them. "What keeps you up at night" is a cliche. "What does your Tuesday morning actually look like before you open Slack" is genuine curiosity. This becomes the fuel for every downstream skill that reads the brief — ads, emails, and pages built from genuine curiosity sound different from ones built from sales objectives. During the interview, ask the founder: "What about your customer's daily experience are you actually fascinated by?" If they can only answer in terms of the product, the curiosity isn't there yet.

**Algorithmic specificity test (Cannell & Travis).** If you can't describe the audience in one phrase specific enough for a platform's targeting algorithm to find them, the definition is too broad. "Business owners" fails the test — no algorithm can act on it. "Shopify store owners running Meta ads with $500K-2M revenue" passes — you could paste it into an ad platform and get a targetable cohort. This isn't just a paid-media concern; it's a clarity diagnostic. An audience definition vague enough to confuse an algorithm will confuse every downstream skill too. Narrow until the platform knows exactly who to show it to.

**Smallest viable audience (Godin).** Push for the narrowest possible audience definition. The goal is not to reach more people but to become essential to the right people. "Entrepreneurs" is not a tribe. "Solo founders building their first SaaS who refuse to take VC money" is a tribe. The narrower the audience, the stronger the belonging — and the more powerful the word-of-mouth. Name who is explicitly excluded. If the brief doesn't state who this is NOT for, it hasn't contracted far enough.

**Worldview statement (Godin).** Before any positioning work, name the audience's existing belief: "These people already believe ___." You can't tell a story that contradicts the audience's worldview — you can only tell a story that fits within it and extends it. The worldview is not the problem, the desire, or the objection — it's the pre-existing lens through which they'll interpret everything you say. "These people already believe that hiring is broken and traditional recruiters don't understand tech" is a worldview. "These people need better recruiting tools" is a need, not a worldview. If you can't name the worldview, you don't know your audience well enough to position anything.

**Status role (Godin).** Diagnose whether the buyer is playing a dominion game (wants to rise above peers — exclusive, premium, scarce signals) or an affiliation game (wants to belong — community, shared identity, inclusion signals). This shapes all downstream messaging: dominion buyers respond to "ahead of the competition" language and scarcity; affiliation buyers respond to "join the movement" language and social proof. Getting this wrong means every skill built from the brief uses the wrong register — selling exclusivity to someone who wants belonging, or selling community to someone who wants an edge.

**Traffic temperature tiers (Brunson).** Define how messaging shifts by audience temperature. Cold traffic (strangers — no relationship, no context) needs more story, more proof, more bridge-building before the ask. The cold version of the pitch leads with a relatable problem, teaches something valuable, and earns the right to present the offer. Warm traffic (engaged — they follow you, read your emails, know your name) needs less setup and more specificity — they already trust the source, so lead with the offer and differentiate. Hot traffic (buyers and fans — they've purchased before or are deep in the ecosystem) gets the most direct, assumptive version — minimal preamble, insider language, "here's the next thing" framing. A brief without temperature tiers produces one-size-fits-all copy, which means cold traffic gets pitched too fast and hot traffic gets over-explained. For each tier, note: what the opening sounds like, how much proof is needed, and what the CTA assumes — and write the actual CTA copy. Not "low commitment CTA" but "Browse creators free" or "Start finding creators." Downstream skills need the exact words, not a description of the words. Downstream skills — ads, emails, landing pages — use these tiers to match the message to the relationship.

**Permission level (Godin).** Where does the audience currently sit on the permission spectrum? *Situation* (cold, no relationship — they don't know you exist), *trust* (engaged, proven value — they've opted in and you've delivered), or *intravenous* (act on their behalf — they've delegated decisions to you, like Amazon's one-click). This determines the go-to-market cadence and ask size. Situation-level permission means the first touch must offer value with zero obligation — a lead magnet, a free tool, educational content. Trust-level permission supports direct offers and product launches to an engaged list. Intravenous permission supports auto-renewals, curated recommendations, and acting without asking. A brief that assumes trust-level permission for a cold audience will produce campaigns that ask too much too soon. A brief that treats an intravenous audience like strangers will under-leverage the relationship. Name the current level and the target level — the distance between them is the nurture sequence's job.

**Three Tens baseline (Belfort).** For the target ICP, estimate starting levels of three certainties on a 1-10 scale:

- **Product certainty:** How convinced are they that this type of solution works? (Not your product specifically — the category approach.)
- **Personal trust:** How much do they trust the person or voice behind the brand? (Cold audience = low. Referral = high. Personal brand with content history = medium-high.)
- **Company trust:** How much do they trust the organization delivering it? (New company = low. Known brand = high. Solo founder with no track record = low.)

This baseline is the diagnostic that all downstream skills use to prioritize what to build. A sales page for an audience at 3/8/2 (low product certainty, high personal trust, low company trust) leads with teaching and social proof, not founder story. An email sequence for 8/3/6 leads with personal vulnerability, not case studies. Every downstream skill should be able to look at these three numbers and know which certainty to prioritize. Mark as `[Hypothesis]` if estimated without research — but estimate anyway. A wrong guess that gets corrected is more useful than a blank field that gets ignored.

**Category vs. reality check.** After defining the ICP, ask: "What do I actually know about this specific audience versus what I'm assuming from the category?" If the answer is mostly category-level assumptions ("enterprise customers have long sales cycles," "DTC brands care about ROAS"), the audience section needs primary research, not inference. Persona-based reasoning replaces investigation — treating every person in a segment identically prevents discovering the subgroup that converts fast or the use case nobody anticipated.

**Belief research through the failure landscape (Albuquerque).** The dominant belief crystallizes in what they tried, what they blamed, and what they concluded. The failure landscape is the history of the prospect's attempts and abandonments — and it reveals the belief more reliably than any direct question. Look for language like "the real problem is...," "nothing works because...," "I've tried everything but..." in forums, reviews, Reddit threads, and call transcripts. These phrases are the belief speaking. A prospect who says "the real problem is that all CRMs are built for enterprise" has a belief about the category, not just a product complaint. A prospect who says "I've tried three agencies and they all overpromise" has a belief about the service model. Map 3-5 of these failure-landscape statements per audience segment — they become the raw material for the dominant belief field in the Problem section, and they give downstream skills the exact language to echo before reframing.

**Jobs to be done.** People don't buy products because of who they are. They buy because of what they're trying to accomplish in a specific moment. Capture the jobs using this structure:

> "When [specific circumstance], I want to [motivation/progress], so I can [desired outcome]."

Each job has three dimensions. Identify all three, then note which one dominates — the dominant dimension is what marketing should lead with:

- **Functional:** What they're practically trying to accomplish ("Log what I actually did, even if it was only 2 minutes")
- **Emotional:** How they want to feel or stop feeling ("Feel like my small efforts actually count") — this often dominates
- **Social:** How they want to be perceived ("Be seen as someone who takes care of themselves")

Don't write JTBD as feature descriptions. "I want to track my workouts" is a feature. "I want my 5-minute session to count toward something" is a job. Don't make circumstances too broad — "when I want to exercise" isn't specific enough. The job is different at 6am before work vs 9:30pm when you skipped the gym.

### Use Cases

Concrete scenarios that show exactly when and how someone uses the product. Not feature descriptions — specific moments with sensory detail.

A good scenario includes: **time, place, action, friction, and feeling.**

Bad: "Users want to log short workouts."
Good: "At 6:45am, he does 5 minutes of pushups in the living room before the kids wake up. He opens Apple Health but there's no way to log 'pushups, 5 minutes, medium intensity.' So he doesn't log it. By Thursday he can't remember if he worked out twice or three times that week."

The scenario should reveal WHERE the current solution breaks down. That breakdown is the entry point for the product.

**Cover different moments, not variations on one.** Use cases should span the product's surface area — not just repeat the primary use case with different details. If three of four scenarios are "finding creators" in different niches, that's one use case told three times. Include at least one scenario for a secondary workflow (team coordination, qualification, reporting, pipeline review) so downstream skills understand the product's full value, not just its headline feature.

**Name the characters.** Every scenario needs a named person with a role — "Sarah, an agency coordinator" not "a user." Named characters make scenarios feel observed, not invented. Apply the "make them see" test (Spence) to each scenario: if the reader can't picture the scene — the time of day, the screen they're looking at, the tabs they have open — it's a workflow description, not a use case. Workflow descriptions tell downstream skills what the product does. Scenes tell them what the product *interrupts.* The before-state scenario (the one showing life WITHOUT the product) is often the most valuable — it gives ad hooks, email openers, and landing page heroes a specific moment to reference.

For pre-launch products, scenarios can come from Reddit threads, app reviews, founder experience, or informal conversations — but they should still be specific. If the scenario reads like a feature list dressed up as a story, it's not a scenario. Mark invented scenarios as `[Hypothesis]`.

### First Session Map

Map what happens in the user's literal first 5 minutes with the product. Not a feature tour — the actual sequence of actions, screens, and decisions that lead to the moment of conviction (or abandonment).

**Structure:**
1. **Entry point:** How they arrive (signup page, free tier, invite link, demo). What do they see first?
2. **First action:** What do they click/search/type? What's the most natural first move for the primary persona?
3. **First value moment:** When do they first think "oh, this is useful"? Name the specific screen, result, or data point that delivers it. How many seconds/clicks does it take to reach?
4. **Conviction moment:** The moment they decide to stay (or leave). What tips it? A search result that matches their niche? A profile card that shows cross-platform data they couldn't find manually? A number that surprises them?
5. **Drop-off risks:** Where might they bounce? Empty results for their niche, confusing UI, forced signup before value, slow load time. Name the specific moments where the product's promise could break on contact.

This map is the bridge between the brief's positioning and the actual product experience. A brief that promises "find 100 creators in 10 minutes" but doesn't map whether a new user CAN do that in their first session is positioning a hypothetical product. Downstream skills use this map for: onboarding copy (what to say at each step), landing page specificity (promising what the first session actually delivers), and ad hooks (referencing the real first-session experience, not an idealized one).

For products with a free tier or trial, the first session IS the sales pitch — the product must deliver the brief's promise before asking for money. If the first session map reveals a gap between what the brief promises and what the product delivers, flag it as a critical risk.

For pre-launch products, map the intended first session and mark as `[Hypothesis — validate with first 10 users]`. After launch, update with observed behavior (where do users actually click first? where do they drop off?).

### Personas

For products with multiple stakeholders, map the buying center — not just user types:

| Persona | Cares about | Challenge | Value we promise |
|---------|-------------|-----------|------------------|
| User | Day-to-day efficiency | [specific pain] | [specific benefit] |
| Champion | Looking good to leadership | [specific pain] | [specific benefit] |
| Decision Maker | ROI, risk reduction | [specific pain] | [specific benefit] |

Skip this for clearly single-user products. Required when the product has team pricing, per-seat billing, or stakeholders beyond the end user. A persona table of three user types ("solo founder, agency owner, team member") is operational but not strategic — it doesn't tell downstream skills who to target with ROI arguments vs. workflow arguments vs. status arguments.

**Every listed persona needs depth, not just a row.** For each persona beyond the primary, capture at minimum: (1) a distinct JTBD that differs from the primary persona's — if two personas share the same job, they're one persona with two titles. A growth operator's JTBD is "find and qualify creators fast." A service provider's JTBD is "find potential clients who are the right size and niche." Those are different jobs requiring different copy. (2) A distinct primary objection — the growth operator worries "will my niche be in here?" while the service provider worries "can I tell which creators actually need my service?" Different objections need different responses in downstream copy. If a persona can't pass both tests — distinct JTBD and distinct objection — it hasn't earned its place in the brief. Merge it into another persona or cut it. A brief with four personas that share the same job and the same objection is a brief with one persona and three labels.

Every persona should include specific daily-life details — the meeting that ran long, the Slack messages piling up, the guilt about the thing they promised to finish yesterday (Carnegie). Not just "Marketing Manager, 35-45, $80K-$120K." The Carnegie test for a persona: could someone in that role read it and say "this is literally me"? If not, the persona is a demographic sketch, not a person. These lived-experience details are what downstream skills use to write opening lines that create recognition — without them, every ad, email, and page defaults to category-level language that sounds like it was written from a spreadsheet.

**Best buyer profile (Holmes).** Demographic personas describe who might buy. Best-buyer profiles describe who SHOULD buy — the customers you'd clone if you could. Analyze existing customers (or, pre-launch, adjacent experience and founder intuition) across five dimensions:

- **Revenue:** Who spends the most? Which customers generate disproportionate lifetime value?
- **Retention:** Who stays the longest? Which customers renew without hesitation?
- **Referral:** Who sends others? Which customers become unpaid evangelists?
- **Fit:** Who gets the most value from the product? Which customers hit every use case?
- **Ease:** Who requires the least support? Which customers onboard fast and rarely file tickets?

The intersection of all five creates a best-buyer profile sharper than any demographic persona. A customer who scores high on revenue but low on ease is profitable but expensive. A customer who scores high on referral but low on revenue is a marketing asset, not a revenue driver. The best buyer scores high across all five — and that profile should drive every downstream skill, even at the expense of total lead volume. It is better to attract fewer leads that match the best-buyer profile than more leads that don't. Content, targeting, qualification criteria, and even product decisions should be designed to attract more of these people specifically. If the brief doesn't name the best buyer, downstream skills optimize for volume by default — which fills the pipeline with customers who churn fast, need heavy support, and never refer.

Mark as `[Hypothesis]` if estimated without customer data, but estimate anyway. A wrong best-buyer profile that gets corrected after 20 customers is more useful than a blank field that lets every downstream skill target whoever responds first.

**Decision-path map (multi-stakeholder).** When the buying dynamic involves multiple stakeholders — B2B, high-ticket with partners, enterprise — add a decision-path map below the persona table. Same product, different paths. For each stakeholder type, define: (1) the specific problem they experience in their role, (2) the implications they feel most acutely, and (3) the payoff language that resonates with their function. The CMO cares about pipeline velocity; the CFO cares about cost-per-acquisition; the CEO cares about competitive positioning. A single landing page or deck that speaks only to one stakeholder's path loses the others. Downstream skills use this map to tailor proof, objection handling, and transformation narratives per role — not per persona archetype.

### Problem

- The struggling moment (not a tagline or a category of pain — a specific scene with a time, place, and emotion. The moment that makes them start looking. "Project management is hard" is a category. "It's 4pm Friday and three people are asking for a status update you can't give because the data is in four different places" is a struggling moment.)
- **"Make them see" test (Spence):** If the reader can't visualize the struggling moment as a scene — a specific time, place, and physical detail — it's too abstract to drive copy. "Project management is hard" fails the test. "It's 4pm Friday and three people are asking for a status update you can't give" passes. Every struggling moment should survive the question: can the reader see this happening? If not, add the when, the where, and the sensory detail that makes it a scene instead of a statement.
- The villain (the root cause or cultural force that creates the problem — not a competitor, but the underlying belief or system. "All-or-nothing fitness culture" is a villain. "Other apps guilt you" is a symptom.) The villain should be named as a specific system, belief, or old method rather than a vague category — "the industry" or "the old way" gives downstream copy skills nothing to work with. "The batch-and-blast email playbook from 2015" is a villain that sales pages, ads, and emails can reference by name. "Outdated marketing practices" is a fog that forces every downstream skill to invent its own enemy or write around the gap. The test: can a copywriter drop this villain into a sentence without further explanation? If the villain needs a paragraph of context to make sense, it's too abstract to function as a nameable enemy (Clemens).
- **Shadow archetype holding them back** (Pearson): Beyond the external problem, name which shadow state the audience is trapped in. Shadow Orphan (victimhood — "nothing works for me"), Shadow Wanderer (commitment-phobia — "I need to research more"), Shadow Warrior (ruthless optimization — "if I work harder at the wrong thing, it'll work"), Shadow Altruist (martyrdom — "I can't ask for help"). The shadow is the psychological failure mode that keeps them stuck even when solutions exist.
- **Three levels of the problem** (Miller): Most briefs only capture the external. Great briefs capture all three:
  - **External:** The tangible, surface-level issue. "I can't track a 2-minute workout."
  - **Internal:** How it makes them FEEL. "My small efforts don't count."
  - **Philosophical:** Why this is just plain WRONG. Should generate conviction — even indignation. Test: would someone share this statement because they agree with it strongly? "People shouldn't have to choose between tools" is mild. "The creator economy minted millionaires and left the people who built the partnerships to find them in browser tabs" has heat. Push until the philosophical problem makes you feel something, not just nod.
  The external gets attention. The internal gets emotion. The philosophical gets conviction. All three should be present.
- **Pain drilling (Sandler):** For each problem dimension above, drill vertically through three levels of pain to reach the layer where buying decisions actually happen:
  - **Level 1 — Surface/Technical:** "What's broken?" The functional symptom they'd put in a support ticket or search query. This is where most briefs stop.
  - **Level 2 — Business/Financial:** "What does it cost?" Revenue lost, time wasted, opportunities missed, resources burned. Quantifiable consequences that justify the purchase rationally.
  - **Level 3 — Personal/Emotional:** "What does it mean about them?" The identity-level pain they won't volunteer unprompted. They're falling behind. They're not the operator they thought they were. They're embarrassed in front of their team. Level 3 is where the sale actually closes — everything above it is just the opening.
  Drilling to Level 3 is not optional. If the brief's problem section stays at Level 1 ("the tool is slow") or even Level 2 ("it costs us 10 hours a week"), downstream skills will write copy that informs but doesn't move. Level 3 pain — "I dread the Monday standup because I know someone will ask about pipeline and I won't have an answer" — is what sales-pages, decks, and email-flows need to write the lines that make prospects stop scrolling. When gathering information, keep asking "and what does that mean for you personally?" until you hit the identity layer. If the answer sounds like a job description, you're still at Level 2.
- **Current-state / future-state map (Keenan):** For each audience segment or persona, map two mirrored three-layer states:

  **Current state:**
  - *Environment:* The circumstances they operate in today — tools, team structure, market position, daily workflow. Not what's broken yet — the landscape where the problems live.
  - *Problems:* The specific issues caused by that environment. These should echo the three-level problem (external, internal, philosophical) and the pain drilling (surface, business, personal) already captured above.
  - *Impact:* What those problems cost — in revenue, time, risk, opportunity, reputation, or identity. Quantify where possible.

  **Future state** (mirrored):
  - *New environment:* What changes in their circumstances after adopting the solution. Same dimensions as current environment, transformed.
  - *Solved problems:* Each current-state problem resolved. One-to-one correspondence — no orphan problems, no miracle outcomes that don't trace back to a named problem.
  - *Positive impact:* The measurable and felt gains. Mirror the current-state impact in reverse — if the current state costs $200K in missed deals, the future state recovers $200K in pipeline.

  This map is raw material for downstream skills. Sales-pages build problem sections from the current state. Decks paint vision sections from the future state. Email sequences walk the reader from one to the other. Offers frame the transformation as the distance between the two. If a downstream skill can't find its raw material in this map, the map has gaps.

  For products with multiple personas, create a separate current/future pair for each — the agency coordinator's current state is not the founder's. A single generic map produces single generic copy.

- **Gap-size estimation:** The distance between the current state and the future state has a magnitude. Estimate it in dollars, time, or risk — whichever dimension the audience feels most acutely.

  A $500K gap (lost revenue, wasted spend, missed deals) supports a $50K offer. A $50K gap supports a $5K offer. A $5K gap supports a $500 offer. The ratio isn't fixed, but the order of magnitude must be proportional — an offer priced at 10% of the gap is easy to justify, an offer priced at 100% of the gap requires extraordinary proof.

  Downstream skills use this estimate to calibrate pricing, structure guarantees, and frame ROI arguments. A sales-page built from a brief with a quantified gap can write "pay $5K to solve a $50K problem" — one built without it resorts to vague value language.

  If the gap can't be quantified — the impact is reputational, emotional, or too diffuse to pin a number on — flag it explicitly: `[Gap not quantifiable — risk: pricing and ROI arguments will lack anchor]`. This is a positioning risk, not a formatting gap. An unquantifiable gap means every downstream offer must rely on emotional justification rather than economic logic, and the brief should acknowledge that constraint so skills don't pretend otherwise.

- Core frustration before they find you
- Why current solutions fall short
- What it costs them (time, money, stress, opportunity)
- **Internal obstacles (Mackendrick):** Name the internal resistances alongside the external problems. "They've tried three tools and stopped believing any tool will work" is more powerful and more specific than "they need a better tool." External obstacles (bad tools, missing data, no time) explain why they haven't solved it yet. Internal obstacles (learned helplessness, identity as "not a tech person," fear that changing systems means admitting the old one was a waste) explain why they haven't *tried* to solve it yet. Internal resistance is the deeper blocker — a prospect who believes the external problem is solvable but doubts their own ability to adopt the solution needs a different message than one who simply hasn't found the right option. Capture both layers: what's in their way (external) and what's in their head (internal). Downstream skills use internal obstacles to write the lines that make the reader feel seen — "You've tried this before and it didn't stick" is recognition, not a sales pitch.
- The emotional weight (what keeps them up at night?)
- **The dominant belief** (Albuquerque): What does the prospect currently believe is TRUE about solving this problem — and how does that belief prevent them from buying? Not an objection ("too expensive") — a mental model ("the only way to get coaching clients is to build a huge following"). State it in one sentence. Test: if this belief were removed, would the sale become easy? The dominant belief is the strategic center of all downstream copy — every landing page, ad, and email either challenges this belief or loses. Recognize that the dominant belief functions as a thought-terminating cliche (Montell) — a phrase the prospect repeats to shut down further inquiry. "That's just how it works" or "you need a big audience first" ends the thinking before it starts. The stronger the cliche, the more it protects the status quo, and the more precisely the reframe must crack it open. Before naming the problem, name this belief — because the prospect's dominant belief about WHY the problem exists shapes how they interpret everything downstream (Albuquerque). "They struggle with hiring" is a problem. "They believe the talent market is broken and no process can fix it" is a belief. The positioning needs to overturn the belief, not just address the problem. If you write the Problem section without surfacing the dominant belief first, every downstream skill will target the symptom while the real blocker — the mental model — sits untouched.
- **The 16-word test:** Fill in: "Most people believe [current belief]. But [mechanism] proves [new belief]." If you can't fill this in clearly, the brief's positioning isn't sharp enough yet. The mechanism must be a **named, memorable concept** — not a compound noun phrase. "Niche-indexed cross-platform database" is a description. "The Niche Index" is a mechanism name. If the mechanism doesn't have a name a user could repeat in conversation ("Have you tried the ___?"), name it. The 16-word test should produce a sentence punchy enough to be a tweet or an ad hook — if it reads like a product spec, rewrite it. Use small, common words — every syllable above first-grade vocabulary is friction that slows repetition and recall. The sentence should pass the breath test: if you can't say it in one breath, compress it.

For emotional weight, push past abstractions to sensory details and metaphors. "Frustrated" is abstract. "Staring at a spreadsheet at 11pm while your partner goes to bed alone—again" is visceral. "The app icon on your home screen becoming a small accusation" is a metaphor that writes its own ad hook. Include at least 2-3 distinct sensory scenes from different moments — different times of day, different social contexts, different emotional registers. One scene is an anecdote; three scenes prove you understand the pattern of their life, not just one bad moment. The Monday standup shame, the Wednesday evening laptop-on-the-couch, the Friday scramble before a client meeting — each scene gives downstream skills a different hook to open with. A single scene gets reused until it's stale. Multiple scenes give ads, emails, and landing pages distinct entry points into the same emotional territory.

**Empathy from evidence, not assumption (Spence).** Empathy constructed from assumption is projection — you're guessing what they feel based on what you'd feel in their situation. Empathy built from customer language, support tickets, and sales call transcripts is strategy. The emotional weight section should be sourced from real artifacts: the exact words a customer used when describing their frustration, the phrasing from a 1-star review, the moment in a sales call where the prospect's voice changed. If the emotional weight reads like a copywriter's imagination rather than a customer's lived experience, it will produce copy that sounds empathetic but misses.

**Thinking sequence (Kennedy).** Map the prospect's internal dialogue at five moments — the exact thoughts they think, in order, as the problem moves from background noise to buying trigger. Downstream skills need this sequence to respond to the next thought the reader will have, not the last one:

1. **Recognizing the problem:** The moment it surfaces — what triggers awareness? ("Wait, why is this taking so long?")
2. **Deciding whether to care:** The internal negotiation over whether this is worth attention right now. ("Is this really that bad, or am I just having a rough week?")
3. **Considering solutions:** What they imagine fixing it looks like — and what they search for. ("Maybe I need a better tool / process / hire.")
4. **Doubting they'll work:** The skepticism that follows — shaped by past failures and the dominant belief. ("I've tried tools before and they all promised the same thing.")
5. **Weighing cost of action vs. inaction:** The final internal calculation. ("Is fixing this worth the money / time / switching cost — or can I live with it another quarter?")

Write each step in the prospect's own language, not marketing language. If step 4 doesn't echo the dominant belief captured above, one of them is wrong. Downstream skills use this sequence to structure landing pages (each section answers the next thought), email sequences (each email meets the reader at the next step), and ads (the hook interrupts at step 1 or 2).

**Agitation angles (Kennedy).** For each problem dimension (external, internal, philosophical), name 2-3 specific ways the problem escalates if left unsolved. Not vague consequences — concrete deterioration with a timeline. "The spreadsheet that takes 2 hours today will take 4 hours in six months as the team grows" is an agitation angle. "Things will get worse" is not. Agitation angles give downstream skills the raw material to agitate before solving — the bridge between naming the problem and presenting the offer. Without them, copy jumps from "here's your problem" to "here's the fix," skipping the emotional pressure that makes the fix feel urgent. Each angle should pass the "and then what?" test: if the prospect hears the agitation and shrugs, the angle isn't sharp enough.

**Name the specific emotion, not the category.** "Emotional weight" is too vague to write from. Map the audience's actual emotional state using precise terms: Are they *angry* (they've been wronged by the current solution)? *Fearful* (consequences are approaching)? *Ashamed* (they should be doing better and they know it)? *Envious* (competitors are ahead)? *Confident but misdirected* (they believe they're handling it when they're not)? Each emotion has a different persuasive response — anger needs a villain, fear needs urgency with a path out, shame needs face-preservation, envy needs proof of a better way. Downstream skills can't write effective copy from "frustrated" — they need to know which kind of frustrated.

Ask: What does this feel like in their body? What scene would a camera capture? What object or image does the frustration remind them of? The best emotional weight sections contain at least one metaphor or image so specific it could only belong to this product's audience.

**Wants over needs (Godin).** The need is the functional thing ("I need a way to track workouts"). The want is the identity or emotional thing ("I want to feel like someone who takes care of themselves"). Position for the want. Needs invite comparison shopping — every competitor satisfies the need. Wants create loyalty — only one brand speaks to the identity. State both explicitly: "The need is [functional thing]. The want is [identity/emotional thing]. Position for the want."

**Hidden motives matter** (Simler/Hanson): People systematically deceive themselves about why they buy. "I want to save time" is the stated motive. "I want to feel competent and ahead of my peers" is the hidden one. The hidden motive is often status, fear, identity, or signaling — things the buyer won't articulate and may not consciously recognize. A good brief captures BOTH layers: the respectable story the customer tells themselves (stated) and the real driver underneath (hidden). Marketing that speaks only to the stated motive is fighting with one hand tied behind its back.

**Satisficing trigger:** People do not evaluate every option and pick the best — they search until they find one that is good enough and stop. The brief should identify one clear, early signal that tells the prospect "this is worth your attention, stop searching." This is not the full argument — it is the single element that earns the right to make the full argument. A specific number, a named mechanism, a reframe that lands in the first sentence. If the comprehensive case comes before the satisficing trigger, the prospect never reads long enough to encounter the comprehensive case. Front-load it. Downstream skills use this field to decide what goes above the fold, in the email subject line, in the first five seconds of the video.

**The reframe:** State the insight that changes how the prospect sees their problem. Not "we're better" — "you've been thinking about this wrong." Example: "You don't have a discovery problem. You have a data structure problem — creators exist across four platforms and no one has indexed them by what they sell." The reframe should make the prospect see their situation differently, not just see your product favorably. If the reframe doesn't change the frame, it's just positioning — write a real one. Test: would someone hear this and say "huh, I never thought about it that way"? The strongest reframes collide familiar words from outside the category — they take language everyone recognizes and combine it in a way nobody has. "Data structure problem" is familiar. Applied to creator discovery, it's novel. Borrow vocabulary from adjacent domains to create the collision that makes the reframe stick.

**Archetype shift** (Pearson): Name which psychological journey stage the product moves them FROM and TO. Example: "From Shadow Wanderer (endless comparison paralysis) to Warrior (committed execution with the right tool)." Or "From Orphan (trapped by what failed before) to Wanderer (empowered to explore new approaches)." The product isn't just solving a problem — it's enabling progression to the next stage of their journey. This shift should align with the reframe and mechanism.

When stuck on the reframe, try **reversal**: Reverse who has the problem (it's not the user's problem — it's the industry's problem). Reverse what's broken (it's not that their approach is wrong — it's that the right approach doesn't exist yet). Reverse the standard frame and extract the insight. Most reframes that feel fresh come from flipping an assumption nobody realized they were making.

**Why now.** What has changed in the market, technology, or culture that makes this product urgent today? Not "the problem has always existed" — what's the catalyst? A new platform shift, a market maturation, a technology unlock, a cultural moment. "Why now" is a distinct persuasive lever from "why this product" — it gives launch copy temporal urgency and gives ads a news hook. Example: "AI can now verify creator profiles at scale — which means niche indexing is finally possible without a 50-person research team." If the honest answer is "nothing changed, I just built it," that's fine — but name it, because downstream skills will look for a timeliness angle and invent one if it's not here.

**Obligatory scenes (Mackendrick).** Every brief creates expectations that the downstream deliverable cannot ignore. If the brief names a struggling moment, the landing page must pay it off with a resolution scene. If the brief promises a transformation, the sales page must show the after-state. If the brief identifies a dominant belief, the webinar must crack it. These are the obligatory scenes — the moments the audience expects because the brief has implicitly promised them. Name them explicitly: "The deliverable MUST: (1) show the reader their current pain in specific detail, (2) reveal the mechanism that changes it, (3) prove the transformation with a real story, (4) address the 'I've tried this before' objection before it calcifies." This becomes the payoff checklist for whoever executes the brief. A brief without obligatory scenes produces deliverables that feel complete on paper but leave the audience unsatisfied — the same way a film that sets up a conflict but never resolves it leaves the audience feeling cheated. The promises live in the brief whether you name them or not. Name them so every downstream skill knows which moments are non-negotiable.

**The impoverished identity (Fladlien):** Beyond what they believe about the problem, capture who they believe they ARE. Most prospects carry a self-limiting identity — "I'm not a salesperson," "I'm not technical," "I'm not the kind of person who can charge that much." Name this identity in one sentence. Downstream skills must challenge it: the problem section names the old identity, the mechanism cracks it, the offer positions the new one. If the brief doesn't name the impoverished identity, copy built from it will address the problem but not the self-image holding them back. Frame the impoverished identity as the "before" in a transformation narrative (Montell) — the brand becomes the dividing line in the customer's story. Before the product, they were one person; after, another. The brief should capture both sides of this divide clearly enough that downstream skills can write the before/after as an identity arc, not just a feature comparison.

**Problem Metaphors Determine Solutions.** The metaphor you choose to describe the customer's problem structures what counts as a valid solution. "Leaking revenue" (BUSINESS IS CONTAINER) implies plugging holes, patching gaps, finding the source of the leak. "Stalled growth" (BUSINESS IS VEHICLE) implies fuel, repair, removing obstacles, getting the engine running again. "Buried in admin work" (WORK IS PHYSICAL WEIGHT) implies digging out, lifting the burden, clearing the pile. Each metaphor highlights certain solutions and hides others — you can't "plug" stalled growth, you can't "fuel" a leak, you can't "dig out" of something that's stalled. The problem metaphor determines which downstream skills can write which arguments.

Audit the Problem section for metaphorical coherence with positioning. If the problem uses JOURNEY metaphors (lost, stuck, wandering, no map) but the solution uses WAR metaphors (dominate, crush, win), the reader's frame collapses — they were expecting a guide and got a weapon. JOURNEY problems demand JOURNEY solutions (map, compass, clear path). CONTAINER problems (leaking, overflowing, empty) demand CONTAINER solutions (seal it, fill it, organize it). BUILDING problems (crumbling, unstable, no foundation) demand BUILDING solutions (reinforce, rebuild, lay a foundation).

When drafting the Problem section, name the metaphor explicitly in brackets after the core frustration: "Their pipeline is leaking qualified leads [CONTAINER]" or "They're running fast in the wrong direction [JOURNEY + MOTION]." This makes downstream skills' job easier — every skill that references this problem knows which solution metaphors are compatible. If the brief doesn't name the metaphor, each downstream skill picks its own, and the messaging fragments.

### Alternatives

- Direct competitors (same solution, same problem)
- Indirect alternatives (different approach to same problem)
- Status quo (doing nothing, spreadsheets, manual work)
- **One-sentence competitive summary:** A single sentence that captures WHY all alternatives fail for this audience. This becomes a reusable positioning line. Example: "They either demand too much structure, cost too much, or capture too little information." If you can't write this sentence, you don't understand the competitive gap yet.

**Worldview competition (Godin).** Beyond feature gaps, map which worldviews competitors serve. Each competitor tells a story that fits a specific audience belief. Which worldviews are being served? Which are being ignored? The ignored worldview is your opportunity. If every competitor speaks to "data-driven marketers who believe in optimization," there may be an unserved worldview of "marketers who believe great products sell themselves and marketing should be invisible." Competing on features is a race to parity. Competing on worldview is a race to a monopoly on a belief.

**Worthy antagonist test (Howard & Mabley).** Is the status quo or competitor being positioned against genuinely formidable? A brief that positions against a weak antagonist — a straw man nobody actually uses, a competitor nobody respects — produces downstream copy that punches down. The antagonist should be strong enough that defeating it feels like a real achievement. "We're better than the free spreadsheet nobody likes" is weak. "We replace the $50K/year analyst your CFO thinks is indispensable" is a worthy antagonist. If the competitive positioning doesn't make the reader think "that's a real opponent," the differentiation won't land.

**Name names.** Don't write "rep tracking apps" — write "Strong, JEFIT, Hevy — rep/set trackers built for lifters optimizing progressive overload." Include pricing where it sharpens contrast. For each named competitor, state the specific failure mode for THIS audience. A competitor table is often clearer than prose. The status quo ("doing nothing," "mental tally," "pay someone on Upwork") deserves as much attention as named competitors — it's often the real incumbent.

### Differentiation

- What you do that alternatives don't
- Why that matters (the benefit, not the feature)
- How it works (the mechanism — named, specific, explainable in one paragraph)
- What the user becomes (not what the product does — what the user can now DO. "Spot the underperforming campaign before it burns budget" not "Access real-time analytics." People recommend products that made *them* great at something, not products that had great features. Write 2-3 transformation narratives for different personas or use cases. One transformation story limits downstream copy to one angle — the agency lead walking into a client meeting is a different "after" than the solo freelancer reclaiming their evenings or the team lead who finally has pipeline visibility. Each transformation should include a vivid after-state picture with sensory detail, specific numbers, and a specific moment (Collier). Not "they'll have a successful business" but "they open Stripe on the 1st and see $10,847 — last month was $2,300 — and they screenshot it before coffee gets cold." When the reader can picture themselves inside that moment, the transformation stops being abstract and starts being something they want badly enough to act on. Every downstream skill that paints a future references this picture.)
- Why customers choose you (in their words, if possible)

**The ONLY test** (Neumeier): Complete the sentence: "Our brand is the ONLY _____ that _____." If you can't fill it in with something true and meaningful, you haven't differentiated. "The only fractional CMO practice that builds demand generation engines for B2B SaaS companies transitioning from founder-led sales" passes. "The only marketing agency that helps businesses grow" does not. This sentence should be derivable from the Differentiation section. If it isn't, the section needs work.

**Less Exchangeable Position (Baker).** The goal of positioning is not differentiation for its own sake — it is to become less exchangeable. An exchangeable firm competes on price, availability, and likability. A less-exchangeable firm competes on insight, and insight commands a price premium because the client has few substitutes. The test: if the client could replace you with another firm and get essentially the same result, your position is too exchangeable. If replacing you would mean starting over with someone who lacks your accumulated pattern recognition in their specific situation, you have a less-exchangeable position.

| Variable | Exchangeable | Less Exchangeable |
|----------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Focus** | "We do marketing" | "We do retention marketing for subscription SaaS" |
| **Insight depth** | Surface-level recommendations | Pattern-based predictions the client cannot make alone |
| **Availability** | Always available, eager for work | Capacity slightly smaller than demand |
| **Deliverable** | Implementation (doing the work) | Strategy + implementation (knowing what to do and why) |
| **Substitutes** | Dozens of firms could do this | Fewer than five firms have this specific depth |

**Vertical vs. Horizontal Positioning (Baker).** Name the primary positioning axis: *Vertical* (industry focus — healthcare, fintech, insurance) or *Horizontal* (problem-type focus — conversion optimization, employer branding, pricing strategy). Each has different lead generation implications. Vertical positions are easier to market (buyable lists, clear conferences, shared vocabulary) but vulnerable to industry downturns. Horizontal positions are harder to market (prospects don't self-organize into industry lists) but more resilient. You must choose one axis as primary. "CRO for healthcare" is valid — it combines both with one primary. "Digital transformation for mid-market" is two vague things combined, neither axis committed.

**Name the enemy (Godin).** Differentiation isn't just what you're for — it's what you're against. The enemy should be an idea, practice, or belief the brand rejects, not a competitor. "We're against X" creates identity faster than "we're for Y." "We're against the assumption that more content equals more leads" is an enemy. "We're against other agencies" is not. The enemy gives the audience something to push against — and that shared opposition creates belonging faster than any value proposition.

**USP test (Abraham).** State in one sentence: the single most advantageous benefit you offer that no competitor can claim, plus the reason to believe it. "We index creator commerce data across six platforms because we built direct API integrations no aggregator has" passes. "We're the best creator discovery tool" fails. The USP is not the tagline and not the ONLY test - it's the one concrete advantage plus the mechanism that makes it defensible. If you can't name both the benefit AND why only you can deliver it, the positioning isn't finished.

The positioning should pass one additional gate: can you describe what makes this product different in ONE phrase that no competitor can claim? "High quality" and "customer focused" are not positions — every company claims them. The position should be ownable, specific, and singular. If the differentiator takes a paragraph to explain, it hasn't been distilled to a position yet.

Before writing the ONLY test, name 3 competitors' positioning statements. Those are the dominant patterns. The ONLY test must produce a sentence that is not a variation of any competitor's claim — if it could be confused with a competitor's positioning with the company name swapped, it fails. Differentiation that's indistinguishable from the competition isn't differentiation.

**Positioning fallback.** When the brief anchors on a specific quantified claim ("10 hours → 10 minutes," "find 100 creators in 10 minutes," "3x conversion rate"), require a secondary positioning angle for when that claim doesn't land. Not every user will experience the headline number — the solo operator searching a thin niche won't find 100 creators, the team that only prospects twice a month won't save 10 hours a week. If the primary claim fails for a segment, what's the backup frame? State it explicitly: "If the time-savings frame doesn't land, the fallback positioning is ___." The fallback should be a genuinely different angle (niche depth, team visibility, cross-platform aggregation), not a weaker version of the same claim. A brief with one positioning anchor is a brief with a single point of failure — every downstream skill inherits that fragility. Two anchors give the sales page a second act, the email sequence an alternative hook, and the ad creative a split-test axis.

**The mechanism matters.** A claim without a mechanism sounds like marketing. "We help you get fit" — how? A named mechanism makes the claim credible and repeatable: "Effort = Duration x Intensity. Two inputs. Ten seconds to enter. Rich analytics out." The mechanism should be simple enough to explain to a friend and specific enough to differentiate from alternatives.

**Reference points (Kahneman).** The brief should define the anchors that downstream skills use when presenting price, time, effort, and results. What does the audience currently compare this to? What does the alternative cost in time or money? What does inaction cost over 12 months? Without defined reference points, each skill invents its own anchors — the sales page compares to one thing, the email to another, the deck to a third — and the messaging fragments. State the reference numbers explicitly: "The anchor for price is $10,500 (the cost of doing this manually for a year). The anchor for time is 10 hours/week (current manual effort). The anchor for results is $0 pipeline (what inaction produces)." Every number downstream is evaluated relative to the last large number the audience saw — control which number that is.

**Design as positioning.** Check the codebase for design tokens, color palette, typography, and UI patterns before deciding whether to include this section. If the product has a distinct visual identity — one that signals who it's for before a word of copy is read — this section is required, not optional. Capture: (1) **Color palette** with hex values and what each color communicates emotionally (Bellantoni). Not "blue" — "#1a1a2e, deep navy — authority, rational decision-making." (2) **Typography** — display font, body font, data/monospace font if applicable, and what each choice signals. Bebas Neue says "bold, industrial, operator." Inter says "clean, modern, neutral." Name the signal. (3) **What the design deliberately avoids** — gamification elements, pastel palettes, stock photography, rounded corners. The absence is positioning. (4) **Overall positioning effect** — what does someone conclude about the product in the first 2 seconds, before reading? "This is a serious tool for professionals" or "This is friendly and approachable for beginners." Design choices are symbolic subtext (Seger) — they communicate brand values before a single word of copy is read. A dark UI with monospace type says "developer tool" without stating it. A pastel palette with rounded corners says "approachable consumer app." When the brief captures design as positioning, name the symbolic meaning — not just what the design looks like, but what it silently communicates. Downstream skills (web-design, sales-pages, decks, render-pdf) need these specifics to produce visually consistent marketing assets. A brief that drops the visual identity forces every downstream skill to guess — and they'll guess differently. Skip this section ONLY if the product has no distinct visual identity (generic template, unstyled MVP, text-only product).

**Brand color as emotional commitment (Bellantoni).** Brand colors are permanent emotional instructions to every person who encounters the brand. The brief should capture not just the colors themselves but the emotional intent behind them: "Deep navy — we want buyers to feel they're making a rational, controlled decision" or "Warm amber — we want the community to feel invited and gathered." Blue claims competence and control. Orange claims warmth and community. Purple claims transformation and premium. Black claims weight and authority. White claims clarity and possibility. If the brief doesn't specify the emotional intent behind brand colors, downstream skills (web-design, sales-pages, decks, render-pdf) will use the colors without understanding what emotional work they're supposed to do.

**Thin coverage strategy (inventory-dependent products).** When the product depends on inventory — a database of creators, a marketplace of freelancers, a library of templates, a directory of tools — the brief must address what happens when a user searches and finds too little. This is not a risk note buried in validation status. It's a messaging strategy. Define: (1) What "thin" looks like for this product — how few results trigger disappointment? (2) The recovery message — what does the product say or do when results are sparse? ("We're adding creators in this niche weekly — request priority coverage" is a strategy. An empty screen with no explanation is a churn moment.) (3) The honest claim boundary — what's the minimum coverage needed before a specific niche can be marketed? If the brief promises "find creators in any niche" but 30 of the 83 sub-niches have fewer than 5 creators, the promise breaks on contact for a meaningful percentage of users. Downstream skills need this boundary to write claims that survive the first session. Skip this section for products without inventory dependency.

**Expressions given off.** Beyond the explicit messaging, what does the product's current experience accidentally communicate? Empty states, loading speed, onboarding friction, free tier limitations, pricing page layout — these leak signals no amount of positioning copy can override. Name the gap between what the brief claims and what the product involuntarily reveals — with specific numbers, not vague warnings. If marketing claims "6,600+ creators" but the free tier shows 88 featured, state that gap explicitly. If the cross-platform promise is uneven (YouTube primary, others depend on enrichment success), say so with the actual coverage ratios. If there's no mobile app yet, name the friction. The instruction is not "what might leak" but "what IS leaking right now, with specifics." Downstream skills need to know which promises the product currently can't keep so they can either avoid those claims or set expectations honestly. A vague "some coverage gaps exist" is useless — "30 of 83 sub-niches have fewer than 5 verified creators" is actionable.

**Vocabulary Repetition Strategy (Bernays).** Identify 2-3 key terms or phrases that will appear everywhere — headlines, CTAs, social posts, case studies, sales conversations, support docs. Repetition creates reality. The term heard 100 times becomes accepted truth. Bernays orchestrated symphonies of messages across different channels expressing the same idea. Your brief should specify which terms get that treatment.

This is NOT about picking category terms ("CRM," "analytics," "prospecting") — it's about proprietary vocabulary that positions how people think about the problem or solution. "The Content Treadmill" as a named problem makes you the authority on content burnout. "The Revenue Acceleration Method" as a named approach makes you the authority on growth. "Real-time pipeline visibility" repeated across every touchpoint becomes the default way your audience describes what they need.

**How to apply:**
1. **Name the phenomenon.** Don't use existing terms if you can coin new ones. Bernays called his work "public relations" instead of "propaganda" — that single reframe made an entire industry acceptable. If you name a trend, problem, or methodology, you own it.
2. **Repeat everywhere.** The same core terms should appear in: homepage headline, email subject lines, ad copy, case study titles, feature names, help docs, sales decks. Not verbatim repetition (which creates fatigue) but varied expressions of the same core idea.
3. **Track consistency.** Every downstream skill should be able to reference this vocabulary list. If the brief doesn't specify which 2-3 terms matter most, every skill invents its own — and the message fragments across channels.

Example: If the brief identifies "creator commerce intel" as a key term, then:
- Homepage: "Creator commerce intel across 6 platforms"
- Email subject: "How commerce intel changes prospecting"
- Case study: "Agency finds $2M creator using commerce intel"
- Feature name: "Commerce Intel Dashboard"
- Help doc: "Understanding commerce intel metrics"

The repetition isn't boring — it's the architecture of how the market learns to describe what you do. Those who frame the conversation rule it (Bernays). The brand that defines the category wins the category. Name your proprietary frame, then repeat it everywhere.

**Positioning as Metaphor Choice.** Positioning isn't just differentiation — it's choosing the conceptual metaphor that structures how buyers think about the entire category. "Project management as cockpit" (MANAGEMENT IS PILOTING) highlights control, instruments, altitude, navigation — you're flying something complex and you need real-time visibility. "Project management as garden" (MANAGEMENT IS CULTIVATION) highlights growth, patience, nurturing, seasons — you're tending something organic that unfolds over time. Each metaphor determines which features feel important and which objections arise naturally.

The category's default metaphor is invisible but powerful. Most project management tools operate within MANAGEMENT IS CONTROL / VISIBILITY / MACHINE — emphasizing dashboards, tracking, efficiency, optimization. If your positioning accepts that frame, you compete on executional details (whose dashboard is clearer, whose tracking is faster). If your positioning rejects it and offers an alternative frame — "project management as conversation" (PROJECTS ARE DIALOGUES) — you shift what buyers notice entirely. Suddenly, async communication quality matters more than Gantt chart precision. The metaphor change IS the positioning.

**Metaphor selection framework:**
1. **Identify the category's default metaphor.** What conceptual frame do competitors share? Sales as WAR (dominate, crush, capture)? Growth as BUILDING (foundation, scale, structure)? Marketing as BROADCASTING (reach, amplify, channels)?
2. **Assess what it highlights and hides.** WAR metaphors highlight aggression and zero-sum thinking but hide collaboration and long-term relationships. BUILDING metaphors highlight structure and planning but hide flexibility and iteration. BROADCASTING metaphors highlight distribution but hide conversation and feedback loops.
3. **Choose an alternative that highlights your strengths.** If competitors use WAR, consider GAME (strategy, changing rules, playfulness). If competitors use BUILDING, consider GARDENING (cultivation, patience, adaptation). If competitors use BROADCASTING, consider CONVERSATION (dialogue, listening, relationship). The alternative metaphor should make your differentiators feel natural and competitors' strengths feel like the wrong focus.

Name the metaphor explicitly in the Differentiation section: "Positioning metaphor: [X IS Y]." Then audit every claim in the brief for coherence with that metaphor. If the positioning metaphor is BUSINESS IS GARDEN but the problem section uses MACHINE metaphors (broken, jammed, inefficient), the brief is working against itself. Every section — Problem, Differentiation, Transformation — should draw from the same metaphor system or compatible systems.

When the chosen metaphor is named and coherent, downstream skills inherit a conceptual spine. Sales pages, email sequences, ad hooks, decks — all build arguments within the same frame. The messaging feels focused not because every sentence repeats the same words but because every claim operates within the same conceptual logic.

**Brand Archetype (Greene).** Which seducer archetype does this brand embody? The archetype is a positioning decision that shapes all downstream copy, content, and communication. Most brands default to "helpful professional" — which is the absence of an archetype, producing invisible marketing.

| Archetype | Strategic Posture | What It Withholds | Marketing Approach |
|-----------|------------------|-------------------|-------------------|
| **Siren** | Sensory appeal, escape from routine | Practicality, restraint | Aesthetic-first, visceral language, promises of pleasure |
| **Rake** | Intense focused attention | Breadth, consistency | Makes the prospect feel singularly chosen, urgent |
| **Ideal Lover** | Mirrors unmet needs | Its own agenda | Deep empathy, reflects back what the prospect wants to become |
| **Dandy** | Defies norms, breaks conventions | Safety, approval-seeking | Provocation, challenges assumptions, polarizes |
| **Natural** | Spontaneous, unpolished authenticity | Calculation, polish | Disarming honesty, casual tone, self-deprecation |
| **Coquette** | Confident withdrawal, scarcity | Eagerness, availability | Creates pull through strategic absence, makes them come to you |
| **Charmer** | Makes the prospect feel exceptional | Focus on itself | Flattery, recognition, makes every interaction feel special |
| **Charismatic** | Bold vision, conviction | Doubt, hedging | Big ideas, movement-building, missionary energy |
| **Star** | Distance, aspiration, mystique | Accessibility, intimacy | Cultivates aura, exclusivity, keeps some things unrevealed |

Choose one primary archetype. Optionally blend a secondary for nuance — but the primary determines the core strategic posture. A Coquette brand (confident withdrawal) produces different marketing than a Charismatic brand (bold vision) or a Natural brand (disarming honesty). The archetype determines not just tone but what to withhold, when to challenge, and how much to reveal.

Name the archetype explicitly: "Brand archetype: [Primary], secondary [Secondary if applicable]." This filters all downstream voice decisions, content strategy, and marketing approach.

### Objections

Common pushback and how to address it. Every objection is a symptom of an underlying certainty gap (Belfort). Tag each one so downstream skills know whether to build product certainty, personal trust, or company trust — or lower the action threshold — rather than just "countering the objection."

| Objection | Certainty gap | Real concern or face-protection? | Response |
|-----------|--------------|----------------------------------|----------|
| "I already have a system" | Product certainty (don't believe the new approach is better) | Often face-protection (admitting the current system fails) | [Why switching is worth it] |
| "It's too expensive" | Product certainty (value not clear enough) or high action threshold (risk feels too large) | Could be either — probe which | [Value justification or smaller first step] |
| "How do I know it works?" | Product certainty (need proof of mechanism) | Usually genuine | [Proof point] |
| "I don't know if I can trust you" | Personal trust (no relationship, no track record with them) | Usually genuine | [Credibility, vulnerability, damaging admission] |
| "I've never heard of your company" | Company trust (no brand recognition, no social proof) | Usually genuine | [Logos, press, longevity, guarantee] |

The certainty tag changes the handling strategy. An objection rooted in low product certainty needs proof and teaching — not rapport-building. An objection rooted in low personal trust needs vulnerability and track record — not more features. An objection driven by a high action threshold needs risk reversal and a smaller first step — not persuasion. Misdiagnosing which certainty is low means addressing the objection with the wrong tool.

Structure prospect resistance across all four budgets -- time ("how long until I see results"), energy ("what do I have to do to make this work"), emotion ("what am I risking if it fails"), money ("is this worth the price") -- because a brief that only maps financial objections produces copy that misses the most common real reason someone doesn't buy, which is usually time or emotional risk, not price (Camp).

Include the top 3-5 objections heard in sales, support, or that you can reasonably infer from the product type.

**Face-work lens.** For each objection, ask whether it's a genuine concern or a face-protection mechanism. "Too expensive" might mean "I can't justify this to my boss" (face). "I need to think about it" might mean "I'm afraid of failing publicly" (face). "I already have a system" might mean "I don't want to admit my system isn't working" (face). The response to a face-threat objection is dignity preservation, not logical rebuttal. Answering a face-protection objection with ROI math misses what's actually blocking the sale.

### Switching

The forces that drive or block switching:

- **Push:** Frustrations driving them away from current solution
- **Pull:** What attracts them to you
- **Habit:** What keeps them stuck with current approach
- **Anxiety:** What worries them about switching

### Language

- How customers describe the problem (verbatim quotes)
- How they describe your solution (verbatim quotes)
- Words and phrases to use
- Words and phrases to avoid
- Glossary of product-specific terms with precise definitions
- The contradicting quote — the one customer phrase that doesn't fit your positioning hypothesis. That contradiction often reveals the real insight. If every quote confirms the positioning perfectly, you're cherry-picking, not listening.
- **Per-persona language.** When the brief has multiple personas with distinct JTBDs, the "how they describe the problem" and "how they describe us" sections should include at least 1-2 quotes per persona, not just for the primary. A service provider prospecting for clients describes the problem differently than a growth operator finding partners — "I waste time pitching creators who can't afford me" vs. "I spend half my week just finding creators." If the Language section only captures the primary persona's voice, downstream ad copy and email sequences will write one voice for two audiences — and the secondary persona will feel like the marketing isn't for them. Mark hypothesized quotes per persona and validate separately, since different personas may use different channels and communities.

**Conscious framing for key numbers (Kahneman).** Every key fact in the brief can be framed multiple ways, and the brief should lock in which frame each downstream skill uses. Price can be framed as daily cost ($2.16/day), monthly ($65/mo), or annual ($780/yr) — choose the frame that makes the number feel smallest. Time investment can be "10 minutes a day" or "5 hours a month." Results can be "4 clients a week" or "208 clients a year." Neutral framing is a missed opportunity — frame positively for the product, negatively for the problem and competition. Once the brief locks a frame, every landing page, email, and ad uses the same one.

**Reception-test every key phrase.** The language section captures what you intend to say — but what does the audience *hear*? For every key term and phrase in the brief, ask "what did they hear?" not "is this clear?" A phrase can be perfectly clear and still land wrong. "Automated outreach" is clear — but a founder hears "spam." "Done-for-you" is clear — but a technical buyer hears "black box I can't control." Test the words from the receiver's end, not the sender's. If the audience would restate your phrase differently than you intended, use their version.

**Scratch beyond the intake.** Don't just capture language from the brief conversation. Before drafting the Language section, actively hunt — Reddit threads, Amazon reviews of competing products, support tickets, competitor review sites, adjacent communities. Look for the exact phrases the audience uses when they think no one is marketing to them. The language section built from scratching is specific and surprising; the one built from a single intake call is thin and predictable.

**Capture rhythm, not just keywords.** A customer who says "I'm drowning in tabs" speaks differently from one who says "My workflow is fragmented." The first is visceral and compressed; the second is clinical and distanced. Note these cadence patterns — sentence length, metaphor preference, whether they speak in images or abstractions. Rhythm is character. Downstream skills that match the customer's natural speech patterns will sound like someone who gets them, not someone who studied them.

**Glossary depth.** Every product has terms that mean something specific internally but could be misunderstood. Define them precisely enough that any skill or copywriter could use them correctly. Include the exact formula, calculation, or rule where applicable — not just the concept. "Effort" means nothing. "Effort = Duration (minutes) x Intensity multiplier (Light=1, Medium=2, Hard=3)" means everything. Once a term is defined in the glossary, it must be repeated identically across all downstream assets — never synonym-rotated for freshness. If the brief says "Niche Index," every landing page, email, and ad says "Niche Index," not "category database" or "vertical directory." Consistency builds belief; variation erodes it. Synonym-rotation feels like good writing but it trains the audience to wonder whether you're talking about the same thing.

**Enumerate from the codebase.** When drafting from a codebase, don't rely on memory or inference for the glossary — actively search the code for terms in each of these categories:

- **Statuses and visibility states** (e.g., verified, pending, featured, hidden, snoozed)
- **User actions** (e.g., save, snooze, export, assign, comment, move to stage)
- **Plan and tier names** (e.g., Free, Friends, Founder, Standard — with what distinguishes each)
- **Limit mechanics** (e.g., 10 saved creators on Free, 1,000 on Pro — the constraint that drives upgrades)
- **Background processes** (e.g., enrichment, scoring, verification — things the system does that users see the results of)
- **Named concepts** (e.g., Niche Index, pipeline stages, discovery keywords)

Search models, enums, constants, UI components, and seed files. A glossary built from recall will always miss terms. A glossary built from code search won't.

**Glossary verification pass.** After drafting the glossary, go back and search the codebase one more time — specifically for status enums, boolean flags, plan/tier constants, and action method names. For each category above, confirm you found ALL terms, not just the first few. If a term appears in the UI or API responses, it belongs in the glossary regardless of how minor it seems. "Snoozed" matters as much as "Verified" — a copywriter who doesn't know what snoozed means will describe it wrong on the landing page. Err on the side of too many terms rather than too few.

**Semantic overload (Montell).** Beyond defining proprietary terms, identify 3-5 common words the brief redefines with specific proprietary meaning. "Offer," "pipeline," "discovery" — words everyone uses, but which mean something precise in your framework. These semantically overloaded words become the brand's conceptual territory: once the audience adopts your definition of a common word, they're thinking inside your frame. Flag these in the glossary with their common meaning and your proprietary meaning side by side, so downstream skills can reinforce the proprietary definition through repetition.

### Voice

**Preeminence posture (Abraham).** Before defining tone, settle a more fundamental question: does this brand operate as a vendor or a trusted advisor? A vendor sells products and optimizes transactions. A trusted advisor acts in the client's best interest even when it means recommending against the purchase. This is not a branding exercise - it's an operational decision that shapes every downstream skill. A vendor voice leads with features, scarcity, and competitive comparisons. An advisor voice leads with diagnosis, honest assessment, and long-term fit. A vendor handles objections by overcoming them. An advisor handles objections by agreeing with the ones that are valid. Name the posture explicitly. If the answer is "trusted advisor," the brief must hold every downstream skill to that standard - no manufactured urgency, no suppressed limitations, no "buy now" when "this might not be right for you" is the honest answer. Most brands claim advisor but write vendor. The brief is where the commitment gets made or exposed.

- **Tone vs mood (Lumet).** These are separate dials, not synonyms. *Tone* is the brand's consistent stance toward the material — irreverent, clinical, conspiratorial, empathetic. It holds across all output. *Mood* is what the audience should feel in a given context — urgency, curiosity, confidence, relief. Mood varies by asset and by section within an asset. The same urgency mood reads completely differently with an empathetic tone ("I know this feels overwhelming — but waiting costs you more") vs a clinical tone ("the data shows a 12% monthly decay rate on uncontacted leads"). Specify both. If the brief collapses tone and mood into one field, every downstream skill will either flatten the emotional range (same mood everywhere) or lose the brand's voice (different tone in every piece).
- Personality (3-5 adjectives)
- What you sound like vs. what you don't sound like
- Messages you deliberately don't send (not just words you avoid — entire communications other products send that you refuse to. "Don't break your streak!" → *Nothing. We don't send this.* The absence is positioning.)

**Attractive Character (Brunson).** Capture the person the audience bonds with — the human behind the brand. Four elements: (1) *Backstory* — where they came from, the origin that makes them credible and relatable, not a resume but a turning point. The origin story must identify the specific messy moment of discovery — not "I spent years in the industry" but the particular day, failure, or accident that created the insight. "I was three months from closing the agency when a client's failed campaign accidentally revealed the pattern that became our entire methodology" gives downstream copy skills a scene they can drop a reader into. "After years of experience, I developed a unique approach" gives them a biography summary they can't use. The backstory field should contain a moment, not a timeline — because downstream skills need to recreate the epiphany bridge, and an epiphany requires a specific before and after, not a gradual accumulation (Clemens). (2) *Parables* — the teaching stories they return to, the recurring narratives that illustrate principles better than explanation. (3) *Character flaws* — the admitted imperfections that make them human, not the humble-brag kind but the real kind that cost them something. (4) *Polarity* — the strong positions that repel some people and magnetize others. Without the Attractive Character captured in the brief, every downstream skill writes as "the brand" — a faceless entity producing competent but boneless copy. The Attractive Character is what turns a product voice into a person's voice. If the brand is a personal brand, this is the founder. If it's a company, it's the spokesperson, the founder story, or the narrative persona the audience connects with. Name them, capture all four elements, and note which element is strongest — that's the one downstream skills should lean on most.

**POV specification (Mackendrick).** Whose perspective does the deliverable adopt? This is a creative direction decision that belongs in the brief, not a copywriting detail to be figured out during execution. Company POV ("we built this because...") centers the brand's story and works for founder-led narratives, origin stories, and thought leadership. Customer POV ("they discovered that...") centers social proof and works for case studies, testimonials, and word-of-mouth content. Reader-as-protagonist POV ("you open your dashboard and see...") centers the transformation and works for landing pages, sales pages, and onboarding. Each POV creates a fundamentally different relationship between the reader and the material. A landing page written in company POV ("we built a tool that...") puts the reader in the audience. The same page in reader-as-protagonist POV ("you open your dashboard Monday morning and...") puts the reader inside the experience. The brief should specify the default POV for the project and note where it shifts — a sales page might open in reader-as-protagonist, shift to customer POV for proof, and close in company POV for the commitment. Without this specification, every downstream skill picks its own POV, and the result is a deliverable that shifts perspective without intention.

**Style rules** — concrete constraints that prevent drift. Sentence length, punctuation rules, capitalization style, person/POV, hard rules. Em dashes: always banned. Use commas, periods, parentheses, or regular dashes instead. Include a word-level simplicity check: audit key phrases for words over two syllables and replace them with shorter alternatives where possible - short words land faster and get remembered longer. Include "Instead of / Say" examples:

| Instead of | Say |
|------------|-----|
| "Seamlessly integrate with your favorite tools" | "Connects to Slack and Notion" |
| "Unlock the power of AI" | "Writes your first draft in 30 seconds" |

"Professional, friendly, approachable" could describe a thousand voices. "Short sentences. No exclamation marks. Second person. Sentence case." describes exactly one.

**Sensory and identity dimensions (Garfinkel).** Voice captures what the brand says — but not what the audience sees, feels, or becomes. Add two explicit prompts: (1) *Sensory signature* — what scenes, textures, or physical details does this brand evoke? A fitness brand might own "the quiet house at 5:30am before anyone else is up." A SaaS brand might own "the clean dashboard with one green number." (2) *Identity shift* — who does the customer become by using this product? Not the transformation (that's in Differentiation) but the identity language the voice uses: "you're the operator who sees what others miss" vs. "you're the founder who finally has time back." These two dimensions ensure downstream skills write copy that paints scenes and frames identity, not just delivers arguments.

### Proof

- Key metrics or results worth citing
- Notable customers or logos
- Best testimonial snippets (with specifics, not "Great product!")
- Before/after transformations
- **Proof calibration note (Fladlien):** Note the audience's starting trust level. Cold traffic needs heavy proof stacking. Warm referrals and established communities can be turned off by overselling — "I chose to omit the testimonials because I didn't want to push it beyond the realm of believability." Downstream skills use this to calibrate proof density.

**Proof diversity audit (Garfinkel).** After gathering proof, categorize each item by type: testimonial, statistic, credential, demonstration, or analogy. Flag which types are missing. A proof section that leans entirely on testimonials convinces one way; stacking testimonial + stat + demonstration convinces three ways. Before finalizing, check: does the brief have at least three of the five proof types? If not, actively seek the missing types — or note the gap so downstream skills know which proof dimension is thin.

**Specificity is a costly signal.** The proof section should contain facts, not adjectives. "847 coaches helped" not "hundreds of coaches." "$15K/month in 11 months" not "significant results." Every adjective in the proof section is a red flag — it means the specific fact is missing. Superlatives ("best," "leading," "revolutionary") are the weakest words in any brief — they cost nothing to claim and the audience discounts them automatically. Replace every superlative with a specific: a number, a timeframe, a named outcome. "43% in 90 days" is credible because it can be verified and disproven; "dramatically increase revenue" costs nothing to claim and therefore communicates nothing. Vague claims are free — anyone can make them, so they carry zero informational value. Specific claims are expensive — a liar would keep things vague to avoid being caught. The more precise the number, the stronger the signal that the person making the claim actually has the data. When briefing proof, push every metric toward falsifiable specificity. If a claim could appear on any competitor's page with the company name swapped out, it is not proof — it is decoration.

**Demonstrated over claimed.** Distinguish between demonstrated credibility (specificity, honest limitations acknowledged, real results shown) and claimed credibility (awards, certifications, years of experience asserted). Demonstrated proof is more persuasive downstream because it lets the reader draw the conclusion themselves. "We've analyzed 14,000 creator profiles across 4 platforms" demonstrates expertise. "We're the leading creator discovery platform" claims it. Brief the proof section so downstream skills can show, not tell.

**Mine for proof that exists today.** Before marking anything as [Hypothesis], check: are there existing metrics from the product itself (database size, feature counts, content library), from analytics (waitlist size, signups, activation data), or from the building process (research findings, competitive intel, patterns observed)? Pre-launch products almost always have real numbers — database size, niche count, content published, beta user behavior. Don't default to [Hypothesis] when real proof exists. A brief with "10,000+ verified creators" is more useful than one with "[Hypothesis — to capture]."

Structure as value themes when possible:

| Theme | Proof |
|-------|-------|
| Time savings | "Find 100 creators in 10 minutes vs. 10 hours manual" |
| Quality | "98% accuracy rate" |

**Name the heuristic each proof triggers.** Not all proof works the same way — different proof types fire different mental shortcuts, and knowing which one you are activating helps downstream skills deploy proof in the right order and context.

| Proof type | Heuristic triggered | Why it works |
|------------|-------------------|--------------|
| Logos, "as seen in," brand names | Recognition | "I have heard of this, so it is probably safe" |
| Case studies, detailed methodology | Effort | "This took real work to produce, so the product is probably real" |
| Testimonials, user counts, "used by X" | Social proof | "Others chose this, so the risk is lower" |
| Premium pricing, paid tiers | Price-quality | "It costs more, so it is probably better" |
| Specific metrics, falsifiable claims | Costly signal | "This claim could be disproven, so they must actually have the data" |

When a proof section leans entirely on one heuristic, it convinces one type of brain and leaves the others cold. A strong Proof section triggers at least two or three different heuristics so that different decision styles all find their satisficing trigger.

**Peak-End Rule (Shotton).** Case studies and transformation stories should peak at the most dramatic result and end on emotional resolution, not fade out with logistics. A case study that opens strong, delivers numbers in the middle, and closes with "implementation took 6 weeks and involved 3 team members" buries the emotional payoff under process detail. The reader remembers the peak and the ending — everything else fades. Structure proof so the most striking result IS the peak, and the final beat is how the person felt or what changed in their life, not how the project was managed. This matters doubly in the brief because downstream skills (sales-pages, decks, email-flows) inherit proof sequencing from here. If the brief's case studies end on logistics, every landing page and pitch deck built from them will too.

### Frame Control Language

**Who controls the conversation frame (Bernays: Invisible Government).** The brief addresses *what* to say but must also specify *who controls the frame* of the conversation. Bernays argued that those who frame the conversation rule it — the real power lies not with those who make arguments but with those who determine WHICH arguments are made.

Positioning isn't just differentiation — it's about establishing yourself as the authority who defines the category itself through proprietary vocabulary. The person who defines the problem controls the solution space. You don't win a debate — you choose which debate to have.

**How this applies to the brief:**

The brief should identify which terms, categories, and mental models the brand will consistently use to own the conversation. Not "project management software" but "the operating system for remote teams." Not "recruiting tool" but "talent intelligence platform." The frame you establish determines which criteria buyers use to evaluate solutions.

**Framing strategy checklist:**

1. **Define your proprietary frame** — What is the category redefinition this brand makes? Not where you fit in an existing category, but what NEW category or frame you're creating.

   Example: Don't compete on "best email marketing tool" (existing frame). Reframe to "email is dead, what you need is conversation automation" (new frame). Now every competitor is solving a problem you've declared obsolete.

2. **Name the terms that carry the frame** — Identify 2-3 proprietary terms or phrases that will be repeated across every touchpoint to establish the frame:
   - The problem you've renamed (not "low engagement" but "the content treadmill")
   - The solution category you've coined (not "analytics" but "creator commerce intel")
   - The methodology you've branded (not "our process" but "The Revenue Acceleration Method")

3. **Control reference points** — What does the audience currently compare this to? What frame do they default to? The brief should explicitly state: "The market currently frames this as [X]. We reframe it as [Y]."

   Example: "The market frames creator discovery as 'influencer databases.' We reframe it as 'commerce intelligence platforms.' The first frame makes us comparable to 12 competitors. The second frame makes us the category creator."

4. **Specify frame-holding language** — Give downstream skills the exact phrases that hold the frame:
   - How to describe the problem (using YOUR frame, not the industry's)
   - How to describe the solution (using YOUR terms, not generic category language)
   - How to describe alternatives (positioning them inside the OLD frame you're replacing)

**Those who name the phenomenon own the phenomenon.** If you name a trend, a problem, or a methodology, you own it. Bernays called his work "public relations" instead of "propaganda" — that single reframe made an entire industry acceptable. "The Content Treadmill" as a named problem makes you the authority on content strategy burnout. "The Revenue Acceleration Method" as a named approach makes you the authority on growth.

**Frame control in action:**

Every downstream skill should be able to answer: "What frame does this brand establish, and what competing frame does it replace?"

- **Sales pages:** Don't describe features within the existing category frame. Open by reframing the category so your product becomes the obvious answer.
- **Cold outreach:** Frame the prospect's problem BEFORE pitching the solution. The email that opens with a reframe — "Most agencies measure growth by premium volume, but the agencies scaling fastest are measuring lifetime policy value instead" — controls the conversation before making any claim.
- **Decks:** The first three slides should establish your frame, not describe your company. By slide 4, the audience should be thinking about the problem differently than they were on slide 1.
- **Content:** Every piece of content should reinforce the proprietary frame. Not just "here's a tip" but "here's how the Content Treadmill trap works and why most advice makes it worse."

**Anti-patterns:**

- **Competing inside someone else's frame.** If a competitor defined the category and you're arguing you do it better, you've already lost. You're strengthening their frame every time you reference it.
- **Letting the prospect define the problem.** If you accept the prospect's framing of their problem, you're now one of many solutions to THEIR problem. Reframe the problem and you're the only solution to YOUR problem.
- **Using existing vocabulary.** If you describe your work with the same words everyone else uses, you're reinforcing everyone else's frame. Invent your own language.

The brief should specify: the proprietary frame, the terms that carry it, the old frame it replaces, and the reference points that anchor it. Every downstream skill will use this to ensure all messaging reinforces the same frame — not fragments across different category definitions.

### Goals

**Growth diagnostic (Hormozi):** Before defining the business goal, ask: is this project trying to get MORE customers (acquisition), BETTER customers (qualification/targeting), or get existing customers to buy NEW things (upsell/retention/referral)? Most briefs assume acquisition by default. If the real lever is retention, every skill downstream — content, email, outreach — should focus there instead. This diagnostic prevents building an acquisition-focused system when the business actually needs a retention-focused one. All three levers matter, but one is usually the binding constraint. Name it explicitly so downstream skills don't default to acquisition when the brief actually calls for something else.

- Primary business goal right now
- **Growth lever:** More (acquisition) / Better (qualification) / New (upsell/retention/referral) — which is the binding constraint?
- **Three-lever audit (Abraham):** Every business grows through exactly three levers: (1) number of customers, (2) average transaction value, (3) frequency of purchase. Most businesses pour all effort into lever 1 and ignore the other two. A 10% improvement in all three compounds to 33% total growth - but most briefs only address acquisition. Name which lever is most neglected for this business right now. If transaction value is neglected, downstream skills should emphasize upsells, bundles, and premium positioning over lead volume. If frequency is neglected, retention emails, usage triggers, and reactivation campaigns matter more than top-of-funnel content. The three-lever audit prevents the brief from defaulting to "get more customers" when "get existing customers to buy more often" would move the needle faster.
- **Permission scope (Godin):** What is the scope of permission this brand has earned? A fitness app has permission to recommend workouts — it does not have permission to sell insurance. Name the territory the brand owns, the adjacent territories it could credibly expand into, and what's off-limits. This prevents scope violations in cross-sells and product launches. The test: would the audience feel betrayed or confused if the brand offered this? If yes, it's outside the permission scope. Amazon expanded from books to everything — but only after earning permission at each step. A SaaS tool that pivots from analytics to coaching without earning intermediate permission will lose trust faster than it gains revenue.
- Key conversion action (what do you want people to do?)
- **Retention thesis:** One sentence: "Users come back because ___." Even pre-launch, hypothesize what drives repeat usage — the pipeline they've built, new creators added weekly, niche alerts, team accountability. This is distinct from the First Session Map (which handles the first visit) and the conversion action (which handles the first purchase). The retention thesis handles the return. Without it, every downstream skill — onboarding emails, nurture sequences, re-engagement campaigns — optimizes for first conversion and has no hook for the second session. A brief that only describes why people *start* using the product, without describing why they *keep* using it, produces a leaky funnel that acquisition can never outrun. Mark as `[Hypothesis]` pre-launch and validate after first 30-day cohort.
- Current baseline metrics (if known)
- Target metrics (what would success look like?)

## Codebase Verification

When drafting from a codebase, verify every number before writing it. Search the actual code for:
- Database counts (seeds, migrations, model scopes)
- Pricing and plan limits (plan models, billing config)
- Feature existence (does CSV export actually exist? Does the pipeline have comments?)
- Niche/category counts (seed files, enums)
- Platform support (constants, API integrations)

**Use current code, not git history.** Only verify numbers against the current state of the codebase — not old commits, previous briefs, or archived marketing copy. A number that was true six months ago may be wrong today. If an old BRIEF.md or landing page says "3,000+ creators" but the current database has 208 verified, use 208. Old briefs are hypotheses from a previous moment; the current code is the source of truth.

**Never project forward.** If the database has 208 verified creators and 2,500 pending, write "208 verified creators, 2,500+ pending review" — not "10,000+ creators." Inflated numbers in the brief cascade into every downstream asset and erode trust when the product doesn't match the promise. Aspirational numbers belong in the Goals section with explicit targets, not in Proof or Product descriptions presented as current state.

**Claim only what the code proves.** If you write "CSV export" in the brief but the codebase has no export functionality, every landing page and email built from this brief will promise a feature that doesn't exist. When in doubt, search the codebase before claiming a feature.

## Quality Check

Before saving the brief, verify:

1. **Could a stranger write landing page copy from this alone?** If they'd need to ask clarifying questions, the brief isn't specific enough.

2. **Are there specific numbers, not vague claims?** "Saves time" fails. "Cuts weekly prospecting from 10 hours to 30 minutes" passes.

3. **Do the customer quotes sound like things real people say?** Read them aloud. If they sound like marketing copy, rewrite or mark as `[Hypothesis]`.

4. **Is the voice section actionable?** Must have "Instead of / Say" examples. Adjectives alone aren't enough.

5. **Are objections answered with real responses?** Not deflections, not "we're working on it." Actual answers or honest "this isn't for you" statements.

6. **Is the emotional weight present?** Not just functional benefits. What does solving this problem *feel* like?

7. **For pre-launch: are hypotheses marked?** Anything not validated by real customer data should be flagged for later verification.

8. **Does the product description cover all differentiators?** Cross-reference "What it does" against the Differentiation section. Every key differentiator should be reflected — even briefly — in the product description. Missing differentiators get dropped from all downstream work.

9. **Is the visual identity captured with specifics?** Check the codebase for design tokens, color palettes, typography, and UI patterns. If the product has a distinct visual identity, the Differentiation section must include: hex color values with emotional intent, font names with what they signal, what the design avoids, and the overall positioning effect. A brief that says "clean, modern design" is useless — one that says "dark mode default (#0a0a0a), neon lime accent (#b4ff39) for energy/edge, Bebas Neue display font for bold/industrial tone, no gamification, no rounded corners" gives every downstream skill a visual system to match. Missing this means every landing page, deck, and PDF guesses differently.

10. **Does every section start from the customer's world?** Read the first sentence of each section. If it starts with the product name, "we," or "our," it's supply-side. Flip it: start from the struggle, the moment, or the user's situation. Pull without push feels like a pitch. Push without pull creates recognition.

11. **Is the audience nameable?** Could you build a list of 50-200 real people or companies who fit the Audience description? If not, it's still a persona, not a targetable segment.

11a. **Five Early Tests passed (Baker)?** Run Baker's positioning diagnostics: (1) 2,000-10,000 viable prospects exist, (2) you can list 20 non-obvious insights about the space, (3) you can buy a list of prospects, (4) fewer than 200 direct competitors, (5) prospects would travel to meet you. A position that fails any test has a specific gap to address — the tests inform strategy, not just go/no-go.

11b. **Is the position less exchangeable (Baker)?** If the client could replace you with another firm and get the same result, the position is too exchangeable. Test: can you name fewer than five firms with your specific depth in this narrow space? If dozens of competitors could deliver equivalent results, the positioning work isn't done.

11c. **Positioning axis named (Baker)?** Is the primary axis vertical (industry) or horizontal (problem-type)? If neither is committed, the position tries to be both and commits to neither. "We do CRO for healthcare" commits. "We help businesses grow" doesn't.

12. **Is the dominant belief identified?** Can you state in one sentence what the prospect currently believes that prevents them from buying? If the Problem section has no dominant belief, all downstream copy will target the wrong thing.

13. **Does the 16-word test pass with a named mechanism?** "Most people believe [X]. But [mechanism] proves [Y]." The mechanism must be a memorable name someone could repeat in conversation — not a compound noun phrase. If it reads like a product spec, it needs a name.

14. **Three-level problem present?** External problem gets attention. Internal problem gets emotion. Philosophical problem gets conviction. If the Problem section only has the external, it's missing the layers that make copy resonate.

14a. **Shadow archetype named?** Beyond the external problem, the Problem section should identify which shadow state (victimhood, commitment-phobia, ruthless optimization, martyrdom) keeps the audience stuck. If missing, downstream copy will address the symptoms but not the psychological failure mode preventing action.

15. **ONLY test passes?** "Our brand is the ONLY _____ that _____." If Differentiation can't produce this sentence, it's not differentiated yet.

16. **Hidden motive acknowledged?** Beyond the stated reason to buy, what's the status/identity/fear driver? If the brief only has functional and stated-emotional layers, it's missing the motive that actually closes the sale.

17. **Is the audience a beachhead?** Small enough to dominate, specific enough that they reference each other. If the brief targets a market, not a segment, everything downstream will be too broad.

18. **Reframe present in Problem section?** There should be an explicit reframe field. Test: would someone hear it and say "huh, I never thought about it that way"? If the reframe just restates the positioning, it's not a reframe — it's a tagline.

18a. **Archetype shift identified?** The Problem section should explicitly name which psychological journey stage the product moves the user FROM and TO. If the reframe exists but the archetype shift is missing, downstream copy will change how they think but not acknowledge the identity transformation that enables action.

19. **Would both the elephant and the rider buy?** The elephant (gut, emotion, identity) must be activated. The rider (logic, ROI, features) must be armed. If the brief only provides rational justification without emotional pull, conversion copy built from it will be logically sound and emotionally dead.

20. **Does the offer pass the 2-sentence test?** State the offer in 2 sentences. If it doesn't produce a "wait, really?" reaction, the offer needs work before the brief can position it effectively.

21. **Is the impoverished identity named?** Beyond what they believe about the problem, who do they believe they ARE? If the brief doesn't name the self-limiting identity, downstream copy will address the problem but not the self-image holding them back.

22. **Plain language test.** Can you explain the dominant belief, the mechanism, and the reframe in language a non-marketer would understand? If not, you're referencing frameworks without understanding them. Jargon in the brief means jargon in every downstream deliverable. The brief that requires a glossary to read hasn't distilled anything — it's just organized the confusion.

23. **Expressions given off.** Beyond the explicit messaging, what does the product's design, response time, pricing page layout, and onboarding experience accidentally communicate? If the brief says "premium" but the product experience says "scrappy startup," downstream copy will be undermined by leaked signals no amount of positioning can override. Audit the gap between what the brief claims and what the product involuntarily reveals.

24. **Diagnostic humility check.** Can you state what evidence would prove this brief's core positioning wrong? If you can't articulate what would change your mind, the brief is held too tightly. Every brief is a hypothesis about who the customer is and what they need — hold it firmly enough to write from, loosely enough to abandon when evidence contradicts it.

25. **Does the brief have a spine?** Can you state the brief's single argument in one sentence? Not the goal ("increase conversions") — the argument ("your audience is ready to buy but your page is talking them out of it"). The spine is the irreducible purpose that every downstream skill should serve. If two skills built from this brief could produce contradictory messages, the spine is missing or too vague. Test every brief element against the spine — any element that doesn't serve it is noise.

26. **Are all specific numbers consistent and locked?** Cross-reference metrics (database size, niche count, pricing, time-saved claims) across Product, Differentiation, Mechanism, Proof, and Use Cases sections. If the Product section says "83 sub-niches" and the Mechanism section says "95 niches," downstream copy will use whichever number it encounters first. When a metric can be expressed multiple ways (95 total niches vs. 83 sub-niches vs. 12 parent categories), the brief must lock a single marketing number and note the full breakdown separately. State it explicitly: "Marketing number: 95 niches. (Breakdown: 12 parent categories, 83 sub-niches.)" Every downstream reference uses the locked number — no synonym-rotating between "83 sub-niches" in one section and "95 niches" in another. A brief is a single source of truth — conflicting numbers undermine that purpose.

27. **Are expressions given off addressed?** The Differentiation section should name the gap between intended positioning and the product's current leaked signals. If it doesn't, the brief is positioning an idealized product, not the real one. Downstream skills will write copy the product experience contradicts.

28. **Is the spine present and is it an argument?** The Product section should have an explicit spine field that states the project's single argument — not a goal, not a tagline. If the spine reads like a business objective ("grow revenue") rather than an argument ("your audience is ready to buy but your page is talking them out of it"), it's not a spine yet.

29. **Multiple transformation narratives?** "What the user becomes" should have 2-3 stories for different personas, not one. If every downstream skill tells the same transformation story, the brief is limiting the copy's range.

30. **Is "why now" articulated?** The Problem section should explain the temporal urgency — what changed that makes this product timely. If it's missing, launch copy will lack a news hook and ads will miss the timeliness angle.

31. **Inter-section priming (Cialdini).** Each section of the brief should pre-suade the next: the Problem section must prime the reader to WANT the Mechanism; the Mechanism must prime them to accept the Differentiation. Read the brief's sections in order — does each one create hunger for the next? If the Problem doesn't make the Mechanism feel like relief, if the Mechanism doesn't make the Differentiation feel inevitable, the brief's sections are independent fields, not a channeled argument. A brief where sections prime each other produces downstream copy where sections prime each other. A brief where sections sit independently produces landing pages, emails, and decks where each section starts from scratch.

32. **Are use cases scenes or workflow descriptions?** Each use case should have a named character, a specific time, and sensory detail. If the reader can't picture the scene, it's a workflow description dressed as a story. The "midnight scroll" before-state scenario is more valuable to downstream skills than a clean product walkthrough.

33. **Are all numbers codebase-verified?** When the brief was drafted from a codebase, every number (database size, niche count, pricing, feature claims) should reflect what the code actually shows — not projections, not rounded-up estimates, not marketing aspirations. Cross-reference the Proof section against the codebase. If a number can't be verified, mark it `[Unverified]`.

34. **Three Tens baseline present?** The Audience section should estimate starting levels of product certainty, personal trust, and company trust. Without this, downstream skills can't prioritize which certainty to build first — they'll default to product certainty (teaching/proof) even when personal trust or company trust is the real bottleneck.

35. **Objections tagged with certainty gaps?** Each objection in the table should identify which certainty it reflects. If objections are listed without certainty tags, downstream skills will counter the surface objection rather than raising the underlying certainty.

36a. **Internal obstacles named?** The Problem section should capture internal resistances (learned helplessness, past failures, identity-level doubt) alongside external ones. If the brief only names external obstacles, downstream copy will prescribe solutions to people who don't believe solutions work for them.

36b. **Obligatory scenes listed — with a primary set piece?** The Problem section should name the moments the downstream deliverable MUST pay off. If the brief creates expectations (a struggling moment, a transformation, a dominant belief) without naming the obligatory scenes, deliverables will feel complete on paper but leave the audience unsatisfied. Distinguish between the full list of obligatory scenes and the primary set piece — the one scene everything builds toward. Every other obligatory scene exists to set up or pay off the set piece; if no single scene carries that weight, the brief has a list but not a center (McPhee).

36c. **POV specified?** The Voice section should name whose perspective the deliverable adopts — company, customer, or reader-as-protagonist — and note where it shifts. If missing, each downstream skill picks its own POV and the result shifts perspective without intention.

36. **Current-state / future-state map present for EVERY persona?** The Problem section should have a three-layer current state (environment, problems, impact) and a mirrored three-layer future state for each persona in the Personas table. If a persona earned a row in the table, it earns a map — no exceptions. If only the primary persona has a map, downstream skills will write the same transformation for every audience. Check: count the personas, count the maps. The numbers must match.

37. **Gap-size estimated with both savings AND revenue impact?** The gap estimate should include direct savings (labor, time, cost reduction) AND downstream revenue impact (deals closed faster, opportunities captured, partnerships won). A gap that only counts labor savings produces weak ROI arguments — the revenue freed up by redirecting effort is often the larger number. If both dimensions aren't present, the estimate is incomplete.

37a. **Thinking sequence present?** The Problem section should map the prospect's internal dialogue at five moments from recognizing the problem through weighing action vs. inaction. If missing, downstream landing pages, email sequences, and ads won't know which mental step to target — they'll default to addressing step 1 (awareness) when the prospect is stuck at step 4 (skepticism from past failures).

37b. **Agitation angles present?** The Problem section should include 2-3 specific escalation angles per problem dimension (external, internal, philosophical). Not "things will get worse" — concrete deterioration with a timeline. If missing, downstream copy jumps from naming the problem to presenting the fix, skipping the emotional pressure that makes the fix feel urgent.

38. **Buying pyramid mapped?** The Audience section should estimate where each segment sits in Holmes' pyramid. If the brief assumes everyone is ready to buy, downstream content will sell when it should teach. Check: does the brief note the primary pyramid position per channel (cold traffic, warm referrals, email nurture)?

39. **Best buyer profiled?** The Personas section should identify the best buyer across all five dimensions (revenue, retention, referral, fit, ease). If missing, downstream skills default to optimizing for lead volume instead of lead quality — filling the pipeline with customers who churn, drain support, and never refer.

40. **Is the offer-first test present in the output?** The Product section must include the 2-sentence offer test with a reaction check. If it's missing, the brief skipped the single fastest diagnostic for offer strength. This is non-optional — a brief without it hasn't been pressure-tested.

41. **Change statement present?** The Product section must have a completed change statement: "We help [specific person] go from [current state] to [desired state]." If the brief leads with features instead of the change being made, it's product-first rather than change-first.

42. **Status role diagnosed?** The Audience section should name whether the buyer plays a dominion or affiliation game. If missing, downstream skills won't know whether to use exclusivity language or belonging language — and mixing them creates dissonance.

43. **Enrollment over persuasion?** Read the brief's Problem and Differentiation sections aloud. Do they sound like recognition ("you've been looking for this") or argumentation ("here's why you need this")? If the brief argues, it's written from the seller's worldview. Rewrite from the customer's.

44. **Worldview named?** The Audience section should have a completed worldview statement: "These people already believe ___." If the brief positions against the audience's existing worldview instead of within it, every downstream story will feel like an argument rather than a recognition.

45. **Wants over needs separated?** The Problem section should explicitly name both the functional need and the identity/emotional want — and flag that positioning should lead with the want. If the brief only captures needs, downstream copy will invite comparison shopping instead of creating loyalty.

46. **Worldview competition mapped?** The Alternatives section should identify which worldviews competitors serve and which are ignored. If the competitive analysis only maps features and pricing, it misses the most defensible positioning angle — owning a belief no competitor speaks to.

47. **Permission level diagnosed?** The Audience section should name where the audience sits on the permission spectrum (situation/trust/intravenous) and the target level. If missing, downstream skills won't calibrate ask size — cold audiences get pitched like subscribers, and loyal subscribers get treated like strangers.

48. **Permission scope defined?** The Goals section should name the territory the brand owns, adjacent territories it could expand into, and what's off-limits. If missing, cross-sells and product launches risk scope violations that erode trust faster than they generate revenue.

49. **Value ladder mapped?** The Product section should name each rung of the value ladder (bait, frontend, middle, backend) and identify which rung the current project serves. If the brief captures a single offer without the ladder, downstream skills will optimize for isolated conversions instead of a system that moves people up through increasing commitment.

50. **Attractive Character captured?** The Voice section should name the person the audience bonds with and include all four elements: backstory, parables, character flaws, and polarity. If missing, downstream skills default to writing as "the brand" — competent but impersonal. The Attractive Character is what makes copy feel like it comes from a person, not a company.

51. **Traffic temperature tiers defined?** The Audience section should describe how the message shifts for cold, warm, and hot traffic. If missing, downstream skills produce one version of the pitch regardless of the audience's relationship with the brand — too aggressive for cold, too cautious for hot.

52. **Removal test passed (Howard & Mabley)?** Mentally remove each element — audience segment, objection, messaging angle, use case — one at a time. If the brief's central positioning still holds without it, the element wasn't serving the spine. Every element in the brief must earn its place by advancing the core positioning. A brief bloated with tangential segments and edge-case objections dilutes the focus that makes downstream copy sharp.

53. **Tone and mood separated?** The Voice section should specify tone (the brand's consistent stance) and mood (the emotional registers used across different assets) as separate fields. If the brief collapses them into one, downstream skills will either flatten emotional range or lose brand voice consistency.

54. **Positioning fallback present?** If the brief anchors on a specific quantified claim, there should be an explicit fallback positioning angle for when that claim doesn't land. A brief with one positioning anchor is a brief with a single point of failure.

55. **Thin coverage addressed (if applicable)?** If the product depends on inventory (database, marketplace, directory, library), the Differentiation section should include a thin coverage strategy — what "thin" looks like, the recovery message, and the honest claim boundary. If missing, downstream skills will make promises the product can't keep for users who search sparse categories.

56. **Each persona distinct?** Every persona in the table should have a distinct JTBD and a distinct primary objection documented below the table. If two personas share the same job and the same objection, they're one persona with two labels — merge or cut. Secondary personas without depth produce downstream copy that treats every user the same.

57. **First session mapped?** The brief should include a First Session Map that traces the user's literal first 5 minutes — entry point, first action, first value moment, conviction moment, and drop-off risks. If the brief promises a transformation ("find 100 creators in 10 minutes") but doesn't map whether a new user can experience it in their first session, the positioning is aspirational, not grounded. For products with a free tier, this is critical — the first session is the sales pitch.

58. **Retention thesis present?** The Goals section should have a one-sentence retention thesis: "Users come back because ___." If the brief only describes why people start using the product without hypothesizing why they return, every downstream nurture and re-engagement skill has no hook for the second session.

59. **Per-persona language captured?** When the brief has multiple personas, the Language section should include at least 1-2 quotes per persona for how they describe the problem. If all quotes sound like the primary persona, the secondary personas' downstream copy will miss their voice.

60. **Pricing in a table (when multi-tier)?** If the product has multiple pricing tiers, the Product section should use a comparison table, not prose. Tables are scannable; prose buries the comparison and forces downstream skills to parse paragraphs to find the right number.

61. **Commitment check (Lumet).** Scan for hedging language: "could be," "maybe," "potentially," "both X and Y audiences," "premium but accessible." Every hedge in the brief becomes a hedge in every downstream asset. If the brief straddles two positions — two audiences, two price tiers, two tonal approaches — force the choice before saving. Name what the brief is giving up. A brief that commits fully to a wrong position is easier to fix than one that hedges between two right ones.

62. **Metaphor Coherence Across Brief.** A brief fails when Problem uses JOURNEY metaphors (lost, stuck, wandering), Positioning uses BUILDING metaphors (foundation, structure, scale), and Transformation uses WAR metaphors (dominate, win, crush) — each section individually clear but together creating cognitive friction. Tag every metaphor in the brief by source domain: Mark JOURNEY (path, destination, stuck, map), CONTAINER (leaking, full, overflowing), MACHINE (broken, tuned, efficient), WAR (dominate, defeat, capture), BUILDING (foundation, scale, crumbling), GARDENING (cultivate, grow, nurture), GAME (play, rules, strategy). Group by domain. Ensure compatibility across Problem, Differentiation, and Transformation sections. Compatible pairings: JOURNEY + MAP/GUIDE, CONTAINER + SEAL/ORGANIZE, BUILDING + REINFORCE/FOUNDATION, MACHINE + TUNE/FUEL. Incompatible pairings: JOURNEY + WEAPON, CONTAINER + ACCELERATE, BUILDING + PLUG LEAKS. If the brief uses 3+ incompatible metaphor systems without awareness, downstream skills will inherit that incoherence — the sales page will feel disjointed, the email sequence will shift frames mid-flow, the deck will lose narrative thread. Audit. Simplify to 1-2 compatible systems. Make the choice explicit in the Differentiation section's "Positioning metaphor" field.

## When to Update the Brief

The brief isn't a one-time document. Revisit it when:

**Customer insights change what you know:**
- After customer interviews reveal new language or objections
- After support tickets show recurring confusion or friction
- After sales calls uncover objections you hadn't anticipated

**The business evolves:**
- After a positioning pivot or new target audience
- When you launch a new pricing tier or change the business model
- When competitors change or new ones emerge

**The copy isn't landing:**
- When conversion copy underperforms and you can't figure out why
- When customers describe the product differently than the brief does
- When testimonials don't match the value themes in the brief

**The raw material is exhausted:**
- When downstream skills produce predictable output and no section contains a surprise
- When every piece of copy built from the brief sounds the same
- When the Language section quotes feel stale or over-used

When the brief feels stale, the problem isn't usually the structure — it's that the raw material reservoir has run dry. Refill it through fresh scratching (new customer conversations, fresh forum research, updated competitive analysis) before updating any section. A brief refreshed with new raw material produces better downstream output than one that's merely reorganized.

A stale brief produces stale copy. Keep it current.
