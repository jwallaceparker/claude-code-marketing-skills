## Product

**One-liner:** Find creators who sell things, fast.

**Change statement:** "We help growth operators go from manually scrolling YouTube and copy-pasting into spreadsheets to finding 100 qualified creators in 10 minutes."

**What it does:** Scouter discovers creators across YouTube, Instagram, TikTok, and Twitter who teach, sell, or promote in specific money-making niches — then unifies their cross-platform profiles so growth operators can qualify, save, track outreach, and export to their CRM. The database is indexed by 95 niches (not vanity metrics), AI-scored for quality, and built for teams running partnership outreach.

**Category:** Creator discovery and outreach management — closer to Apollo for creator partnerships than Grin for influencer campaigns.

**Business model:** Freemium SaaS with team-based, seat-based billing.

**Pricing:**

| Tier | Monthly | Annual | Per-seat/mo | Per-seat/yr | Who gets it |
|------|---------|--------|-------------|-------------|-------------|

Free: 10 saved creators, 1 seat, featured creators + saved only. Pro: 1,000 saved creators, 1 seat + purchasable (up to 10 extra), all verified creators visible. Annual = 30% off.

**Pricing lock / commitment rules:** Pricing tier is locked when a user first subscribes. Friends ($19), Founder ($29), Standard ($39). Cancel = lose the grandfathered rate permanently. This is a real commitment lever — early adopters get a rate that disappears if they leave. Downstream copy should name this explicitly: "Lock in Founder pricing before it's gone."

**Price anchor:** The cost of doing this manually. A growth operator spending 10 hours/week on research at $50/hr = $2,000/month in labor. $29/month replaces $2,000/month in time cost. The competitor anchor is Apollo ($49/mo for sales intelligence) or a freelance researcher ($500-2,000/month on Upwork). Present the $2,000/month manual cost first, then the $29/month price — the ratio (69:1) does the selling.

**Rhetorical genre:** Deliberative — "here's what you'll gain." The primary communication is future advantage: you'll find creators faster, close partnerships sooner, stop wasting time.

**Spine:** Every piece of Scouter marketing should make the reader feel that manual creator prospecting is an embarrassing waste of their talent.

**Offer (2-sentence test):** "Search 6,600+ verified creators across 95 niches and 4 platforms — save, qualify, and export the ones worth partnering with. Start free, upgrade when you need more." Reaction: moderate — the free tier lowers risk, but the offer relies on database quality landing in the user's specific niche. Needs proof that their niche is covered.

## Use Cases

**1. "The Tuesday Pipeline Build" (Primary)**
Marcus runs growth at an online education company. Every Tuesday, he blocks 3 hours to find new creators to pitch on their affiliate program. He opens YouTube, searches "real estate investing," scrolls through results, opens 15 tabs, checks each channel's About page for social links, copies handles into a spreadsheet, looks up their Instagram to see if they're active there too. By hour 3, he has 8 qualified creators. He's exhausted and behind on his actual outreach. With Scouter, he filters by "Real Estate > Wholesaling," sorts by engagement, and has 40 qualified creators with cross-platform profiles in 10 minutes. He saves the top 15, assigns 5 to his teammate, and starts writing DMs.

**2. "The Niche Nobody Covers" (Differentiation)**
Elena runs a SaaS tool for auto detailers. She needs creators who teach auto detailing as a business — not car enthusiasts, not review channels, specifically people teaching others how to start a mobile detailing business. On YouTube, she searches "auto detailing business" and gets 90% hobbyist content. On Scouter, she browses "Service Business > Auto Detailing" and finds 12 creators who specifically teach the business side. Three of them have audiences under 50K — small enough to actually respond to a partnership pitch.

**3. "The Agency Monday Morning" (Team)**
Ryan manages outreach at a growth agency with 3 clients. Each client needs creators in different niches. His team of 4 needs to know who's been contacted, who responded, who's in negotiation. Before Scouter, this lived in a shared Google Sheet that was always out of date. With Scouter, each team member sees the same pipeline — Fresh Lead → Outreached → Engaged → Call Booked → Won/Lost. Ryan opens the board Monday morning and sees exactly where every creator relationship stands across all three client niches.

**4. "The Midnight Scroll" (Before-State — No Product)**
It's 11pm. Priya promised her founder she'd have a list of 30 AI creators to pitch by tomorrow's standup. She's on tab 23 of YouTube, cross-referencing Instagram follower counts in another window, and her spreadsheet has 11 names — half of which she's not sure are even active. She copies another handle, switches to Instagram, searches, waits for it to load, checks their bio... "Do they even sell anything?" She can't tell. She adds them anyway with a question mark. ## Problem

**The struggling moment:** It's Tuesday afternoon. You've been on YouTube for 2 hours. You have 47 tabs open. You've found 6 creators who might be worth reaching out to, but you're not sure because you haven't checked their Instagram yet. You copy a handle, open a new tab, search Instagram, wait for it to load, scroll their feed... do they even sell anything? You can't tell. You add them to your spreadsheet with a question mark and move to the next tab. You have 24 more to check. Your actual outreach — the work that generates revenue — hasn't started yet.

**The villain:** The creator economy grew up without infrastructure for the people partnering with creators. Platforms optimize for consumers watching content, not operators finding business partners. YouTube search shows you what's popular, not what's relevant to your partnership criteria. Instagram hides business signals. There's no "LinkedIn for creator partnerships" — so operators do the work of a database manually, one tab at a time.

**Shadow archetype holding them back:** Shadow Warrior — ruthless optimization of the wrong thing. They've built elaborate spreadsheet systems, hired VAs, created SOPs for manual research — and they're proud of the process they've built, even though it's fundamentally inefficient. The sunk cost of their current system is an identity investment: "I'm the person who figured out how to do this." Admitting a tool could replace their process means admitting the process was never the competitive advantage they thought it was.

**Three-level problem:**
- **External:** Finding qualified creators across 4 platforms takes 10+ hours per week of manual research.
- **Internal:** "I feel like I'm wasting my skills on grunt work. I got hired to close partnerships, not scroll YouTube."
- **Philosophical:** Growth operators deserve tools as sophisticated as the sales reps using Apollo and ZoomInfo. The creator economy is a $250B industry — it shouldn't still run on browser tabs and spreadsheets. The fact that it does is an indictment of how seriously the industry takes the people who build the partnerships.

**Core frustration:** The ratio of research time to outreach time is inverted — 80% finding, 20% reaching out, when it should be the opposite.

**Internal obstacles:** "I've tried creator databases before and they were all influencer marketing tools — wrong audience, wrong metrics, wrong use case. I don't trust that another tool will actually understand what I'm looking for." Past failures with mismatched tools have created learned skepticism about the category.

**What it costs them:**
- 10+ hours/week on manual research (at $50/hr loaded cost = $26,000/year)
- Missed creators they never found because they searched the wrong keywords
- Delayed outreach because the list wasn't ready
- Team confusion about who's been contacted
- Low partnership close rates because the initial qualification was thin

**Emotional weight:** The 11pm laptop glow — your partner went to bed an hour ago and you're still cross-referencing Instagram handles because the list is due tomorrow. The Monday standup shame — "how many new creators did we contact last week?" and you know the number is lower than it should be because you spent half the week finding them instead of reaching them. The quiet frustration of watching a junior competitor announce a creator partnership and wondering: "How did they find that person before we did?"

**Wants over needs:** "The need is a faster way to find creators. The want is to feel like a strategic operator, not a research assistant. Position for the want."

**Hidden motive:** Status and competence signaling — operators want to be seen as resourceful and ahead of the curve. Walking into a meeting with a qualified pipeline of 50 creators nobody else has found is a power move. The hidden motive isn't efficiency; it's the feeling of having an unfair advantage.

**Satisficing trigger:** Seeing their specific sub-niche (not just "Real Estate" but "Section 8 Housing" or "Land Flipping") with 20+ verified creators. That one moment — "they actually have my niche" — is the signal that stops the search.

**The reframe:** You don't have a creator discovery problem. You have a data structure problem — creators exist across four platforms and nobody has indexed them by what they sell. YouTube indexes by popularity. Instagram indexes by hashtag. Nobody indexes by "people who teach wholesaling to audiences of 10K–100K." That's the index you need, and it didn't exist until now.

**Archetype shift:** From Shadow Warrior (optimizing a manual process they're proud of but shouldn't be) to Warrior (executing at speed with the right tool, redirecting energy from research to closing).

**Why now:** Three converging forces: (1) The creator economy hit $250B but partnership infrastructure stayed at spreadsheet-level. (2) AI can now evaluate creator profiles at scale — which means niche indexing is finally possible without a 50-person research team. (3) Cross-platform data aggregation became feasible through API ecosystems (YouTube Data API, Apify, Serper) that didn't exist or weren't accessible 3 years ago.

**Impoverished identity:** "I'm not a tools person — I'm a relationships person. I find creators through hustle, not software." This identity makes them resist systematizing the one part of their workflow that should be systematized, because they've conflated manual effort with authenticity.

**Dominant belief:** "The only way to find good creator partners is to do the research yourself — tools and databases are for influencer marketing, not real partnerships."

## Differentiation

**What we do differently:**
1. **Niche-indexed, not follower-indexed.** 95 specific niches (not "Lifestyle" — "Section 8 Housing"). Organized by what creators sell, not how famous they are.
2. **Cross-platform unification.** YouTube + Instagram + TikTok + Twitter in one profile. No tab-switching.
3. **AI-scored for quality.** Claude evaluates each creator for professional presence, niche relevance, and audience engagement. Score ≥7 = verified.
4. **Built for outreach teams, not campaign managers.** Kanban pipeline, assignees, comments, CSV export — CRM features for partnership operators, not influencer campaign dashboards.
5. **Free tier that proves value.** 10 saved creators, no credit card. See if your niche is covered before paying.

**ONLY test:** "Scouter is the ONLY creator discovery platform that indexes creators by what they sell — across 95 specific money-making niches and 4 platforms — for growth operators running partnership outreach."

**Mechanism:** The Niche Index — a taxonomy of 95 specific money-making verticals (from "Amazon FBA" to "Pressure Washing") applied to every creator in the database. Instead of searching by keywords and hoping, you browse by the exact vertical you need and see every creator who teaches, sells, or promotes in that space — with their YouTube, Instagram, TikTok, and Twitter profiles unified in one view. AI scoring (Claude with vision) evaluates each creator for quality, filtering out brand accounts, inactive channels, and pure entertainment.

**Positioning fallback:** If the niche coverage claim doesn't land (user's niche is thin or missing), the fallback positioning is cross-platform unification: "See every creator's YouTube, Instagram, TikTok, and Twitter in one profile — stop copy-pasting across tabs." This angle holds regardless of niche depth.
