# Objections

*How to identify, categorize, and address what stops people from buying.*

Sources: Russell Brunson (Expert Secrets), Alex Hormozi ($100M Offers), Dixon & Adamson (The Challenger Sale), Kevin Simler & Robin Hanson (The Elephant in the Brain)

---

## Why This Matters for Briefs

The Objections section directly feeds sales pages, FAQs, and email sequences. Weak objection handling leaves money on the table. But most briefs either miss objections or address them with deflections instead of real answers.

---

## The Three False Beliefs

Every prospect has three types of beliefs blocking them. Miss one and they won't buy — even if they believe the other two.

| False Belief | What they think | How to break it |
|--------------|-----------------|-----------------|
| **Vehicle** | "This type of solution won't work" | Proof that this vehicle works (results, mechanism) |
| **Internal** | "I can't do it / I'm not capable" | Story of someone like them who succeeded |
| **External** | "Something outside me prevents it" | Address the specific blocker (time, money, approval) |

### Applying to the Brief

**Objections section:** Categorize each objection by type, then match the response:

| Objection | Type | Response approach |
|-----------|------|-------------------|
| "Creator databases don't have my niche" | Vehicle | Proof: "83+ sub-niches including..." |
| "I'm not systematic enough for a tool" | Internal | Story: "Growth operators who thought the same..." |
| "I don't have time to learn something new" | External | Remove blocker: "Time to first result: 5 minutes" |

### Common Objections by Type

**Vehicle objections:**
- "I've tried tools like this before"
- "This type of solution doesn't work for my situation"
- "The data probably isn't accurate"

**Internal objections:**
- "I'm not technical enough"
- "I'm not organized enough to use this"
- "I'll probably forget to use it"

**External objections:**
- "I don't have budget"
- "I don't have time"
- "My team won't adopt it"
- "I need approval from someone else"

## Problem → Solution Mapping

List every problem between your prospect and success. Then solve each one.

Process:
1. What problems do they face achieving the outcome?
2. What problems arise from using your solution?
3. What problems do they *think* they'll face (even if they won't)?

### Applying to the Brief

For each problem, decide: Is this solved in the core offer, addressed as a bonus, or handled in messaging?

| Problem | Type | How we address it |
|---------|------|-------------------|
| "Don't know which creators are good" | Real | Core offer: Verified profiles with metrics |
| "Team will step on each other's outreach" | Real | Core offer: Team features, assignees |
| "Will take forever to learn" | Perceived | Messaging: "5 minutes to first result" |
| "Data might be wrong" | Perceived | Guarantee: "Flag any profile, fixed in 24hrs" |

## Response Framework

Every objection response should: **Acknowledge → Reframe → Prove**

1. **Acknowledge:** Show you understand the concern (don't dismiss)
2. **Reframe:** Shift how they see the problem
3. **Prove:** Back it up with specifics

### Examples

**Objection:** "I already have a spreadsheet system"

❌ Deflection: "Our tool is better than spreadsheets."

✅ Full response:
- **Acknowledge:** "Spreadsheets work — to a point."
- **Reframe:** "But they can't show cross-platform presence, go stale instantly, and break when you try to collaborate."
- **Prove:** "Keep your spreadsheet for deal tracking. Use Scouter for discovery — that's where the 10 hours go."

**Objection:** "Enterprise tools have more features"

❌ Deflection: "We have great features too!"

✅ Full response:
- **Acknowledge:** "They do."
- **Reframe:** "They also cost 10-50x more and are built for brand campaigns, not partnerships."
- **Prove:** "You don't need content approval workflows and influencer payments. You need to find people fast. That's what we built."

## Reframe-Based Handling

Sometimes the right response to an objection isn't answering it — it's changing the question.

The Challenger approach (Dixon/Adamson): instead of addressing the objection within the prospect's existing frame, reframe how they see the problem. This makes the objection dissolve rather than requiring a direct answer.

### When to reframe vs. respond directly

| Situation | Approach |
|-----------|----------|
| Specific, factual concern ("Does it integrate with Shopify?") | Respond directly |
| Frame-based objection ("I don't think tools like this work") | Reframe |
| Price objection masking value confusion | Reframe the cost of inaction |
| "I already have a system" | Reframe what the system is actually costing them |

### Reframe examples

| Objection | Direct response (OK) | Reframe response (better) |
|-----------|---------------------|--------------------------|
| "Too expensive" | "Here's our ROI calculator" | "The cost of not solving this is $340K/year. At our price, payback is 11 weeks." |
| "We already have a tool" | "We have more features" | "Your current tool has you spending 23% of team time on status updates. That's the tool's real cost." |
| "I need to think about it" | "Take your time" | "Every month without this costs 400 hours. What specifically do you need to think through?" |

### Applying to the Brief

**Objections section:** For each objection, decide: does it need a direct answer or a reframe? If the objection comes from a flawed mental model, answering within that model reinforces it. Reframe first, then answer.

## Stakeholder-Specific Objections

In multi-persona sales, different stakeholders have fundamentally different objections. A single objections list doesn't serve them all.

| Stakeholder | What they object to | How to address it |
|---|---|---|
| **Economic Buyer** (CFO/CEO) | ROI unclear, risk too high | Quantify cost of inaction, show payback period |
| **End User** | Adding complexity, learning curve | Show before/after of their daily workflow |
| **Technical Buyer** (IT) | Integration risk, maintenance burden | Architecture details, migration path |
| **Champion** (internal advocate) | Career risk of recommending wrong thing | Give them ammunition — the deck to send their boss |
| **Blocker** (skeptic) | Change disruption, being proven wrong | Acknowledge concerns directly, show evidence from similar orgs |

### Applying to the Brief

If the Personas section identifies multiple stakeholders, the Objections section should have stakeholder-specific rows:

| Objection | Stakeholder | Response |
|-----------|-------------|----------|
| "How do I justify this to the board?" | Champion | [Internal ROI case they can forward] |
| "Will this integrate with our stack?" | Technical | [Specific integration details] |

## Status-Based Objections

Some objections aren't genuine concerns — they're status performances.

When a prospect says "I'm too sophisticated to be easily sold," solving with logic misses the point. They're performing expertise, not requesting information.

### Signs of a status objection

- Displays of expertise ("I've been in this space for 20 years")
- Performative skepticism ("I've seen a hundred tools like this")
- Discount requests as negotiation theater
- Contrarian posturing ("Everyone says X but I think Y")

### How to handle

**Validate the status first, then present the case.** Don't dismiss their performance or try to out-expert them.

- ❌ "Don't worry, everyone hesitates at first!" (diminishes their status)
- ✅ "You're right to be skeptical. Most of what's out there doesn't work. Here's why this is different." (validates expertise, then redirects)

### Applying to the Brief

**Objections section:** When an objection sounds like "I'm too smart for this," the response needs to honor the buyer's intelligence before presenting evidence. Acknowledge → Validate expertise → Then reframe with new insight.

## Objection Patterns by Product Type

Use these as starting points, then validate with real customer research.

### SaaS
- "I already have a tool for this"
- "Too expensive for what it does"
- "Will my team actually use it?"
- "How is this different from [competitor]?"
- "What about data security/privacy?"

### Courses / Info Products
- "I can learn this free on YouTube"
- "I don't have time to go through a course"
- "Will this work for my specific situation?"
- "How do I know you're qualified to teach this?"
- "What if I don't finish it?"

### Coaching / Services
- "Can I just hire someone cheaper?"
- "How do I know you're good?"
- "What results have you gotten for others?"
- "What if it doesn't work for me?"
- "Is this worth the investment?"

## Questions to Answer

When writing the Objections section:

1. **Coverage:** Have I addressed vehicle, internal, AND external?
2. **Source:** Are these objections from real customers or invented?
3. **Response quality:** Acknowledge → Reframe → Prove, or just deflection?
4. **Specificity:** Are responses concrete or vague?
5. **Honesty:** Am I willing to say "this isn't for you" when true?
6. **Reframe vs. respond:** For each objection — does it need a direct answer or a reframe?
7. **Stakeholder coverage:** If multiple personas, are objections tailored per stakeholder?
8. **Status awareness:** Are any objections actually status performances? If so, validate before addressing.

## Common Mistakes

- **Only addressing vehicle objections.** Missing internal and external beliefs.
- **Deflecting instead of responding.** "We're working on that" isn't an answer.
- **Logic against emotional objections.** Internal beliefs need story, not argument.
- **Generic responses.** "We're the best" doesn't address specific concerns.
- **Missing the real objection.** They say "too expensive" but mean "I don't see the value."
- **No "this isn't for you" responses.** Sometimes the honest answer is they shouldn't buy.
- **Answering within a flawed frame.** When the objection comes from a wrong mental model, answering reinforces it. Reframe first.
- **One-size-fits-all objections.** Different stakeholders have different concerns. A single list serves none well.
- **Solving status objections with logic.** When the objection is a performance, validate expertise before presenting evidence.
