# Positioning

*How to frame your product so it stands apart.*

Sources: April Dunford (Obviously Awesome), Russell Brunson (Expert Secrets), Eugene Schwartz (Breakthrough Advertising), Al Ries & Jack Trout (Positioning), Dixon & Adamson (The Challenger Sale), Geoffrey Moore (Crossing the Chasm)

---

## Why This Matters for Briefs

The Differentiation section is where most briefs fail. They list features instead of framing the product as something fundamentally different. Good positioning makes objections irrelevant and price secondary.

---

## The Five Components of Positioning

Positioning has five interlocking components. Change one, and the others must adjust.

| Component | Question it answers | Brief section |
|-----------|---------------------|---------------|
| **Competitive Alternatives** | What would customers do if you didn't exist? | Alternatives |
| **Unique Attributes** | What do you have that alternatives lack? | Differentiation |
| **Value** | What benefit do those attributes enable? | Differentiation |
| **Target Customers** | Who cares most about that value? | Audience |
| **Market Category** | What context makes your value obvious? | Product (Category) |

### The order matters

You can't know your differentiation until you know alternatives. You can't know value until you know attributes. You can't pick a category until you know what makes you different and who cares.

Most positioning fails because it starts with category ("We're an AI platform") instead of alternatives ("They'd use spreadsheets").

### What Claude gets wrong

- Picks a category before understanding alternatives
- Lists features without connecting to what alternatives lack
- Defines audience too broadly ("professionals who want efficiency")
- Treats positioning as a tagline exercise

## Competitive Alternatives (Start Here)

Your real competitors aren't always who you think. Customers compare you to whatever they'd do without you.

### Three types of alternatives

1. **Direct competitors:** Same solution type (other creator databases)
2. **Indirect alternatives:** Different approach, same outcome (hiring VAs, buying lists)
3. **Status quo:** Doing nothing, manual workarounds (YouTube search + spreadsheets)

### What Claude gets wrong

- Lists only direct competitors
- Treats "do nothing" as trivial (it's often the main competitor)
- Misses indirect alternatives from adjacent categories

### What good execution looks like

The Alternatives section should start from customer behavior:

> "Our alternative isn't Grin or CreatorIQ—most of our customers were using YouTube search + spreadsheets. The status quo is the competitor."

## Positioning by Analogy

"X for Y" or "Like X but Y" positions by borrowing context from something known.

### When to use

Analogy works when:
- The reference point is well-known to your specific audience
- The comparison is accurate in the dimension that matters
- Plain language would take too long to explain

### Applying to briefs

The Category field can use analogy:

> "Creator discovery tool—closer to Apollo for creators than Grin for brands"

This positions by borrowing:
- From Apollo: the "database + outreach" frame
- Against Grin: "not enterprise influencer marketing"

### What Claude gets wrong

- Uses tired analogies ("Uber for X")
- Picks reference points the audience doesn't know
- Forces analogy when plain language would be clearer

## The Mental Ladder

For every product category, prospects carry a mental ladder. Each rung holds a brand. Most ladders have room for two or three brands. Your strategy depends on which rung you occupy.

| Your rung | Strategy | Copy tone |
|-----------|----------|-----------|
| **Rung 1 (leader)** | Set the criteria. Reinforce dominance. | "More people choose us than any alternative." |
| **Rung 2 (challenger)** | Relate against the leader. | "If you've outgrown [Leader], we're the next step." |
| **Rung 3+ (niche)** | Own a sub-category or get a new ladder. | "Built exclusively for [narrow segment]." |
| **Not on the ladder** | Earn awareness first. | Don't claim leadership — prove relevance with a narrow, specific claim. |

### Applying to the Brief

**Differentiation section:** A rung-2 brand writing rung-1 copy ("We're the best") sounds delusional. A rung-1 brand writing rung-2 copy surrenders the advantage. Determine your rung before writing any claims.

**Alternatives section:** Map which brands occupy each rung for your audience. This reveals who you're actually competing against in the mind.

### What Claude gets wrong

- Writes rung-1 copy for unknown brands (superlatives for brands nobody recognizes)
- Ignores the ladder entirely — writes in a competitive vacuum
- Doesn't distinguish between rung strategy and aspiration

## New Opportunity vs. Improvement

People don't want to improve what they're already doing. They want a new vehicle — a new way to get the result that bypasses their past failures.

| Improvement Offer | New Opportunity |
|-------------------|-----------------|
| "Do the same thing, but better" | "Here's a completely different path" |
| Reminds them of past failures | Gives them a fresh start |
| Requires admitting they were wrong | Lets them blame the old vehicle |
| Incremental gains | Transformation |

### Applying to the Brief

**Differentiation section:** Frame as new opportunity, not improvement.

- ❌ "Better prospecting tools" (improvement)
- ✅ "This isn't a better spreadsheet. It's a replacement for the spreadsheet." (new opportunity)

**Problem section:** Name what they've tried and why it failed. This gives them permission to abandon the old way.

**Alternatives section:** Position alternatives as "the old way." Your product is the new vehicle.

## The Big Domino

One core belief that, if accepted, makes all other objections fall.

Pattern:
> "If they believe that [new opportunity] is the key to [what they desire] and is only attainable through [your specific vehicle], then all other objections become irrelevant."

### Applying to the Brief

Before writing the Differentiation section, identify your Big Domino:

**Example for Scouter:**
> "If they believe that a pre-built creator database saves 10 hours/week AND that database is only valuable if it's niche-focused (which we uniquely are), then price objections, feature objections, and 'I already have a system' objections all fall."

The Differentiation section should focus on proving this one belief. Everything else is secondary.

## Repositioning the Competition

When you can't be first on the ladder and can't climb it, change the ladder. Repositioning means redefining the evaluation criteria so the leader's strengths become irrelevant — or even weaknesses.

Expose the gap between what the leader claims and what the prospect actually experiences. Then redefine what "better" means.

| Competitor's frame | Your reframe |
|-------------------|--------------|
| "We have the most features" | "Features you'll never use. We have the three that matter." |
| "Enterprise-grade security" | "Enterprise-grade complexity, too. We ship fast." |
| "All-in-one platform" | "All-in-one means all-in-mediocre. We do one thing best." |

### Applying to the Brief

**Differentiation section:** Instead of claiming to score higher on the competitor's criteria, change the criteria. "We're faster" loses to the leader. "Speed is the wrong metric — accuracy is what matters" wins.

**Problem section:** Name what they've tried and why the OLD evaluation criteria led them astray. This gives them permission to adopt your frame.

### What Claude gets wrong

- Attacks competitors directly instead of reframing the criteria
- Copies the competitor's framing, just claims higher scores
- Gets defensive instead of changing the conversation

## Positioning by Sophistication

In mature markets (Stage 3+), direct claims don't differentiate. You need mechanism.

| Sophistication | Positioning approach |
|----------------|----------------------|
| Stage 1-2 | Lead with the outcome: "Find creators fast" |
| Stage 3 | Lead with mechanism: "The unified profile system..." |
| Stage 4 | Lead with unique mechanism: "The only niche-first database..." |
| Stage 5 | Lead with proof/identity: "Join 500+ growth operators..." |

### Applying to the Brief

**Alternatives section:** Identify what claims competitors make. This tells you the stage.

**Differentiation section:** Match your positioning to the stage. Don't use Stage 1 tactics in a Stage 4 market.

## Category Design

Sometimes the best positioning is creating a new category where you're the default.

Instead of competing in "influencer marketing platforms," define a new category: "creator discovery tools for growth operators."

### Applying to the Brief

**Product section (Category field):** Consider whether you should:
1. Compete in an existing category
2. Subcategorize (narrow the existing category)
3. Create a new category

New categories work when:
- Existing categories have negative associations
- Your differentiator is so fundamental it deserves its own space
- You want to avoid direct comparison with established players

## The Reframe

The most powerful positioning move: "You think your problem is X, but it's actually Y." This disrupts the prospect's mental model and creates a gap only your solution fills.

### The Commercial Teaching choreography (Dixon/Adamson)

The reframe works best as a structured teaching sequence:

1. **Warmer** — Start in their world. A challenge they recognize.
2. **Reframe** — Introduce an insight that challenges their thinking.
3. **Rational Drowning** — Quantify the cost of the current approach.
4. **Emotional Impact** — Connect the numbers to a human consequence.
5. **New Way** — Present a new framework for the problem.
6. **Your Solution** — Only now reveal how your product delivers.

### Applying to the Brief

**Problem section:** The "struggling moment" should contain the seed of the reframe — the gap between what they believe and what's actually true.

**Differentiation section:** The mechanism should deliver on the reframe. If the reframe says "the problem isn't X, it's Y," the mechanism solves Y.

### What Claude gets wrong

- Leads with benefits instead of disruption
- Softens the reframe ("You might want to consider...")
- Reframes without evidence (sounds arrogant, not insightful)
- Introduces the product before the reframe lands

## Positioning for Pragmatists

Pragmatists buy from market leaders. In the beachhead phase, you're not the broad market leader — you're the leader of your segment. Position accordingly.

### Moore's positioning formula

| Element | What it answers |
|---------|----------------|
| **For** (target) | Who specifically? |
| **Who** (need) | What pressing problem? |
| **Our product is** (category) | What frame of reference? |
| **That** (benefit) | Primary reason to buy? |
| **Unlike** (alternative) | What would they do instead? |
| **We** (differentiation) | Why us for this specific segment? |

### Applying to the Brief

**Product (Category):** Position as leader of a narrow category, not challenger in a broad one. "The #1 workflow tool for mid-market agencies" beats "a better alternative to Monday.com."

**Alternatives section:** Name the status quo as the primary competitor. Pragmatists compare you to what they currently do, not to other startups.

### What Claude gets wrong

- Positions against other startups when the real competition is the status quo
- Uses broad category positioning when narrow ownership would be stronger
- Writes challenger copy when pragmatists respond to leadership claims within a segment

## Questions to Answer

When writing the Differentiation section:

1. **New opportunity:** Am I framing as transformation or improvement?
2. **Big Domino:** What's the one belief that makes everything else fall?
3. **Stage:** What sophistication stage is this market? Am I matching my approach?
4. **Category:** Am I competing, subcategorizing, or creating?
5. **Ladder:** What rung do I occupy? Is my copy tone matched to that rung?
6. **Reframe:** Am I changing the evaluation criteria or just arguing within the leader's frame?
7. **Pragmatist check:** Am I positioning as beachhead leader or broad-market challenger?

## Common Mistakes

- **Listing features instead of framing.** Features don't position; frames do.
- **Improvement language.** "Better," "faster," "more efficient" — all improvement words.
- **No mechanism in Stage 3+ markets.** "Saves time" without explaining HOW.
- **Fighting on competitor's terms.** Instead of reframing, accepting their category.
- **Multiple Big Dominos.** Trying to prove too many things; dilutes focus.
- **Wrong rung copy.** Using superlatives and leadership claims for unknown brands.
- **No reframe.** Arguments within the existing frame always favor the incumbent.
- **Visionary copy for pragmatists.** "Revolutionary" and "transformative" repel pragmatist buyers who want proof and peer references.
