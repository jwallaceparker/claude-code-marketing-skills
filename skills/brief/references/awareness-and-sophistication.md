# Awareness and Sophistication

*How to assess where your audience is before writing anything.*

Sources: Eugene Schwartz (Breakthrough Advertising)

---

## Why This Matters for Briefs

The Problem, Differentiation, and Voice sections of a brief all depend on knowing where your audience starts. Write to the wrong awareness level and you'll either bore them (too basic) or lose them (too advanced).

---

## The Five Awareness Levels

Your prospect exists at one of five levels. This determines your opening, your assumptions, and how much work your copy must do.

| Level | What they know | Brief implication |
|-------|----------------|-------------------|
| **Most Aware** | Know you, trust you, ready to buy | Brief focuses on offer, not education. Problem section is short. |
| **Product-Aware** | Know your product, not convinced | Brief focuses on differentiation. Why you vs. alternatives? |
| **Solution-Aware** | Know solutions exist, don't know you | Brief connects your product to the solution they're seeking. |
| **Problem-Aware** | Feel the pain, don't know solutions exist | Brief must crystallize the problem first. Heavy Problem section. |
| **Unaware** | Don't know they have a problem | Brief must start with identity or story. Can't lead with product. |

### Applying to the Brief

**Audience section:** Note which awareness level your primary audience occupies. Different segments may be at different levels.

**Problem section:** For Problem-Aware audiences, this section does heavy lifting — make the pain vivid. For Product-Aware audiences, keep it brief; they already know.

**Differentiation section:** Matters most for Product-Aware and Solution-Aware. If they don't know solutions exist, differentiation is premature.

**Voice section:** Unaware audiences need warmer, story-driven voice. Most Aware audiences can handle direct, offer-focused language.

## Market Sophistication

How many times has your prospect heard similar claims? Sophistication increases as a market matures.

| Stage | Market state | What works |
|-------|--------------|------------|
| **1** | You're first | Direct claim: "Find creators fast" |
| **2** | Competitors appeared | Enlarge claim: "Find 100 creators in 10 minutes" |
| **3** | Claims feel stale | Add mechanism: "The unified profile system that..." |
| **4** | Mechanisms copied | Unique mechanism: "The only niche-first database with..." |
| **5** | Nothing believed | Proof and identity: "Join 500+ growth operators who..." |

### Applying to the Brief

**Alternatives section:** Research what claims competitors make. This tells you the sophistication stage.

**Differentiation section:** In Stage 1-2 markets, lead with the claim. In Stage 3-4, lead with mechanism. In Stage 5, lead with proof.

**Proof section:** More critical as sophistication increases. Stage 5 markets need heavy proof; Stage 1 markets can get by with less.

---

## Questions to Answer in the Brief

Before drafting, determine:

1. **Awareness:** Are they Problem-Aware, Solution-Aware, or Product-Aware?
2. **Sophistication:** What stage is this market? What have they already heard?
3. **Implication:** Does this mean leading with problem, solution, mechanism, or proof?

If you're unsure, assume Problem-Aware and Stage 3. You'll rarely go wrong crystallizing the problem and explaining your mechanism.

---

## Common Mistakes

- **Writing Product-Aware copy to Problem-Aware audiences.** Jumps to features before establishing pain.
- **Using Stage 1 tactics in Stage 4 markets.** "Save time" when everyone says "save time."
- **Assuming sophistication is fixed.** Different segments may be at different stages.
- **Over-educating Most Aware audiences.** They just need the offer.
