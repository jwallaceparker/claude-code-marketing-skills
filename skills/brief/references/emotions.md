# Emotions and Desire

*How to connect product benefits to primal human desires.*

Sources: Drew Eric Whitman (Cashvertising), Jonathan Haidt (The Righteous Mind), Kevin Simler & Robin Hanson (The Elephant in the Brain)

---

## Why This Matters for Briefs

The Problem section's "Emotional Weight" is where most briefs fall flat. They describe frustration in abstract terms ("inefficient," "time-consuming") instead of connecting to the desires that drive all human behavior.

People buy on emotion and justify with logic. If the brief doesn't capture the emotional reality, the copy won't resonate.

---

## The Life Force 8

Eight biological desires hardwired into every human. These aren't learned—they're survival instincts.

| # | Desire | What it means |
|---|--------|---------------|
| 1 | **Survival** | Living, enjoying life, extending life |
| 2 | **Food and drink** | Enjoyment of food and beverages |
| 3 | **Freedom from fear, pain, danger** | Safety, security, protection |
| 4 | **Sexual companionship** | Attraction, relationships, intimacy |
| 5 | **Comfortable living conditions** | Comfort, convenience, ease |
| 6 | **Being superior** | Winning, being better, status |
| 7 | **Care for loved ones** | Protecting family, providing |
| 8 | **Social approval** | Being liked, accepted, respected |

### For B2B briefs

B2B products connect to LF8 too—buyers are still humans:

| LF8 Desire | B2B Connection |
|------------|----------------|
| Freedom from pain (#3) | Escape the grind, stop the chaos |
| Comfortable living (#5) | Work-life balance, less stress |
| Being superior (#6) | Be better at your job, outperform competitors |
| Social approval (#8) | Look good to boss, be the one who found the solution |

## The Connection Chain

Every benefit connects to an LF8 desire through a chain:

> **Feature** → **Functional Benefit** → **Emotional Benefit** → **LF8 Desire**

### Example for Scouter

| Feature | Functional | Emotional | LF8 |
|---------|------------|-----------|-----|
| Cross-platform profiles | See all platforms at once | Stop the tab chaos | Freedom from pain (#3) |
| 10,000+ creators | Find faster | Close laptop earlier | Comfortable living (#5) |
| AI qualification | Pitch only qualified people | Close more deals | Being superior (#6) |
| Built-in pipeline | No spreadsheet juggling | Feel organized, in control | Freedom from pain (#3) |

### Applying to briefs

The Emotional Weight field should trace at least one benefit chain to its LF8 root:

**Surface level (weak):**
> "Frustration with manual prospecting."

**LF8 connected (strong):**
> "You're trapped in researcher mode while competitors close deals. The work that should be strategic has become grunt work. You close your laptop at 9pm, again."
> (Connects to: Freedom from pain, Being superior, Comfortable living)

## The Camera Test

Specific scenes land. Abstract emotions don't.

### What it is

Ask: "Could a camera capture this moment?"

| Abstract (camera can't see) | Specific (camera could film) |
|------------------------------|-------------------------------|
| "Feeling frustrated" | "Staring at 47 open tabs at 10pm" |
| "Overwhelmed" | "Copy-pasting the same creator into your third spreadsheet this week" |
| "Relieved" | "Closing your laptop before dinner for the first time in months" |
| "Stressed" | "Refreshing your inbox at midnight hoping for a reply" |

### Applying to briefs

Every "Emotional weight" field should contain at least one scene a camera could capture.

### What Claude gets wrong

- Uses abstract emotion words ("frustrated," "overwhelmed," "anxious")
- Describes feelings instead of moments
- Stays general when specifics would land harder

## Fear: The Strongest Motivator

Fear of loss is stronger than desire for gain.

### The fear formula

1. **Arouse fear** — Make the threat vivid and personal
2. **Specific recommendation** — What exactly should they do?
3. **Perceived efficacy** — Show that it works
4. **Self-efficacy** — Show that THEY can do it

### Applying to briefs

The Problem section should evoke fear of the status quo—not just inconvenience:

**Low fear (weak):**
> "Manual prospecting takes time."

**Fear-connected (strong):**
> "Every hour you spend scrolling YouTube is an hour your competitor spends closing deals. You're not just wasting time—you're losing ground."

### What Claude gets wrong

- Avoids fear entirely (too polite, too positive)
- Uses fear without offering solution (just scares people)
- Makes fear vague instead of specific and personal

## Before/After Contrast

The emotional journey from pain to pleasure.

### Structure

| Before (Pain) | After (Relief) |
|---------------|----------------|
| Vivid current reality | Vivid desired reality |
| LF8 desire blocked | LF8 desire fulfilled |
| Scene of frustration | Scene of resolution |

### Example

**Before:** "Staring at a spreadsheet at 11pm while your partner goes to bed alone—again."

**After:** "Finding 100 qualified creators in your first afternoon. Closing your laptop at 5pm with a full pipeline."

### Applying to briefs

- Problem section = Before (make the pain vivid)
- Proof/Value themes = After (show the contrast)
- Both should use camera-test-passing specifics

## Status and Superiority (LF8 #6)

People want to be better than others—or at least not worse.

### When it matters

B2B products often connect to status:
- Being better at your job
- Having insider knowledge others lack
- Using tools the best operators use
- Not being left behind by competitors

### Applying to Problem section

The status angle:

> "Your competitors are closing deals while you're still building lists. You're not just slow—you're behind."

### Applying to Proof section

Social proof invokes LF8 #8 (social approval):

> "Join 500+ growth operators who've made the switch."

## The Elephant and the Rider

The mind has two systems: an automatic, emotional process (the elephant) and a rational process (the rider). The elephant decides. The rider rationalizes.

When someone reads your sales page and feels "this isn't for me," that's the elephant rejecting you before the rider reads a single bullet point. The rider is a press secretary, not a president — constructing post-hoc justifications for decisions the elephant already made.

### Applying to briefs

The brief must arm BOTH:

| Layer | Speaks to | Brief sections | Function |
|-------|-----------|----------------|----------|
| **Elephant** | Identity, fear, desire, belonging | Problem (emotional weight, hidden motive), Audience (JTBD emotional dimension), Voice | Triggers the "I want this" feeling |
| **Rider** | Logic, ROI, features, proof | Proof, Differentiation (mechanism), Pricing | Provides the "here's why" justification |

**Problem section (Emotional weight):** This IS elephant territory. If it reads like a logical argument ("manual prospecting is time-consuming"), the elephant never engages. It needs to FEEL like something ("Your competitors are closing deals while you're still building lists").

**Proof section:** This is rider territory. Metrics, testimonials, and specifics exist so the buyer can tell their spouse, boss, or themselves why they bought.

### What Claude gets wrong

- Writes logically airtight, emotionally inert briefs — the rider nods, the elephant doesn't move
- Leads with features and comparisons (rider material) before activating the elephant
- Treats emotion as decoration — adding it after the argument. Emotion is the gift, not the wrapping paper.

## Moral Foundations as Emotional Entry Points

Beyond LF8, people have six moral "taste buds" that create powerful emotional responses. Most marketing only activates two of them.

| Foundation | Triggered by | Copy angle |
|---|---|---|
| **Care / Harm** | Suffering, nurturing, vulnerability | "Protect your team from..." |
| **Fairness / Cheating** | Justice, equal treatment, exploitation | "Stop getting ripped off by..." |
| **Loyalty / Betrayal** | Group solidarity, belonging | "Join 10,000 founders who..." |
| **Authority / Subversion** | Respect, expertise, order | "The industry standard for..." |
| **Sanctity / Degradation** | Purity, cleanliness, elevation | "Clean, simple, no bloat" |
| **Liberty / Oppression** | Freedom, autonomy, resistance | "Take back control of..." |

### Applying to the Brief

**Problem (Emotional weight):** Which moral foundation does the problem violate? A data breach violates Sanctity. A competitor outpacing you violates Authority. Getting overcharged violates Fairness. The foundation tells you which emotional nerve to hit.

**Voice section:** The brand's moral tone should match its audience. LinkedIn audiences weight Authority and Fairness. Tech libertarians weight Liberty. Wellness audiences weight Sanctity.

### What Claude gets wrong

- Defaults to Care and Fairness for everything (the WEIRD bias)
- Ignores Loyalty, Authority, Sanctity, and Liberty — which drive half the population
- Mixes too many foundations in a single asset. One or two per piece. Six feels like nothing.

## Hidden Motives and the Press Secretary

People systematically deceive themselves about why they buy. The stated motive is the press release. The hidden motive is the real acquisition strategy.

| Product | Stated motive | Hidden motive | Copy that serves both |
|---------|---------------|---------------|----------------------|
| Business coaching | "I need help growing" | Validate I'm serious, access higher-status network | "This isn't for everyone. It's for founders who've outgrown free advice." |
| Organic food | "I care about what goes in my body" | Signal moral/health superiority | "You already know this matters. Now your kitchen matches what you believe." |
| Online course | "I want to learn new skills" | Signal competence, feel ahead of peers | "The people getting promoted this year learned what you're about to." |

### The two-layer structure

Effective marketing serves both the hidden motive (which drives the decision) and the respectable narrative (which the buyer tells themselves and others):

1. **Trigger the president** (unconscious) — status, fear, identity, belonging
2. **Arm the press secretary** (conscious) — ROI, features, proof, risk reduction

Sequence matters: president first, then press secretary. Logic without emotion = the rider nods, nobody buys. Emotion without logic = they want it but can't justify it.

### Consumption as signaling

Every purchase broadcasts: identity, status, taste, values, intelligence. Your brand becomes part of the customer's signal. What does buying your product let them SAY about themselves?

- "I use [cool tool]" (identity signal)
- "I invested in [premium thing]" (status signal)
- "I found [hidden gem]" (intelligence signal)

When the signal is clear and attractive, the product sells itself through the customer's desire to broadcast.

### Applying to the Brief

**Problem (Hidden motive):** Beyond stated frustration, what status/identity/fear driver is at play? "I want to save time" → "I want to feel competent and ahead of my peers."

**Proof section:** Social proof works because it's a tribal signal. "Used by the growth team at Stripe" is more powerful than "10,000 users" because it's a more specific identity signal.

**Differentiation:** What does the user BECOME? Not a product capability — an identity upgrade.

### What Claude gets wrong

- Takes stated motivations at face value
- Writes only the "respectable story" layer — misses the status/identity/fear driver
- Treats emotional selling as "add enthusiasm" instead of naming specific fears, status gaps, or peer comparisons
- Produces "one-layer" messaging that serves the rider but ignores the president

## Questions to Answer

When writing emotional sections of a brief:

1. **LF8 connection:** Which desire(s) does this connect to?
2. **Camera test:** Can I picture the emotional moment as a scene?
3. **Before/after:** Is the contrast between pain and relief vivid?
4. **Fear:** Am I acknowledging what they're afraid of losing?
5. **Specificity:** Are there sensory details or just abstract words?
6. **Status:** How does solving this make them feel about themselves?
7. **Elephant first?** Does the brief hit emotion/identity before logic?
8. **Moral foundation:** Which foundation does the problem violate? Am I activating it?
9. **Hidden motive:** Beyond the stated reason, what's the status/identity/fear driver?
10. **Two-layer check:** Does the brief arm both the president (elephant) AND the press secretary (rider)?

## Common Mistakes

- **Staying functional.** "Saves time" without the emotional payoff.
- **Abstract emotions.** "Frustrated" instead of the scene that causes frustration.
- **Ignoring fear.** Only talking about gains, never losses.
- **B2B = no emotion.** False. B2B buyers are humans with LF8 desires.
- **Too polite.** Avoiding the uncomfortable truth of the problem.
- **Too many desires.** Hitting all 8 instead of the 1-2 that matter most.
- **Desire without proof.** Promising emotional payoff you can't deliver.
- **Rider-only briefs.** Logically airtight but emotionally dead. The elephant never moves.
- **Care/Fairness default.** Only hitting two moral foundations when the audience responds to Authority, Loyalty, Sanctity, or Liberty.
- **Ignoring hidden motives.** Writing to the stated motive ("save time") when the real driver is status, identity, or fear.
- **One-layer messaging.** Serving either the elephant or the rider, but not both.
