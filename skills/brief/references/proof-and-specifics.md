# Proof and Specifics

*How to make claims believable through specificity and reason-why.*

Sources: Claude Hopkins (Scientific Advertising)

---

## Why This Matters for Briefs

The Proof section is where claims become credible. Without it, everything else in the brief is just assertion. Specifics convert; generalities don't.

---

## Specifics Over Generalities

Specific claims are believable. General claims are not.

"Best quality" means nothing — everyone says it. "Aged 18 months in oak barrels" is specific and therefore credible.

Hopkins: "Platitudes and generalities roll off the human understanding like water from a duck."

### Applying to the Brief

Every claim in the brief should be made specific:

| Section | General (weak) | Specific (strong) |
|---------|----------------|-------------------|
| Product | "Large database" | "10,247 verified creators" |
| Problem | "Wastes time" | "10+ hours/week on prospecting" |
| Differentiation | "Many niches" | "83 sub-niches including Real Estate > Section 8" |
| Proof | "Fast results" | "First saved creator in under 5 minutes" |
| Pricing | "Affordable" | "$29/month vs. $500/month for enterprise tools" |

### The Specificity Test

For each claim in the brief, ask:
- Is there a number? If not, can I add one?
- Is there a comparison? (vs. what?)
- Is there a named example? (which niche, which feature, which customer?)
- Is there a timeframe? (how fast?)

## Reason-Why Advertising

Don't just make claims — explain WHY the claim is true.

"Best coffee" is a claim. "Best coffee because we source single-origin beans from one farm in Colombia and roast in small batches every morning" is reason-why.

### Applying to the Brief

Every major claim needs a reason-why in the same section or in Proof:

**Claim only (weak):**
> "Scouter has the best creator database."

**With reason-why (strong):**
> "Scouter has 10,000+ verified creators because we built custom scrapers for each platform, then manually verified every profile for niche, monetization, and contact info. Enterprise platforms scrape and dump. We curate."

**Claim only (weak):**
> "Saves you 10 hours a week."

**With reason-why (strong):**
> "Saves 10 hours a week because you're not rebuilding the same data from scratch. Every creator already has YouTube + Instagram + TikTok unified. No tab-switching, no spreadsheet copy-pasting."

## Proof Types

Different claims need different proof types:

| Claim type | Best proof |
|------------|------------|
| Speed/time savings | Specific numbers, before/after |
| Quality | Methodology, process, credentials |
| Results | Customer outcomes, case studies |
| Credibility | Logos, testimonials, media mentions |
| Superiority | Direct comparison, mechanism explanation |

### Proof Section Structure

Organize proof by value theme, not by proof type:

| Theme | Proof |
|-------|-------|
| Time savings | "Find 100 creators in 10 minutes vs. 10 hours manual" |
| Coverage | "10,247 creators across 83 niches, 4 platforms" |
| Quality | "Every profile manually verified within 24 hours" |
| Accessibility | "$29/month vs. $500+ for enterprise alternatives" |

## Pre-Launch Proof

No customers yet? You can still build proof:

1. **Product proof:** Database size, feature coverage, methodology
2. **Founder proof:** Relevant experience, adjacent results
3. **Design proof:** Screenshots, demos, tangible product
4. **Early access proof:** Waitlist size, beta feedback

Mark what's validated vs. hypothesized. Structure future testimonials before you have them:

> **Target testimonial structure:**
> "[Before state]. [Action with product]. [Specific outcome]. [Timeframe]."
>
> Example to capture: "I was spending 15 hours a week on prospecting. Found Scouter, built a list of 200 qualified creators in my first afternoon. Closed 3 deals that month."

## Testimonial Quality

Good testimonials are specific. Bad testimonials are generic.

| Weak testimonial | Strong testimonial |
|------------------|-------------------|
| "Great product!" | "Found 50 qualified creators in my first 30 minutes." |
| "Saved me so much time" | "Cut my weekly prospecting from 12 hours to 1." |
| "Really helped my business" | "Closed 3 partnerships in my first month. Paid for a year of the tool." |
| "Easy to use" | "Had my first list exported in under 10 minutes. No tutorial needed." |

### What makes testimonials credible:
- Specific numbers (time, money, quantity)
- Before/after contrast
- Named outcomes, not feelings
- Real person attribution (name, company, role)
- Unexpected details (things you wouldn't have invented)

## Salesmanship Test

Hopkins: Advertising is salesmanship in print. If your proof wouldn't convince someone in a sales conversation, it won't work in copy.

Read the Proof section aloud as if you're pitching a skeptical prospect:
- Do the numbers sound real or inflated?
- Would a reasonable person believe this?
- Have you answered "why should I believe you?"
- Is there anything that sounds like marketing BS?

---

## Questions to Answer

When writing the Proof section:

1. **Specificity:** Is every claim backed by a number, example, or comparison?
2. **Reason-why:** Have I explained HOW/WHY each claim is true?
3. **Source:** Where did this proof come from? Can I cite it?
4. **Credibility:** Would a skeptic believe this?
5. **Themes:** Am I proving the value themes, not just listing facts?

---

## Common Mistakes

- **Vague superlatives.** "Best," "amazing," "revolutionary" — no one believes these.
- **Rounded numbers.** "Thousands of users" vs. "10,247 users" — specifics are credible.
- **Claims without reason-why.** Says what but not how.
- **Proof that doesn't match claims.** Proving the wrong thing.
- **Generic testimonials.** "Great product!" proves nothing.
- **Hiding proof.** Buries specifics instead of featuring them.
- **Inflated claims.** "$10,000 value" for a $50 product damages credibility.
