# Customer Research

*How to find the language, pain, and desire that makes copy resonate.*

Sources: Gary Halbert (The Boron Letters), Bob Moesta (Demand-Side Sales 101), Michele Hansen (Deploy Empathy), Rob Fitzpatrick (The Mom Test)

---

## Why This Matters for Briefs

The Language section is only as good as the research behind it. Invented customer quotes sound like marketing. Verbatim customer language sounds like truth.

You cannot write a compelling brief about people you don't understand.

---

## The Starving Crowd

Before researching language, confirm you have a hungry audience.

Halbert's test: Given a choice between a better product or a starving crowd, always choose the crowd. The market matters more than the copy.

### Applying to the Brief

**Audience section:** Don't just describe demographics. Describe the hunger:

- ❌ "Marketers who want to find creators"
- ✅ "Growth operators spending 10+ hours/week manually prospecting — who already know they're wasting time"

The hunger: They feel the pain NOW. They're actively looking. They know they're being inefficient.

If you can't articulate the hunger, the audience might not be starving.

---

## Research Before Writing

Halbert's method:

1. **Become the customer** — Use competing products. Feel the pain firsthand.
2. **Talk to real customers** — Not hypotheticals. Real conversations.
3. **Read what they read** — Forums, subreddits, communities, newsletters in their niche.
4. **Find exact words** — Verbatim phrases, not paraphrased.
5. **Understand what failed** — What they've tried before and why it didn't work.

The goal: Know them better than they know themselves.

## Interview Methodology

Halbert says talk to real customers. Hansen and Fitzpatrick teach you HOW.

### The Mom Test (Fitzpatrick): Three rules

1. **Talk about their life, not your idea.** Don't pitch. Don't describe what you're building. The moment you describe the product, you contaminate the data.
2. **Ask about specifics in the past, not hypotheticals about the future.** "Would you use an app that..." → useless. "Tell me about the last time you..." → gold.
3. **Talk less, listen more.** If you're talking more than 30%, you're pitching, not researching.

### Good vs. bad questions

| Bad question (Mom Test fail) | Why it's bad | Good question (Mom Test pass) |
|---|---|---|
| "Would you use a tool that does X?" | Hypothetical. Everyone says yes. | "Walk me through the last time you needed to do X." |
| "Is finding creators a pain point?" | Leading. You're supplying the answer. | "How did you find the last 5 creators you reached out to?" |
| "Would you pay $79/year for this?" | Hypothetical pricing. | "What are you currently paying? What made you decide to pay?" |
| "Do you like our approach?" | Fishing for compliments. | "What have you tried before? What made you stop?" |

### The interview mindset (Hansen)

You are a documentary filmmaker, not a prosecutor. Your job is to understand their world so completely you could describe their day back to them.

- **Silence is a tool.** The first answer is the safe answer. Wait. The answer after the pause is the real one.
- **Their words are sacred.** Write down exactly what they say, not your interpretation. "I feel like I'm drowning" and "I have too much to do" are not the same sentence.
- **Follow the energy.** When their voice changes, when they lean forward, when they pause — those moments reveal what actually matters.

### What Claude gets wrong

- Generates survey questions ("On a scale of 1-10...") instead of conversation prompts
- Accepts surface answers without digging into specifics
- Writes rigid 15-question guides instead of 3-4 open prompts that follow energy
- Asks about the product instead of the problem

## The Three Dimensions

Every customer decision operates on three layers. Most research only captures the first.

| Layer | What it is | How to surface it |
|---|---|---|
| **Functional** | What they're trying to accomplish | Ask about the task |
| **Emotional** | How they want to feel or stop feeling | "How did that make you feel?" / "What was the worst part?" |
| **Social** | How they want to be perceived | "What did your team/boss think?" / "If someone saw your setup, what would they think?" |

The emotional and social layers are where buying decisions actually happen, but customers rarely volunteer them unprompted.

| What they say | Functional | Emotional (hidden) | Social (hidden) |
|---|---|---|---|
| "I need a better CRM" | Track contacts | Stop the panic of not knowing where deals stand | Be seen as a professional operator |
| "I want to learn copywriting" | Write better ads | Stop feeling like a fraud | Be seen as someone who knows what they're doing |

### Applying to the Brief

**Problem (Emotional weight):** If it only describes functional frustration, dig for emotional and social layers.

**JTBD:** Each job should note all three dimensions and flag which one dominates.

**Language:** Listen for emotional and social language separately. "I just wanted to know if it was working" is functional. "I felt like a fraud every time someone asked" is emotional. Both go in the Language section, but serve different copy purposes.

## The Decision Timeline

Every purchase has a history. Map it to find your best hook material.

| Stage | What to ask | What it reveals |
|---|---|---|
| **First thought** | "When did you first realize you needed something?" | Triggering circumstance → ad hooks |
| **Passive looking** | "What did you notice before actively searching?" | Channels that register before intent |
| **Active looking** | "What made you go from thinking to searching?" | Tipping-point event → strongest hook |
| **Deciding** | "What almost stopped you from buying?" | Objections and anxieties → landing page copy |
| **Consuming** | "What happened in the first week?" | Onboarding friction → activation emails |
| **Evaluating** | "Knowing what you know now, same choice?" | Satisfaction → testimonial framing |

### The critical transition

The shift from passive to active looking is your best marketing material. Something specific happened that converted ambient awareness into urgent search. That's the moment emotion peaks.

| Generic hook (misses the trigger) | Trigger-based hook (from the timeline) |
|---|---|
| "Need a better CRM?" | "That moment you realized you lost a deal because it fell through the cracks" |
| "Tired of manual prospecting?" | "You just spent 4 hours on LinkedIn and have 3 names to show for it" |

## The Behavior Hierarchy

Not all customer signals are equal. Weight them by reliability.

| Signal | Reliability |
|---|---|
| They already paid for something similar | Highest |
| They actively tried to solve it (built a spreadsheet, hired someone) | High |
| They spent significant time on it | High |
| They describe a specific recent incident | Medium-high |
| They describe an ongoing frustration | Medium |
| They express interest in your solution | Low |
| They say they would use or pay for it | Lowest |

### Applying to the Brief

**Problem section:** Calibrate severity by the behavior signal. "They say it's a problem" is different from "they built a 47-tab spreadsheet to work around it." The second is proof of pain.

**Alternatives section:** What they ACTUALLY use (from behavior) matters more than what competitors exist (from research).

**Language section:** If you can't point to a specific transcript or review where a real person said something, tag it `[Hypothesis]`.

## Where to Find Verbatim Language

| Source | What to look for |
|--------|------------------|
| Reddit/forums | Complaint threads, "how do you..." questions, rants |
| Twitter/X | Threads about the problem, quote tweets of competitors |
| Reviews | 3-star reviews especially — specific frustrations |
| Support tickets | Recurring confusion, feature requests, complaints |
| Sales calls | Objections, questions, how they describe their situation |
| Customer interviews | Open-ended "tell me about..." questions |
| Community Slack/Discord | Casual conversation about the problem |

### What to Extract

- **Problem language:** How do they describe the pain in their own words?
- **Outcome language:** How do they describe what they want?
- **Objection language:** What concerns do they voice before buying?
- **Alternative language:** How do they describe what they've tried?

## The Mirror Test

After drafting the Language section, apply the mirror test:

> Would the customer recognize themselves in this?

If the language sounds like marketing, they won't see themselves. If it sounds like something they'd say to a friend, they will.

### Red Flags

- Polished phrasing (real people don't speak in complete, eloquent sentences)
- Marketing buzzwords (customers don't say "streamline" or "leverage")
- Abstract pain ("inefficiency" vs. "I'm drowning in tabs")
- Generic outcomes ("save time" vs. "close my laptop at 5pm")

### Green Flags

- Specific details (numbers, timeframes, named tools)
- Emotional language (frustration words, relief words)
- Incomplete sentences or fragments (how people actually talk)
- Unexpected phrasing (things you wouldn't have invented)

## Applying to Brief Sections

**Language section:**
- "How they describe the problem" — Direct quotes, verbatim
- "How they describe us" — Capture after early users, mark as hypothesis until then
- "Words to use / avoid" — Based on what you hear vs. what you invent

**Problem section:**
- "Core frustration" — Describe in their language, not yours
- "Emotional weight" — Use the specific feelings they express

**Audience section:**
- The description should sound like something a customer would nod at
- Anti-persona language should come from real "this isn't for me" moments

## Don't Translate

The biggest mistake isn't failing to find customer language — it's finding it and then cleaning it up. Customer says "I was drowning in spreadsheets." Claude writes "streamline your data management workflow." The original was better.

Use customer words raw. Edit for length, never for vocabulary.

| What they said | What Claude "translates" it to | What to put in the brief |
|----------------|-------------------------------|--------------------------|
| "I was copying and pasting between 6 tabs" | "Streamline your workflow" | "I was copying and pasting between 6 tabs" |
| "I just wanted to know if it was working" | "Gain actionable insights" | "I just wanted to know if it was working" |
| "I hated opening that app every morning" | "Users experience friction with existing solutions" | "I hated opening that app every morning" |
| "My team actually uses this one" | "Empower your team with seamless adoption" | "My team actually uses this one" |

Real customer language is messy, specific, and imperfect. That's what makes it credible. If a phrase in the Language section sounds like it came from a brochure, it probably got translated somewhere between the customer's mouth and the brief.

## Analyzing Interview Data

Raw notes are not insights. Move from "we talked to 12 people" to "here's what we know."

### The process

1. **Harvest exact language.** Pull emotional statements, metaphors, workarounds, contrast statements. Tag each by emotional weight (1-5) and which brief section it maps to.
2. **Cluster by jobs, not demographics.** "Small business owners said X" is wrong. "People trying to solve Problem A said X" is right — regardless of company size.
3. **Find emotional peaks.** Moments where energy changed — animation, frustration, relief, pride. These moments should appear in marketing because they matter to the customer.
4. **Map workarounds.** When customers build solutions (spreadsheets, manual processes, duct-tape workflows), that's proof the problem is real. The more elaborate the workaround, the more severe the pain.
5. **Identify patterns.** When 3+ people independently describe the same frustration or use similar phrases, that's marketing gold.

### What Claude gets wrong

- Clusters by demographics instead of by jobs
- Paraphrases during analysis (destroying the raw data)
- Skips workaround analysis (the strongest signal of problem severity)
- Generates research summaries instead of mapping each insight to a specific brief section

## Pre-Launch Research

No customers yet? You can still research:

1. **Competitors' reviews** — What do people complain about?
2. **Adjacent communities** — Where do your future customers hang out now?
3. **Founder knowledge** — If you've done the job yourself, you know the pain
4. **Direct outreach** — Cold DM 10 people in your target audience and ask questions

Mark pre-launch language as `[Hypothesis]` and note validation triggers for when to update with real data.

---

## Questions to Answer

Before finalizing the Language section:

1. **Source:** Where did each quote come from? (If you can't answer, it's invented)
2. **Mirror test:** Would they recognize themselves?
3. **Specificity:** Are there concrete details or just abstractions?
4. **Validation:** What will you do to confirm this after launch?
5. **Three dimensions:** Have you captured functional, emotional, AND social?
6. **Timeline mapped:** Do you know the trigger that moved them from passive to active?
7. **Behavior evidence:** Are problem claims backed by behavior (paid, built, spent time), or just stated interest?
8. **Patterns:** Have 3+ people independently described the same frustration or workaround?

## Common Mistakes

- **Inventing customer language.** Sounds plausible but isn't real.
- **Translating customer language.** Finding real words and then polishing them into marketing-speak. Hate is a better word than friction.
- **Paraphrasing instead of quoting.** Loses the texture and specificity.
- **Researching the product instead of the customer.** Knows features, not desires.
- **Stopping at demographics.** Knows age/role, not how they feel at 10pm.
- **One source only.** Triangulate across multiple sources.
- **Asking hypotheticals.** "Would you use..." gets polite agreement. "Tell me about the last time..." gets truth.
- **Only probing functional needs.** Missing the emotional and social layers where buying decisions happen.
- **Skipping the timeline.** Not asking what triggered the shift from passive to active looking.
- **Treating all signals equally.** "They said they'd use it" is not the same as "they built a spreadsheet to work around it."
