# Headlines and One-Liners

*How to write product one-liners that do the work of headlines.*

Sources: John Caples (Tested Advertising Methods)

---

## Why This Matters for Briefs

The Product section's one-liner is the hardest line in the brief. It must select the right audience, promise a benefit, and differentiate—in under 10 words. This is headline craft applied to positioning.

---

## The One-Liner's Job

A one-liner must do three things:

1. **Select:** Signal who this is for (and who it's not for)
2. **Promise:** State a benefit worth caring about
3. **Differentiate:** Say something competitors can't claim

If it fails any of these, it fails.

### What Claude gets wrong by default

- Writes descriptions, not headlines ("Creator discovery and outreach management")
- Uses corporate language ("Streamline your creator partnerships")
- Focuses on category instead of benefit ("AI-powered creator platform")
- Tries to sound impressive instead of useful

---

## The 4 U's Test

Every one-liner should pass this test:

| U | Question | Example pass |
|---|----------|--------------|
| **Useful** | Does it promise a benefit? | "Find creators in your niche" ✓ |
| **Urgent** | Is there a reason to act? | "in 10 minutes, not 10 hours" ✓ |
| **Unique** | Is this specific, not generic? | "90+ sub-niches" (not just "many niches") ✓ |
| **Ultra-specific** | Are there numbers or details? | "100 creators in 10 minutes" ✓ |

### Applying to brief one-liners

❌ "Streamline your creator outreach" — Useful (barely), not urgent, not unique, not specific

✅ "Find 100 creators in 10 minutes" — All four U's pass

## Five Headline Types for One-Liners

### 1. Benefit Headlines (Default)

Promise the outcome directly. When in doubt, use this.

> "Find creators who sell things, fast."

### 2. How-To Headlines

Works when the benefit is a capability they gain.

> "How to find qualified creators without scrolling for hours"

Note: Often too long for a one-liner. Use the structure mentally, then compress.

### 3. Direct Command Headlines

Tell them what to do. Works when audience is ready to act.

> "Stop scrolling. Start closing."

### 4. News Headlines

Announce something new. Only works with genuine news.

> "Now: Find creators across 4 platforms in one search"

### 5. Comparison Headlines

Show the contrast. Numbers help.

> "10 minutes vs. 10 hours"

---

## The Compression Technique

Most one-liners start too long. Here's how to compress:

### Process

1. Write the full benefit statement (however long)
2. Remove filler words ("that," "which," "in order to")
3. Replace phrases with single words
4. Cut adjectives that aren't doing work
5. Check if meaning survives

### Example

**Full:** "A platform that helps growth operators discover and qualify creator partners across multiple platforms without spending hours on manual prospecting" (21 words)

**Compressed:** "Find qualified creators across 4 platforms—in minutes" (7 words)

### What to keep vs. cut

| Keep | Cut |
|------|-----|
| Numbers ("100 creators," "10 minutes") | Vague modifiers ("many," "various") |
| Specific benefits ("find," "qualify," "close") | Abstract benefits ("streamline," "optimize") |
| Differentiators ("4 platforms," "90+ niches") | Generic claims ("comprehensive," "powerful") |

## The "You" Focus

Write about the reader, not the product.

### What it means

The one-liner should describe their outcome, not your features.

| Product-focused (weak) | Reader-focused (strong) |
|------------------------|------------------------|
| "AI-powered creator database" | "Find creators in your niche" |
| "Multi-platform creator aggregation" | "See YouTube, Instagram, TikTok in one view" |
| "Comprehensive outreach management" | "Track deals from first contact to close" |

### The rewrite test

If your one-liner starts with your product name or "We," rewrite it.

---

## Specificity Beats Vagueness

Specific claims are more believable AND more compelling.

| Vague | Specific |
|-------|----------|
| "Find creators fast" | "Find 100 creators in 10 minutes" |
| "Large database" | "10,000+ verified creators" |
| "Many categories" | "90+ sub-niches" |
| "Save time" | "Cut prospecting from 10 hours to 1" |

### The number rule

If you can add a number, add a number. Numbers make claims concrete and testable.

## One-Liner Structures That Work

Templates to start from:

| Structure | Example |
|-----------|---------|
| [Verb] + [what] + [qualifier] | "Find creators in your niche, fast" |
| [Number] + [outcome] + [timeframe] | "100 creators in 10 minutes" |
| [Action] + [alternative pain] | "Stop scrolling. Start closing." |
| [Benefit] + vs + [old way] | "10 minutes vs. 10 hours" |
| [What you do] + [for who] | "Creator discovery for growth operators" |

### The positioning-by-analogy option

Sometimes a reference point does the work:

> "Apollo for creator partnerships"

Only use if the reference is known to your audience and accurate.

---

## Common One-Liner Failures

| Failure | Example | Fix |
|---------|---------|-----|
| **Corporate speak** | "Comprehensive creator management platform" | "Find and close creator deals in one place" |
| **All features** | "Search, filter, pipeline, export, team features" | Pick the one benefit that matters most |
| **No benefit** | "AI-powered creator intelligence" | Say what the AI does FOR THEM |
| **Too long** | "The all-in-one platform for growth operators who want to find, qualify, and manage creator partnerships efficiently" | "Find creators. Close deals. One tool." |
| **Too clever** | "Scroll less, deal more" | Be clear before clever |

---

## Before Finalizing

Test the one-liner against these questions:

1. **Benefit?** Does it promise something they want?
2. **Specific?** Is there a number, timeframe, or concrete detail?
3. **Differentiated?** Could a competitor claim this exact line?
4. **Short?** Under 10 words?
5. **Clear?** Would a stranger understand it?

If any answer is "no," rewrite.

---

## The Brief Connection

The one-liner should be derivative of (not separate from) the brief's other sections:

- **Differentiators** → What makes the one-liner unique
- **Problem** → What pain the one-liner promises to solve
- **Audience** → Who the one-liner selects for
- **Proof** → What makes the one-liner's claim credible

A one-liner that doesn't connect to these sections is probably wrong.
