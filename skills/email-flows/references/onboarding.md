# Onboarding Email Flow

*The complete email arc from purchase confirmation to retention point — the moment they stop "trying it out" and start saying "this is how I do things."*

Sources: Coleman (Never Lose a Customer Again), Skrob (Retention Point), Cialdini (Influence), Hormozi ($100M Money Models)

---

## Onboarding Intake

Before writing, gather these details from the user. Use `AskUserQuestion` or conversational follow-up — don't dump all questions at once.

**Required:**
- What's the product? (course, membership, SaaS, coaching, service)
- What's the first quick win? (what can they achieve in under 10 minutes?)
- What's the Retention Point (Skrob)? (the specific behavior or moment that predicts they'll stay long-term)
- Key activation milestones? (what must happen in week 1? month 1?)
- Common reasons people churn or request refunds?

**If available:**
- Is there a community or group component?
- What does the ideal 90-day journey look like?
- Behavioral triggers available? (login data, feature usage, email engagement)
- Whether different product tiers should get different sequences

If the user doesn't have all of this, draft with placeholders marked `[PLACEHOLDER: ...]` and note what's missing.

## Coleman's Eight Phases — Mapped to Email

Every customer moves through 8 emotional phases after purchase. Most businesses design for one (Activate) and ignore the other seven. The onboarding flow must cover all of them.

| Phase | Timing | Emotional job | Email type |
|-------|--------|---------------|------------|
| **Assess** | Pre-purchase | Handled by other flows | — |
| **Admit** | Purchase moment | Validate the decision | Confirmation + celebration |
| **Affirm** | 0-48h | Prevent buyer's remorse | Social proof + "you made the right call" |
| **Activate** | Day 1-3 | First quick win | One action, one result |
| **Acclimate** | Week 1 | Orient without overwhelming | Progressive disclosure |
| **Accomplish** | Week 2-4 | First real result | Milestone celebration |
| **Adopt** | Month 2-3 | Habit formation, identity shift | "You're the kind of person who..." |
| **Advocate** | After Accomplish | Turn customers into promoters | Ask for referral/review — only AFTER results |

### Admit — The Confirmation Email

**Feel:** Celebration, not logistics. They just made a decision — make them glad.

**Include:** One clear next step (not a feature tour), social proof ("you just joined X people"), excitement lead. **Leave out:** Feature lists, full account setup instructions, everything they'll need eventually but not right now.

### Post-Purchase Upsell — BamFam (Hormozi)

**Every purchase confirmation sets up the next interaction.** The confirmation email is the highest-engagement email you'll ever send — they just bought, they're excited, they're reading every word. Use it. After confirming the purchase and celebrating the decision, present the next offer using the appropriate upsell type:

- **Classic Upsell:** "People who got [what you bought] also get [complementary thing]." Works when the upsell genuinely enhances what they just purchased. The upsell should feel like a natural extension, not a bolt-on.
- **Menu Upsell:** "You chose [tier]. Here's what [next tier] includes." Present options at different price points. Works for tiered products where upgrading is a clear step up.
- **Anchor Upsell:** Lead with the premium option at full price, then present the mid-tier as the recommendation. The anchor makes the real offer feel reasonable.
- **Rollover Upsell:** Offer a trial of the next product built into the current purchase. "Your first month of [advanced tier] is included — see if it's right for you." Reduces friction to zero because they experience it before deciding.

The upsell email is NOT a separate campaign — it's part of the confirmation flow. It arrives in the same email or within 30 minutes of purchase. Timing matters because buying momentum decays fast. An upsell presented 3 days later is a new sales conversation. An upsell presented immediately rides the existing decision energy.

**The BamFam principle:** every email in the onboarding sequence should set up the next interaction — not just the next email, but the next action, the next milestone, the next offer. The confirmation email sets up the upsell. The upsell confirmation sets up onboarding. The onboarding sets up the first win. The first win sets up expansion. No email should be a dead end.

### Affirm — The Buyer's Remorse Window (0-48h)

**Feel:** Warm reassurance. Like a friend saying "you're going to love this" — not a company saying "thank you for your purchase."

**Include:** Stories of people like them who succeeded, specific outcomes, identity-matched proof. **Leave out:** Feature education, upsells, anything that feels like more selling.

| Before (skipping Affirm) | After (Affirm-aware) |
|---------------------------|----------------------|
| Day 0: "Here's your login." Day 3: "Have you tried Feature X?" | Day 0: "You're in — here's your first step." Day 1: "Here's what Sarah achieved in her first week." Day 2: "How's it going? Reply if you're stuck." |

The first 48 hours determine whether buyer's remorse solidifies or dissolves. Silence is the worst response.

### Activate — The Quick Win (Day 1-3)

**Feel:** Energizing, specific, achievable. Like a coach handing them the ball and saying "make this one shot."

**Include:** One specific action that produces one visible result in under 10 minutes. **Leave out:** Options, alternatives, "you could also try..." — those create decision paralysis.

| Before (feature tour) | After (quick win) |
|------------------------|-------------------|
| "Welcome! Here's a tour of our 12 features." | "Do this one thing right now — it takes 5 minutes and you'll see your first result." |

### Acclimate — Progressive Disclosure (Week 1)

**Feel:** Steady, encouraging. Like a guide revealing the trail one turn at a time.

**Include:** ONE new concept or feature per email, tied to what they've already done. **Leave out:** Feature dumps, "here's everything you have access to," advanced capabilities.

### Accomplish — The First Real Result (Week 2-4)

**Feel:** Proud recognition. Like a coach saying "look what you did."

**Include:** Celebration of their specific milestone, what's now possible, the next level. **Leave out:** Generic congratulations, immediate upsells.

| Before (skipping Accomplish) | After (Accomplish-aware) |
|------------------------------|--------------------------|
| Week 3: "Here are some tips for power users." | Week 3: "You just hit [milestone]. Here's what that means — and what power users do next." |

### Adopt — Identity Shift (Month 2-3)

**Feel:** Belonging. They're not "using a product" — they're "the kind of person who does X."

**Include:** Identity language, insider framing, cumulative value summaries. **Leave out:** Beginner language, basic tips they've already outgrown.

### Advocate — After Results Only

**Feel:** Collaborative, not transactional. "Share your result" not "refer a friend for $10."

The referral ask happens AFTER the Accomplish phase. Never before. Asking someone to recommend something they haven't gotten results from destroys trust. Incentives amplify advocacy — they don't create it.

## Skrob's 90-Day Window

Retention is won or lost in the first 90 days. After 90 days, churn rates stabilize. The onboarding flow isn't a one-week welcome sequence — it's a 90-day program with three distinct phases.

| Phase | Timing | Job | Risk if missed |
|-------|--------|-----|----------------|
| **Activation** | Days 1-7 | Quick win, orient to the product, reduce overwhelm | "This doesn't work" |
| **Integration** | Days 8-30 | Build the habit, show progress, celebrate milestones | "I keep forgetting to use it" |
| **Habit** | Days 31-90 | Reinforce identity, expand usage, introduce advanced features | "I stopped and never came back" |

Each phase needs different email tone, content, and cadence. Day 45 emails should not sound like day 3 emails.

**Activation (Days 1-7):** Every email points to the quick win. No feature education beyond what's needed for that one result. Tone is energetic and directive — "do this now."

**Integration (Days 8-30):** Emails shift from "here's your next step" to "here's how this fits your life." Show the product inside real workflows. "Here's how Sarah's team uses this every Monday morning." Celebrate milestones to reinforce momentum.

**Habit (Days 31-90):** Reinforce identity. Surface value the member might not see on their own. "You've saved 14 hours this month." Introduce advanced features only after core features are habitual. This is where the Retention Point lives — engineer every email toward it.

## The New Member Assurance Sequence (First 48 Hours)

The highest-risk window. Buyer's remorse peaks here. These four messages are non-negotiable.

| # | Email | Timing | Job | Length |
|---|-------|--------|-----|--------|
| 1 | "You're in" | Immediate | Confirm + ONE clear next step. Not a feature dump. | Short (50-70 words) |
| 2 | Quick win prompt | 1-2h later | "Do this one thing right now." Achievable in under 10 minutes. | Short (40-60 words) |
| 3 | Social proof | 24h | "Here's what others achieved in their first week." Real results. | Medium (80-120 words) |
| 4 | Check-in | 48h | "How's it going?" Genuine question, not a disguised pitch. | Short (40-60 words) |

The first message must arrive instantly. Not 30 minutes later. The gap between purchase and first contact is where doubt lives.

The 24-hour social proof should feature someone like them — same role, same stage, same doubts. Identity proximity beats generic testimonials (Cialdini: social proof is strongest from similar others).

The 48-hour check-in must feel human. Not "Dear valued member." Not a survey. A real question: "Did you get your first [result] yet? If you're stuck, reply and I'll help."

## Engagement Triggers vs. Traps

Every re-engagement email either advances progress or creates guilt. Know the difference.

| Trigger (do this) | Trap (never this) |
|--------------------|-------------------|
| "12 new templates added this week" (value-forward) | "We miss you! Log back in" (guilt-forward) |
| "You're 2 steps from completing X" (progress-forward) | "Your account is inactive" (shame-forward) |
| "Sarah just hit her first 1,000 subscribers using X" (proof-forward) | "Don't let your subscription go to waste" (loss-forward) |
| "New feature: here's how it helps with [their goal]" (benefit-forward) | "You haven't used feature Y yet" (neglect-forward) |

The rule: triggers move toward the Retention Point. Traps create guilt without advancing progress. If an email makes the reader feel bad about themselves instead of excited about what's next, it's a trap.

## Churn Prediction & Intervention

Members don't churn the day they cancel. They churn days or weeks earlier. Intervene during the prediction window — not after the cancel button is clicked.

| Signal | Intervention |
|--------|-------------|
| No login in 7 days | Check-in email — helpful, not guilt. "Here's a quick way to get [result] this week." |
| Completed setup but never used core feature | Targeted tutorial email — "Here's how [person like them] uses [feature] to get [result]." |
| Active for 2 weeks then dropped off | "What happened?" + quick win reminder — re-orient to the easiest path to value. |
| Approaching renewal without engagement | Value summary email — show what they'd lose, not what they haven't used. |

Lead with value in every intervention. Guilt triggers churn faster than silence does.

The intervention ladder should escalate in personal touch as signal strength increases. Automated value emails for minor dips. Personal check-ins for strong signals. Save conversations for cancel intent.

## The Overwhelm Antidote — Progressive Disclosure

The instinct is to show everything. Every feature shown before the member needs it is noise that buries the signal.

- **Week 1:** Only the core feature. One thing at a time.
- **Week 2:** Introduce feature 2 — but only after they've used feature 1.
- **Week 3-4:** Advanced features, personalized based on usage.
- **Never:** "Here are all 47 features you now have access to!"

Each email introduces ONE concept or ONE action. Not "here are 5 things you can do this week." If the email has more than one CTA, it has too many.

## Full Email Map

The complete onboarding sequence. Adapt timing and count to the product.

| Day | Email | Phase | Job | Length |
|-----|-------|-------|-----|--------|
| 0 (immediate) | Welcome | Admit | Validate + one next step | Short (60-80 words) |
| 0 (+1-2h) | Quick win | Activate | One action, one result | Short (50-70 words) |
| 1 | Social proof | Affirm | Show others' early wins | Medium (100-130 words) |
| 2 | Check-in | Affirm | Genuine "how's it going?" | Short (40-60 words) |
| 4 | Feature spotlight | Acclimate | Introduce ONE next feature | Medium (80-120 words) |
| 7 | Week 1 recap | Acclimate | Celebrate progress, preview week 2 | Medium (100-140 words) |
| 10 | Deeper tutorial | Acclimate | Show an advanced use case | Medium (100-150 words) |
| 14 | Milestone | Accomplish | Celebrate first real result | Short (60-80 words) |
| 21 | Case study | Accomplish | Show what's possible at 30 days | Medium (120-160 words) |
| 30 | Month 1 recap | Adopt | Value summary + identity framing | Medium (100-140 words) |
| 45 | Advanced feature | Adopt | Expand usage | Medium (80-120 words) |
| 60 | Progress report | Adopt | Show cumulative value | Medium (100-140 words) |
| 75 | Pre-renewal prep | Adopt | Value summary (if subscription) | Medium (100-140 words) |
| 90 | Advocate ask | Advocate | Referral or review request | Short (60-80 words) |

**Adapt to product type:** SaaS might need more frequent early emails. Courses might front-load week 1. Memberships need ongoing community touchpoints. Coaching needs fewer, warmer emails.

## Product-Type Adaptations

| Product type | Emphasis | Key difference |
|-------------|----------|----------------|
| **Course** | Front-load activation, celebrate module completions | Linear path — email tracks progress through modules |
| **Membership** | Community integration, ongoing value signals | No "finish" — identity and belonging matter more than completion |
| **SaaS** | Feature adoption, usage milestones | Behavioral triggers > time-based sends |
| **Coaching/Service** | Relationship warmth, expectations | Most personal tone, fewest emails |

## Before/After Examples

### 1. Confirmation Email

| Before (logistics-only) | After (celebration + quick win) |
|--------------------------|--------------------------------|
| "Your order has been confirmed. Order #4821. Here's your login: [link]. Your username is [email]. If you have questions, contact support." | "You're in. 2,400 operators made this same decision — here's what happened in their first 30 days. But first, one thing: log in and [specific quick win action]. It takes 5 minutes and you'll see why this works." |

The "before" treats the purchase like a shipping notification. The "after" validates the decision, creates belonging, and drives the quick win — all in under 80 words.

### 2. Re-engagement Email

| Before (guilt trap) | After (value trigger) |
|----------------------|----------------------|
| "We miss you! It's been 14 days since you logged in. Don't let your subscription go to waste. Log in now to make the most of your membership." | "3 new templates dropped this week that are perfect for [their use case]. The fastest one takes 10 minutes and members are reporting [specific result]. Here's the one to start with: [link]" |

The "before" makes them feel guilty about not using something. The "after" gives them a reason to come back by showing value they'd actually want. Notice the trigger names a specific result other members are getting — Cialdini's social proof working as a re-engagement mechanism rather than a guilt lever.

## Anti-Patterns

- **Logistics-only onboarding.** "Here's your login. Here are all your features. Good luck." No emotional design, no quick win, no progressive disclosure.
- **Asking for reviews before results.** Advocacy before accomplishment destroys trust. The referral ask comes AFTER they've achieved something worth sharing.
- **"We miss you" guilt emails.** These are engagement traps, not triggers. They make people feel bad without advancing progress.
- **Overwhelming with everything at once.** Feature dumps in the welcome email kill activation. One thing at a time.
- **Same emails regardless of engagement.** No behavioral segmentation means the active user and the disengaged user get identical messages. The active user gets bored; the disengaged user feels irrelevant.
- **Feature-first language.** "You now have access to X" vs. "Here's how to get [result] in 10 minutes." Lead with the outcome, not the feature.
- **Skipping the Affirm phase.** Most buyer's remorse happens in the first 48h. Silence during this window is the worst possible response.
- **Treating onboarding as a one-week event.** It's a 90-day journey with three distinct phases. The welcome sequence is phase 1 of 3.
- **Automated "your account is inactive" emails.** These make people feel bad instead of helped. Replace with value triggers that give them a reason to return.
