# Product Launch Email Flow

*The complete email arc from pre-launch anticipation through cart close.*

Sources: Walker (Launch), Brunson (Expert Secrets), Cialdini (Influence, Pre-Suasion), Fladlien (One to Many), Hormozi ($100M Money Models)

---

## Launch Intake

Before writing, gather these details from the user. Use `AskUserQuestion` or conversational follow-up — don't dump all questions at once.

**Required:**
- What's the offer? (name, price, what's included)
- Launch timeline? (pre-launch start date through cart close date)
- Pre-launch content vehicle? (webinar, video series, challenge, PDF sequence)
- List size and warmth? (cold, warm, buyers)
- Bonuses or fast-action incentives?

**If available:**
- Payment plans available?
- Previous launch data? (if relaunching — conversion rate, EPC, refund rate)
- Known objections specific to this offer (check BRIEF.md Objections section)
- Whether engaged (watched PLCs) and cold (didn't watch) should get different sequences
- JV/affiliate partners involved?

If the user doesn't have all of this, draft with placeholders marked `[PLACEHOLDER: ...]` and note what's missing.

## The Sideways Sales Letter

A traditional sales letter stacks everything — problem, agitation, proof, offer, close — on one page. A launch takes that same architecture and turns it on its side: delivering the pitch across multiple content pieces over 7-14 days.

| Traditional sales letter | Sideways sales letter (launch) |
|--------------------------|-------------------------------|
| One long page, read in one sitting | 3-4 content pieces delivered over 7-14 days |
| Prospect must commit hours upfront | Prospect consumes in small bites |
| Persuasion is concentrated | Persuasion compounds — each piece builds on the last |
| No community or social proof in real-time | Audience reacts together, creating live social proof |
| Easy to abandon halfway through | Each piece re-hooks and re-engages |

The key insight: each email and content piece stands alone as genuinely valuable AND advances the sale. The gaps between sends are not dead time — they are where anticipation builds. The time dimension is itself a persuasion tool. A prospect thinking about your offer between content drops is something a sales page never achieves.

## The Six Phases

Every product launch follows six phases. Each has a distinct job. Skip a phase and the launch underperforms.

### Phase 1: Pre-Pre-Launch (PPL)

**Timing:** 7-14 days before PLC 1
**Job:** Seed anticipation. Survey the audience for objections and desires. Activate the list.
**Goal:** Build curiosity and identify the dominant desire (Collier).

Emails: 2-3 teasers.
- "Something's coming" — pure curiosity, no details, identity signal
- "Quick question for you" — survey email that surfaces objections and desires
- "The response was incredible" — social proof of interest, builds community

The PPL creates the audience expectation that makes PLC 1 open rates high. Skipping it means PLC 1 lands cold.

### Phase 2: PLC 1 — The Opportunity

**Job:** Show the gap between where they are and where they could be. Massive value, no pitch.

This is reciprocity (Cialdini) in action — give genuine value before asking for anything. The email's job is to drive them to the content piece (video, PDF, live event). Teach WHAT and WHY, not HOW. The HOW is what they buy.

PLC 1 answers: "Here's what's possible and why most people miss it."

### Phase 3: PLC 2 — The Transformation

**Job:** Demonstrate credibility. Break false beliefs (Brunson's three beliefs: vehicle, internal, external). Show case studies and proof.

The email references PLC 1 value and builds on it — this is one continuous arc, not three independent articles. Authority and social proof are the primary triggers here.

PLC 2 answers: "Here's the mechanism and proof it works."

### Phase 4: PLC 3 — The Ownership Experience

**Job:** Let them see themselves succeeding. This is the most proof-heavy phase.

Testimonials, case studies, "imagine when..." framing. By PLC 3, they should be thinking "I need this." The pre-suasion principle: paint the ownership experience so vividly that buying becomes the consistent next step.

PLC 3 answers: "Here's what your life looks like on the other side."

### PLC Reminder Emails

Each PLC should have a reminder email 24-48 hours later for non-openers. The reminder uses a different subject line and a different angle into the same content. Not "Did you miss this?" but a new hook that stands on its own: "The part about [specific insight] is what changed everything for me." Reminders typically recover 15-25% of the audience that missed the original send.

### Phase 5: Cart Open

**Job:** Reveal the offer. Full value stack.

This email is the longest in the sequence. Components: what's included, who it's for, bonuses, pricing, guarantee, deadline. Don't bury the CTA — it should appear early AND at the end. By this point, reciprocity, authority, social proof, community, and anticipation are all active. The offer is the payoff, not the pitch.

### Phase 6: Cart Close

**Job:** Drive the final decision. This is where 50%+ of revenue happens (Walker).

The close is not one email — it is an escalating sequence over 48 hours. Each email has a different angle and rising urgency.

| Timing | Email focus | Emotional register | Length |
|--------|-----------|-------------------|--------|
| 48h before close | "Doors closing soon" + FAQ/objection handling | Helpful, informative | Medium (150-200 words) |
| 24h before | Social proof dump + "last chance to ask" | Confident, proof-heavy | Medium |
| Morning of close day | Direct, one clear CTA | Urgent, clean | Short (60-80 words) |
| 4h before | Scarcity + one testimonial | Emotional, specific | Short (50-70 words) |
| Final (1h or at close) | "Closing now" | Pure urgency, no fluff | Shortest (30-50 words) |

Critical rule: the scarcity must be real. Fake deadlines destroy trust permanently. The reason the close works is because the audience trusts that you mean it — because during the pre-launch, you built that trust through genuine value delivery.

## Mental Triggers Deployment

These triggers are not isolated tactics — they are layered so that by cart open, the prospect is experiencing all of them simultaneously. Then scarcity closes the deal.

| Phase | Primary trigger | How it shows up |
|-------|----------------|-----------------|
| PPL | Anticipation, Community | "Something's coming" + survey creates involvement |
| PLC 1 | Reciprocity | Massive free value creates obligation |
| PLC 2 | Authority, Social Proof | Credentials demonstrated through teaching + case studies |
| PLC 3 | Social Proof, Liking | Testimonials + "people like you" framing |
| Cart Open | Commitment/Consistency | They've consumed 3 pieces — buying is consistent with their behavior |
| Cart Close | Scarcity, Loss Aversion | Real deadline, real consequences of not acting |

The cart-open email should not just announce — it should remind the prospect of every trigger they have already experienced, then add the final one. "Over the last 10 days, you've seen the framework. You've heard from Sarah and Marcus about their results. Now it's time."

**Pre-suasion across the sequence (Cialdini):** Each email is not just content — it is pre-suasion for the next email. PLC 1 reframes the problem so PLC 2 lands on prepared ground. PLC 2 builds authority so PLC 3's testimonials feel credible. PLC 3 creates the ownership feeling so the cart-open email feels like the natural next step, not a pivot to selling. The email before the pitch is always doing the pitch's heavy lifting. If you write each email in isolation, you lose this compounding effect.

**Unity (Cialdini):** The entire pre-launch should position the brand and the prospect on the same side. Shared enemy framing — "the old way of doing things," "the broken playbook everyone follows" — creates instant unity. The prospect is not being sold to from the outside. They are being invited in by someone who has been where they are.

| Day | Email | Job | Key element |
|-----|-------|-----|-------------|
| Day -14 | PPL #1 | Seed curiosity | "Something big is coming" |

| Day -7 | PLC 1 email | Drive to content | Opportunity + value |
| Day -5 | PLC 1 reminder | Catch non-openers | Different angle, same content |
| Day -4 | PLC 2 email | Drive to content | Transformation + proof |
| Day -2 | PLC 3 email | Drive to content | Ownership + testimonials |
| Day 0 | Cart Open | Reveal the offer | Full value stack |
| Day 1 | Cart Open reminder | Catch non-openers | Different angle/bonus highlight |
| Day 2 | Social proof | Build confidence | Testimonial + FAQ |
| Day 3 | Objection handler | Remove barriers | Address #1 objection |
| Day 4 | 48h warning | Begin close sequence | Urgency + FAQ |
| Day 5 | 24h warning | Escalate | Social proof dump |
| Day 6 AM | Morning of close | Final push | Direct, short |
| Day 6 -4h | 4h warning | Scarcity | One testimonial |
| Day 6 close | Final | Close | "Doors closed" |

This map is a template. Compressed launches (3-5 days) and extended launches (21+ days) need different pacing — see Timing Compression below.

### Downsell Sequence — After Cart Close (Hormozi)

When the primary offer is declined (cart closes without purchase), don't repeat the same offer with more urgency — offer structured alternatives. This sequence fires after the cart-close deadline for non-buyers only.

| Email | Timing | Downsell Type | Emotional Job |
|-------|--------|---------------|---------------|
| DS 1 | +24h after close | **Payment Plan** | "The price isn't the problem — the payment structure is." Offer the same product split into installments. Frame as access, not discount. Same value, different structure. |
| DS 2 | +48h after close | **Trial with Penalty** | "Try it for [period] — if it's not right, cancel before [date]." Lower the commitment threshold. The penalty (auto-charge after trial) creates a real decision point without discounting the product. |
| DS 3 | +72h after close | **Feature Downsell** | "The full program isn't for everyone. Here's [lighter version] at [lower price]." Strip features to create a genuine lower-tier offer. Not the same product at a discount — a different product at a different price point. |

Each downsell email acknowledges the reader's decision not to buy without judgment. "You decided [offer] wasn't right — I respect that." Then presents the alternative as a different solution to the same problem, not a consolation prize. The sequence stops as soon as the reader purchases any downsell tier. Never stack downsells on someone who already said yes to one.

**Anti-pattern:** Repeating the same offer with increasing desperation. "Last chance!" → "Extended deadline!" → "One more day!" teaches the reader that your deadlines are fake and that waiting is rewarded. Downsells offer genuinely different structures, not the same structure with a longer fuse.

**Segmentation note:** After cart open, segment engaged (watched PLCs) from cold (didn't watch). Engaged prospects get direct offer emails. Cold prospects get condensed value + offer emails — they need the case made in the email itself because they missed the content. Never send cart-close emails to people who already bought.

## Timing Compression

Not every launch has 21 days of runway. Adapt the sequence to fit.

**Compressed launch (3-5 days):**
- Skip PPL entirely — not enough time to build anticipation
- Combine PLC 1 + 2 into a single piece, PLC 3 stays separate
- Cart open follows PLC 3 by 24 hours (not 48-72)
- Cart-close sequence compresses to 3 emails: 24h warning, morning of, final
- Every email does double duty: value + offer mechanics

Compressed map example (5-day flash launch):

| Day | Email | Job |
|-----|-------|-----|
| Day 0 | PLC 1+2 combined | Opportunity + transformation in one piece |
| Day 1 | PLC 3 | Ownership experience + testimonials |
| Day 2 | Cart Open | Full value stack |
| Day 3 | Social proof + objection | Build confidence, handle #1 objection |
| Day 4 AM | 24h warning | Urgency + final testimonial |
| Day 4 PM | Final hours | Short, direct, deadline + link |
| Day 4 close | Final | "Doors closed" |

**Standard launch (10-14 days):**
- The full email map above. PPL seeds, PLCs educate, cart runs 5-7 days.

**Extended launch (21+ days):**
- Add a third PPL email (case study teaser or behind-the-scenes)
- Add a PLC 1.5 email — a follow-up that shares audience reactions to PLC 1
- Add bonus reveal emails during the cart-open window (Day 1-3)
- Space urgency emails further apart — don't cluster them too early
- Add a "case study" email on Day 2-3 (full result story from a past customer)
- The close sequence stays the same — always 48 hours, always escalating

**Key principle across all timelines:** The close sequence never compresses below 3 emails. Even in a 3-day launch, the final 24 hours should have a morning email, an afternoon email, and a final-hour email. This is where the revenue lives.

## Before/After Examples

### Cart-Close Email: 48 Hours Before

**Before (generic urgency):**
> "Just a reminder that our program closes soon! Don't miss out on this amazing opportunity to transform your business. Click here to join before it's too late."

**After (specific, emotional, proof-driven):**
> "Quick update: 127 people have joined the [Program Name] since Thursday. Enrollment closes at midnight on Sunday.
>
> If you're on the fence, here's what I'd consider: the live cohort starts Monday. That means you'll be building your first [specific outcome] with real-time feedback within 48 hours of joining. Wait for the next round, and that's September.
>
> Here's what Sarah said after going through it last month: '[specific testimonial with numbers].'
>
> If you have questions, reply to this email. I'm reading everything today.
>
> [CTA link]"

The strong version uses real social proof (127 people), a real reason behind the deadline (cohort starts Monday), a specific testimonial, and a low-friction way to handle objections (reply to the email).

### PLC Email: Driving to Content

**Before (teaching the HOW):**
> "Email marketing is one of the most powerful tools for your business. Here are 5 tips to write better emails: 1) Use a strong subject line. 2) Personalize the opening. 3) Keep it short. 4) Include one clear CTA. 5) Test your send times. Join me on Thursday to learn more advanced strategies!"

**After (teaching WHAT/WHY with open loop):**
> "There's a shift happening in how the most profitable small businesses sell online. They've stopped relying on ads and started using a launch model that turns a 500-person email list into $15K-$50K in a single week.
>
> I've done it. My students have done it. And the opportunity is wide open because 95% of creators still launch the old way — build it, post about it, hope for sales.
>
> I just released a free training that breaks down the entire model. No fluff, no pitch — just the framework. Watch it here: [link]
>
> In 3 days I'll show you the exact sequence that turns this into revenue. But this training is where it starts."

The weak version teaches HOW (the tips) and kills the reason to show up for the content. The strong version teaches WHAT (the model exists) and WHY (the opportunity is open), then creates an open loop to the next PLC.

## Anti-Patterns

- **Launching without pre-launch content.** Going straight to cart open skips the sideways sales letter entirely. No reciprocity, no authority, no anticipation — just a cold pitch. The pre-launch IS the selling.
- **All urgency, no value in the cart-close sequence.** Pure "BUY NOW" without addressing objections. Each close email needs a reason beyond the deadline — an FAQ answer, a testimonial, a reframed objection.
- **Same emotional energy for every email.** No escalation. The sequence should build from curiosity (PPL) to value (PLCs) to excitement (cart open) to urgency (cart close). Flat emotional energy kills momentum.
- **Fake scarcity or manufactured urgency.** "Only 10 spots left!" when there are unlimited spots. Use real deadlines with real reasons (cohort starts, bonuses expire, price increases). The audience can smell manufactured scarcity.
- **Teaching the HOW in PLCs.** Giving away the full solution removes buying motivation. PLCs teach WHAT is possible and WHY it works — the HOW is what they pay for.
- **Cart-open email that buries the CTA below 500 words of intro.** The offer email is long, but the first CTA should appear within 150 words. Let eager buyers buy immediately. The rest of the email is for those who need more.
- **No segmentation between engaged and cold.** Someone who watched all three PLCs needs a different cart-open email than someone who opened nothing. Same message to both means one group gets too little context and the other gets too much.
- **Sending cart-close emails to people who already bought.** Nothing erodes trust faster than urgency emails hitting someone who already said yes. Suppress buyers from the close sequence.
- **One cart-close email instead of an escalating sequence.** 50%+ of launch revenue happens in the final 24 hours. A single "last chance" email leaves most of that revenue on the table. The close is 4-5 emails in 48 hours, each with a distinct angle: recap/value stack, objection handling, social proof dump, final warning. Don't apologize for the volume — the people still on the fence need every angle to push past the final hesitation.
- **Each PLC as standalone content.** The pieces must form a narrative arc. PLC 1 sets up what PLC 2 resolves. PLC 2 builds the case that PLC 3 makes personal. Claude treats them as independent articles — they are chapters in one story.
