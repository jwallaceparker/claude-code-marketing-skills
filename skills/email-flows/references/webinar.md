# Webinar Email Flow

*The complete email arc from registration confirmation to replay expiration.*

Sources: Fladlien (One to Many), Brunson (Expert Secrets, Dotcom Secrets), Walker (Launch), Coleman (Never Lose a Customer Again), Collier, Kennedy, Cialdini, Cardone (The Closer's Survival Guide — authority/partner closes, proxy seller arming)

---

## Webinar Intake

Before writing, gather these details from the user. Use `AskUserQuestion` or conversational follow-up — don't dump all questions at once.

**Required:**
- Webinar date, time, and timezone
- Webinar title or topic
- Replay expiration date (when does the replay come down?)
- Is there an offer/pitch at the end? What is it? What's the price?
- Who is the host/presenter? (name for the "from" line and sign-off)

**If available:**
- Registration page URL
- Webinar room link (Zoom, etc.)
- Fast-action bonuses or limited-time sweeteners
- Known objections specific to this offer (check BRIEF.md Objections section)
- Whether attendees and no-shows should get different post-webinar sequences

If the user doesn't have all of this, draft with placeholders marked `[PLACEHOLDER: ...]` and note what's missing.

## The Three Phases

Every webinar flow has three phases. Each has a different emotional landscape and a different job.

| Phase | Timing | Emotional state | Job |
|-------|--------|-----------------|-----|
| **Confirmation** | Immediate on registration | Excitement + mild doubt | Validate the decision, build anticipation, deliver logistics |
| **Pre-call** | Registration → show day | Anticipation → urgency | Shift beliefs, build show rate, make missing feel costly |
| **Post-call** | Show day → replay expiration | Interest → consideration → decision pressure | Replay access, objection handling, urgency escalation, close |

The phases are one continuous emotional arc, not three separate campaigns. Confirmation plants seeds that pre-call waters. Pre-call builds beliefs that post-call harvests. Open loops tie them together.

---

## Phase 1: Confirmation Email

**Send:** Immediately on registration
**Length:** ~80-100 words
**Lead type:** Direct (they just took action — match their energy)

The confirmation email is the most-opened email in the entire sequence. Treat it accordingly.

**Lead with excitement, not logistics.** "You're in" not "Registration confirmed." "Here's what you'll walk away with" not "Here are the details of your upcoming webinar."

Coleman's Admit phase: the moment after a decision, people need confirmation they chose well. The confirmation email's job is to make them glad they signed up — not to dump logistics on them.

**Must include:**
- Excitement lead (1-2 sentences)
- What they'll learn or walk away with (specific, not vague)
- Date, time, timezone
- Calendar link (Add to Google Calendar / Add to Outlook)
- Room link or "we'll send the link before the event"
- First open loop: tease something specific they'll learn ("I'll show you the exact framework that...")

**What Claude gets wrong:** Writes the confirmation email like an order receipt. "Thank you for registering for [Webinar Title]. The event will take place on [Date] at [Time]. Click here to add it to your calendar." This is correct but dead. It should feel like getting accepted to something, not receiving a confirmation number.

| Before (transactional) | After (excitement-first) |
|------------------------|--------------------------|
| "Thank you for registering for 'Scale Your Agency.' The webinar is on Tuesday, Feb 18 at 2pm ET. Click below to add to your calendar." | "You're in. Tuesday at 2pm ET, I'm showing you the exact system I used to go from 3 clients to 27 — without hiring a single salesperson. Add it to your calendar now so you don't miss it. I'll send the room link the morning of." |

## Phase 2: Pre-Call Sequence

The pre-call sequence does two jobs: shift beliefs and protect show rate. Every email should do at least one.

### Belief Shifting and Persuasional Foreplay

People register with three unspoken doubts (Brunson's three false beliefs). The pre-call sequence addresses them one at a time — never all at once.

| Belief | The doubt | How to shift it |
|--------|-----------|-----------------|
| **Vehicle** | "Will this approach actually work?" | Share a result, a case study, or a mechanism that makes the approach feel inevitable. Teach WHAT and WHY, not HOW (Fladlien). |
| **Internal** | "Can I actually pull this off?" | Show someone like them who did it. "Sarah had no technical background and closed her first deal in week 2." Identity proximity. |
| **External** | "Will my situation allow this?" | Pre-empt the circumstantial excuse. "This works whether you have a team of 10 or you're a solo operator." |

Each pre-call email targets ONE belief. Don't stack all three into one email — they dilute each other.

**Persuasional foreplay (Fladlien):** Pre-call emails can seed desire for offer components without revealing they're included. Mention a tool, spreadsheet, or framework you'll show during the webinar — "I'll walk you through the exact spreadsheet I use to track this." During the webinar, show it. During the pitch, reveal it as a bonus. The email planted the seed, the webinar watered it, the pitch harvests it.

**Blind bullets in pre-call:** Use blind bullets to build desire for the webinar content. "I'll show you the 3-minute audit that finds hidden revenue in any pipeline" — the benefit is clear, the method is hidden. This is different from open loops (which point to the next email). Blind bullets point to the webinar itself.

### Show Rate Protection

Show rate drops as time passes. Emails closer to the event should be shorter, more urgent, and more direct. The emotional job shifts from "anticipation" to "don't miss this."

### Pre-Call Email Map

Adapt based on how many days between registration and webinar. This assumes 5-7 days of lead time.

| # | Email | Timing | Emotional job | Belief | Length |
|---|-------|--------|---------------|--------|--------|
| 1 | Confirmation | Immediate | Excitement, validation | — | ~80-100 words |
| 2 | Why this matters | Day after registration | Curiosity, importance | Vehicle | ~120-150 words |
| 3 | "Someone like you" | 2-3 days before | Identification, possibility | Internal | ~100-130 words |
| 4 | "No excuses" | Day before or 2 days before | Confidence, anticipation | External | ~60-80 words |
| 5 | Morning of | Morning of webinar | Urgency, anticipation | — | ~40-50 words |
| 6 | 1 hour before | 1 hour before | Direct urgency | — | ~25-35 words |
| 7 | Live now | Start time | Immediate action | — | ~15 words |
| 8 | Late joiner | 15-20 min after start | FOMO recovery | — | ~30-40 words |

**Key pattern:** Length decreases as the event approaches. The further out, the more room for story and belief-shifting. The closer in, the more the email becomes a directional signal: "Go here. Now."

### The "Live Now" Email

The shortest email in the pre-call sequence. Subject line does the heavy lifting. Body is one line and a link.

**Subject:** "We're live — join now"
**Body:** "[First name], we just started. Join here: [link]"

That's it. No recap. No "here's what you'll learn." They already know. Get them in the room.

### The "Late Joiner" Email

Sent to non-openers of the "Live now" email, 15-20 minutes after start. For people who missed the start or are on the fence.

**Subject:** "Still time to join [First name]"
**Body:** "We're 15 minutes in and I just covered [specific thing]. Still time to catch the rest. [link]"

### Pre-Call Anti-Patterns

- **The identical reminder:** Don't send 5 emails that all say "Don't forget, the webinar is Tuesday!" with slightly different subject lines. Each email needs a distinct emotional job.
- **Logistics-only emails:** Every pre-call email should shift a belief OR build anticipation — not just repeat the date and time.
- **Teaching too much:** Pre-call emails should teach WHAT and WHY, not HOW. If you give them the full framework before the webinar, they have no reason to show up. Create "I need to learn this" not "I already know this."

## Phase 3: Post-Call Sequence

The post-call sequence converts interest into action. The emotional arc: excitement from the webinar → consideration → objection resolution → urgency → decision.

### Certainty Targeting (Belfort)

Each post-call email should target ONE specific certainty rather than repeating the full pitch. The webinar already delivered the complete argument. The follow-up sequence fills whichever gap remains for each reader.

| Email | Certainty target | What it looks like |
|-------|-----------------|-------------------|
| Email 1 (Replay) | Product certainty | New case study or result not shown in the webinar. "One thing I didn't have time to share..." |
| Email 2 | Personal trust | Founder story, behind-the-scenes, or vulnerability. Why you built this, what you got wrong first, what you care about beyond the sale. |
| Email 3 | Company trust | Milestone, team depth, guarantee detail, or institutional proof. "We've now served 400+ operators" or "Here's exactly what the guarantee covers." |
| Email 4 | Action threshold | Urgency + cost-of-inaction. Lower the threshold by amplifying what staying the same costs them. |

This doesn't replace the existing post-call email map — it layers onto it. When writing each email, identify which certainty it serves first, then apply the appropriate technique (objection reframe, urgency type, two futures, etc.) in service of that certainty.

### The Loop Pattern (Belfort)

Follow-up emails should loop, not just remind. The difference:

**Reminder + CTA (weak):** "Don't forget, the replay expires Sunday. Watch it here."

**Loop (strong):** [Hook] → [Raise one certainty with new proof] → [Bridge back to offer] → [CTA]

The loop introduces new information that raises a specific certainty, then re-presents the offer from that elevated position. Each email earns the right to show the CTA again by giving the reader a new reason to believe — not just a new reminder to act.

Example loop for a product-certainty email:
> "A creator named Marcus sent me his results yesterday. He implemented the system from the webinar 3 weeks ago. His pipeline went from 12 to 47 verified leads — using the exact framework I walked through on Tuesday. The replay is still up until Sunday. If Marcus's situation sounds like yours, watch the section starting at 22:15. That's where the framework clicks. [link]"

The hook is Marcus's result. The certainty raised is product certainty (the system works for real people). The bridge connects his result to the webinar content. The CTA follows naturally. Compare this to "Just a reminder — the replay expires Sunday!" which adds nothing new.

### Replay Email

**Send:** Immediately after webinar ends or the following morning (test both)
**Length:** ~50-80 words

The replay email is NOT the sales email. Its job is to get them to watch — not to close. If you put the full offer, all the bonuses, and a "buy now" button in the replay email, you're asking someone to buy before they've experienced the pitch.

**Use ownership language (Fladlien):** Frame the webinar content as something they now HAVE. "You now have the exact framework" not "I taught a framework." This creates psychological investment — losing it (by not acting) triggers loss aversion.

**Include:**
- "Here's the replay" (don't bury the link)
- One highlight from the webinar ("The part about [specific moment] starts at 14:22")
- Tease: "Watch through to the end — the offer I made is only available until [deadline]"

### Replay Page Strategy

If the user controls the replay page, note these Fladlien principles:
- **Autoplay the video** — not autoplaying loses 15%+ of viewers who never press play
- **Delay the buy button** until the pitch point (~75 min in). Showing it early lets them dismiss the offer before the webinar builds the case.
- **Edit the replay** — cut dead air at the start. Open strong.
- **Expire the replay when the offer expires** — this forces consumption and creates genuine urgency. "We often make more money from the replay than from the webinar itself."

### Objection Handling Emails

One objection per email. Not all at once. Each email addresses the most likely concern at that point in the decision process.

Use Fladlien's four reframes:

| Reframe | What it does | Example |
|---------|-------------|---------|
| **Price** | Changes the denominator — cost per day, per use, per result | "$997 sounds like a lot. That's $2.73/day for a year. You spend more on coffee." |
| **Time** | Shows net time — what they'll save vs. what they'll invest | "The course takes 6 hours. You'll save 6 hours every week. ROI in week one." |
| **Identity** | Connects the purchase to who they want to become | "The operators who scale past $50K/month all have one thing in common: a system for this." |
| **Risk** | Inverts the risk — the risk of NOT buying | "You can keep spending 10 hours/week prospecting. Or you can spend 6 hours learning a system that eliminates it." |

**The linguistic formula (Fladlien):** For each objection email, follow this structure:
1. **Acknowledge and soften** — "You might be wondering..." not "You're worried about..." Wondering is soft. Worry is hard.
2. **Validate and reframe** — agree with the feeling, change the frame using one of the four reframes above
3. **Present a target** — give them something specific to look for that disproves the objection. "Watch the replay at 34:15 — that's where Sarah explains what happened in her first week."

**Cycle objection types** — don't handle them in a block. Alternate: value email, price reframe, insight email, fit reframe. The reader never knows when the next close is coming, so they can't put their guard up (Fladlien's cycling close pattern adapted for email).

**Sequence the objections by likelihood.** Most common first. The typical order:
1. Price ("Is it worth it?")
2. Timing ("Is now the right time?")
3. Fit ("Will this work for my situation?")
4. Trust ("Can I trust this person/product?")

### Urgency Architecture

Don't bolt urgency onto the end. Layer it across the post-call sequence using Fladlien's four urgency types:

| Layer | What it is | When to introduce | Example |
|-------|-----------|-------------------|---------|
| **Contextual** | Market conditions that make acting now smarter than later | Early post-call | "The algorithm changed last month. Operators who adapt now will dominate Q2." |
| **Competitive** | Others are already doing this | Mid post-call | "47 operators enrolled during the webinar. Your competitors might be among them." |
| **Offer** | The bonuses or pricing expire | As deadline approaches | "The 3 bonus templates disappear when the replay comes down." |
| **Cost-of-inaction** | What staying the same costs them | Final emails | "Every week without this system is another 10 hours of manual prospecting." |

Each layer is introduced once, then reinforced. Don't lead with "offer urgency" — it feels like a sales tactic. Start with contextual (the world is changing) and build to offer (the deal is ending).

### The Two Futures Close

The most powerful close technique for the final 1-2 emails (Fladlien). Paint two futures:

**Future A (they buy):** Specific, sensory, time-bound. "Thirty days from now, you open your laptop on Monday morning. Your pipeline has 50 verified creators. Your team knows exactly who's talking to whom. You spend your morning closing deals, not searching YouTube."

**Future B (they don't):** Equally specific. Not threatening — just honest. "Or you close this email, go back to your spreadsheet, and spend another Tuesday night with 47 tabs open. Same process. Same results. Same frustration."

The contrast does the selling. Don't argue for Future A. Let the reader feel the gap.

### Post-Call Email Map

Adapt based on how many days between webinar and replay expiration. This assumes a 5-day window.

| # | Email | Timing | Emotional job | Length |
|---|-------|--------|---------------|--------|
| 1 | Replay | Webinar evening or next morning | Get them to watch | ~50-80 words |
| 2 | Key insight | Day after replay | Remind, create value, tease offer | ~100-130 words |
| 3 | Objection: Price | Day 2-3 | Reframe the investment | ~80-120 words |
| 4 | Objection: Fit / Trust | Day 3-4 | "Someone like you" result story | ~80-100 words |
| 5 | 48 hours | 48 hrs before deadline | Recap value, remind of deadline | ~60-80 words |
| 6 | 24 hours | 24 hrs before deadline | Handle last objection, two futures | ~60-80 words |
| 7 | Final morning | Morning of deadline | "Today is the last day" | ~40-50 words |
| 8 | Final hours | 4 hrs before deadline | Short, direct, deadline + link | ~25-35 words |
| 9 | Last call | 1 hr before deadline | Shortest email. Deadline. Link. Done. | ~15-20 words |

**Key pattern:** Same as pre-call — length decreases as the deadline approaches. Emails 1-4 do the persuasion work. Emails 5-9 are about decision pressure, not new information.

### Post-Call Anti-Patterns

- **"Watch the replay" x7:** Every post-call email says "watch the replay" with no escalation. The replay is a vehicle, not the message. Lead with the insight, objection, or urgency — then link the replay.
- **All objections in one email:** "Here are 5 reasons to join" is a listicle, not persuasion. One objection per email, handled with depth.
- **Fake urgency:** "Only 10 spots left!" when there are unlimited spots. Use the real deadline — the replay expiration. That's genuine and it's enough.
- **Same email to attendees and no-shows:** Attendees saw the pitch. No-shows didn't. If the user has segmentation capability, note which emails should differ.
- **Selling before the replay:** Don't put the full offer in the replay email. Let the webinar do the selling. The email's job is to get them to the replay.

### Non-Buyer Segmentation (Fenton & Waltz)

Non-buyers are not a monolith. The subscriber who watched the full webinar and didn't buy is a fundamentally different person from the one who never opened the replay email. Treating them identically wastes the best leads and annoys the rest.

Segment non-buyers by behavior and match each segment to its recovery path:

| Segment | Signal | What it means | Recovery path |
|---------|--------|---------------|---------------|
| **Full-watch non-buyer** | Watched 75%+ of replay or stayed through live pitch | Timing or fit objection — they heard the full case and weren't ready | New proof in 30-60 days: a fresh case study, an updated result, or a reframed angle. They don't need more information — they need a new reason to revisit the decision. |
| **Partial-watch drop-off** | Started replay, dropped before the pitch (watched 20-60%) | Info gap — something in the content didn't land or lost their attention | Content that fills the gap they missed. If most drop-offs happen at minute 22, the belief being presented at minute 22 isn't landing. Send an email that addresses that belief from a different angle, then re-link the replay at the relevant timestamp. |
| **No-show** | Registered, never attended, never opened replay | Reflex registration or life got in the way — they never experienced the pitch | Treat the replay as a new opportunity, not a reminder. "I hosted this last Tuesday — here's what 400 people learned" reframes it as social proof, not a guilt trip about missing. |
| **Never-opened** | Didn't open any post-call email | Not engaged with this channel — email is the wrong medium or the subject lines missed | Remove from the webinar follow-up. Continuing to email non-openers trains the spam filter and drags deliverability for the segments that matter. If you have other channels (SMS, retargeting), try those. If not, let them go. |

**Partner-pending segment (Cardone):** One segment doesn't fit the behavior-based model above: the attendee who is personally sold but needs a partner, spouse, or boss to sign off. They watched the full webinar, maybe even clicked the buy button, but they can't pull the trigger alone. Sending them another objection email treats them like a non-buyer when they're actually an unsupported buyer.

For partner-pending prospects, send a shareable summary email framed as "here's everything your [partner/spouse/co-founder] would want to see." Include:
- The offer in one sentence (what it is, who it's for, what it costs)
- The guarantee in one sentence
- One proof point (the strongest case study or result from the webinar)
- Replay link with a key timestamp ("the 3-minute section starting at 34:15 covers exactly how it works")

The framing matters: this email is not pitched at the subscriber. It's pitched at the person they need to convince. Write it so the subscriber can forward it directly without editing. No "Hey [First name]" opener that makes forwarding awkward. The subject line should signal its purpose: "Share this with [your partner] — everything they'd want to know about [offer name]."

This arms the prospect as a proxy seller. They wanted to buy. Give them the ammunition to close the deal on your behalf.

If the ESP supports behavioral segmentation, build these as separate branches in the post-call flow. If it doesn't, note the segmentation logic in the flow map so the user can implement it manually or upgrade later.

## Timing Compression

Not every webinar has 5-7 days of pre-call lead time or 5 days of post-call runway. Adapt the sequence:

**Short pre-call (1-3 days):**
- Merge belief-shifting into fewer emails (one email, two beliefs)
- Keep confirmation, morning-of, live, and late joiner — cut the middle
- Every email does double duty: belief + logistics

**Short post-call (2-3 days):**
- Merge objection emails (price + fit in one email)
- Compress the 48h close sequence: 24 hours, morning of, final hours
- Two futures close moves earlier — it's the penultimate email, not email 6 of 9

**Long post-call (7+ days):**
- Add a "bonus content" email (extra value not in the webinar)
- Add a "case study" email (full result story from a past customer)
- Space urgency emails — don't cluster them too early

## Default Timing Template

For a **Tuesday 2pm ET webinar** with **Sunday replay expiration**, here's the default schedule:

### Pre-Call
| Email | Send | Note |
|-------|------|------|
| Confirmation | On registration | Immediate |
| Why this matters | Day after registration | Vehicle belief |
| Someone like you | Saturday or Sunday | Internal belief |
| No excuses | Monday | External belief |
| Morning of | Tuesday 9am ET | Show rate |
| 1 hour before | Tuesday 1pm ET | Direct |
| Live now | Tuesday 2pm ET | Trigger: event start |
| Late joiner | Tuesday 2:20pm ET | Non-openers of "Live now" |

### Post-Call
| Email | Send | Note |
|-------|------|------|
| Replay | Tuesday 5pm ET or Wednesday 9am ET | Test both |
| Key insight | Wednesday PM or Thursday AM | Value + tease |
| Objection: Price | Thursday | Reframe |
| Objection: Fit | Friday | Result story |
| 48 hours | Friday PM | Deadline awareness |
| 24 hours | Saturday | Two futures |
| Final morning | Sunday 9am ET | Last day |
| Final hours | Sunday 4pm ET | Short + direct |
| Last call | Sunday 7pm ET | 15 words |

Adjust all times to match the webinar's timezone and the audience's habits. B2B audiences: avoid weekends for send times where possible — but deadline emails go out regardless.

## Funnel Assets

The email flow is one piece of the webinar funnel. If the user needs the surrounding pages, coordinate with the decks skill's `references/webinar.md` which covers:
- **Registration page** — news-based headlines, countdown timer, friction calibration
- **Thank-you page** — withhold webinar link (force email open), frame as action not learning
- **Post-pitch sign-up page** — treat as order form not sales page; bonuses > core offer; testimonials everywhere
- **Replay page** — autoplay, delayed buy button, edited start, expires with offer

These pages feed the email flow. The thank-you page establishes the inbox relationship. The sign-up page converts post-pitch. The replay page is where the post-call emails point.

## Re-Run Strategy

"New information creates new decisions." (Fladlien) Every six months, re-run the same webinar to the same audience. Each time produces a new pile of customers. The email flow can be reused with minimal changes — update dates, refresh the hook, adjust proof points. The webinar itself is the constant; the funnel refreshes.

When re-running, segment out previous purchasers and adjust the pre-call sequence for people who've seen it before. The replay and post-call can remain the same — if they didn't buy last time, the objections haven't changed.
