# Platforms

*Platform-specific execution for content systems. Native formats, algorithm behavior, signal design, voice adaptation, and anti-patterns per platform. Use when building the Platform Strategy section of a Content System Document.*

Sources: Brunson (Traffic Secrets — platform-native formats, Dream 100), Berger (Contagious — STEPPS levers per platform), Simler/Hanson (Elephant in the Brain — signal design, countersignaling), Cole (Art and Business of Online Writing — headline-first, scannable structure, 1/3/1 rhythm), Holiday (Perennial Seller — owned platforms, newsletter as anchor)

See SKILL.md for core content system principles that apply across all platforms. This playbook adds the platform-specific layer: what works where, why, and what to avoid.

---

## Platform Selection

Go where your Dream 100 lives, not where's trending. The question is not "which platform is growing fastest?" — it's "where does my audience already congregate and engage?"

**Primary platform:** The one where you go deepest. Daily or near-daily presence, native format, full engagement. Choose based on: where your Dream 100 operates, where your audience already spends time, and where the format matches your natural strengths (writing → Twitter/LinkedIn, talking → YouTube/podcast, visual → Instagram/TikTok).

**Secondary platforms:** Where you repurpose. Lower cadence, derivative content from the anchor. Don't create from scratch for secondary platforms — extract from the anchor and reformat.

**The one-platform rule:** One platform done well beats four done poorly. A creator who posts daily on Twitter with sharp, consistent content will outperform a creator who posts inconsistently across Twitter, LinkedIn, Instagram, and TikTok. Depth before breadth.

## Twitter/X

**What it is for:** Real-time commentary, sharp insights, opinion-driven authority building. The "smart insider" platform — content that makes the sharer look informed and ahead of the curve.

**Native formats:**
- **One-liners:** 1-2 sentences. Opinion, observation, or reframe. The atomic unit of Twitter.
- **Threads:** 5-15 tweets. Breakdowns, frameworks, stories. The anchor format on Twitter.
- **Quote tweets:** Commentary on someone else's post. Adds your lens to existing conversation.
- **Reply engagement:** Commenting on Dream 100 posts. Not self-promotion — adding genuine value to their conversation.

**Algorithm behavior:** Replies and quote tweets generate more reach than standalone posts. Early engagement in the first 30-60 minutes matters. Threads that get engagement on tweet 1 get served further. Bookmark and share signals are weighted heavily.

**Voice register:** Confident, direct, no hedge words. Shorter sentences. Personality shows through word choice and rhythm, not explanation. Twitter punishes verbosity — every word earns its place or gets cut.

**Signal design:** Sharing a tweet = "I'm smart enough to find this" or "I agree with this smart person." Content that makes the sharer look informed, contrarian, or ahead of the mainstream gets retweeted. Content that's merely useful gets liked but not shared.

**STEPPS emphasis:** Social Currency (insider knowledge, surprising reframes) + Triggers (link to daily cues — "Every Monday when you open your analytics..." or "That moment when the client says...")

**Content type emphasis:** Opinion (40%), Teaching (30%), Story (15%), Proof (10%), Curation (5%). Twitter rewards takes and frameworks more than how-tos.

**Anti-patterns:**
- Thread intros that announce the thread: "A thread on X 🧵" — the first tweet IS the hook, not a table of contents
- Bland tips without a specific angle: "5 tips for better emails" dies. "The email trick that got me a 43% reply rate from cold leads" lives.
- Engagement bait: "Like if you agree! RT to share!" — this is begging, not content
- Over-politeness: "I think maybe it could be argued that..." — Twitter rewards directness
- Cross-posted LinkedIn content: Different platform, different register. LinkedIn posts on Twitter read as corporate.

## LinkedIn

**What it is for:** Professional authority, B2B visibility, thought leadership. The "serious professional" platform — content that signals competence, credibility, and industry insight.

**Native formats:**
- **Text posts:** 1,300 characters is the sweet spot (before the "see more" fold). Hook in the first line — that's all that shows in the feed.
- **Carousels:** Slide-format documents (PDF uploads). 8-12 slides. Visual frameworks, step-by-step breakdowns. High save rate.
- **Articles:** Long-form. Lower reach than posts but signals depth. Better for evergreen thought leadership.
- **Video:** 1-3 minutes. Face-to-camera or screen share. Native upload only — linked YouTube gets throttled.

**Algorithm behavior:** Comments drive reach more than likes. Posts that generate conversation in the first hour get pushed to broader network. Dwell time (people stopping to read) is a signal. Posting 1x/day is optimal — 2x/day can cannibalize reach.

**Voice register:** Authoritative but human. Professional without being corporate. Less aggressive than Twitter, more personal than a white paper. First-person perspective works well — "I learned," "I noticed," "Here's what happened when we..."

**Signal design:** Sharing a LinkedIn post = "I'm a thoughtful professional who stays current." Content that makes the sharer look competent and forward-thinking gets reposted. Case studies and frameworks perform well because they signal "I care about getting better at my job."

**STEPPS emphasis:** Practical Value (frameworks, templates, actionable breakdowns) + Social Currency (insider knowledge, data-backed insights)

**Content type emphasis:** Teaching (35%), Proof (25%), Story (20%), Opinion (15%), Curation (5%). LinkedIn rewards usefulness and demonstrated expertise more than hot takes.

**Anti-patterns:**
- Corporate speak: "We're excited to announce that our team has synergized..." — nobody shares this
- Hashtag stuffing: #Marketing #Growth #Leadership #Entrepreneur — this isn't 2018
- "I'm humbled to announce" / "I'm thrilled to share" — performative humility reads as inauthentic
- Walls of text with no spacing: LinkedIn's format needs line breaks. One idea per line. White space = readability.
- Inspirational quotes with no context: "The journey of a thousand miles..." — add your own insight or don't post

## Instagram

**What it is for:** Visual storytelling, personality-driven brand building, aspirational identity. The "I live like this" platform — aesthetic and lifestyle signaling.

**Native formats:**
- **Reels:** 30-90 seconds. Primary reach driver. Face-to-camera, text overlay, trending audio optional. Hook in the first second.
- **Carousels:** 5-10 slides. Educational or story-based. High save and share rate — the "practical value" format.
- **Stories:** 24-hour content. Polls, Q&A, behind-the-scenes, daily engagement. Retention tool, not reach tool.
- **Static posts:** Single image with caption. Declining reach — use sparingly for high-impact visuals.

**Algorithm behavior:** Reels get reach (shown to non-followers). Carousels get saves (algorithm rewards save-to-impression ratio). Stories get retention (keep existing followers engaged). Shares via DM are the strongest signal — content people send to friends gets pushed hardest.

**Voice register:** Casual, visual, personality-forward. Shorter captions than LinkedIn. Emoji acceptable but not mandatory. Authenticity over polish — overly produced content underperforms raw, genuine content.

**Signal design:** Sharing an Instagram post = "This represents who I am (or want to be)." Aspirational content gets shared to stories. Relatable content gets DM'd to friends. Design for the share-to-stories or DM-to-friend behavior.

**STEPPS emphasis:** Emotion (awe, humor, inspiration — high-arousal visual content) + Public (visible engagement, shareable to stories)

**Content type emphasis:** Story (30%), Proof (25%), Teaching (20%), Opinion (15%), Curation (10%). Instagram rewards personality and visual storytelling more than pure education.

**Anti-patterns:**
- Text-heavy graphics: Instagram is visual-first. A tweet screenshot as a post looks lazy.
- Repurposed Twitter content as images: Different platform, different audience behavior
- Overproduced, staged content: The "Instagram aesthetic" era is over. Authenticity outperforms perfection.
- Long captions that bury the hook: If the caption matters, the first line must earn the "more" click
- Posting reels with no hook: First second determines whether they watch. No intro, no warmup — hook immediately.

## TikTok

**What it is for:** Discovery, virality, personality-first content. The "I found this before you" platform — novelty and entertainment.

**Native formats:**
- **Short video:** 30-90 seconds optimal. Face-to-camera, screen recording with voiceover, trending format adaptations.
- **Series content:** Multi-part videos that build on each other. Algorithm rewards series viewers returning.
- **Duets/Stitches:** Responding to other creators' content. Built-in collaboration format.
- **Stories:** Ephemeral content for engaged followers.

**Algorithm behavior:** Watch time and replays are the primary signals — not followers. A video from an account with 12 followers can reach 1M views if the watch metrics are strong. Completion rate matters most: shorter videos with high completion outperform longer videos with drop-off. The algorithm tests content on small audiences first, then expands reach if signals are strong.

**Voice register:** Conversational, unpolished, personality over production. Talk like you're telling a friend, not presenting to a boardroom. Raw footage outperforms studio quality. Energy and authenticity beat polish.

**Signal design:** Sharing a TikTok = "I discovered this cool thing" or "This is so me." Discovery is the currency — people share TikToks to be the person who found it first. Content that feels like a secret or a surprise gets the most shares.

**STEPPS emphasis:** Emotion (humor, surprise, awe — the platform rewards emotional reaction) + Social Currency (discovery, being first, insider knowledge)

**Content type emphasis:** Story (30%), Opinion (25%), Teaching (25%), Proof (10%), Curation (10%). TikTok rewards personality and entertainment. Pure education without entertainment gets scrolled past.

**Anti-patterns:**
- Overproduced content: Studio lighting, teleprompter delivery, stock music — this reads as "ad" and gets skipped
- Slow intros: "Hey guys, today I want to talk about..." — you've lost them. Hook in the first second or they scroll.
- Corporate tone: TikTok's audience can smell inauthenticity instantly. Talk like a person, not a brand.
- Ignoring trends: TikTok rewards trend participation with reach. Original content matters, but trend-adapted content gets the algorithm push.
- Repurposing without reformatting: A cropped YouTube clip is not a TikTok. Shoot native.

## YouTube

**What it is for:** Long-form education, deep authority building, searchable content library. The "I invest in learning" platform — depth and expertise.

**Native formats:**
- **Long-form:** 8-20 minutes. The anchor content format. Tutorials, breakdowns, interviews, case studies.
- **Shorts:** Under 60 seconds. Reach driver for new audiences. Hook-driven, single-idea clips.
- **Community posts:** Text, polls, images. Engagement tool for existing subscribers.
- **Live streams:** Real-time interaction. Best for established audiences.

**Algorithm behavior:** Two metrics dominate: click-through rate (CTR) and average view duration (AVD). The thumbnail and title determine CTR. The content determines AVD. A video with a great thumbnail and boring content gets punished after the initial push. A video with a mediocre thumbnail never gets tested. Both must be strong.

**Voice register:** Conversational authority. Teach while entertaining. More structured than TikTok, less formal than a webinar. Personality matters — viewers subscribe to people, not topics.

**Signal design:** Sharing a YouTube video = "I'm the kind of person who watches substantive content" and "You should learn from this person too." YouTube shares signal intellectual investment and taste. Content that makes the sharer look like a curator of quality information gets forwarded.

**STEPPS emphasis:** Practical Value (useful, specific, educational content) + Stories (narrative-driven tutorials, case study storytelling)

**Content type emphasis:** Teaching (40%), Proof (20%), Story (20%), Opinion (15%), Curation (5%). YouTube rewards depth and specificity. "The Complete Guide to X" outperforms "Quick Tips on X."

**Anti-patterns:**
- Clickbait without payoff: The title promises, the content must deliver. Bait-and-switch kills retention and damages the channel.
- No clear takeaway: Every video needs to leave the viewer with something concrete. "That was interesting" isn't enough — "I know exactly what to do now" is the target.
- Talking head with no visual variety: B-roll, screen share, diagrams, text overlays — visual variety maintains attention through long-form content.
- Ignoring thumbnails: The thumbnail is the ad for the video. It gets more views than the video itself. Invest in it.
- Publishing inconsistently: YouTube rewards consistent upload schedules. One video per week on a predictable day beats sporadic bursts.

## Newsletter / Email

**What it is for:** Owned audience, direct relationship, not subject to algorithm. A strong anchor content channel — especially for creators who prioritize owned audience over platform-dependent reach. Everything else can be derivative.

**Why it matters:** Every other platform is rented land. Algorithm changes can kill reach overnight. An email list is the only audience you own completely. Newsletter → social derivative is more durable than social → newsletter conversion.

**Native format:** Weekly or bi-weekly. One core idea per issue. Consistent structure readers come to expect. Personal voice — this is a letter from a person, not a broadcast from a brand.

**Voice register:** The most personal register. Write like you're emailing one person, not a list. Conversational, opinionated, specific. The newsletter voice is the creator's core voice — other platforms adapt from this baseline.

**Signal design:** Forwarding a newsletter = "I'm connected to insider knowledge and I'm sharing it with you." Newsletters with exclusive insights, frameworks, or data get forwarded. Newsletters that are just blog post summaries don't.

**STEPPS emphasis:** Practical Value (the reader should want to forward it to a specific person) + Social Currency (insider knowledge, exclusive access)

**Content type emphasis:** Teaching (35%), Story (25%), Opinion (20%), Proof (15%), Curation (5%).

**Anti-patterns:**
- Roundups with no POV: "Here are 5 links I found interesting this week" — without your commentary, this is a robot's job
- Corporate tone: The newsletter exists because people want to hear from YOU. If it reads like a company blog, they'll unsubscribe.
- Inconsistent publishing: A weekly newsletter that ships every 10 days trains readers to stop expecting it. Pick a cadence and hold it.
- No clear value per issue: Every issue should have one thing the reader can use immediately. Not a theme — a takeaway.

## Cross-Platform Voice Adaptation

The creator's core voice stays consistent across platforms. The register — formality, sentence length, emoji, personality emphasis — shifts per platform.

| Platform | Formality | Sentence Length | Emoji | Personality Emphasis |
|----------|-----------|----------------|-------|---------------------|
| Twitter/X | Low | Short (punchy) | Minimal | Hot takes, wit |
| LinkedIn | Medium | Medium (structured) | None | Expertise, insight |
| Instagram | Low | Short (captions) | Moderate | Visual, lifestyle |
| TikTok | Very low | Spoken rhythm | None (it's video) | Energy, authenticity |
| YouTube | Medium | Conversational paragraphs | None | Teaching, depth |
| Newsletter | Low-Medium | Natural (varied) | Minimal | Full personality |

The test: if someone reads your Twitter, your LinkedIn, and your newsletter, they should recognize the same person — even though the format and register are different. Core voice = constant. Platform register = variable.
